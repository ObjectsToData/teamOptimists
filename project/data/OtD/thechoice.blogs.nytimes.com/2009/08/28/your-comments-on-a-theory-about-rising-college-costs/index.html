    Getting into College and Paying for it

    Your Comments on a Theory About Rising College Costs

    By JACK KADDEN
    August 28, 2009 9:59 amSeptember 7, 2012 3:32 pm

    Professors were quick to take issue with the argument — described in a post on Thursday –that college tuition increases are, at least in part, a result of professors who devote most of their time to research.

    The post described an opinion article in The Atlanta Journal-Constitution by John Zmirak that argued that many professors choose to focus on research, rather than teaching, driving up tuition.

    While one comment described professors as “underworked and overpaid,” and another described them as “overpaid, inept, lazy do-nothings,” others shot back that most professors work 60-hour weeks, or longer, and are paid far less than people in private industry with comparable education and experience.

    Underworked and Overpaid? I dont think so. I work 60-65 hours a week for a mid- 5 figure income, I had higher SAT scores than most doctors and lawyers, I had 11 years of higher education and have 12 years of college teaching experience.

    Others took issue with Mr. Zmirak’s logic.

    Zmirak provides absolutely no evidence for his contention that professor salaries are what has driven up the cost of higher education, and anyone who is in the field or who knows anything about it will find this claim laughable.

    And some saw political bias in Mr. Zmirak’s argument:

    Zmirak and Bauerlein rehearse a favorite canard of the right, one which is remarkable for its willful omissions, unsupported generalizations, and distortions. This is hardly surprising when the only study cited is one sponsored by the American Enterprise Institute, hardly an impartial authority on higher education.

    Many comments argued that the real culprits in the rising cost of tuition are the growing number of well-paid college administrators and the luxurious dormitories and fancy fitness centers that colleges have been building. And several pointed out that scientific research, financed by grants, not only pays for itself, but also brings in additional revenue for the university.

    The author of a study cited in Mr. Zmirak’s article, Prof. Mark Bauerlein of Emory University, was moved to respond to some of the critiques:

    My paper on research costs focused on the humanities, not the sciences. And heres what we see: an army of professors, lecturers, and graduate students being pushed to produce essays and books of little to no value. Just consider how many units a literary monograph sells today: around 300, and around 250 of those sales are standing library orders (that is, the library orders everything the press publishes). That means that, taking away friends and family, sales of these books to individuals runs in the low-two figures.

    Now, what is the cost?

    Well, the direct cost is the amount of money the press spends to review, edit, publish, advertise, and distribute the manuscript.

    The indirect cost is this: Lets say the book was written by a professor making $65K salary and $15K in benefits. The university expects that professor to devote half his or her time to research, essentially paying $40K per year for the steady production of books and articles. If that book took four years to research and write, were up to $160K in support.

    Paying for College, Emory University

    Frustrated Ex-Student August 28, 2009 · 9:21 pm

    Where, oh where does the money go?

    I don’t know. I recently graduated from Princeton with no debt, which in this market, has been a blessing. In my particular case, going to Princeton was cheaper than it would have been for me to go to a SUNY school. Schools with huge endowments can afford generous financial aid, so the price you see on the tag is not necessarily what you pay, and for some people, the cost of attending school has actually gone down, especially with new financial aid regimes at certain schools.

    That being said, I can see lots of really nice, but rather frivolous amenities provided at Princeton and other schools which drive up the price on the tag: world-class gym facilities, multiple sports teams, pep-rallies, manicured lawns, new dorms, museums, better food, campus-wide wi-fi, manicured lawns, student centers, free t-shirts, manicured lawns, cafes all over campus, subsidized travel abroad, study breaks, reunions, dances, artistic performances, free laundry, student publications, student government, and, did I mention, the perfectly manicured lawns?

    The problem isn’t the “frivolous” professors who have to do research and teach in order to get tenure and have to support their families, but rather the fact that in the “Everyone is special” generation, colleges have become less places of learning and more like glorified summer camps which go on for four years. Does the fact that I had a sandwich made to order for dinner every night in the dinning hall, or that I was able to get orchestra tickets for Wicked for $25 make me more employable? Nope. And yet, someone had to pay for that.

    As long as students and their parents (the clients) continue to be wooed by these niceties, and as long as they are willing and able to pay, universities will continue to add on to their campuses and their tuition bills.

    Humanities professors aren’t to blame. Administrators, alumni, current students, and parents are.

    Does the system need to be reformed? Yes. But no one will die because of its problems. Fix health care first, then make sure everyone has an internationally competitive elementary and secondary education. Maybe then a BA in BS won’t appear so essential…

    Bill August 28, 2009 · 10:14 pm

    Unconvinced,

    The point still holds. The average Sarah Lawrence student doesn’t pay $53k a year, but that is the figure tossed out there to bolster the argument about out-of-control costs. At the end of the day, Sarah Lawrence and every other private school deeply discounts their price for middle class students. The “real” price of Sarah Lawrence is something much lower.

    Only the poorest candidates are eligible for Pell Grant supplements. Sarah Lawrence is not having trouble filling its seats at the prices it is charging its largely middle and upper class students base. If the price was “too high” those students could have chosen from many other options.

    Bill August 28, 2009 · 10:22 pm

    Just did the math on what my salary and benefits package costs my college each year compared to the tuition dollars payed by students enrolled in my classes. (I have no TAs, aides, or even significant secretarial support.) It turns out that I get about 31% of those dollars. Is that high? It might be. After all, there are administrative costs, buildings to heat and cool, and technology to purchase and maintain. I am sure it is a MUCH higher percentage of the total cost than the Vietnamese worker who made my Nike shoes received for their labor.

    Dr John Munford August 29, 2009 · 2:22 am

    We have similar problems in Britain, where the minimum cost of tuition is more than $10,000 pa for a humanities or social science subject. Yet lecturers are poorly paid, and staff student ratios are unimpressive. Most seminar teaching is performed by post grads, and only at Oxford and Cambridge do students benefit from regular weekly tutorials.

    Where is the money going? Bloated administration, presided over by a parasitic new class of overpaid ‘university managers’, and new buildings to enable ever expanding student numbers.

    The solution? Facilitate a massive increase in competition through allowing external students to sit common exams with internal students at elite institutions. Lecturers can then get together to set up small private teaching institutions which offer the same qualifications at much lower cost, escaping from endless committee meetings and paperwork, and enjoy enough free time to engage in worthwhile private scholarship.

    the_real_drmark August 29, 2009 · 8:32 am

    I don’t know about the humanities in particular, but certainly the increase in faculty salaries over the past 20 years is one factor in rising higher ed costs.

    There was a time when faculty were paid very little. The general cost of living was low and faculty lifestyles had an almost bohemian quality. But times have changed. Salaries in the other major professions have grown as has the cost of living. What are institutions to do? Already, the faculty at many second tier colleges and universities are not the brightest, not to mention that they are often questionable mentors for young people. If schools suppress salaries, they will get an even more marginal bunch or teachers.

    newfreshman August 29, 2009 · 9:38 pm

    I agree with comment #1…there are many factors involved but a large one must be healthcare costs. We all know how much healthcare costs have ballooned recently, and as I understand it most universities offer good benefits packages to most of their employees. This has to be a factor, among other things, in how much the cost of college has been going up.

    The whole concept of employer-sponsored healthcare is really something that needs to be addressed in the United States. We’re being ripped off for healthcare, paying more than any other Western country with comparable care and these costs are being passed down to the consumer because the employers are expected to cover benefits.

    Scott White August 29, 2009 · 9:56 pm

    There is no one reason for the cost rising but a combination. For one, costs, in the decades prior to 1980, actually rose quite less than the rate of inflation (as did professor’s salaries). From sometime in the 1980′s to somewhere around 2006, price increases were double the rate of inflation and the cost of college eats up twice as much as income as it did 25 years ago. Also, the fears in the 80′s of the results in the number of students graduating from high school led colleges to be more more conscious of taking the steps to lure students, from recruiting nationally (even regional schools), even internationally, to having the best science labs, libraries, dorms, athletic complexes, dining halls, social centers, you name it. And yes, that meant paying more for professors. And colleges kept funding it all by stretching the limits of the elasticity of demand for expensive colleges (and of the value of the prestige of the right college). Add this to vastly increased energy costs, health insurance costs, pension costs, etc. and you have the perfect brew for some very expensive colleges.

    I believe the cost of research by faculty is much more so at large research universities than smaller liberal arts colleges. But these smaller schools suffer from the lack of economy of scale. Every expense per pupil, from food preparation, to shovelling snow to buying toilet paper, is higher at a smaller college.

    Ann Moody August 30, 2009 · 8:29 am

    I studied communication in the late 1980′s with the intention of becoming a career professor. It was the emphasis on research and publishing, and the lack of interest in practical skills, that changed my mind about academia. Mark Bauerlein is correct regarding the pressure for humanities scholars to produce useless research.

    This leads to increased prestige for the institution, which can then attract better grants, graduate students to teach basic courses, and the better researchers – who often are poor instructors – continuing a bizarre circle in higher education.

    Andrew August 30, 2009 · 9:11 am

    The cost of adding a research chemist to a faculty runs about $1m (lab renovations, start-up packages). Cost of a philosopher of ethics or professor of American Lit? Maybe a fresh coat of paint on a used office and light bulbs. Chemists are paid more annually and need far more in materials support. Chemistry journal costs ludicrously exceed those of a few humanities books, not to mention the relative costs of text books used in class. Few students major in chemistry and teaching loads are often half those of an English teacher. Grants from the National Science Foundation (socialism?) do not cover the costs of the chemist. We are convinced that progress in science is worth the high cost, but why must humanistic values struggle so hard to justify themselves? No doubt because they teach people to think critically about the world around them, always a threat to ideologues.

    It’s hard to quantify the good to society that comes from having a broadly educated populace. I, for one, wouldn’t want to live in a society that didn’t value the genius of Mark Twain or Toni Morrison, that didn’t know how to think about the difference between right and wrong.

    MW August 30, 2009 · 10:30 pm

    Bill- Unconvinced is right on here.

    As the price continues increasing (much faster than inflation) fewer and fewer families will be able to pay the full tuition, which is subsidizing tuition for those not paying full price. Many liberal arts colleges, including St. John’s, did see a decline in applications this year (WP 8-27-09).

    It isn’t true that basically all students receive tuition rebates. Since we are using Sarah Lawrence as an example, 683 of 1,328 undergrads received a need-based award, averaging $26,000 each. (4 received non-need-based awards). That leaves 49% who paid the full cost of $53,000+.

    What kind of system is this where, because of the outrageous sticker price, half of the student body is subsidized by the other half? It’s a great deal for students from lower class and middle-lower class backgrounds. And the most upper class students presumably can afford it. But I imagine the middle-class student presence at Sarah Lawrence and other like schools is lacking. If middle class/upper-middle class students are there, they will be mired in debt when they finish.

    Unconvinced August 31, 2009 · 12:16 am

    Bill,

    I guess I wasn’t sufficiently clear. I realize that only a small percentage of families pay the “sticker price” at most private colleges; what I was saying is that those few families who are expected to pay sticker price are being asked, in effect, to subsidize all the other students, and that those families may soon conclude that they don’t care to do so, especially when the colleges are cutting programs, faculty, etc. I believe that is already happening, and that a growing number of the applicants being turned away are either unwilling, or unable, to pay the sticker price. That is, I don’t think that colleges are turning away a great number of students who are willing and able to pay the stated tuition, so this isn’t a case of colleges operating within an “efficient market” model.

    Bill August 31, 2009 · 6:11 pm

    Unconvinced,

    Time will tell if you are right. If the rich no longer see elite liberal arts colleges as worth the sticker price, they will stop sending their kids there, and those schools will have to rethink their pricing strategy. But it seems to have been working for them for decades, when there are an abundance of much cheaper options out there.

    I teach at one of those much cheaper options (total tuition costs about 40% of Sarah Lawrence’s cost) and many of the people complaining about the costs of the elites wouldn’t even think of sending their kids to a college like mine, whose name brings with it no status. If they did, they’d find that their kid would probably get far more personal attention than they get at an elite school.

    In the end, I think that college tuition appears to be driven by market forces, albeit a market in which institutions are involved in complex pricing strategies based on each applicant’s family income. This pricing strategy is less complicated than that employed by the airlines, which alter the price of the same seat sometimes multiple times on the same day, and which tries to use preferred leaving and returning times to calculate exactly how much that flight is worth to a particular customer.

    Describing Colleges and universities as an oligopoly involved in price-fixing, as some posters have, is just silly. Below the elite colleges, there is a vast sea of colleges and delivering high quality educations who are competing fiercely for every student they can get. Many of those complaining that they cannot afford the elite schools could save themselves buckets of money by looking at the unlimited alternatives. The fact that they aren’t isn’t because someone is FORCING them to send their kids to an elite. It is because they perceive the value of a degree from one of these institutions as having an extraordinary high value.

    PR September 1, 2009 · 9:42 am

    Do the arithmetic. The student-faculty ratio at most liberal arts colleges is about 10:1. If the students are paying $40,000 a year, they generate $400,000 per faculty member. No more than a quarter of that goes to the professor. Moreover, tuition is only a part of university income, so faculty salaries will account for less than a quarter of any university budget. So its unlikely that the increase in costs is due to faculty salaries.

    Lizzie September 2, 2009 · 8:20 am

    New York is home to the Star Professor. If you live here and have any social or professional connection to academia, you probably know a half dozen professors who make a quarter million dollars, teach only one [graduate] course per semester, and spend the rest of their time going to conferences in Paris and London. Then, every seven years, they get a paid year off, when they’re relieved of having to teach their one course, so they can “concentrate on their research.”

    It’s easy to see this and assume it’s the norm in academia. But it’s really one of those only-in-New-York (and perhaps Cambridge) situations. The is really the underpaid, untenured adjunct with no benefits.

    Tom E. September 8, 2009 · 3:22 pm

    Don’t pound down research, people.
    Remember that research is supposed to be a very important part of what universities do, especially in USA. University is not just the next chapter of education after high school. Consider that it may be quite silly to ask a research professor to begin more teaching — it is not what he/she does best, and it is not what he was hired to do in the first place. And if the research efforts are being managed well, the research grants BRING funds to the university and into your community.
