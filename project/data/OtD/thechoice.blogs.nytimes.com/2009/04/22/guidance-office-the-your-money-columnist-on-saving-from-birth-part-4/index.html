    Getting into College and Paying for it

    Guidance Office: The ‘Your Money’ Columnist on Saving From Birth, Part 4

    By RON LIEBER
    April 22, 2009 11:58 amSeptember 7, 2012 3:39 pm
    Guidance Office
    College Savings Q & A

    Ron Lieber, The Times’s “Your Money” columnist, replies to reader-submitted questions.

    • All Guidance Office Posts

    Each day this week in the “Guidance Office,” Ron Lieber, who writes the “Your Money” column for The Times’s business section, is answering selected reader questions related to long-term savings for college (and short-term, too.) You can put a question to him by using the comment box on our original post. The questions below may have been edited for style, and length.

    We have one child, currently finishing eighth grade and are older parents (47 and 50). Our strategy to save for college has been to aim to pay off our mortgage before she starts college and to use the monthly amount we would have used for the mortgage for college expenses. Most of our savings are going to fully fund our retirement accounts, but we have additional funds in a savings account and a mutual fund. We are on track with our mortgage payoff. Can finanical aid packages offered by colleges require us to use home equity for college expenses and to take out a line of credit against a house that is paid for? It is my understanding that they cannot require use of our designated retirement funds – is that true?

    –Minta Keyes

    I put this one to Kevin McKinley of McKinley Money. Here’s what he said: Parents should certainly take potential financial aid awards into consideration when making savings and investment decisions, but those ramifications shouldnt dictate overall strategies. A familys financial situation and the financial aid climate can change dramatically in the next four years, so its difficult to count on what is true now to be true by the time their child reaches college. Even when need-based aid is awarded, it is more likely to be loans, rather than grants.

    Schools use at least two financial aid formulas when calculating awards: The Federal Methodology (FM) and The Institutional Methodology (IM). Right now, home equity is not usually included in the FM, but is often added under the IM. So one school might ignore equity the family has accumulated in their home, and another might include the equity in the calculation (the family can get an idea of how they might be affected by using the calculators available at the College Board Web site.

    Im more concerned that the parents maintain liquidity so that they can pay as much out-of-pocket as possible for any future expenses, including college. I would prefer that the family divert some of their savings and money that is going to paying off their mortgage into a 529 college savings plan, which may reduce their current and future taxes, yet may still be somewhat sheltered from financial aid calculations. (Ron interjecting here: Please see the answer to the next question for more on how much of a 529 you might have to use each year).

    They are wise to continue saving money into their retirement plans, as they can borrow money to pay for college, but so far there is no such thing as a retirement loan. Plus, retirement plan assets generally are not included in either of the financial aid formulas, but money contributed to plans while the child is in school can be considered in the parental income portion of the equationeven if the contributions are going into a pre-tax plan.

    Also, the parents should establish a home equity line of credit so they can tap that portion of their assets for college expenses, or other financial emergencies.

    Our family values a quality education in the humanities and if our son is accepted to a liberal arts college or an excellent private university, we would support his being a philosophy or history major. In that case, we would apply for aid and hope that he gets some assistance for tuition. If we save more, would additional savings be factored? Could more savings lower our ability to quality for some type of assistance with the high tuition?

    –Rod

    It depends on where you place the money and how many assets you already have. According to Kal Chany, author of “Paying for College Without Going Broke,” the various financial aid formulas will generally allow you to keep a certain amount of assets for emergencies say, before they assess it for college costs. The amount may depend on the age of the parents, the number of parents involved, the number of kids that they have and the age of the children.

    No matter what, however, the formulas won’t ask you to pay more than 5.65 percent of your assets towards college costs each year, though some schools may also ask you to tap equity in your home. That percentage is for parental assets; if you make the mistake of placing assets in a child’s name, schools may take up to 25 percent of them each year.

    The bottom line is that you’re better off saving money (in the parents’ names) than not having it all, in an attempt to qualify for a bit more aid. As Mr. Chany notes, after four years you’ll still have more than 75 cents left for every dollar of assets you saved before your child started college.

    For Juniors, For Parents, For Seniors, Guidance Office, Paying for College, College Savings Q&A, Ron Leiber, Your Money

    A word of advice re 529 plans — don’t have them in the parents or the kids names — have a relative (preferably childless) — e.g. grandparents, bachelor uncles, etc., be the holders of the 529, with the kids as the beneficiaries. This way the 529 amount isn’t “captured” at all in the financial aid formulas — basically adding up to 25% of the value of the 529…

    boulderite April 22, 2009 · 2:32 pm

    We also chose to accelerate payment on our mortgage to get it paid off a year before our daughter started college. While I don’t think it helped/hurt the financial aid particularly, it definitely makes the EFC portion at least vaguely possible. I truly don’t know what we would have done if we had an EFC anything like our EFC and a mortgage payment. — particularly one in the last few years of a mortgage when interest payments are relatively low.

    Personally I’d rather have the house paid off than have $ in the 529 plan, particularly since we’re a single wage-earner household and you never know on the job front when or whether there is going to be a layoff. Financial aid may or may not be adequate, but there was no doubt that if we paid off our mortgage that we could fund any state university and many out-of-state public universities. We considered that to be the cake — if our kid wanted the frosting of an expensive LAC, she’d need to get some help with some combination of merit aid, financial aid, and loans.
