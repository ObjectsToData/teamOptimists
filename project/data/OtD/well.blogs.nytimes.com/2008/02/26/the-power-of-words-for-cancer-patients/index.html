    The Power of Words for Cancer Patients

    By TARA PARKER-POPE
    February 26, 2008 3:50 pmFebruary 26, 2008 4:04 pm
    Keeping a journal may improve the lives of cancer patients. (Angel Franco/The New York Times)

    When my mother was first diagnosed with cancer, she did something she had never done before. She started to write down her feelings.

    My mother had always been too busy for something she felt was as indulgent as keeping a journal, but in the early days of her cancer diagnosis, she found that writing down her thoughts helped her cope with the prospect of dying.

    This month, a medical journal confirms what many cancer patients intuitively know. Expressive writing, which involves writing down your deepest thoughts and feelings, may improve the quality of life for cancer patients, according to a new report in The Oncologist.

    Previous research conducted in controlled laboratory experiments has suggested that expressive writing helps physical and psychological well-being. However, the recent study was a real-world experiment, conducted in the waiting rooms of an oncology practice.

    Researchers from the Lombardi Comprehensive Cancer Center in Washington, D.C., studied the effects of expressive writing on 71 adults with leukemia or lymphoma who journaled their thoughts while waiting for their regular oncology appointments. The patients were asked to write their thoughts in answer to the question: How has cancer changed you, and how do you feel about those changes?

    After the writing assignment, about half of the cancer patients said the exercise had changed their thinking about their illness, while 35 percent reported that writing changed the way they felt about their illness. Three weeks after the writing exercise, the effect had been maintained. Writing had the biggest impact on patients who were younger and recently diagnosed. While a change in the way a patient thinks or feels about a disease may not sound like much, the findings showed that the brief writing exercise led to improved quality of life.

    “Thoughts and feelings, or the cognitive processing and emotions related to cancer, are key writing elements associated with health benefits,” said Nancy P. Morgan, director of the center’s Arts and Humanities Program. “Writing about only the facts has shown no benefit.”

    The researchers also analyzed the content of the patient writings. Most of the patients noted that cancer had been life-changing. Many patients wrote that the changes were positive ones and that cancer had altered their views about family, spirituality, work and the future. One patient wrote: “Don’t get me wrong, cancer isn’t a gift, it just showed me what the gifts in my life are.”

    My husband died almost 5 years ago after 5 years with cancer. His personality really changed during his illness and during that time I tried to find things I could read that would help me understand both his feelings and my role as his caregiver. So much of the reading seemed to be very upbeat and treated cancer as a gift. It wasn’t until I found Larry Seiver’s blog that I began to come to terms with those years. His blogs so captured the struggles, emotions, highs and lows of we faced that I was finally able to see that others had similar experiences. One of my colleagues walked into my office one day and said “you’ve really let your husgand’s illness take over your life.” Five years later, Larry wrote about how cancer takes over one’s life. I cried with relief. What a gift that his blog has become! Writing about cancer and the feelings it brings is so, so important for not only the person with cancer, but for family, caregivers, friends.

    Rodney Ezrapour January 28, 2009 · 4:10 pm

    Bravo-
    Sharing your feelings in words, written and spoken, is the best therapy.

    As a survivor of lymphoma, I am fortunate that my experience created some of the very sentiments noted in the article, namely of cancer being a life-changing experience, and in my case (like many others), the increased compassion and desire to give back.

    I wrote about my experiences to many friends through email updates; not only expressing my thoughts, but thoughts and stories from many survivors and caregivers I met, of course with no names mentioned. Besides the therapeutic effect of sharing my feelings, the feedback was incredible.

    So much so that I established an organization in support of children survivors of cancer, in efforts to harness and enhance the positive psychological effects of cancer, and enabling children survivors to help other kids, with programs of their own choice.

    Our efforts and words will also go towards dispelling myths about cancer- it is NOT a taboo, nor is it contagious.

    Anyone wanting to share their experiences, ideas, and to learn more about kids helping kids can contact me at rodney@cancerrocks.org.

    Cancer is not a choice, so rock with it – in WORDS and DEEDS.

    -Rodney

    Mletta April 2, 2009 · 11:33 am

    Sandy Gillespie writes:
    “Dad expressed regret, in his final days, that he had not audiotaped the thoughts, stories, and recollections he wanted to leave the family. Writing was not an option for him as his vision had declined severely for several years before his death. I have since written my memories of those times, based on my and my sisters’ conversations with him. This final memoir is no substitute for what he might have written himself, but it is something to share with each other and his grandchildren and great grandchildren.”

    As someone who personally and professionally believes in the importance of words and memories, we, too, tried for years to get family members to somehow commit their stories and memories to paper, tape or video.

    To no avail. For most, it seemed, it was as if this was an admission that they were dying or would die. Well, they all did die, of course, as will we, too.

    But how extraordinary a gift it would be for those generations who are here, and to come, to hear the words and thoughts of those who came before–whether or not they knew them.

    One of the reasons we are so disconnected in this world is that we no longer have links to our ancestors and the past. No storytellers in the families, few, if any, folks who act as the ongoing connectors with oral history.

    People talk about having time. But the truth is, we DO have time for what we deem important. The same folks of an age (retired) who have no time for journaling, videotaping, etc. are the same ones who are on the golf course, taking cruises, playing bingo and/or cards.

    Too many people simply do not want to deal with their true feelings and thoughts, or even their memories.

    Illness is the one time in fact when it becomes very important to really go within and honestly acknowledge your feelings (anger, fear, regret, etc.). Ironically, people who do feel freer to go beyond their illness and truly live out their remaining days.

    Those who have left a legacy of memories (the young mother with breast cancer who leaves a video diary for her young daughter addressing things she’ll want to know as she grows. How marvelous is that? What a sign of true love and caring. What a memory for her child.) seem far more at peace at the end of their life.

    The end of life, for many, is much like the rest of their life. People who were selfish and unthinking about others become even more so. (Sorry. Serious illness doesn’t give anyone a pass on how they treat others. )

    As a result, families are often left with nothing positive to remember about a family as the last days (and all their horror) become all about the difficult stuff and overshadow other memories.

    Even in dying, we can make a difference to our families and friends. Even in dying, we can leave memories, make memories and repair damage with our words.

    It’s something we all need to think about.

    yes, we should show and tell people that we love them while alive. But also leave them something tangible.

    I’ve written letters to my friends for after I’m gone. I want them to have something that says: You made a difference. You mattered. And you made my life worth living.

    That, and more.

    Words can help you deal with your illness, and, equally important, help those you love.

    Give the gift to yourself and others.

    The hardest thing is not saying goodbye. It’s people NEVER saying goodbye, not acknowledging their leaving.

    A friends husband passed from pancreatic cancer. In the months before his illness progressed, he wrote and left notes all over the house for his wife and 11 year-old daughter. To this day, they are still finding them.

    Can you imagine the joy they experience with those notes?

    That even as he struggled with his own death, he left them signs of his love and affection. Wow. What are YOU doing for your loved ones?

    Do it now, while you can.
