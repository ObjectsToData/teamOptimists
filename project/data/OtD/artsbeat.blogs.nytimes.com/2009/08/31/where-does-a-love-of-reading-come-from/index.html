    Where Does a Love of Reading Come From?

    By MOTOKO RICH
    August 31, 2009 11:52 amAugust 31, 2009 2:17 pm

    An article on Sunday about one English teacher’s conversion from assigning classics like “To Kill a Mockingbird” and “Lord of the Flies” to a method that allowed her students to choose their own class reading, is eliciting passionate comments from readers — more than 400.

    Some loved the method embraced by Lorrie McNeill, who teaches seventh- and eighth-graders in Jonesboro, Ga.; others detested it. A reader who identified himself as Greg from North Carolina responded to the article with the following: “if we aren’t going to consider the quality of what is being read, then a love of reading is no more or less beneficial than a love of television-watching, slot-car-racing, or pizza-eating. The student who loves reading ‘Captain Underpants’ but who is never forced to move beyond that material has done nothing to prepare for a life of active, intelligent, inquisitive citizenship (which is something training in the humanities ought to prepare us for) and has been poorly served by her educators.

    “It sounds as though teachers such as Ms. McNeill are presenting reading to students as a hobby, not an academic discipline. Undoubtedly it can be both. But we don’t send our children to school just to pick up hobbies.”

    I responded here. It is a valid question, and one that other readers explore in the context of science or math education: would students ever learn difficult equations or formulas if they were allowed to choose what to study in these classes?

    It is true that education is not merely about fun, but also about discipline and exposure to ideas that students would not come across on their own. The question that teachers like Ms. McNeill and her mentor, Nancie Atwell, are grappling with is how to make students most receptive to those ideas. The traditional approach is to present books to the class as a mandated group exercise. Several readers have argued here – and I also experienced it in my own education –that a good teacher can inspire students to understand and enjoy the most challenging works. There are wonderful examples of students who never would have read or understood “The Waste Land” or Plato or “Ulysses” or “Great Expectations” or (insert your favorite classic here) if not for a great teacher.

    Ms. Atwell and Ms. McNeill believe that the first step toward introducing such works to students is to persuade them that reading can be fun. Otherwise, too many of them will be so resistant that they may not read the assigned classics anyway. Of course it is not clear that all students who begin to enjoy reading will eventually read more challenging or classic works. But most of the teachers who use reading workshop make a concerted effort to guide their students to more challenging books.

    Of the many readers who say they love books, I’m curious: how many of you discovered that love in school, and how many found it at home, through the guidance of parents, siblings and friends? And for those of you who learned to love reading at school, was it because a teacher assigned a book you would never have otherwise read, or because you were allowed to choose books you wanted to read? Share your thoughts.

    Earl August 31, 2009 · 12:26 pm

    Sixty years ago, when I was in the 9th grade, my English teacher, Mrs. Korn, said “Instead of assigning reading homework, I’m going to spend the first part of the term reading Hamlet to you. I had no interest in hearing or reading Shakespeare and thought I’d be bored. To my surprise, she captured my imagination as she read each part, naming the characters who spoke the lines. Thanks to that teacher I fell in love with Shakespeare. Hamlet has been my favorite work to this day.
    Earl Warman, Age 75

    Jeffrey August 31, 2009 · 12:33 pm

    I absolutely trace my love of reading to my parents. From the easy access of reading material around the house to probably most crucially, being read to each night, it was evident to me from the start that books held adventures and information.

    Rebecca August 31, 2009 · 12:45 pm

    My dad used to take me to the flea market every weekend, where I would buy terrible paperbacks of the horror genre – Stephen King, Dean Koontz, etc. All of it was complete crap, and inappropriate by near any measure for an elementary school child. But I loved to read and had a highly active imagination. Eventually, I quit high school and joined a carnival. Years and years later, I went back to school to study English Lit and Classics. Love Homer, T.S.Eliot and Mina Loy. Defeated, still, by Milton.

    Lucy Redoglia August 31, 2009 · 12:46 pm

    My love of reading comes primarily from my mother’s influence when I was a child. Early on, I was encouraged to choose my own books and read outside of school. By my mother’s recommendation I chose titles by Marguerite Henry, Judy Blume and Roald Dahl; and went on to follow a series of books about horses, and the “Wayside School” (while my peers were all reading “Goosebumps”).

    In school, we were required to read dozens of short stories and novels, which I reluctantly skimmed, favoring my choices. By the time I reached high school, I had become and avid reader, disappointed that outside of summer I had little time to read on my own. Instead, “Great Expectations,” “Lord of the Flies,” “Watership Down,” “The Iliad,” “Wuthering Heights,” and “Cannery Row,” were all shoved down my throat — I would skim through, sometimes not reading more than ten pages, then make up for it by participating in class discussions and writing excellent papers.

    I had one teacher who stood out ‘stellarly,’ that I read the entirety of Dante’s “Inferno” and Homer’s “The Odyssey.” I loved their rich symbolism and carefully executed themes; and admittedly, would have never read them on my own.

    To this day, I’m always ‘meaning to read the classics,’ but instead find myself engrossed in popular modern fiction and the errant trashy novel. I read what makes me happy and I believe that I have developed a taste for excellent writing.

    So, to answer the question, I believe a love of reading comes from the experience of enjoying the activity itself. Whether it’s in a classroom environment or outside, when one connects with a story, a spark ignites. These teachers are right in providing a foundation for the enjoyment of reading — we know not every child is as lucky as I to have the encouragement of his or her parents. And regardless of the “quality” of literature, the majority of books are written well enough to improve vocabulary and sharpen the mind.

    Peggy Outon August 31, 2009 · 12:47 pm

    Reading is like breathing for me. And reading for pleasure is a certain joy. Did I learn it at school? Yes, of course. The shared experience of reading the same book and talking about it with others was essential to the establishment of my life-long habit. Some teachers sucked the very life out of the book by plodding their unimaginative way through it; while others opened up the story and my world by their own delight in the words, the story, the characters that taught us what it is to be human.

    At home, books were revered. My mother bought the American Heritage editions of the classics and we built bookshelves in the den just for them. The bookmobile and reading under the whole house fan in the hot Dallas summers of the 50s were my favorite moments in childhood. My mother and father read to me…My brother (four years older) read me Black Beauty one summer and we all read to each other – a habit that continues in my 59th year!

    Books prescribed in school and the expected discipline to read and study them was important. Home was where I learned the joys of reading for my own delight. People should have both experiences…children should be led to great literature, but they should also have the opportunity to select on a whim a book that calls to them and makes them stop – and read.

    barbara August 31, 2009 · 12:48 pm

    I am a lifelong lover of reading. When I was in elementary school in Canada (Grade 4, I believe) I scored very high on a reading exam. (I think the result was the 10th month of the 11th grade in comprehension)

    My mother had a strenuous debate with a reading expert who thought that mom had taught me to read before entering school. She did not. Then he asked how much my parents read, how much they read to me, and noted that by the time I began school I had a burning desire to learn to read (true).

    The first book I read cover-to-cover in a day was in Grade 4, and it was “A Wrinkle in Time”. By that time I was through all the Nancy Drew books, and began to read my mum’s Agatha Christie novels.

    When I got to assigned novels, for the most part I hated them. There are only a couple of novels from middle and high school that I remember fondly; more I remember with confusion–because of bad teaching.

    Even so, I always had, and continue to have library books on my bedside table–usually a mix of professional reading and novels. My lifelong love of reading was fostered long before “required” reading lists entered my life.

    Daphne August 31, 2009 · 12:50 pm

    My father worked late, so the kids ate early. My mother read aloud to the three of us at each dinner meal. Despite an age range of 7 years it gave us a common topic to discuss, an eagerness to hear the next installment, and on most nights it kept the peace. We’re all avid readers to this day.

    Jeanne Fuchs August 31, 2009 · 12:57 pm

    I’ve loved to read from the day I figured out what the letters meant. I read Alice in Wonderland and Through the Looking Glass when I was 7 – I read Gone with the Wind when I was 10. I’m still reading about 3 books a week at age 82. Who did this to me? My parents said anything in print is OK to read and placed no restrictions on my reading. Teachers gave me extra assignments because I read so much and so fast. I think some readers are born and not made My favorite book is one called So Many Books, So Little Time, by Sara Nelson – another compulsive reader.

    Mae Gentry August 31, 2009 · 12:57 pm

    As a student in public schools, much of my reading was assigned. However, in my senior year of high school, I took “independent study,” an English course that allowed me to choose which books to read. I chose books by African-American authors, exposing myself to the works of James Baldwin, Richard Wright and others. Those authors generally were not among lists of required reading, but they should have been.

    Liska August 31, 2009 · 1:02 pm

    My love of reading came from my parents- i remember when I was small, My father gave me his first edition copys of White Fang and the Call of the Wild- they belonged to his father. Of course they are antiques and I don’t think i’ve ever done anything, including turning the pages as I was reading them, that would potentially harm them- even as far as not wanting anyone else to read them.

    I remember reading in school and I never had any objections to them (I’ve never been a TV person anyway) however, the moment I got those two books I became immediately fascinated with the old, with the classic works, and how books used to be way back when.

    To this day, I absolutely love browsing around garage sales and books sales to see what I can find, to find those books that long went out of print and don’t exist in bookstores anymore. They have a quality to them, and something a lot of books these days don’t– they foster the imagination.

    The first book I bought on my own was the Secret Garden and I still remember making up my own garden, trying to imagine what it looked like, in my head as I sat on the couch in our living room, with my book in the light coming from the front window. It’s an experience i’ll never forget, and I miss to this day now that the responsibilites of adult life has limited my reading to either a few hours on the weekend, or bringing a book with me when I go out to dinner.

    Nancy August 31, 2009 · 1:05 pm

    I am absolutely of the opinion that the teacher should assign most of the reading and then make sure the students read it. I’m a high school teacher, and I do not consider it my job to turn my students into people who “love to read.” It’s my job to make sure they’re literate – that’s a different thing. But interestingly, I’ve seen kids “love” to read the books I assign if I present them in the right way. I had an entire class of tenth graders who literally begged me to do nothing all year but read the complete works of Shakespeare aloud in class because they had so much fun reading MacBeth and Much Ado About Nothing. It’s all in the attitude and presentation of the teacher. My own love of reading came entirely from my family. My teachers were terrible.

    Both parents and teachers contributed to my love of reading and writing.
    Ms.McNeill’s approach, unfortunately, is just another example of “dumbing down” and making things fun and easy for those that can’t be bothered.
    Reading assignments in school are not only about developing a love of reading, but about developing rigor, discipline and exposure and experience in critical thought.
    A teacher is supposed to weather the passage of a student’s desire to read for fun… and expose them to this elevated and very beneficial experience precisely by forcing them to read something that is not necessarily fun.

So i started reading and i loved it and i haven’t stopped since. Now I’M the one with the rooms full of books and my life has forever been enriched because of it.

    L. Nelson August 31, 2009 · 1:33 pm

    Comic Books: There I said it. It is not fashionable or chic to say but without the adventures of Superman, Batman and the Justice League of America I would not be a reader. 12 cents a piece (A quarter for the “Annuals”) Oh the anticipation at the paper store…and I had stacks and stacks of them and MAD magazines too. Junk? maybe… but they allowed me to train my eyes and build the synapses necessary to be a reader. In a household where neither of my parents read, save for the newspaper and the odd magazine, comics books trained my eyes to go quickly across the page while comprehending the plot and nuance of exactly what The Joker was planning.

    Later, there was time for Dickens and Twain and Chabon and Lodge and many other favorites; but the comic books of my youth gave my brain the neural muscles that allowed me to read and enjoy everything else. So thanks Archie and Aqua Man and Richie Rich and Little Lulu, I am indebted to you all!

    C. REYNOLDS August 31, 2009 · 1:41 pm

I think it was that kind of parenting I had along with the exposure to books as thrilling adventures that led me to love books. I’m not sure that would have happened if my family had had the internet or cable TV.

    MM August 31, 2009 · 1:47 pm

    My parents encouraged my reading, but at around age 8 I took off and surpassed their reading interests and volume and kept going. They wanted me to be a good student and well educated, while non-readers themselves, and I typically have 5-10 books in process (some are for before bed, some for the recumbent bike, others are dense and saved for prime evening time, then some non-fiction will be picked up and put down on other schedules….). But the really fierce passion I have for reading is my own – I think that by age 13 or so my family couldn’t understand why I was reading so much (what’s in those books?) and I didn’t pay that much attention to my teachers (and loathed a lot of the assigned texts – Mockingbird, Ivanhoe and others come to mind).

    G. Adam Dempsey August 31, 2009 · 1:57 pm

    I discovered my love of reading at home when I was a high school student. I was an above average student who took many advanced courses; however, even though I enjoyed reading, I avoided the advanced English classes my high school offered because I was intimidated by the required readings.

    I am now 26-years old and I regret not taking those advanced English courses. Over the past few years, I have read some of the books that other students at my high school were required to read (e.g., Slaughterhouse-Five, Catcher in the Rye, etc.). I have thoroughly enjoyed those books and I wish I had someone well versed in literature available to point out the nuances of those books that I surely missed while reading them.

    That being said, had I been forced to read those novels during high school, I do not think I would have gotten very much out of the experience. While the literary classics offer an invaluable reading experience, I believe if our teachers can increase the number of students who enjoy reading (whether it be an Orwell novel or the latest Dan Brown book), the future of America will be a brighter one.

    Ellen August 31, 2009 · 2:01 pm

    I don’t know if a love of reading can really be taught. At the end of the day, reading is a subversive activity, one that takes place in isolation and intimacy with the printed word rather than one’s friends or family. Someone who doesn’t like to be by themselves with a book will never love reading.

    Jeannike August 31, 2009 · 2:06 pm

    I wonder if Ms. McNeill teaches honors classes (or their equivalent in Jonesboro) or average classes? If so, what are the standards for honors students? Are those students given free reign to choose their classroom reading assignments? I have heard of an English teacher who assigned rock lyrics to his average students while his advanced classes studied poetry. In that school system, ‘extrapolation’ was limited to classes with high-achieving students. Students in average classes are often immature though intelligent. It seems unfair to limit their intellectual reach. And if McNeill’s students are in an honors class, what are the adults’ (parents, teachers, administrators) aspirations for those children? Where is this ‘philosophy’ coming from?

    Carol Lewis August 31, 2009 · 2:47 pm

    My love of reading came from being surrounded by books at home and at my grandparents’, and overcome by curiousity about what was in them. I had a number of wonderful teachers and yet…In first grade, several weeks after school started, my teacher gave us our “reading book” to take home. The next morning, I informed her that I had read it (the whole thing) and she exclaimed that I was only supposed to read the first chapter; I had to spend the whole year bored to tears in reading group because I already knew what Alice and Jerry were going to get into…In third grade, I asked my teacher to get “Little Women” off the library shelf for me because the “A”s were too high. She informed me that I was too young to read it, which even at the time I thought was a weird thing for a teacher to say. I don’t know why, but I was determined to read it, received it as a Christmas gift from my parents, and promptly read and enjoyed it greatly, alternating identification between Jo and Amy. Overall, even in high school and college, I had the feeling that teachers and professors were holding me back rather than pushing me forward; I know (and knew) it was an unintentional result of their needing to be sure that specific books and ideas were taught.

    c.a.a. August 31, 2009 · 2:55 pm

    My parents read to us incessantly. It was as natural as eating or taking a bath. More than that–learning to read was for me a game, just like learning to count and learning state capitals was a game. I adored the challenge. But I quickly surpassed either of their loves for reading, so maybe it’s inborn too.

    About Fine Literature, though: I had the privilege of having four excellent English teachers in high school who relentlessly pushed us to see the themes, the historical background, the symbols and figurative language, and–cruelly!–forced us to write long essays and short commentaries and our own version of a Cliff’s Notes for each and every novel or play we read.

    This had the result of making most of us very, very good at making things up–but as one teacher put it, “I don’t mind if you B.S., because B.S. still means you’re thinking.” And it had the added side effect of making most of us damn good literary critics by graduation, to where we would go see a movie and somehow end up talking about themes and characterization as we walked out. To this day, I feel equipped to think critically about any novel, short story, play, movie or poem I see, hear, or read, and let me say that I don’t think this diminishes my enjoyment of them.

    Suzanne Rhoades August 31, 2009 · 2:59 pm

    I’m an English B.A. home school mom whose home school fantasy included discussing Wuthering Heights with my three daughters over cups of tea. The reality is that my three daughters possess three very different reading abilities

    My oldest prides herself on tackling classic literature. She’s 15 and reading college level authors. She pretty much despises “contemporary” literature (Harry Potter, etc.). She wasn’t really taught to read as much as she was prepared to use a system she already understood at age five.

    My middle daughter (13) has been reading Pride and Prejudice for about 4 months now. She would like to be as snobby as her sister when it comes to books but her reading level is hovering around Treasure Island and Robin Hood. Whatever brain synapse allows for grappling with page-long paragraphs she doesn’t have it. Whether she ever will remains a mystery.

    My youngest is dyslexic (and, no, I didn’t believe there was such a thing until the 155th time she read “saw” for “was”) and even though I tried my best to prevent the “hate to read” scenario, she hates to read. This is devastating to me but I haven’t given up. The thing about dyslexic kids is that it HURTS them to read. I don’t want her to be a “struggling reader”. I don’t want her to miss out on Anne Frank’s Diary. But she might just have to listen to it from a CD instead of read it. And no, I have no idea what this will do to her. At the very least, she will know Anne Frank. Isn’t that the point?

    What are we trying to instill in our kids when we want them to be readers (beyond utilitarian reading)? I, myself, use reading as a way of getting OUT of the world around me rather than participate in it. After reading hundreds of books considered classics I’m not sure I have any greater insight to life than my sisters who don’t read. It’s simply ONE way to communicate a certain depth and complexity of thought and feeling. Some of us have a need for greater complexity. I would say most of the women I know who consider themselves readers have “settled” for certain easy/Oprah books and that’s the level they’ll be reading from now on. (Whatever happened to Oprah’s big hoodo about reading the classics? A friend of mine who does the Oprah stuff had to borrow East of Eden because O had put it on her list. I asked her how she liked it when she returned it and she said she “didn’t get it.” Perhaps she was confused because there were no reader book club questions in the back?

    The range of abilities of my kids is a pretty good synopsis of what teachers are dealing with. I think if a teacher KNOWS a student she will be able to challenge him to read better books. Oftentimes when a difficult book is assigned, the students who struggle just don’t read it at all. I don’t want to give up on the notion that oftentimes what’s worthwhile doing is difficult. However, by allowing so much instant gratification every where else in their lives (I include myself in this indictment) I’m wondering if we aren’t sabotaging their efforts at difficult tasks. Are any of our kids hungry or thirsty for more than 20 minutes? Sometimes I think we have softened their brains by instantly supplying them with their needs. On the other hand, I see from my own children that much of their reading ability, as with math ability, is simply inborn.

    I would also like to note that my dyslexic daughter has other outlets to express/understand life’s complexities. She plays piano, guitar and is a dancer. Which brings me to my final question. Do any of us really worry about whether Yo-Yo Ma has read Huck Finn?

    Victor August 31, 2009 · 3:12 pm

    I thoroughly enjoyed reading the comments that people left.

    Like many others, I developed a love of reading from both my mother and school. Some of my best memories growing up are of sitting in my pajamas next to my mother on our couch, and listening as she read to me.

    When school began, I knew immediately that I had an affinity for the written word and throughout my childhood, I read everything I could get my hands on.

    I think that Ms. McNeil’s approach, while seemingly unpopular with some on this board, is actually quite innovative, and provides a window into the world of literacy for many students who may not be naturally inclined to read. We make choices as adults around the types of things we read so why shouldn’t we at least have some say about it as children?

    Lastly, some of the most amazing things in writing I have ever come across are not works that would ever be included in a traditional syllabus in either grade school or college. If a teacher, parent or mentor can impart a love for the written word, along with guidance from an adult who loves reading, students will learn to discover words for themselves in places both familiar and obscure.

    Joseph Kays August 31, 2009 · 3:13 pm

    We have been blessed with three children who are voracious readers of virtually everything they can get their hands on. In addition to his assigned summer reading of “A Thousand Splendid Suns,” my high school junior picked up “The World is Flat,” “Sophie’s World” and “Before the Dawn,” and devoured them. My eighth-grader read all of the Twilight series and is preparing for her sixth year of our county’s Book Bowl. And, among the series my fourth grader has read this summer are Harry Potter (for the fourth time), Maximum Ride and Percy Jackson. I believe that by encouraging children to read what they like, we instill in them a love of reading that will ultimately prepare them to read the “classics” as they mature. To require them to read “Moby Dick” or “The Return of the Native” before they’re ready can only discourage them from reading and drive them back to TV and the computer.
