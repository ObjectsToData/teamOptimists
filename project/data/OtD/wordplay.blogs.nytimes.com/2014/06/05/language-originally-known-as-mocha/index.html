    Language Originally Known As Mocha

    By DEB AMLEN
    June 5, 2014 10:00 pmJune 5, 2014 10:00 pm
    Chad Batka for The New York TimesThe singer Kesha at Roseland Ballroom as part of her “Get Sleazy” tour in 2011.
    The Crossword
    Friday Puzzle »

    By Kameron Austin Collins

    Jun 6, 2014

    Play
    • Across Lite »
    • PDF »
    • Print | Answers »
    • Syndicated crossword »

    The New York Times Crossword is available through subscription only.

    FRIDAY’S PUZZLE – Every now and then, people write to me asking for advice on constructing and, although I sadly no longer have time to actually collaborate with constructors, I always enjoy discussing the aspects of construction. Once in a while, among those who write to me, there emerges a truly nice person with charmingly good manners, who is not only smart but loves the process of learning and furthermore, puts it to good use.

    And while I don’t decide who gets published here in The New York Times — that’s left to exceptional editors like Will Shortz — I can imagine that that is why we are seeing a young man like Kameron Austin Collins make his debut in The New York Times Crossword Puzzle today.

    You might remember him too, as he explains in his Notes below. He had a brief foray as a commenter here at Wordplay, but it sounds to me like he has more than enough occupying his time. Let’s welcome him and talk about the puzzle Mr. Collins hath wrought.

    Some constructors like to start by making themeless grids, because it cuts out that pesky step where you have to think up a fun and hopefully unique idea and turn it into a tight set of theme entries. Even so, on a Friday, the words are allowed to be tough and the cluing even tougher in order to give solvers a challenge. I don’t think anyone will argue with the fact that Mr. Collins has succeeded in salting his grid with some tough words (SERIA, SPITAL, KARST, and ANABIOSIS, among others), but what really impressed me was that he crammed so much in there that is fun and unique and fresh. Oh, and young. You could take an educated guess at Mr. Collins’s approximate age if you wanted to, just by looking over the fill in his puzzle.

    There are a whopping 14 entries that are unique today, and among those, I loved NARUTO, the manga and anime series, the band ARCADE FIRE, KESHA (the artist formerly known as “KE$HA”), JACOB ZUMA (no matter how you feel about him, he’s been in the news quite a bit), and JAVASCRIPT (which, with the clue “Language formerly known as Mocha,” really should have had something to do with BARISTA, if you ask me.)

    Also, the crossing of 36 Across and 24 Across, but that’s just because I’m 12 and had to mention it.

    Let’s meet Mr. Collins:

    Constructor’s Notes:

    I’ll introduce myself by outing myself: I used to post here, very occasionally, as ‘YoungNewcomer.’ Hi, everyone.

    About me: I’m a 26-year-old Jersey native and proud resident of the one part of Manhattan’s Lower East Side that still somewhat resembles the great Loisaida. I’m entering my sixth, final year as a joint Ph.D. in English and Interdisciplinary Humanities at Princeton, where I’m writing an intellectual history of legal personhood with the goal of becoming a long-form journalist [Praise Pulitzer -- it still exists, for the moment. --D.A.] But who knows.

    I’m most interested in crosswords as incubators of what’s new and exciting in language and culture, and this puzzle, constructed in August, clued in December and submitted in January, is very much a product of its cultural moment. ARCADE FIRE’s ‘Reflektor’ had just dropped in late October, and I had it on constant rotation as I completed the puzzle. Nelson Mandela passed away in December, as I was cluing, and JACOB ZUMA, in the news for something other than a political scandal (for once), was doing his best to eulogize his forebear, despite the apparent the apparent irony. Freida Lee Mock had recently completed and released ‘Anita,’ a chronicle of ANITA HILL’s involvement in the Clarence Thomas affair, and, having been raised a captive audience to this and a few other scandals in the ’90s, I couldn’t resist when her name appeared as an option.

    Despite ANITA HILL and my seeds (JAVASCRIPT & DAKAR RALLY), I’m most proud of NARUTO, which I worried wouldn’t pass muster as having New York Times crossword-level relevance, even if I think it’s relevant. Will thankfully liked [it], and — my favorite — ANABIOSIS, which some might guess is a subpar “glue” answer, since no one knows what the heck it is. In fact, it’s the coolest thing here. Thanks, Will! Thanks, too, to the many others (Deb included) who helped along the way.

    Feel free to harass me on Twitter (@melvillmatic), and enjoy.

    Congratulations, Mr. Collins, and welcome. Let’s dance out to ARCADE FIRE’s “Reflektor”:
