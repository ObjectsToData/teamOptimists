    Five Teachers Report on the First Month of School

    Eighth Grade English

    September 25, 2006 11:00 pmSeptember 5, 2008 1:34 pm

    My eighth grade English class is about reading, writing, and talking, essentially. We read books, mostly novels, closely, and we spend a fair amount of time discussing them in class. I usually begin with a brief introduction about the authors who they were, when and where they lived, what social realities each was writing out of, and what each was trying to accomplish, both technically, as writers, and culturally, as people who live or lived in the world. I try to normalize the concept of making literature. I want my students to understand that books are written by people, that writing is a thing that regular humans do.

    I also try to make clear that while feelings, experiences, values, and observations naturally inform the act of writing, on the most practical level good books are made of carefully constructed sentences that fit together. Everything a book makes you think, everything a book seems to say, you ought to be able to point to it. Ultimately, its all just words on the page.

    I develop reading assignments, usually by chapter, and we frequently spend class time, especially at the beginning of a novel, reading out loud. We sit in a circle around a big table or around a bunch of small tables pushed together, and I sit in the circle too, and we each read at least a paragraph apiece. Typically, if we get through a chapter in class, I assign the next one or two as homework, and students are expected to choose and highlight at least five significant passages in each chapter as they read. Students are free to highlight any passages that strike them; Im very flexible about significance.

    We do discuss and suggest various options beforehand: things they find important to the story, things they find particularly beautiful or awkward, things they find expressive of the authors style, things they dont understand, and things they feel compelled to choose for mysterious or unknown reasons. We use their highlighted passages a lot in class, often as the basis for discussion, but also as sources for quotations in the eventual five-paragraph essay they will write on each book. I absolutely encourage my students to not ignore the obvious. Although I am by temperament a deep meaning kind of guy, as a teacher my first allegiance is to a clear, accurate, literal understanding of story, and character.

    Along with the reading, I ask the students for written responses to assigned questions, about two or three paragraphs long. These are either given as homework and then discussed in the beginning of the next class or started in class, discussed about half-way through, then finished at home.

    For example, weve just started reading Of Mice and Men, by John Steinbeck. In our first class we talked about Steinbeck and the Depression, read most of the first chapter out loud, and for homework read to the end of the second chapter and highlighted five significant passages in both. About halfway through the second class it was time to write. I gave the assignment: Using complete sentences and solid, proper paragraphs, explain everything you know about George and Lennie so far. Explain their relationship. The air was already filled with thoughts about George and Lennie, so starting wasnt much of a problem. The kids pretty much worked quietly for the next 15 minutes. I circulated around the room and peeked over peoples shoulders, often engaging in whispered conversation about their thoughts. Right near the end of the period, a few people read out loud what theyd done so far. I then asked the kids to finish the assignment for homework, so we could pick up on it in the next class.

    My classes are a kind of feudal democracy. In the classroom, I reign at least theoretically. I pretty much organize things and direct, piping in as needed. But I generally try to get the kids to run the show. I try to draw important points that I want made out of their own observations and opinions, and many times they come up with truly brilliant things about the text that Ive never even considered. During discussion, everybody gets to speak. If faced with a sea of hands, I order the flow. O.K., you, then you, then you, then you, then me, then you, then you. When faced with less than a sea or no sea, I call on people. Thats the beauty of the highlighted passages. During discussion, I can always ask a quieter participant to pick something theyve highlighted and share it. I really try not to put people on the spot. I tend to warn people before I call on them. If a student really has nothing to say, I try to get him or her off the hook. You know what? Think about it, and well come back to you.

    In addition to structuring discussion, the written work is submitted to me, and I read it and return it with comments. I also use it to determine what grammar and mechanics topics to include in our regular fray. So far, weve done a lesson on complete sentences subjects, predicates, fragments, independent clauses, commas in relation to conjunctions, etc. And were definitely going to keep going over punctuation, paragraphing, syntax, and organization throughout the year the point being to approach these concepts through their own writing, and in the service of their own writing.

    Also, the students are responsible for three to five independent reading books each semester. I have them hooked up with the school library, and each meets twice a semester for a chat with a librarian who keeps track of what they are reading and helps steer them to compelling material if needed. I also hand out extra books to interested parties. Ive been ordering books and squirreling them away whenever theres money in the book budget. I try to have several books available for each author we study in class. Along with Of Mice and Men, I have a bunch of copies of Cannery Row and The Red Pony. These can count as independent reading as well. I want my students to write a lot, and I want them to read a lot. It seems that a lot is the magic amount. When it comes to reading and writing, practice is everything.

    And of course we do creative writing. I try to devote one class period out of each six-day cycle for Writers Workshop. Students work independently on creative projects: stories, poems, creative non-fiction, whatever. And I do present models, assignments, and project deadlines for finished pieces, but thats a whole little essay in itself, so lets just mention it here and move on.

    This year I plan to work along with Ms. Walsh, the eighth grade history teacher, to make an official, historical, research-oriented term paper the subject of a joint history/English project in the spring. And Ms. Walsh wants to have them do creative writing as a part of her curriculum as well, so that way well have a little back and forth between the humanities. It will be interesting to see how that turns out.

    Tonight this sounds like a lot of work, and it isnt even all of it (my sources inform me that its late, and I need to bring this piece to some kind of close). But you have to remember; we do a lot in class. I dont necessarily send my kids home loaded down with homework. Sometimes there are days off between assignments and due dates. And I try to give plenty of notice about extended projects. The pace can even seem somewhat leisurely on good days, and the work feels like fun, if you can believe it. I get to spend time between classes hanging out with my students, and we communicate regularly by e-mail. No wonder Im exhausted! How do these kids do it? Can I possibly finish all these George and Lennie things by tomorrow morning? Holy Toledo, after midnight already! Thats it; Im outta here.

      Your class sounds fabulous. It reminds me a lot of the two years of English I had in middle school with one of my favorite teachers ever. We read a lot of classic short stories, and had vocab lists from the stories that we then had to use in our own short stories. The best part was Fridays: we’d have “Writing Workshop” every week. The students would bring in tapes cued to songs we chose (it later turned into cd’s labeled with track numbers on post-it notes) and as the music played, we’d write whatever we felt like. Journal entries, stories, poetry… anything. It would all be turned in at the end of the period, and returned on Monday with comments. If a piece was particularly good, he would (with our permission) copy it out onto colored paper in calligraphy and post it on the wall.

      I’m glad there are other teachers out there carrying on the torch in their own way.

      jess September 26, 2006 · 7:06 pm

      What happens in an 8th-grade English class? In most cases, absolutely nothing.

      You sound like a passionate, hard-working teacher. But I take exception with some of your methods. First, reading out loud? This is not kindergarten. 8th graders should be mature, focused and smart enough to read on their own. There is no need to be having reading time in 8th grade, a time when students should be engaging with great works of literature. Second, why are the kids basically running the show (to paraphrase)? You are the teacher, you are the expert, you should be running the show. Similarly, highlighting excerpts they feel are important? How about highlighting excerpts that ARE important? The problem with American education today is that we spend so much time getting our kids to want to learn (or to engage, or however you want to put it) that we forget that there are some parts of teaching that are not relative. Some parts of “Of Mice and Men” are objectively more important than others. This is more of a problem in math class (how much longer before we get to, “How does two plus two make you feel?”) but it’s true in English as much as it is in any other subject. Yes, we absolutely need to stop teaching to the test — especially American-style multiple-choice exams that have no true purpose — but we should still be teaching.

      I really value the fact that you are a passionate teacher … I just worry that we are spending too much time getting creative in the classroom and too little time teaching.

      Joe Bellacero September 26, 2006 · 10:04 pm

      Bill, thank you for this view of the visible part of the iceberg. Teaching English in the middle school is a complex process and large amounts of that process remain hidden even in this detailed account.
      You provide some hints here of the guantlet that must be walked as you try to value student individuality, bring structure to the group, honor the need to prepare them for high-stakes tests, give them a clear writing structure to build on, help them learn the conventions that make written communications understandable and move them to an realization of the place literature holds in the larger world. We can see you inculcating and practicing skills the students will need to maneuver our information society.       I’m not sure what jess (above) is so grumpy about, but if he thinks that your classroom is some kind of touchy-feely, we’re-all-buddies, no-responsibilities kind of place, then he either has seriously missed what you were saying or simply does not have enough context to understand how many balls you are keeping in the air and how skillfully you are doing it.

      About

      In an age of charter schools, home schools, No Child Left Behind, standardized testing, overstretched public schools, the battle for resources, voucher plans and evolving notions about special education and special needs, the act of teaching has never been more complicated. In Lesson Plans, a group of teachers chronicle their experiences during the first weeks of the school, offering first hand accounts of today's classroom challenges from diverse perspectives.
