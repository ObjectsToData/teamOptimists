    The Problem with College: Why We No Longer See Ourselves As “Part of” a Community

    September 24, 2007 12:00 amSeptember 25, 2007 7:32 pm

    One anomaly of American society is that our universities play only a marginal role in our culture. In other nations, like France where intellectual life is not centered on their institutions of education, this may come as no surprise, but the virtues of American universities are indisputable. Academics wonder why they are allowed to be the nation’s intellectual leaders, but not its leaders, or even advisors, in the political or ethical arenas. Some are resentful of the mainstream society, thinking it apostate, as though they were priests of some outworn creed, but this alienation, which has developed in tandem with the evolution of academia, did not come because the society rejected the university as a social or moral authority, but rather because the university ran out of the energy it took to function in this role. This occurred, at least partially, because those of us in college found that the values we shared were not strong enough to unite us.

    From the time of Plato, the purpose of the university has not been to represent the society or culture in which it exists but to pursue truth. Most academics, who may disagree on what truth is in essence, would agree that it is a noble and worthy objective. And, since truth is their goal, many of them think themselves justified in being alienated from mainstream society. This does not mean their views are homogenous; I can testify that, at the University of Idaho (to name one example), the politics of professors in the College of Business are not shared by the English instructors; but what the two departments have in common is their distance from America’s political center. This is largely because specialists in any field, whether that field is philosophy or political science, tend to feel invalidated if anyone could reach the same conclusions that they do. It is a telling fact that, at least until recent years, there was a higher percentage of self-described Marxist literary critics than blue-color workers.

    Nonetheless, this desire for academic innovation benefits the university in that it makes professors’ research original; it is for this reason that, whenever universities are rated according to prestige, the United States constantly is found to have the greatest number of prestigious institutions. Even so, there is another facet to a professor’s job, teaching. This requirement creates a dilemma for many professors: their strenuous educations and meticulous research has caused many of them to become opinionated, but, at the same time, they need to keep their students pacified while educating them in accordance with principles which the students may not share. Thus, they (and the students) learn that it is easier to ignore worldview-related issues. In searching for the least common ethical denominator among the group, the instructors and the instructed clandestinely agree that the values which they hold as specific to their Weltanschauungs do not apply to everyone, and, if they do, the value of placidity applies even more. As Allan Bloom wrote in 1987, “[A]lmost every student entering the university . . . says he believes . . . that truth is relative” (1). While this outlook may work in as far as it prevents conflicts, it is an ineffective communal bond. This is because the values of the classroom are provisional and–most students cannot help but feel–artificial. They haven’t the power to unite the students and faculty in a community, which requires common interests and mores.

    It would seem that the university would disappear altogether, since so many students feel more at home in their sororities and fraternities, with people of different interests and aspirations, than in their colleges, where their knowledge overlaps with that of their peers and professors. But, ironically, as the university has slowly shifted away from providing education in the traditional sense, society has pressured the young more than ever to obtain a college degree.

    Hence, business became by far the most popular major while other fields of study, like the classics, fell by the wayside. This trend, I believe, will continue; the humanities will lose influence as the importance of the practically-oriented schools swells. Apart from the greater economic security these schools offer, they have the advantage of not pursuing truth; students who major in business or management need not question their fundamental beliefs. Realizing this, departments of English and philosophy have de-emphasized their purposes as the guardians of morals; for example, students in these disciplines no longer focus on what Edmund Spenser thought, but on how to analyze his work from a postcolonialist or deconstructionist point-of-view. Students no longer study philosophy in pursuit of wisdom, but learn how to logically parse arguments; the line between philosophy and mathematics has never been so blurred.

    This is not meant as an argument for structural change; it seems altogether pointless to require students to study Latin or read the great works of Western literature in college, when they should have done so in high school and, while ignorance of these works may degrade us culturally in years to come, the new educational emphasis on business, pre-law, or engineering has, admittedly, allowed the United States to keep well ahead of the rest of the world in economically and politically.

    But, if we intend for the university to function as a community again, what is needed is much more freedom not just at the college level, but, perhaps more importantly, for students in high school and grammar school. In other words, the academy should start with a community, composed of citizens who share common political, social and moral views but who are also open to pursuing truth within the parameters which their worldviews have set for them. Of course, this will probably never come to pass, as it would require government to, at the very least, lower the costs of higher education and provide a greater amount of subsidy to all students or, at the very most, to privatize educational institutions and help the students through alternative means, like vouchers or tax-credits. But, given the state of public universities and colleges today, the prospect of them ever functioning as a community is unlikely.

    Historians will object that there was never a time when the college community was stronger than in the 1960’s and 70’s, when students were united by civil and women’s rights and the peace movements. But, unlike our era, these were revolutionary; diversity was what united the students then. Now that diversity has become the status quo, student bodies have become tired of fighting to maintain it; we still catch a glimpse of it sometimes, when students organize tear down walls with bigoted insults written on every stone as though bigotry were still an imminent force on their campus, or when they take to the streets waving signs that protest of rape (what civilized man or woman, at any point in our nation’s history, would have advocated rape?), but we are mainly satisfied with the routine of classes, relishing the final May when we can finally have a degree to list on our resumes and take another step toward becoming the individuals that we want to be.
