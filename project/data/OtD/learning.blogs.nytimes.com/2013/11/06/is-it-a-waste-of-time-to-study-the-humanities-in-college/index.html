    Is It an Unaffordable Luxury to Major in the Humanities in College?

    By KATHERINE SCHULTEN
    November 6, 2013 5:06 amNovember 6, 2013 11:13 am

    Questions about issues in the news for students 13 and older.

    • See all Student Opinion

    What do you plan to study in college? Why? How much do you think your choice will be based on preparing yourself for the job market, and how much will be about following an intellectual passion? Why?

    Is going to college mostly about making yourself employable, or are there other reasons to study for four years?

    In “As Interest Fades in the Humanities, Colleges Worry,” Tamar Lewin writes about how, since the recession, there has been a profound shift toward viewing college education as a vocational training ground:

    At some public universities, where funding is eroding, humanities are being pared. In September, for example, Edinboro University of Pennsylvania announced that it was closing its sparsely populated degree programs in German, philosophy, and world languages and culture.

    At elite universities, such departments are safe but wary. Harvard had a 20 percent decline in humanities majors over the last decade, a recent report found, and most students who say they intend to major in humanities end up in other fields. So the university is looking to reshape its first-year humanities courses to sustain student interest.

    Princeton, in an effort to recruit more humanities students, offers a program for high school students with a strong demonstrated interest in humanities an idea Stanford, too, adopted last year.

    Both inside the humanities and outside, people feel that the intellectual firepower in the universities is in the sciences, that the important issues that people of all sorts care about, like inequality and climate change, are being addressed not in the English departments, said Andrew Delbanco, a Columbia University professor who writes about higher education.

    The future of the humanities has been a hot topic this year, both in academia and the high-culture media. Some commentators sounded the alarm based on federal data showing that nationally, the percentage of humanities majors hovers around 7 percent half the 14 percent share in 1970. As others quickly pointed out, that decline occurred between 1970, the high point, and 1985, not in recent years.

    …College is increasingly being defined narrowly as job preparation, not as something designed to educate the whole person, said Pauline Yu, president of the American Council of Learned Societies.

    Students: Read the entire article, then tell us …

    • Do you think the main role of college is to make students ready for the job market? If so, why? If not, why not?
    • What do you want to major in? What do your parents want you to major in? How much does the hope of having a secure job, paying off loans and making money after graduation influence that choice?
    • Do you agree with professors who say that employers should value the critical thinking skills one learns through studying the humanities?
    • What other skills or habits of mind do you think studying the humanities can teach you?
    • What should colleges to do convince more young people to major in the humanities?

      I feel that in a time of rising college expenses, it’s important to consider college an “investment” – in the sense that it will yield a return in the form of a secure, well-paying job. For those who can afford it, the non-vocational humanities could be valuable for individual growth; however, it’s the humanities departments with stable careers at the end (such as law school, politics) that are more important when considering the investment justification.

      I want to major in computer science or chemical engineering – something in the STEM area, for sure. My parents also want me to go into engineering – both as a cultural value and an “investment” value. Personally, it’s imperative that a field I enter provide me with a secure job and the ability to pay off loans – to avoid debt in the long term. The joy of a few years of undergraduate study in the humanities would not be worth the deficits incurred by the higher chance of a lower-paying, more-unstable occupation. In my opinion, it’s better to graduate, get a stable job, and return to school later for non-vocational training.

      Yes, I believe it’s important to consider “critical thinking skills gained through the humanities” as valuable for employment, but I think it’s more important to weigh in the actual hard skills that students receive in specialized college vocational training. The benefits students receive from the humanities are not as concrete, and it’s likely that statements valuing these “critical thinking skills” are created for the purpose of justifying the humanities in the first place.

      I think the humanities can teach introspection, empathy, social knowledge, healthy skepticism, understanding, and personal growth. To convince more young people to major in the humanities, colleges should create a new paradigm in which they *openly* recognize two different goals in their programs: vocational training (in majors such as Engineering, Law, Medicine) and individual growth (in majors such as English and Philosophy). That way, it would be more clear *why* students are studying the humanities, instead of giving false hope to those who, in the end, discover that their job prospects are not as bright as statements such as “We value the critical thinking skills taught by the humanities” suggest. Making end goals more clear would bring the right kind of students in and give them a better sense of direction when pursuing a major.

      About

      The Learning Network provides teaching and learning materials and ideas based on New York Times content.

      Teachers can use or adapt our lessons across subject areas and levels or contribute their own ideas.

      Students can respond to our Opinion questions, take our News Quizzes, learn the Word of the Day, try our Test Yourself questions, enter contests, do crosswords, learn about what happened on this day in history, answer 6 Q's About the News, speculate on What's Going On in This Picture? or read our Poetry Pairings.

      Join the conversation by commenting on any post. We'd love to hear what you think!
