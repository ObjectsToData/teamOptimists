    Common Core Practice | Lexicography, Lab Lit and Ethical Investing

    By JONATHAN OLSEN, SARAH GROSS and KATHERINE SCHULTEN
    December 7, 2012 9:21 amDecember 20, 2012 1:20 pm

    Common Core

    Teaching ideas based on New York Times content.

    • See all in Category
    • See all lesson plans

    Another Friday, another installment of our Common Core Practice prompts. We collaborate with a classroom in New Jersey each week to test and publish three short writing ideas that address Common Core Standards and that are grounded in New York Times content.

    Below, exercises that ask students to learn new vocabulary words (and understand how words get in dictionaries in the first place); argue their positions on an issue currently roiling some college campuses; and write short fiction that incorporates what they have read in science class.

    Vocabulary

    Op-Ed: Lies! Murder! Lexicography!
    Common Core Standards: RI4, RI6, RI10, L4, L5, L6, RH4

    “Here’s a tip: if you see the words ‘dictionary’ and ‘scandal’ in a sensational headline, prepare to be disappointed,” begins Ben Zimmer’s Op-Ed article about the “unglamorous” work of creating dictionaries, and the ways that the media can exaggerate lexicographical “scandals.”

    Your Task: While reading Lies! Murder! Lexicography!, find at least five unfamiliar words and, using clues from the text, try and define them as they would appear in a dictionary. When finished, use a dictionary to see how closely you matched their true definition. (You might use this Learning Network graphic organizer to help.)

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • Underline or highlight unfamiliar words you encounter while reading.
    • Read the sentences near the unfamiliar word using context clues to guess what it means.

    Extension Activity:

    Mr. Zimmer cites an episode from The Sarah Silverman Program as one of his favorite recent portrayals of lexicography. (Note: teachers should view the episode for appropriateness before showing it to students.)

    As Ms. Silverman does, make up a word you think is needed in the English language. Define your word and explain why it is a necessary addition to our vocabulary. (Mr. Olsen and Ms. Gross’s humanities class suggests humanicarians: people who care about and love the humanities.)

    For more inspiration, you might visit the weekly “That Should Be a Word,” in the “The One Page Magazine” section of the Sunday Magazine. The writer Lizzie Skurnick has suggested words like snoopervise, duvious and bagriculture — and she lists great reader submissions in this 6th Floor Blog post.

    Argumentative Writing

    Business Day: To Stop Climate Change, Students Aim at College Portfolios
    Common Core Standards: RI2, RI10, W1, W4, W7, W10, RH5

    Many colleges and universities have investment portfolios financed by large endowments. Recently, students at many of these schools have begun to demand that they no longer invest in fossil fuel companies. Blamed for many of the grave climate issues facing our world, fossil fuel companies continue to represent a large portion of the United States stock market and have historically provided a strong return on the investment.

    Should these schools listen to their students or their bank accounts?

    Your Task: Should colleges continue to invest in fossil fuel companies in spite of student protests if those investments are earning them more money — money they say is used to support education? Write a letter to the editor explaining your position.

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • Draft a thesis, or statement of purpose, stating your opinion on the issue.
    • Use this Debatable Issues graphic organizer to organize your thinking.

    Extension Activity:

    • The article compares this movement to one among college students in the 1980s to pressure colleges and other institutions to divest themselves of the stocks of companies doing business in South Africa under apartheid, a policy of racial segregation. Research those efforts. What might students today learn from the past? Here’s a Times article from 1987 that can get you started.
    • The Learning Network provides many resources for teaching about climate change, including suggestions about how to take action.
    • They also ask students: How concerned are you about climate change? Share your thoughts on this Student Opinion question, which will remain open to posts indefinitely.

    Narrative Writing

    Science Times: In Lab Lit, Fiction Meets Science of the Real World
    Common Core Standards: R10, W3, W4, W9, W10, L5, WHST2b, WHST2d

    Recent books like Barbara Kingsolvers “Flight Behavior” and Ann Patchetts “State of Wonder” and classic books like Mary Shelleys “Frankenstein” can all be categorized as lab lit.” Focused on contemporary science and situations, lab lit is not science fiction or historical fiction. Katherine Bouton explains in this article how issues like climate change, genetic engineering and biotechnology are constantly in the news and are now making their way into novels.

    Your Task: Try to write your own version of lab lit. Compose a two-paragraph scene featuring a scientist in a lab. Be sure to include sensory details and figurative language.

    (Remember, the scientist should be working on something realistic and contemporary. This should be realistic fiction, not science fiction).

    Here is the beginning of a writing sample from our freshman student Sean K. as an example:

    What was he doing here? Why did he even decide to enter this bacteria-free lab, with its reflective linoleum floor, and perfect white walls? It was a cold, harsh chamber, bent on perfection, yet he loved it. It was his second home now, a room echoing with the loud, thrashing fury of inspiration. He looked at the data again, and again, and again, looking for some comfort. Was this worth it? Was it worth going out of his mind to chase a dragon which now seemed far out of his reach? He wasnt the first to come to this fork in the road. Countless geniuses before him had tried and ultimately failed in this same attempt. That was in the past, though; we know more about medicine than ever before, right? Right? The exhausted scientist broke down. He lay on the floor dreaming and wailing about what could have been….

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • Prewrite by coming up with a scientist character. You can “meet” some real “scientists at work” here.
    • Think about what a science lab would look and sound like.
    • Determine what kind of action will be taking place during your scene.
    • Incorporate these elements into a creative narrative as Sean has.

    Extension Activity:

    Lab lit has become increasingly popular over the last few years, with books like Flight Behavior, about an entomologist studying monarch butterflies, and Allegra Goodman’s Intuition, about a biotech lab. Jennifer L. Rohn runs lablit.com, where readers can find lists of books that fall under the lab lit genre. The site includes lists of books, plays, films and television. However, there is no list for childrens and young adult lab lit books. With your classmates, brainstorm a list of books that you think should be on a Lab Lit for Kids or Lab Lit for Teens list. As a class, submit your final list to the Web site.

    Meanwhile, this topic was so obviously useful for the classroom that The Learning Network published a full lesson plan about it this week. Check it out to find more suggestions — for lab lit reading groups, for ways to create book and movie treatments using science, and more.
