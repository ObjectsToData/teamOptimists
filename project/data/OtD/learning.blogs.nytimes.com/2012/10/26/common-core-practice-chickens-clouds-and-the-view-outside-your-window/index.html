    Common Core Practice | Chickens, Clouds and the View Outside Your Window

    By SARAH GROSS, JONATHAN OLSEN and KATHERINE SCHULTEN
    October 26, 2012 10:03 amDecember 20, 2012 1:18 pm

    Common Core

    Teaching ideas based on New York Times content.

    • See all in Category »
    • See all lesson plans »

    Each Friday we post three Common Core-aligned reading and writing tasks inspired by New York Times content, and classroom-designed and tested by the teachers Sarah Gross and Jonathan Olsen, along with their ninth-grade humanities students.

    Tell us what you think, and how you use The Times to teach and learn.

    Common Core Practice Tasks | Week of Oct. 22 – 26, 2012

    Argumentative Writing

    New York Region: “Chickens Threaten to Divide Brooklyn Community”
    Common Core Standards: W1, W7, SL4, SL6, RI1, RI8, RH2, RH8

    Typically, when residents of a neighborhood protest the addition of an unwanted entity in their neighborhood, it is a big-box store or a cellphone tower. It isn’t because a brood of chickens is moving in temporarily.

    But that is what is happening in Park Slope, Brooklyn. While the eight chickens will be spending only a few months at the local community garden, their clucking has caused heated dispute among residents.

    Your Task: Should the chickens be allowed to stay in the Warren-St. Marks Community Garden? Why or why not? Using information from the article, draft a brief speech that you could deliver to Park Slope residents supporting your opinion.

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • Make a list of the reasons you would or would not want chickens in your own neighborhood.
    • Brainstorm issues over which neighbors have been divided in your area. What, if anything, do those situations have in common with this one?
    • Using information in the article, make a list of pros and cons for having chickens in a residential neighborhood.

    Extension Activities:

  1. What issues have divided your community recently (whether you define “community” as your neighborhood, church, school or even extended family)? Consider interviewing people on both sides of the issue and writing it up as a newspaper article, using “Chickens Threaten to Divide Brooklyn Community” as a model.
  2. Many people have been taking chickens into residential neighborhoods as a way to gain access to fresh eggs and poultry. Not all towns support this, however. Find out how livestock is handled in your neighborhood. Can you have chickens on your property? Are there specific laws governing livestock in your neighborhood?

    3. Narrative Writing:

    Science Times: “Finding Zen in a Patch of Nature”
    Common Core Standards: W3, W10, RI4, RI10, RH4

    Have you ever sat and really observed the world around you? Do you notice the line of ants at the base of the tree in your yard? How about the bluejay calling from its perch in the oak tree?

    David Haskell, a biology professor, has written a book about observing nature. As the Times article puts it, “He thinks like a biologist, writes like a poet, and gives the natural world the kind of open-minded attention one expects from a Zen monk rather than a hypothesis-driven scientist.”

    Your Task: Look out the window of your classroom. What do you glimpse in that small view? Write a descriptive paragraph, using figurative language and sensory details that give the reader a picture of what you observe. Be sure to include small details, as Dr. Haskell did in his work.

    For example, here is how a ninth-grade student, Devin L., approached this essay prompt:

    Jumbles of wires lay strewn, gathered in tangles like vines. The pale beige carpet lies in stark contrast to the verdancy outside. Lockers lie open, their depths filled with textbooks and papers swallowed by the cavernous size. The computer carts lie in wait filled with excited electricity ready to be put to use on an important project. The watched gaze of the emergency lamps’ silvery eyes stare back. When you look at the tiled ceiling and fluorescent lights, the hum reminds you briefly of the outdoors.

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • List everything you see when you look out the window.
    • Enrich your writing by considering the use of adjectives, interesting detail, or similes or metaphors. What do the things you see remind you of? How could you help others really see them?
    • From your list, select what you think are the most important details and include them in your narrative

    Extension Activity:

    • Who is Andy Goldsworthy? Research him, his eco-art or other artists like him. What are your favorite works of this kind? Why?
    • Look at the Voices From the Land Web site for environmentally focused interdisciplinary project ideas to use in your class.

    Note: Ms. Gross and Mr. Olsen’s class will be completing a Voices From the Land project over the next few weeks and posting the photos and posters that they create with the Twitter hashtag #art4me.

    This is an interdisciplinary project they will be working on in English and history, as well as in biology and the school’s Computer Applications class. Because it is a project that arose naturally from their work with The Times in class for our Common Core Practice project, we will update readers on the class’s progress in the coming weeks.

    Informative Writing:

    Business: “Gleaning Clues on Sunny Days From the Clouds”
    Common Core Standards: W2, W4, RI2, RI8, RH2, RH5, RST1, RST6

    What do you see when you look up at the clouds? Animals? Faces of presidents? When Carlos F. Coimbra, a University of California professor, looks at clouds, he wonders if he can accurately predict where they are going to go.

    Using sophisticated computer programs, Professor Coimbra has been collecting data on cloud movements for years to try and create weather forecasting models useful for dozens of industries.

    Your Task: Write a one-paragraph summary explaining why cloud forecasting is worth pursuing. In your paragraph, be sure to include one direct quote from the article.

    Before you do the task, you might…

    • Use a K/W/L chart, like this one to note what you know, want to know, and learn as you read.
    • Use the text to determine what kind of value cloud forecasting might have.
    • List the industries that might benefit from accurate cloud and weather forecasting.

    Extension Activity:

    Visit The New York Times Green blog, which covers energy and the environment, and click on the section titled Q & A: Green Interviews.

    Choose an interview that interests you and read the transcript. Then create a list of questions about the environment and interview someone you know who might be knowledgeable in this area. (You might start with a science teacher.) Write it up in the style of Q & A: Green Interviews. With your classmates, you might then make a book, exhibit or Web site of the collected results.

Teachers can, of course, take the ideas further and include additional reading, as many of our formal lesson plans (like this one) do, but these short prompts are meant to be one-period activities. –Katherine Schulten (editor)

      Sarah Gross October 26, 2012 · 10:15 pm

      Marshall-

      Thanks for the feedback! What we try to do with the Common Core prompts is cover a wide variety of the standards, from English to History to Science and Technology. All of the prompts require the students to read an informational article from the paper (which is full of informational text every day!) and then respond in writing. Because the Common Core focuses on argumentative, informative, and narrative writing we try to include all sorts of responses each week. The narrative writing doesn’t focus just on fiction writing, but asks the students to write using sensory language, details, and figurative language. The article and response in the second prompt is fabulous for that! Dr. Haskell’s science book, The Forest Unseen, is a fabulous example of real-life science writing with a narrative.

      Sheri Stein October 27, 2012 · 8:37 am

      This is fantastic! I’m a member of the steering committee of the Warren St. Marks Community Garden as well as an adjunct lecturer at BMCC, where I teach ESL writing. I’d actually thought of using our controversy as a lesson, and I like what you’ve done with this.

      Of course, there’s a great deal more to the controversy than the brief article is able to cover. One thing that might be interesting for your students to know about is the enormous amount of soul-searching and heartfelt discussion among garden members who have been involved in the decision-making–most of it through wonderfully written email exchanges.

      In fact, I’ve been amazed at our individual and collective writing skills. We write lengthy emails detailing our concerns and opinions. We’ve needed to produce letters and fliers to distribute to the community and send to our garden membership. One member in particular has done a remarkable job of taking all of our suggestions to produce these documents, which have then gone back around the steering committee for comments. This is a perfect example of the importance of learning how to write and why it’s necessary to revise and edit—not to mention to work collaboratively.

      A final note–this situation could make a fascinating lesson in the way democracy works–or attempts to work. It is an arduous process, especially if it is undertaken with seriousness and integrity. Despite disagreements, the members who have been struggling with this conflict have shown great compassion and intelligence. It is not always easy, to say the least, to face those of an opposing viewpoint with respect and equanimity. We have taken the complaints of the community very seriously and have actually made new friends with some of the most vocal chicken opponents. And many people in the neighborhood have stopped in to tell us that they absolutely adore the chickens.

      If you’re interested, I can let you know about actions we’re taking and how the situation is ultimately resolved.

      Thank you so much for sharing your lesson.
