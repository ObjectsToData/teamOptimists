PUBLISHING: MATHABLANE GETS CORNELL FELLOWSHIP

By EDWIN McDOWELL
Published: March 2, 1987

Mark Mathabane, who wrote a widely acclaimed memoir of growing up black in a South African shantytown, has won a writing and teaching fellowship in the graduate humanities program at Cornell University. ''I'll be there next fall and through the spring of '88, conducting a seminar once a week,'' Mr. Mathabane said. ''The rest of the time I'll be writing a sequal to 'Kaffir Boy.' ''

''Kaffir Boy,'' published last April by Macmillan when the author was 26, tells of Mr. Mathabane's growing up with parents who were illiterate, of his struggle with apartheid and of how with the help of the American tennis champions Stan Smith and Arthur Ashe he won a tennis scholarship to the United States. ''Ashe was the first role model I had, because he was the first free black man I had ever seen,'' Mr. Mathabane said. Mr. Mathabane moved last year from New York to High Point, N.C., although he still lectures all over the country. ''I average a lecture a week,'' he said, ''mostly to colleges but to a sprinkling of churches and corporations.'' While his ostensible subject is his book, ''I use that as a springboard to talk about apartheid in human terms,'' he said.

Edward T. Chase, editor at large at Macmillan, acquired ''Kaffir Boy'' for $35,000. The New American Library bought the paperback edition for $40,000, the Reader's Digest Condensed Books paid $35,000 and The Bodley Head bought British rights for about $41,000. Mr. Chase also brought Mr. Mathabane to the attention of Cornell, where Mr. Chase's son-in-law, Prof. Jonathan Culler, heads the Society for Humanities.

Today's headlines are spawning a number of books for tomorrow - literally tommorow, in the case of Bantam Books' edition of the Tower Commission report, which was released last Thursday. Copies of the $5.50 paperback, co-published by Times Books, will be in selected bookstores today, but national distribution of the scheduled 300,000 first printing is expected to be completed tomorrow.

Next month Indiana University Press will publish ''Belzec, Sobibor, Treblinka,'' by Yitzhak Arad, the prosecution's first witness in the Nazi war crimes trial under way in Israel. In that trial, Mr. Arad, a historian and chairman of a Holocaust museum in Israel, testified that John Demjanjuk of Cleveland was ''Ivan the Terrible,'' responsible for the death of thousands of Jews in Treblinka. More than 1.5 million Jews were exterminated in Treblinka and the two other Polish camps, Belzec and Sobibor. Drawing upon primary materials never before published, Mr. Arad's book recounts the grisly history of those camps.

Donald I. Fine Inc. has signed up an unauthorized account of the life and career of Oliver North, the Marine lieutenant colonel who is a central figure in the Iran-Contra affair. Titled ''Guts and Glory,'' it will be written by Ben Bradlee Jr., political correspondent for the Boston Globe. Publication is planned for next January.

Japan, Italy and Spain, which specialize in color reproduction, often manufacture art books for American publishers, and many children's pop-up books are manufactured for American publishers in South America. In an unusual turnabout, the Banta Company of Menasha, Wis., has for several months been manufacturing books in Portuguese for Distribuidora Record, Brazil's largest trade book publisher.

''We print for almost all major American publishers, mostly educational books, but this is our first big effort for a foreign publisher,'' said D. S. Koskinen, president of Banta.

Books manufactured in the United States cost Distribuidora Record about 30 percent more, including transportation to Rio de Janeiro by ship, but under the circumstances the publisher considers it a bargain. ''Before last Christmas I was faced with having about 200 titles out of print, books that I couldn't get in time,'' said Alfred Machado, the company president. But by providing Banta with camera-ready copy, some 500,000 copies of 18 titles were delivered in time for Christmas. The publisher has since ordered a million copies of 64 additional titles, which should keep Banta busy for at least another year.

Mr. Machado attributed the shortage of Brazilian printing facilities to two factors - a law a year or so ago that provided free textbooks for school children, and the national elections late last year, after two decades of military rule, which created a big demand for posters, handbills and similar accouterments of democracy.

One reason Mr. Machado chose Banta, he said, is that it manufactures books on a Cameron belt system - that is, a system that in one continuous operation prints, folds, cuts, collates and binds books. Distribuidora Record plans to install that same system in its printing plant in Rio. Meanwhile, according to Mr. Machado, ''We wanted our production people to learn how to work with Cameron belt equipment, and we wanted the books produced with the same characteristics they'll have when we start manufacturing them ourselves.''

The finalists for the $50,000 Ritz Paris Hemingway Award for the best novel of 1986 are: ''A Summons to Memphis'' (Alfred A. Knopf) by Peter Taylor, an American author; ''The Handmaid's Tale'' (Houghton Mifflin) by Margaret Atwood, a Canadian; and ''Across'' (Farrar, Straus & Giroux) by Peter Handke, an Austrian.

Nominations for the award, which will be presented April 6 at the Hotel Ritz in Paris, were solicited from scholars, literary critics and authors. Finalists were selected by James Nagel of Northeastern University, Henry Louis Gates of Cornell University and Melvin J. Friedman, the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee.
