LONG ISLANDERS; A HISTORIAN OF EINSTEIN REFLECTS ON THE PROGRESS OF SCIENCE

By LAWRENCE VAN GELDER
Published: March 1, 1987

TALK to David Cassidy for a while and you'll learn that Albert Einstein used to summer at Nassau Point on the North Fork, take part in local chamber music performances and spend time with local astronomers.

Actually, Mr. Cassidy picked up these tidbits, during a flight from London to New York four years ago, from the woman who was to become his mother-in-law. But there is much more that the 41-year-old Mr. Cassidy, who lives in Rockville Centre, can tell you about Albert Einstein.

That's because Mr. Cassidy, a physicist and historian, is also an associate editor of ''The Collected Papers of Albert Einstein.'' Sometime this spring, the first volume of what may be a 30- to 40-volume project, co-sponsored by Princeton University Press and The Hebrew University of Jerusalem, is scheduled to be published by Princeton University Press.

Einstein was born in 1879 and died in 1955, and Volume 1 takes the great physicist from birth to 1901. Among its contents are his birth certificate, his citizenship papers (he gave up German citizenship at 16 to become Swiss), reports of his school grades, a manuscript written as a 12-year-old (on the state of ether in a magnetic field), his notes for a physics course he took, and letters to the fellow physics student who would become his first wife.

''His letters to his first wife before they were married contain a lot of science, which will be a new discovery,'' said Mr. Cassidy, whose career is devoted to a relatively new field known as history of science. Mr. Cassidy pointed out that in 1905 Einstein propounded his relativity theory, a quantum theory and explanation of Brownian movement.

That was the year when ''he presented a series of papers, each of which started a whole revolution in its own way. It turns out that back in 1900 or 1901, he's thinking about these things.''

Under Einstein's will, his papers went to the Hebrew University, and the joint publishing project with Princeton University Press is being carried out - with funds from the National Science Foundation and the National Endowment for the Humanities, among others - under the editorship of John Stachel, a professor of physics at Boston University.

It was Professor Stachel who recruited Mr. Cassidy for the project in 1983. Mr. Cassidy, whose mother is a librarian and whose father was an executive of the Ford Motor Company and a professor of business and history, was born in Richmond and did some of his growing up in the vicinity of Detroit. He attended two high schools in Kentucky and two in New Jersey, graduating from Ridgewood (N.J.) High School in 1963.

As early as grade school, when he was interested in dinosaurs and astronomy, he had decided on a career in science. ''I liked physics,'' he said. ''It had to do with nature and the elementary forces of nature, what I felt were basic problems in nature.''

Mr. Cassidy earned a bachelor's degree in physics from Rutgers University in 1967 and a master's in 1970 before deciding to transfer to Purdue University for his doctorate.

''I guess this is where the turning point occurs,'' he said. ''I realized I really didn't want to be a physicist, but I liked physics. I had done a lot of reading in philosophy and various other things. I discovered there was a field called history of science.''

He said: ''I've always been a sort of independent person, and I could see the physics profession not providing what I wanted to be and being intellecturally more stimulated by this new field of history of science - so many interesting things to be done, so many opportunities. Also the fact it would give me ideas like, what is science? It would get me more into the questions I had. Why is science the way it is? Where is it going? How does it operate? What is it really?

''I was also impressed there was a human element to all this, that scientists are human beings after all. They live just the way we do. They have the same needs and desires. And there is a whole history to it - like art.''

Purdue's physics department granted Mr. Cassidy special permission for a doctoral dissertation on the history of physics, specifically on the five years (1920-25) leading up to the German physicist Werner Heisenberg's work on quantum mechanics.

Mr. Cassidy received a doctorate in physics/history of science in 1976, pursued further studies at Berkeley, won a fellowship that took him to Germany, where he became an assistant professor at the University of Regensburg, and continued work on a biography of Heisenberg, which he is currently pursuing during a leave of absence from the Einstein project.

Mr. Cassidy, married since November to the former Janet Hardy, who was sleeping next to her mother on the plane while Mr. Cassidy was learning about Einstein on Long Island, said of Einstein and his legacy:

''Science is probably one of the greatest creations that humankind has been able to produce, and this is one of the greatest scientists of our 20th century. It's really a cultural treasure. The more we do now to preserve what he did, the more we'll preserve a part of our culture and understand what science is all about.''
