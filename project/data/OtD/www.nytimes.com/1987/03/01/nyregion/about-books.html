ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: March 1, 1987

MARCH is a month of literary events in the state, many of them taking place at Seton Hall University in South Orange.

Tonight, for example, Josef Skvorecky, author of the much-praised ''The Engineer of Human Souls,'' published in Canada in 1977, will be at the school's Theater-in-the-Round on the South Orange campus, 400 South Orange Avenue.

Mr. Skvorecky's novel, subtitled ''An Entertainment on the Old Themes of Life, Women, Fate, Dreams, The Working Class, Secret Agents, Love and Death,'' has been called ''wise, witty, bawdy.'' It is about an author who, like himself, emigrates from Czechoslovakia to Canada to become a professor of English.

The playwright Arthur Miller will be at the Theater-in-the Round March 10 to ''read from his works and reflect on a lifetime in the American theater.''

Mr. Miller's classic ''All My Sons'' was recently revived on the Public Broadcasting System series ''American Playhouse.'' His ''Death of a Salesman,'' first staged in 1949, won a Pulitzer Prize.

On March 30, Susan Sontag, critic and novelist, and Richard Howard, poet and translator, will participate at the Theater-in-the Round in a dialogue on ''The Decline and Fall of the Literary Arts in America.''

''The entertainment/sermon,'' Mr. Howard said, ''will range over fiction, drama, poetry and criticism . . . to render some account of where these noble and venerable institutions now stand.''

Tickets are $8 for these Seton Hall literary events, all of which begin at 8 P.M. They can be bought at the door.

To reserve seats, call (201) 678-1680 or (201) 761-9388.

On Tuesday from 1:30 to 3 P.M., the first of five sessions of a public discussion program, ''American Writers and Their Lives,'' will be held at the Monmouth County Historical Association's Freehold headquarters, 70 Court Street.

The discussions, financed by the New Jersey Committee for the Humanities, will be led by Dr. Gretchen Besser, a literary scholar. Subsequent sessions will be held March 17 and 31 and April 14 and 28.

Participants will receive a copy of ''An American Procession,'' by Alfred Kazin, the text for the programs.

The fee is $3 for association members, $5 for nonmembers. For advance registration only, call (201) 462-1466.

The annual Writers Conference -entitled this year, ''Writing and Publishing: Techniques and Technology'' - will be held March 14 at the New Jersey Institute of Technology in Newark.

The conference, from 9 A.M. to 4 P.M., includes a full day of writing-related discussion panels and workshops, in addition to the annual New Jersey Authors Luncheon, autograph sessions and poetry readings. Tickets range from $20 to $35.

Information: Call Joan Buchanan at (201) 596-3449.

''One on One,'' a program for beginning and professional authors and illustrators of children's books, will be held March 21 by Rutgers University's Advisory Council on Children's Literature and its Graduate School of Education.

The site is the university's Continuing Education Center, 10 Seminary Place, New Brunswick, and the hours are 9 A.M. to 4 P.M.

Walter Dean Myers, author of 22 books, including ''Motown and Didi,'' will deliver the keynote address. Participants will be individually paired with established authors, illustrators or editors to discuss their craft.

There is a fee of $35. Registration is limited to the first 50 paid registrants.

Information: Call Claudia Knezek at (201) 932-7156.

Trenton State College in Ewing Township will hold its annual Writers Conference March 30, beginning at 9 A.M. and continuing through the evening.

The program, at Brower Student Center. will include workshops, readings and discussion panels on publishing and play production. Participants will include agents, editors, publishers, writers and directors.

Those wishing their work - journalism, fiction, poetry, plays, scripts, children's literature - discussed in a workshop should submit it before Friday.

At 8 P.M. on March 30, the novelist Joseph Heller will deliver the keynote address and answer questions from the audience.

Mr. Heller is the author of ''Catch-22,'' the 1961 dark comedy of military ineptitude that has sold more than 8 million copies and become a catchword for the no-win paradox.

More recently, Mr. Heller's ''God Knows'' - this time the setting was biblical and the hero, King David -won praise for its ironic rendition of human vagaries.

In a recent telephone interview, Mr. Heller said he hoped that his appearance would encourage new writers.

When Mr. Heller was reminded that in the wildly funny ''Good as Gold'' (1979), his protagonist, Bruce Gold, a professor of English literature living in Manhattan, is described as staring ''across the river at the house lights in New Jersey and . . . glad he had never had to live there,'' the author responded:

''In all my novels, there's a certain abrasiveness.''

Mr. Heller, who has never lived in New Jersey, explained why that thought was ''characteristic'' of Dr. Gold, who, in the novel, is striving for a high post in government.

''I do think it's almost what most upwardly mobile, ambitious people in Manhattan would feel,'' Mr. Heller said, adding:

''I have no apology for that line. Although offending people in New Jersey was not a major objective, there is that sense that New Yorkers have that New York is New York.

''No slight is intended, but it reflects the kind of snobbishness that most Manhattanites feel as well for the rest of the boroughs. It's the way people feel.''

Mr. Heller recalled the time ''when the discotheque Studio 54 was very popular, and they used it keep people out.''

''The doormen, who allowed people in, had a term of contempt for those who were not allowed in,'' Mr. Heller said. ''The term was 'bridge people.' And, occasionally, I'll still hear it or read about it.

''It referred to everybody who lived outside of Manhattan, who had to cross a bridge or tunnel to get to Manhattan. Manhattan has historically been one of the centers of the country. As far as a literary scene, there is only one major place - and that is Manhattan.''

Mr. Heller, who left Manhattan for East Hampton, L.I., in 1982, added:

''I could do without the city. It wouldn't bother me if I never set foot in Manhattan again. I've had enough of it.'' Information: (609) 771-3254. On March 31 at 8:15 P.M., the poet Lawrence Raab will appear as part of the Visiting Writers Series at Stockton State College in Pomona.

Mr. Raab, a recipient of a National Endowment for the Arts Creative Writing Fellowship, teaches poetry at Williams College in Williamstown, Mass.; his poems have been published in The New Yorker and Poetry magazines. His most recent book of poetry is ''Other Children.''

The next reading, on April 21, will be by Stephen Dunn of Port Republic, a poet and professor of creative writing at Stockton State. Both events are free.

Information: (609) 652-4893.
