FUTURE ENGINEERS GETTING MORE HUMANITIES REQUIREMENTS

By JENNIFER A. KINGSON
Published: July 19, 1987

MANY engineering students have traditionally regarded humanities courses as educational frills. Recently, however, in an effort to prepare students to better deal with the social and ethical implications of their chosen fields, schools such as the Massachusetts Institute of Technology are increasing their emphasis on arts and social sciences.

Educators at these institutions say that their aim is to push students beyond the point of being merely ''well-rounded.'' At technical colleges where knowledge of the humanities was once treated as a luxury, professors are showing an increased awareness that moral issues, such as those arising from the Challenger accident and the disasters at Chernobyl and Bhopal, require students to think more deeply about the society in which they will operate.

The number of undergraduates who take degrees in engineering more than doubled from 1975 to 1985, according to the Department of Education. During the same period, the number of history degrees declined by 49 percent, and English and literature degrees by 34 percent. But many educators say that corporations are becoming interested in broadly educated employees rather than those who are well-versed in a technology that may soon be obsolete.

At a growing number of technical schools, courses that once were electives have become requirements, producing engineers who are conversant with medieval chivalry or computer scientists who write African percussion music - or, simply, better English sentences. The trend has spread to such diverse institutions as the United States Naval Academy in Annapolis, Md., where humanities requirements were enhanced three years ago, and the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena, where students now must specialize in one of five humanistic fields in addition to their technical work.

''Among the engineers, there is a realization that the social and political implications of the technology are as important as the technology itself,'' said Samuel Jay Keyser, associate provost for educational policy and programs at M.I.T., where the faculty voted in May to restructure the curriculum and create a minor in the humanities, arts and social sciences.

It is not clear, however, just how schools can fit both types of courses into a four-year program that many agree is already far too brief. ''We should recognize that, in four years, we cannot educate an engineering student to the level of a beginning professional,'' said Paul E. Gray, president of M.I.T. The school's curriculum changes may be a step toward acknowledging that professional training now requires graduate studies, he said.

With so many potential graduate students being lured from academia by high-paying jobs, some members of professional engineering societies question whether schools are really concerned with the enlightening aspects of Greek or with hanging onto a captive clientele.

''It seems clear that industry is satisfied with engineers with a four-year training,'' said Henry Bachman, president of the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers. The rest of the training, he said, can be done on the job.

Indeed, this new interest in the humanities comes at a time when an explosion of technical knowledge has prompted the organizations that accredit engineering schools to tighten their standards and compel their members to offer more vocational training. To help counter that pressure, the National Endowment for Humanities has granted money in the last three years to almost 150 projects at schools that are sharpening their emphasis on liberal arts.

The new interest in humanities may be a cyclical occurrence. In the 1960's, liberal arts emerged as a reaction to the career-oriented curriculums of the 1950's. While academic courses in the 1970's were judged on the merits of their ''relevance,'' today's watchwords seem to be ''coherence'' and ''integration.''

In fact, some professors see a new climate of respect between ''hard'' science faculties and their counterparts in the humanities. At the Colorado School of Mines, an interdisciplinary honors program for engineers brought faculty members in the sciences and humanities together to teach seminars in teams. ''You can now see a metallurgist talking about Chinese literature,'' said Thomas Philipose, the program's principal tutor.

''One of the things we used to have to battle was the idea that engineers weren't good in humanities or shouldn't really get themselves too concerned about political things,'' said William R. Grogan, dean of undergraduate studies at Worcester Polytechnic Institute. Engineers who graduate from broader programs come to see that the creative spirit they take pride in is shared by other disciplines. ''Scientific theories are like Michelangelo's 'David,' '' said Dr. Keyser, ''They are both solutions to problems, and they have their own esthetic.''

While future engineers may be learning more about humanities, at many liberal arts colleges students are allowed to graduate with minimal scientific and mathematical training. Dr. Gray of M.I.T. criticized colleges that lack strict science requirements. ''We probably provide an education that is more liberal,'' he said.
