2 BUSINESSES END SUPPORT OF A SCHOOL

By JANE PERLEZ
Published: July 24, 1987

Two major corporations say they are withdrawing their support for an experimental high school program because of protracted ''obstruction'' and ''doublespeak'' by school officials.

The withdrawal by Time Inc. and CBS Magazines, who were enthusiastic participants for two years in a High School of Writing program, comes as the president of the New York City Board of Education, Robert F. Wagner Jr., is seeking more cooperation from the business community in efforts to improve the school system.

The two corporations said that officials had procrastinated about the location and length of the program ever since its inception in 1984. Despite requests from the Board of Education, the corporations said they would not continue to support the program.

The program, intended at its beginning in 1985 as a four-year high school program, started in the ninth grade and has been extended to the 10th grade. 'Indecisive and Obstructive'

''One would think that school administrators would nurture such a program,'' said J. Richard Munro, chairman of Time Inc., in a stiffly worded letter to Dr. Frank L. Smith Jr., the executive director of high schools. ''Instead, Board of Ed administration has been indecisive and obstructive, leaving the project in limbo since 1984.''

Mr. Munro said he had been ''misled'' by board officials on the future of the program and that Time Inc. would not support a request to assist the program at a new location.

He said he had been led to believe that the school, situated on several floors of the old School of Performing Arts at 120 West 46th Street, would be moved to the High School of Humanities at 351 West 18th Street. Instead, the school is being moved to a vocational school. ''I have little confidence in the board's fifth switch of plans for this program,'' Mr. Munro said. 'Absolute Doublespeak'

The vice president for special projects at CBS Magazines, Peter P. Hanson, made a similar point in a letter to Dr. Smith. ''I don't respect the absolute doublespeak you have given the parties involved - the students, the parents, the faculty, the administration and the private sector partners,'' he said.

Dr. Smith, who met with Mr. Munro last month, said in an interview that the program was being integrated into a revamped curriculum of the High School of Graphic Communication Arts.

''The youngsters were already going there,'' Dr. Smith said. ''They were spending as much time in transit as they were in instruction.'' Dr. Smith said that moving the writing program to the High School of Humanities had been ''one proposal'' but had never been agreed to by board officials.

Time Inc. and CBS Magazines' decision comes as Mr. Wagner is talking with business leaders about becoming involved in the school system. The board president has been a strong supporter of a program in East Brooklyn under which several banks work with the East Brooklyn Churches, a coalition of churches, to encourage students to improve their grades in order to qualify for jobs. But Mr. Wagner has conceded that in the first year of the program, which was just completed, board officials did not give sufficient support to the church coalition and the banks. 'Mentors' for Students

The writing program which is a half-day long, gives students a double period of writing exercises and one period of English grammar. Employees from Time Inc., CBS Magazines, Essence magazine and Hal Honig, the president of a textile company, Jack L. Honig Inc., acted as ''mentors'' for some of the 152 students enrolled in the program. The business people helped students with school work, took them to their offices and encouraged them with special projects.

The other half of the day, students attended classes at a vocational school, the High School of Graphic Communication Arts, at 439 West 49th Street.

Essence and Mr. Honig said they would continue to support the writing program despite strong misgivings about the change in plans.

The community relations manager of Time Inc., Tiaga Ermansons, said that the strength of the writing program had been its small size and the sense of community that the students felt with the teaching staff and the tutors from the business community.

Most disappointing about Dr. Smith's decision, Ms. Ermansons said, was that the program had been successful. ''We could tell the improvement,'' she said.
