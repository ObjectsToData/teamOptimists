LONG ISLANDERS; MIXING PUBLIC SERVICE, PRIVATE CAUSES

By JAMES KIM
Published: July 5, 1987

THOUGH he has traveled the world on business and as a United Nations delegate, Arthur Ross makes a point of spending his summers in East Hampton.

''I've long had my summer house there and always appreciated the ambiance,'' he said. And when Mr. Ross, a Manhattan native, appreciates a place, he often does more than just talk about it.

Last month, for example, Mr. Ross made a contribution to the Fine Arts Center at the Southampton Campus of Long Island University. The money, the exact amount of which was not disclosed, will be used to design and construct a new entrance to the building. The entire project, which will be named in honor of him, will include a landscaped plaza and new walkways.

''I like to make contributions to the area in which I am living,'' he said. ''I was delighted to have the opportunity to suggest and provide for this project.''

His summer house may be in East Hampton, but Mr. Ross's roots are firmly planted in Manhattan, where he was born and spent his childhood, and graduated from Columbia University. In particular, he remembers the times that his parents took him to Central Park. It wasn't always fun, he said.

''I hated it because they took me skating and my feet got cold,'' he said with a laugh. Despite his feet, Mr. Ross has warm memories of the park and has done his part to maintain it.

''The role of Central Park in our city has been somewhat neglected and underappreciated by New York City,'' he said. He remembers taking walks through the North Lawn with his three children and noticing how bare it seemed. In 1971, Mr. Ross decided it was time he did something about it.

He established the Arthur Ross Pinetum, a collection of pine trees north of the Great Lawn. And during the next five years, he contributed 1,500 pine trees and 1,000 Chinese elm trees to the city. ''I'm a New Yorker,'' he said. ''I've lived here all my life. I'm happy now to return to the city the many benefits I've had.''

For someone with a bent for contributing trees, it seems fitting that Mr. Ross works for an international paper company. Currently he is vice chairman at Central National-Gottesman, where he started as an analyst and economist and worked his way up.

While his office is in New York, Mr. Ross is frequently abroad on business, something he said gives him the opportunity to ''to feel the pulse of world business and world political affairs.''

He has put his international experience to good use as a United States delegate to a number of United Nations conferences. He attended the 38th, 39th and 52d assemblies of the Economic and Social Council of the United Nations. He was a member of the 18th conference (held every two years) in Paris, the 21st in Belgrade and the 22d in Paris. He also served as the foreign affairs adviser to the United States Mission to the United Nations in 1976 and 1977.

But he doesn't regard such positions as honors. He said he takes it as his duty to speak up about the world and its problems, and what nations should do about them.

He is thankful for the opportunity to present his views of world problems at international forums, he said, adding that he goes not necessarily as a government representative but as a private citizen concerned with world affairs. In addition to serving the United Nations, Mr. Ross is a member of the Council on Foreign Relations and the International Institute or Strategic Studies, a London-based research organization. He is on the advisory board of the Center for Strategic and International Studies at Georgetown University in Washington, and the editorial board of The Washington Quarterly.

His fascination with other countries and cultures doesn't cease when he returns to New York. He is a trustee of the Asia Society and the Spanish Institute.

His contributions and service have earned him considerable recognition. He is most proud of the Frederick Law Olmsted Award, which was presented to him by the Central Park Conservancy for service to the park.

He also received the Gold Medal Award of the National Institute of Social Sciences in 1982, for contributions to the humanities, and the City of New York Mayor's Award of Honor for Arts and Culture in 1983.

Said Mr. Ross: ''I feel my efforts have been appreciated and been recognized. It's satisfying to know you've put your footprints on the sands of time.''
