INVOKING AMERICA: A GITCHE GUMEE MEMOIR

By HELEN YGLESIAS; Helen Yglesias's new novel, ''The Saviors,'' will be published in August.
Published: July 5, 1987

WHEN I was a kid growing up in a New York City slum, the public schools I attended put a lot of emphasis on memorizing. ''By the rude bridge that arched the flood,'' we chanted, and my blood tingled. Nothing in my experience, bounded by the Flatbush Extension or, later, by Southern Boulevard, connected me to that rural image I barely comprehended. What in the world was a rude bridge anyway? I was born in the U.S.A., as Bruce Springsteen has it, uneasy heir yet ardent adherent to the bragging neighborhood glories that spilled over on our fourth of July. Fear of firecrackers marred the great day, as did the boring speeches of local politicians. But there were magical fireworks after dark, and at the last, softly exploding sparklers rained on the hands of every kid on every stoop of every tenement on the block. The U.S.A. simply was the best, the greatest, the bravest and the freest.

Why then uneasy? Perhaps it was too soon after my parents' tumultuous arrival for me to claim a comfortable taken-for-granted birthright. My very marrow was stained with the dyes of the immigrant imagination. I carried about a fevered vision of having been part of a great movement of conscious choice, of a journey undertaken in search of an idea and, yes, of an ideal. I was the outsider yearning to work my way in, heavily hampered by problems of language. Mostly Yiddish was spoken at home. Yet ''the shot heard round the world'' aimed itself directly at my breast and injected me with the brilliant idea of the Republic, our Republic, unique in the world.

But where was it, this idea, if not this ideal? Where did it actually exist? Certainly not in the hotly contested streets where I struggled to gain a footing among all those other immigrants crowding to beat me out; certainly not in the cramped rooms of the cold-water flat where the personal passions exploding among my brothers and sisters and my mother and father may have had much to do with ideals of individual freedom and happiness, but not much that I could attach to concepts expounded in my history and civics books.

''Only connect,'' E. M. Forster said. That's what I desperately longed to do. Somewhere out beyond the el-shrouded cave of Southern Boulevard, the real U.S.A. opened out into light and airy spaces peopled by real Americans, transcendent beings, descendants of the actual heroes and heroines who landed with the Mayflower, built the Massachusetts Bay Colony, established a peaceable kingdom and celebrated Thanksgiving in a blessed land called New England.

Writers search out history not for scholarly purposes but for use in fictions. I look to history to make myself at home in it and in the world. Historians may react with distaste to the cannibalizing playfulness with which writers make use of the terrible twists and turns history has meted out to the children of the earth, but it is my way of bringing history close, not to distort or falsify, but to make its examination bearable. I enter a scene a willful ignoramus, a naif, a know-nothing, the better to connect with the great bulk of humanity, suffering at the hands of history even as we are prime participants in creating it.

I am a child learning by rote, ''By the shores of Gitche Gumee, / By the shining Big-Sea-Water.'' My newly English-speaking Mama is delighted with these sounds and marches behind me, mocking, repeating, but with affectionate amusement. She will summon these lines whenever she needs a little cheering up in her hard-working life. ''By the shores of Gitche Gumee'' becomes her mantra.

She doesn't mock ''This is the forest primeval. The murmuring pines and the hemlocks.'' Her eyes widen with dreaming and sadness. She thinks of the forests of her youth in Russian Poland, of her mother and father left behind, never to be seen again, of her brothers and sisters dispersed about the world - England, Germany, Australia, South Africa. ''When I was a little girl,'' she tells me, ''I saw gold shining in the distance beyond our village and I pleaded with my father to help me collect the fortune that would make us rich. My father took my hand and walked with me across the frozen, rutted roads to show me that it was only the late winter setting sun, and how the gold was fading from the windows of the houses in the neighboring village. My father was a saint.'' Many years later I would write a novel, ''Family Feeling,'' using this incident. I built a New England frame around it: ''Spare bits of information make a stilted history which I construct painstakingly into a sampler of my mother's early life. . . . I embroider the grandfather I have never seen in golden thread and give him her heavy-lidded, deep-set, brown eyes, flecked with gold.''

In the way that writers turn happenings into fiction, I have altered what she told me, to fit my needs. In this process, Longfellow, dear old Longfellow, is dropped out. But not the New England he invokes. In that first chapter of ''Family Feeling,'' in which my aim was to do an immigrant Jewish mother, New England again is the frame. After the death of the mother, after the ritual consolation of the reading of the Kaddish, the daughter-narrator writes: ''I have other magic. Now I keep my mother's candlesticks in my kitchen on the Franklin fireplace mantel of my New England cape. From my garden I cut a spray of fresh dill and add it to the last cooking minutes of the chicken soup and evoke her in the delicate, sharp scent. I insist on her existence, and on my own.''
