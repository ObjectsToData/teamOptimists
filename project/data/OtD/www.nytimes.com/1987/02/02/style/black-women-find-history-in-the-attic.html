BLACK WOMEN FIND HISTORY IN THE ATTIC

By ANDREW H. MALCOLM, Special to the New York Times
Published: February 2, 1987

One afternoon a few years ago a group of black women showed up in the Lafaytte, Ind., living room of Darlene Clark Hine, a professor of Afro-American history at Purdue University.

The women unloaded a station wagon full of boxes, documents and memorabilia they had collected from black families all over the state.

''We'll be back in six months for our book on blacks in Indiana,'' they said, and left.

''These are some bold women,'' Professor Hine said recently, with a smile. ''But they transformed my life.''

They may also have helped to change the course of local history, or at least how it will be presented in the future in books and classrooms. For the determination of those women and the organization and scholarship of Professor Hine led to the ''Black Women in the Middle West Project,'' a grass-roots effort to assemble historical data, diaries and anecdotes dealing with the lives of black women in Illinois and Indiana. Little Had Been Written

The project, which was eventually financed by a $150,000 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities, recently released its report, a 238-page resource listing of every item collected and a guide to how the gargantuan effort was organized. Professor Hine hopes the project will serve as a model for other areas to help insure that black women are included in future histories.

''You read most histories of the Midwest and, indeed, the whole American past,'' said Professor Hine, ''and you won't find much, if anything, about the contributions that black women have made to this country. There's Harriet Tubman, a leader of the underground railroad, and Sojourner Truth, an antebellum antislavery activist. And that's it.''

The idea of assembling historical material on black women began with Shirley Herd, a black public-school teacher in Indianapolis who was distressed at the dearth of such material for her classes. She felt that the libraries were full of resources on white men and women and some on black men, but the history of black women was overlooked and decaying in old boxes, desks, family albums and dusty attic corners.

''More of these priceless documents and resources are being destroyed every day,'' said Professor Hine, ''because the younger generation does not appreciate the learning value, for instance, of an 1890 marriage certificate, some old letters or photos. We've got to put this material where researchers go to look for it, namely in historical societies, archives and libraries.''

Professor Hine did not plan such an ambitious undertaking when Mrs. Herd and her friends showed up in her living room in 1980.

''How do you think you got to Purdue?'' Professor Hine recalled one of the women asking her. ''You're here now because we were out protesting years ago to make these opportunities available to you. You owe us this book. Oh, and keep those funny little numbers, the footnotes, out of it.''

Professor Hine wrote a monograph on black women in Indiana - the book the women had originally asked for. And then, to her dismay, all the historical material was returned to its owners, as promised. Professor Hine, taken with the urgent need to protect such material, set out to launch a larger Midwest project.

''My intellectual development was shaped by this pivotal experience,'' said Professor Hine. ''The experience forced me to study and come to grips with a dimension of our past I had never even contemplated.''

After myriad financing proposals and rewrites, the project received the humanities grant on condition that it be confined to Indiana and Illinois.

Professor Hine and an assistant, Patrick K. Bidelman, held two dozen training workshops all over both states to organize the grass-roots search teams, few of whom had ever been involved in academic research. But their inexperience was compensated for by their enthusiasm. More than 1,000 women, and a few men, worked on the project, They ranged in age from 21 to 98.

They held teas and coffee hours, printed signs soliciting historical contributions, got publicity in local newspapers and on radio and television and, perhaps most important, talked about the project to everyone in sight. ''Word of mouth turned out to be the best gathering method we had,'' Professor Hine said.

Many people offered old photos or diaries, bundles of letters, minutes of meetings. Poking through a friend's attic, Mrs. Herd found a history of a black women's social club from the 1920's. Years before, someone had stuffed the yellowing sheets into an unused toilet tank.

For a historian, such club records can indicate the social issues that concerned blacks in those days. If, for instance, one issue was medical care, what might that say about the health of blacks there at the time?

''Or,'' added Professor Hine, ''we could track down some club members' names through the census, learn their social class and occupation and peek into the social dynamics of the black community.''

Professor Hine is drawing on such material for a book she is completing for the University of Illinois Press, titled ''Black Women in White: A History of Black Women in the Nursing Profession.''

The donations - over 300 - ranged from several boxes of records to a handful of photos. One of the collectors was told about an elderly woman who had been an opera singer. They tracked her down and got a trunkload of newspaper clippings plus a collection of letters rejecting the woman's applications to sing because she was ''colored.''

''Maybe she also wasn't good enough,'' Professor Hine said, ''But all these little items help tell a story.'' A Sense of Competition

All the material has been catalogued and a complete listing of every item has been published by the Indiana Historical Bureau (140 North Senate Avenue, Indianapolis 46204).

For example, under the listing ''Greer, Reverend Hester A. Collection,'' a researcher would learn that an unpublished autobiography of the minister, a missionary in the Caribbean for 41 years, now is in the Indiana Historical Society collection.

There were problems, of course. Some groups and individuals began competing for material, so specific territories had to be assigned, each with its own coordinator. ''Besides creating historical collections,'' said Professor Hine, ''we also created an awful lot of titles.''

But she found that the project had considerable personal impact as well. The groups developed a unity and camaraderie and many were disappointed when the project ended.

For Professor Hine it proved doubly rewarding. ''You know,'' she said, ''professional historians get so cynical and jaded and we only talk to each other. But these people believed so fervently in the power of history.

''They believed that somehow you could change your present circumstances if only your history was told. Then they would finally be accorded recognition and legitimacy. It knocked me on my heels and taught me, the historian, what history can really teach.''
