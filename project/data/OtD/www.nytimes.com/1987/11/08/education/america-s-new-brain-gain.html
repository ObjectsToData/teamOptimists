America's New Brain Gain

By Dirk Johnson; Dirk Johnson is a national correspondent in The New York Times's Chicago bureau.
Published: November 8, 1987

GEOFFREY Parker opened the letter and could scarcely believe his eyes. Professor Parker, a historian at St. Andrews University in Scotland, was being offered a job at triple his salary. He had not applied for it. ''This is not an invitation to apply,'' he said, paraphrasing the wording in the letter, from the University of Illinois at Champaign-Urbana. ''The post is yours. We've created it for you.''

A few hours later, Professor Parker quickly decided to join the ranks of countless European scholars now flocking to American universities.

Not since the influx of scholars fleeing Europe before World War II and during the upheavals of the 1950's have so many academicians crossed the Atlantic to join American schools, according to education experts. Many of them are coming from Britain, where cuts in salaries and resources have left top-flight academicians ripe for the plucking by American schools.

''English universities have taken a real pounding,'' said Neil J. Smelser, a sociologist at the University of California at Berkeley who follows education trends worldwide. ''And American universities are drawing on these troubled academic systems.''

Deep government cuts in English university funding, together with an already glutted market of professors there, have stalled the promotion of many younger scholars. At the same time, American colleges are facing an imminent shortage of young scholars. And they are looking to the most distinguished academicians of Europe, especially in Britain, to fill the faculty gaps and sharpen their competitive edge. To get these scholars, the American schools are offering nearly irresistible salaries, in some cases exceeding $100,000.

One sociologist, for example, who had struggled financially at Oxford for nine years recently sorted through lucrative job offers from Princeton University, Columbia University, the University of Chicago and Berkeley. ''He finally chose Columbia,'' Mr. Smelser said. ''I think they offered him the RCA building.''

While top salaries are the most obvious lure in getting the scholars to come to America, universities here use other inducements, such as free tuition for their children. And several of the European scholars said money was not the determining factor in making the transfer.

Brian Charlesworth, a 42-year-old evolutionary biologist and geneticist who left the University of Sussex for the University of Chicago, said the eminence of the Chicago faculty figured most prominently in his decision. For Professor Parker, the size of the library at the University of Illinois was among the biggest attractions. With seven million volumes, it is about 10 times the size of the library at St. Andrews. ''I have more access to European history here,'' he said, ''than I did in Europe.''

For Britain, this exodus amounts to a brain drain, sapping that country of many of its best academic minds. But for American schools, the influx has enriched faculties and enhanced the image and influence of institutions of higher learning here.

''America is simply the biggest consumer of cutting-edge knowledge in the world,'' said Mark A. Kishlansky, a native New Yorker who is now a professor of history at the University of Chicago and editor of the Journal of British Studies. ''We are the Japanese of the education industry.

''Free-market education in this country has become enormous big business,'' Professor Kishlansky continued. ''Getting grants is the name of the game. And the way you get those grants is to have a distinguished faculty.''

Though British humanities scholars have been responding to the siren song from America, such inducements have been less successful with British scientists and engineers, according to the Royal Society, the nation's science academy. A recent study by the society concludes that only about 2 percent of new Ph.D.'s in the sciences and one-half percent of experienced researchers emigrate each year.

The competition among American universities to snare the best foreign teachers is not confined to the elite private institutions, according to education experts. ''The great state universities - Michigan, Wisconsin, California, New York - all live in this same world of academic competition,'' said Professor Kishlansky. ''In fact, some of the state schools, with their large foundations, are able to outbid the private schools.''

In the last school year, out of a total of 700,000 teachers in all American universities and colleges, about 2,750 were foreigners - a number that some education experts believe has doubled in the last decade. At the University of Illinois, for example, the 270 foreign teachers now comprise about 12 percent of the faculty, up from about 7.5 percent 10 years ago. During that time, the number of British scholars has doubled, to 50.

''It's more than a trickle,'' Mr. Smelser said. ''It's not gigantic. And it's very selective. But it's become significant. And there are no signs that it will change.''

The influx has centered on the very top European scholars. And they are being sought not unlike professional athletes who have declared free agency. The most prestigious schools are eager to keep their place in the standings, and the others try fervently to climb the rungs. ''The highest echelon schools are picking off the stars, of course,'' Mr. Smelser said. ''But certain institutions like Duke, Iowa, Northwestern, which are not in the top rank but very close to it, are making special efforts to get the foreigners - people who will add luster to their faculties.''
