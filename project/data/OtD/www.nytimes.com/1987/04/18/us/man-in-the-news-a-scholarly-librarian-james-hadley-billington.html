MAN IN THE NEWS; A SCHOLARLY LIBRARIAN: JAMES HADLEY BILLINGTON

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY, Special to the New York Times
Published: April 18, 1987

It was rush hour on the Moscow subway, with thousands of commuters hurrying to and fro. In the middle of the crowd, oblivious to the tumult about him, an American scholar was pointing out mosaics on the walls of the subway station. The scholar, James

H. Billington, was named today by President Reagan as the nation's Librarian of Congress, a nomination long expected. If the Senate approves the nomination, Dr. Billington will become the 13th person to head the Library of Congress.

The position, which is being relinquished by Daniel J. Boorstin, is at the pinnacle of American intellectual life. The library serves as the nation's repository of literature and other art forms and plays important roles in copyright matters and conservation of the printed word. The Librarian of Congress earns $85,500 a year.

The Moscow incident, related today by Lynn V. Cheney, chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, shows, she said, ''why he is such a marvelous scholar - it is an example of his devotion to the life of the mind.''

Mrs. Cheney serves on the board of directors of the Woodrow Wilson International Center for Scholars, where Dr. Billington, the center's director, has his office at the Smithsonian Institution.

The Moscow subway is not an extraordinary place to find Dr. Billington. He is a Russian scholar who has been consulted by Mr. Reagan on the Soviet Union, and is the author of ''The Icon and the Axe,'' a study of Russian culture.

Dr. Boorstin said he saw some significance in the President's reaching out to a Sovietologist for this most American of posts. 'A Symbol of Our Openness'

''It is a happy coincidence that this profound scholar of Russian culture should be nominated to head our national library when we are more than ever aware of our need for a deeper and more sympathetic understanding of the Russian past,'' Dr. Boorstin said. ''His nomination becomes a symbol of our free nation's openness and our desire to understand the whole world's possibilities.''

James Hadley Billington was born on June 1, 1929, in Bryn Mawr, Pa., the son of Nelson and Jane Billington. In an interview today in his house in the Georgetown section of Washington, Dr. Billington traced his love of books to his father, who had to go to work early in life because of the death of his own father.

''My father didn't go to college but he filled our house with books that he bought at Leary's,'' he said, with warm recollections of the secondhand bookstore that once nestled into a niche of the old Gimbels department store in downtown Philadelphia.

''We got lots of books that were cheaper because their owners had marked them up, and it made me wonder, 'Why did this reader underline this passage?' As the librarian, I guess I shouldn't be saying this because people are not supposed to do that.''

He grew up in the Philadelphia suburbs and was graduated from Lower Merion High School in 1946. He received a Bachelor of Arts degree from Princeton University and a doctorate from Oxford University's Balliol College, where he was a Rhodes scholar. He later served in the Army, became a history instructor at Harvard University and then a professor of history at Princeton.

Dr. Billington is married to the former Marjorie Anne Brennan. They have four children: Susan Billington Harper, herself a Rhodes scholar at Balliol; Anne Billington Fischer, James H. Billington Jr. and Thomas K. Billington.

Asked today what he might do as Librarian of Congress, Dr. Billington said it would be premature to go into detail. But he added: ''In a general way, the possibilities of the library involve maintaining, enhancing and celebrating this extraordinary collection of human memory. It's a question of bringing out the music that's already there.''

Dr. Billington also praised Dr. Boorstin, saying, ''He's done an extraordinary job and has been an important spokesman and moral force for the importance and glory of this extraordinary facility.''

Dr. Boorstin was subjected to a tough hearing when he was nominated by President Ford, both for his conservative views and for his prolific writing, which some senators feared would interfere with his duties as librarian. Old Skeleton in His Closet

Dr. Billington is expected to face no such difficulties unless a skeleton is found in his closet. Actually, there is one, but it is a very old.

''Governor Bradford said that the Billingtons were 'ye profanist family in all New England,' '' Dr. Billington said, referring to William Bradford, the governor of Plymouth Colony. The reason for that description, Dr. Billington said, came not from the fact that five Billingtons had just arrived aboard the Mayflower but ''because one of them, John Billington, was the first man hanged in the New World.''

Dr. Billington said he did not know what crime the man had committed, adding, ''I'm not sure we're really related, but we've always kind of claimed it.''
