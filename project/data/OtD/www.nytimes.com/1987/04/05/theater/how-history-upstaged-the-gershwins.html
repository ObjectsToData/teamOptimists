HOW HISTORY UPSTAGED THE GERSHWINS

By Arthur M. Schlesinger; Jr. Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., a historian whose works include ''The Age of Roosevelt,'' is professor of the humanities at the City University of New York.
Published: April 5, 1987

THE GERSHWIN CELEBRATION that closed last Sunday at the Brooklyn Academy of Music was a public service. The double-feature bill of ''Of Thee I Sing'' and ''Let 'em Eat Cake'' offered a new generation the chance to hear great Gershwin songs and to sample two classics of American musical comedy, even if in a telescoped concert version. ''Of Thee I Sing,'' of course, was an enormous success in its day, while ''Let 'em Eat Cake'' was a relative failure; and the Academy of Music evening raised the question of why one show worked and the other didn't.

I did not see the original production of ''Of Thee I Sing'' in 1931. But I can still remember the delight with which my father, a historian addicted both to politics and to musical comedy, reported on the show. ''Of Thee I Sing'' had the bounce, the breeziness, the gaiety he had so much liked in the musicals of the 1920's. At the same time, the George Kaufman-Morrie Ryskind book and the Ira Gershwin lyrics broke away from conventional musical comedy formulas to undertake a satiric exploration of politics. For the critics and audiences of 1931, it was an enchanting and liberating departure. It ran for 441 performances and beat out ''Mourning Becomes Electra'' for the Pulitzer Prize.

I did see the 1952 revival. It was a considerable disappointment - to the critics, to the audiences and to me. What had seemed mordant satire 20 years before now came over as silly tomfoolery. From the perspective of the 1950's, ''Of Thee I Sing'' surprisingly appeared more a show of the 20's than of the 30's. Far from discarding the old formulas, it preserved them. Its theme was love; its plot, the obstacles in the way of lovers: back to Bolton and Wodehouse.

As for the political commentary, it was straight out of the 20's in its Menckenesque dismissal of the electorate as a bunch of boobs who would fall for anything: ''Wintergreen for President. . . .He's the man the people choose/Loves the Irish and the Jews.'' The politicos were presented - most entertainingly - as cynics, and as crooks as well; this, too, was just as Mencken had seen them. One party leader fears that the voters may be getting wise to them. ''If they haven't got wise to us in 40 years,'' remarks another, ''they'll never get wise.'' ''Yeah,'' the first replies, ''but I don't like the way they've been acting lately. You know, we never should have sold Rhode Island.'' ''Rhode Island!'' says John P. Wintergreen, ''Nobody missed it!''

Nonetheless, they feel they need an issue -''something that everybody's interested in, and that doesn't matter a damn.'' They settle on love; genial John P. Wintergreen proposes to demure Mary Turner in every state; and soon ''love is sweeping the country.'' Old-fashioned musical comedy obstacles intervene, till Mary saves her husband from impeachment by producing twins. The politicos are not only cynics and crooks; they are boobs, too, and the running gag is a failure to recall whom they nominated as Vice President.

Mencken was not always wrong about voters or about politicians. A member of the original cast of ''Of Thee I Sing,'' the dancer George Murphy, was elected to the Senate 30 years later on a platform hardly more substantial than John P. Wintergreen's. And appreciative laughter of recognition at the Academy of Music - when John P. Wintergreen stonewalls a press conference by singing ''It's A Pleasant Day - That's All I Can Say'' and when he later promises the voters a new song on radio every night - suggests that 55 years later these scenes bring the White House's present recumbent to mind. Kaufman and Ryskind built it better than they knew.

''Of Thee I Sing'' had a cheeky irreverence about political institutions - the Presidency, the Vice Presidency, the political convention, the Cabinet, the Supreme Court, the Senate, foreign diplomats - that foretold the more corrosive skepticism of the New Deal. But the irreverence lacked the sharp focus of really trenchant satire. This was certainly not due to a shortage of material. When ''Of Thee I Sing'' opened the day after Christmas 1931, there were eight million people unemployed, more than 16 percent of the labor force and twice as many as the year before. But the Depression was hardly mentioned, and then only to be dismissed: ''Who cares what banks fail in Yonkers - long as you've got a kiss that conquers.'' At a time when great financiers were in the dock or fleeing the country, there was not a joke about Charlie Mitchell or Sam Insull, not a crack about Wall Street. The political personalities of the day were ignored, except for a passing quip about Calvin Coolidge as a columnist (and this was cut after Coolidge's death in January 1933). Herbert Hoover, an inviting target, might as well not have existed.

The show's great and enduring creation was Vice President Throttlebottom, the forlorn little man whose name no one could remember, who got the nomination because straws were put in a hat and he lost, and who dared not mention his new office lest his mother find out about it. But Throttlebottom was based on a platonic idea of Vice Presidents, not on anyone in the daily headlines.
