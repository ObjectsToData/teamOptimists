NEW JERSEY OPINION; WHY NEWARK'S ARK SHOULD BE SAVED

By CAMILO J. VERGARA; Camilo J. Vergara, a Revson fellow at the Columbia University Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation, collected and forwarded to The New York Times copies of letters sent to Ms. Tawana and to government officials about the ark. Following his article are excerpts from some of those letters.
Published: April 26, 1987

For five years, Kea Tawana has been building an ark with material from abandoned buildings in Newark's Central Ward. She sees the vessel - 86 feet long, 20 feet wide and 28 feet high - as a symbol of new life for a neighborhood racked by riots 20 years ago.

Until Superior Court Judge Harvey A. Margolis issued a temporary restraining order on April 14, city officials planned to raze the ark, which they said violated zoning ordinances. Ms. Tawana has applied to the Newark Zoning Board of Adjustment for a variance to let it stay in the parking lot of the Humanity Baptist Church on Bergen Street. A hearing on her request for a preliminary injunction against further action by the city is scheduled for May 5 before Judge Margolis.

AT THE beginning of March, when the City of Newark decided to demolish the ark built by Kea Tawana, Ms. Tawana began the fight to preserve her vessel.

From the low ground of City Hall came memos and threats of lawsuits and fines. On high ground, Ms. Tawana responded by making her 31-foot-high boat into a billboard by painting it with large signs asking for help and informing people of the city's threat. On the side of the boat she wrote the telephone numbers of the Mayor, the Chamber of Commerce and The Newark Star-Ledger.

Things happened very fast, and today images of the large brown ship -rising like a phoenix out of the weeds of the surrounding empty lots - and the face and voice of Kea Tawana are known to millions across America. The story of the ark has been on national and local television and in most of the major newspapers in the area, The Central Ward of Newark, one of the most unlikely places for tourism, gets hundreds of out-of-town visitors daily. People of all classes, seeing in Ms. Tawana a true American original, have responded with letters of support, donations and visits to the boat.

One can hear at all times sympathetic passers-by shouting, ''Don't let them tear it down.''

In barely two weeks, the ark became the most famous structure in Newark. People feel the spell of its presence. In the words of Taisha, a sixth-grader from the neighborhood, ''The ark should be a monument, like the Statue of Liberty.''

While this structure . . . reflects Ms. Tawana's unique vision, it is part of a much larger phenomenon of American folk expression. Such folk environments exist all over the country, more often in rural settings than in a city. Another example . . . is the Watts Towers in Los Angeles.

I spent a recent Sunday afternoon visiting Kea's ark, and was extremely impressed by the outpouring of support from a steady stream of neighborhood people. Kea Tawana and her ark have become compelling symbols of hope and human potential in a community that has suffered a troubled past.

I urge you to do whatever you can to stop the demolition.

From Didi Barrett, editor of The Clarion, America's Folk Art Magazine (New York), to Governor Kean, with a copy to Mayor Sharpe James. . . . I must constantly weigh my personal love of art, cultural enrichment and folklore in concert with my oath of office to uphold the law for all citizens. No one can be above the law.

Please rest assured that a fair and honest evaluation of this situation will be conducted by my office. I also have requested that our municipal ombudsman immediately explore this situation . . .

From Mayor James to Didi Barrett.

During the last few days, we have received 10 phone calls and correspondence from some of our members and others who have read The New York Times, The Washington Post and The Record [ of Hackensack ] concerning Kea's ark. I'm sure you are aware of the national interest in Kea's ark.

What is going on parallels activities that surrounded the threat of destruction of the Watts Towers in 1959. Esthetics of the site were found lacking by the Los Angeles Building Department and some of the immediate neighbors, and there were code violations. Fortunately, there was time to assess the loss of the structure.

Today, the towers still stand. They are a property of the State of California, a National Register of Historic Places monument managed by the City of Los Angeles.

The towers, a focal point and symbol of culture and renewal for that part of Los Angeles, inspired an art center built with private contributions and are a magnet for tourists . . .

Some of my correspondence suggests that the ark could generate many needed community services without burdening the city . . .

Personal esthetics of building officials should not determine the cultural and social needs of Newark or the world view of your city. Violations of building codes can be corrected.

From Seymour Rosen, director of Saving and Preserving Arts and Cultural Environments, Los Angeles, to Mayor James.

As an anthropologist with special training in material folk culture, I would like to register my support for your ark. Your work is a monument of visionary art, a genre which, if rare, is especially American.

Kea's ark belongs in the category of art and architecture that recognizes the Watts Towers as significant public art. This genre has been included in the collections of the Smithsonian Institute.
