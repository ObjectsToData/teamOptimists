THE TEACHER; MOUNTING COMPETITION FOR COLLEGE TEACHERS

By STEVEN PROKESCH
Published: April 12, 1987

IF THERE is a talented black historian out there driving a taxicab because he thinks there is still an oversupply of college professors, Wesleyan University has a message for him: Reports of the glut have been greatly exaggerated. Indeed, after losing its black historian to the University of California at Davis last year Wesleyan has not been able to recruit someone of equal caliber to replace him on the Middletown, Conn., campus.

Nor is that the only job Wesleyan has had difficulty filling: 20 of its 275 faculty positions are now open in areas ranging from Soviet studies to classics. There have been plenty of people who have applied for the positions. But time and time again, Wesleyan has narrowed its choice for a given position to five or six finalists only to discover that rivals had already made them more attractive offers. ''The competition,'' said Nathanael Greene, Wesleyan's vice president for academic affairs, ''is very intense.''

For nearly two decades, the number of talented scholars seeking positions on the faculties of the nation's colleges and universities has far exceeded demand. But as officials of Wesleyan and scores of other institutions of higher education can attest, the days of plenty in a growing number of fields are rapidly drawing to a close.

Leaders of the academic community have long warned that the stampede of the nation's best and brightest students into law, medicine and business would leave the United States with a dearth of top-notch college professors. With their predictions now becoming reality, their concerns are giving way to alarm.

The reason is understandable: The shortages of highly qualified professors in science, engineering and a handful of other fields promises to become a full-blown crisis within 10 years. A major portion of academia's 440,000 full-time and 225,000 part-time faculty members were hired in the 1950's and 1960's to teach the baby-boom generation. They will start retiring in droves in the mid-1990's - right at the time that the baby boom's boomlet begins to pour into colleges.

''Between 1985 and 2010, institutions of higher education will have to hire between 450,000 and 500,000 new faculty members,'' said Jack H. Schuster, professor of education and public policy at the Claremont Graduate School in California and co-author of the book ''American Professors: A Natural Resource Imperiled,'' published last year. ''The substantial bulk of that hiring will have to be done in the last 15 years of that period,'' he said.

The plight of Columbia University, 15 percent of whose faculty of arts and sciences is expected to retire by 1990 and more than a third by 1995, has received a lot of attention. But in the eyes of officals at other institutions, Columbia is lucky: by having to hire new faculty members now, they say, it will probably avert the severe competition expected to occur in the late 1990's and beyond. Concerned about that prospect, colleges, private foundations, the states, Washington and industry are taking a variety of steps to encourage the nation's most gifted students to pursue academic careers. These programs include:

* Early-retirement packages designed to persuade older professors to step down and make way for younger professors.

* Hiring younger replacements for older tenured professors long before they reach retirement or there is an urgent need.

* Apprenticeships or internships aimed at giving promising undergraduates and baccalaureates a first-hand taste of teaching and scholarship in the hope that that they will want to take another bite.

* Increased funding for graduate and postdoctoral studies and research, including financial-assistance programs that forgive all or part of educational loans if the recipient goes into academia. To Maintain the Quality

These efforts share one goal: to make certain that the quality of the nation's college professors does not deteriorate when the current outgoing generation hangs up its caps and gowns. It is not that academic leaders are worried about having enough people to teach in the 1990's and beyond; the number of Ph.D.'s awarded annually has remained relatively constant, at more than 30,000. Rather, their big worry is that the quality of those who are pursuing academic careers is slipping. ''The issue of quality is a serious one,'' said Ernest L. Boyer, president of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching. Robert M. Rosenzweig, president of the Association of American Universities, agrees: ''What we need,'' he said, ''isn't a doubling of students going into Ph.D. programs. What we need is a larger fraction of the best graduates going into them.''

ATTRACTING those gifted students promises to be a difficult task. Academia is still struggling to recover from its difficulties in the 1970's and early 1980's. For one thing, the dearth of academic jobs in general and in the humanities and social sciences in particular scared away many bright young people interested in the learned life. Even though job prospects for those now entering graduate school in virtually all fields are better than they have been for decades, the fear of earning a Ph.D. only to wind up driving a cab or waiting on tables persists, various surveys suggest.
