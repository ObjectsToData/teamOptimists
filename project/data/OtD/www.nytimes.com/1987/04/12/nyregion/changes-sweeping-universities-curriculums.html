CHANGES SWEEPING UNIVERSITIES' CURRICULUMS

By EDWARD B. FISKE
Published: April 12, 1987

Students at Wesleyan University in Middletown, Conn., will soon be taking more science courses. At Colgate University in Hamilton, N.Y., students are studying ''non-Western'' cultures. The faculty at Hobart and William Smith Colleges in Geneva, N.Y., recently voted to require students to delve into ''issues of gender.''

Throughout the New York metropolitan region and across the country, colleges and universities are reassessing and changing their curriculums, introducing new requirements built around a more coherent definition of what an educated person should know.

Researchers for the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching reported earlier this year that two-thirds of the colleges in the United States had revised, or were revising, their general education programs. In a survey released last July, the American Council on Education, the largest college umbrella group in Washington, put the figure at 86 percent.

From Trinity College in Hartford to the University of Washington in Seattle, faculties are approving ''distribution'' requirements that force students to take courses across the academic spectrum, and are instituting proficiency requirements in areas such as writing, mathematics and foreign languages.

Many colleges have begun requiring courses in specific academic areas, including the effects of modern technology on society. Beginning next fall, freshmen at Wheaton College in Norton, Mass., must take a course on non-Western civilizations and a course that focuses on ''the diversity of race, class or ethnicity within U.S. society.''

Interdisciplinary courses are now as prolific as laboratory mice. Colgate's new general education program requires a sequence of four classes ''organized to make students think across the boundaries that divide the traditional disciplines.'' Students entering Trinity College this fall will have to select not only a major field of study but also a six-course minor that embraces at least three disciplines.

In part because of the large number of institutions involved in the process, no single pattern of curriculum revision has emerged. COURSES HAVE GROWN OUT OF A 'SENSE OF CRISIS'

College officials cite different reasons for their new concern about what undergraduates study. Several national reports in the last four years have assailed the quality of college teaching. The Secretary of Education, William J. Bennett, has asserted that American colleges are ''ripping off'' their students with ill-conceived curriculums.

Some educators see the new concern for structure and coherence as part of a general return to traditional values and ways of doing things within the academic world. Others see it as a reflection of broader social anxieties.

''Curriculum changes usually arise out of political and social crises,'' said Leon Botstein, the president of Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, N.Y.

''Columbia's general education curriculum grew out of World War I,'' Mr. Botstein said. ''Chicago's came out of the crisis of democracy in the 1930's. The current reform movement reflects a sense of urgency about American economic and political power. A curriculum is the imposition of one generaton's sense of crisis on the next generation.''

Educators say the new wave of curriculum reform must be understood against the background of the student unrest of the 1960's and 1970's. Acceding to student demands for more control over the courses they took, many faculties reduced, and in some cases abolished, curriculum requirements.

''The result was intellectual anarchy,'' said Peter R. Pouncey, the president of Amherst College. ''There was a decline in what students knew. Certain skills like good writing fell by the wayside.''

Reaction against this situation began in the mid-1970's, much of it inspired by Harvard College's re-examination and tightening of its own core curriculum. Scores of colleges moved to restore distribution requirements, which typically set the number of courses students must take in arts and humanities, social sciences and natural sciences. A GROWING CONCERN FOR ACADEMIC EXCELLENCE

While faculty members ''reasserted their control'' over the curriculum, ''they did nothing to challenge the autonomy of individual departments or make a statement about what it means to be an educated person,'' said Ernest L. Boyer, the author of ''College,'' a study of undergraduate education published in January.

''In retrospect it's clear that the earlier changes were political and territorial,'' Mr. Boyer said. The recent flurry of changes, he said, is ''driven mainly by the growing national concern about academic excellence.''

Not all institutions are making changes. The faculty of Brown University has rejected the imposition of distribution or other requirements, saying that students should be viewed as mature enough to design their own courses of study. At other institutions, including Columbia College, traditional standards were never abandoned.

But in the last year, many institutions have been making changes.
