WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: April 12, 1987

MEDIEVAL MUSICAL PLAY ''Ordo Virtutum,'' which means virtuous rite, is generally acknowledged to be the first musical play ever written - around 1150 in Germany. It will be performed at Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers at 8 P.M. Saturday and next Sunday in Reisinger Hall, and again at 4 P.M. April 26 at the Church of St. James the Less in Scarsdale.

Rebekah Wilshire, a senior at the college, is the producer and a soprano performer, having come across the work through a German record album of the same title. ''My mother is a feminist writer and she was intrigued by Hildegard von Bingen, a 12th-century abbess who wrote it. Only the obligatos, or sung melodies, were written down. The accompaniment was probably a drone - a single note to keep everybody on pitch,'' she said.

The abbess appears to have been an early women's liberationist. Unwilling to resolve her differences with the monks who raised her, she left the monastery, taking 18 other artistic young women with her, and started her own abbey, where she wrote poetry and music without fear of the monks' disapproval. She continued to be criticized by the public at festival times for dressing the nuns in pretty clothes, jewels and fancy hair styles - which she did, Miss Wilshire noted, because ''she thought the brides of Christ should be as attractive as other brides.''

Her music sounds much like Gregorian chant, ''with some innovative differences - it's very beautiful,'' Miss Wilshire said. The poetic text, which will be sung in the original Latin with English translations distributed to the audience, deals with the struggle of the Soul against Satan. The soul and her helpers - the virtues of Hope, Mercy, Chastity, Humility and others - are personified by women. Only the role of Satan is written for a man, and without music. ''His is the only spoken role,'' Miss Wilshire said.

Early musical instruments, choreography and costumes in the classic Greek mode, which was the type of dress adopted in such medieval morality plays, add to the authenticity of the production, which takes about one-and-a-half hours. The Oxford Dictionary of Music classifies the piece as ''a preopera containing 82 melodies.'' The music is described as ''of strong individuality and complexity.''

Admission to all performances is free, as is a lecture about Hildegard von Bingen to be given by the Rev. Nancy Roth at 2:30 P.M. April 26 at St. James The Less. Call 337-0700, extension 423 for more information. DANCE CLASSICS

Rondo Dance Theater has distinguished itself as a chamber dance ensemble specializing in presenting choreography devised between 1930 and 1970 - now defined as modern dance classics. Rondo's 10 dancers will be performing at 4 P.M. today at the Paramount Center for the Arts in Peekskill.

Two Jose Limon works, ''La Malinche'' from 1947 and ''The Winged'' from 1966, were directed by Laura Glenn, with music by Norman Lloyd and Hank Johnson, respectively; and ''Woody Sez,'' drawing on the music of Woody Guthrie, was created in 1941 by Sophie Maslow. The single contemporary work, choreographed this year by Addison Hoffman, is called ''Templum'' with music by Paul Hindemith.

Tickets are $8, $10 and $12; students and the elderly, $6. Call the box office for reservations and more information, 739-2333. AN ARTIST'S OBSERVATION

Synchronized videotapes shown on seven large monitors will tell a story of man versus nature in ''Wilderness,'' a video installation by Mary Lucier, opening today at the Neuberger Museum of the State University of New York at Purchase. In it, Ms. Lucier first studies the idyllic 19th-century landscapes of Frederic Church and other Hudson River and northeastern American painters, then returns with her camera to the scenes as they are today. Ranging from Westchester County to Newfoundland, some sites have remained unspoiled, but others have been paved with highways, sliced through by railroads, defaced, deteriorated or destroyed one way or another. ''It is an artist's observation, not a judgmental tract,'' said George Calderaro, public-information officer for the museum. Music for the sound track was composed by Earl Howard.

Ms. Lucier will give a talk titled ''Wilderness: Video in the Landscape'' at 3 P.M. today in the Humanities Building auditorium, to be followed by a public reception. Admission to the talk is $4 at the door.

The video, which takes 20 minutes, will run continuously during museum hours, from 10 A.M. to 4 P.M. Tuesday through Friday, 1 to 5 P.M. weekends, through June 7. There is no admission charge. THE IMPLACABLE TURTLE

The voice of the turtle may be difficult to hear, but the sight of one at this time of year, moving slowly across the road at its peril, or over the lawn to the consternation of the family dog or cat, is not uncommon. Westchester is home to the box turtle, the wood turtle, painted turtle, diamondback terrapin, ''stinkpot'' musk turtle, snapping turtle and red-eared slider. A slide-talk and discussion about these implacable creatures, their life cycles and habitats, will be given at 8 P.M. Wednesday in the Trailside Nature Museum at the Ward Pound Ridge Reservation in Cross River. Ken Soltesz, naturalist from the Teatown Lake Reservation, will be the speaker. Admission and parking are free. TEMPLE BETH EL PROGRAM

A program of music mostly by Jewish composers will be presented at 8 P.M. Saturday by the Westchester Chamber Orchestra at Temple Beth El, 220 South Bedford Road, Chappaqua. ''We tried to do an all-Jewish program,'' said Barry Charles Hoffman, the orchestra's director, ''but most Jewish composers are contemporary and that meant learning a whole new repertoire in a very short rehearsal time. We had to substitute some Bach.''

The premiere of David Stern's ''Concerto Grosso for Flute, Violin and Strings in C major'' will be performed by Cecilia Sparacio, flutist; Abraham Appleman, violinist; and 13 string players of the two--and-a-half-year-old chamber orchestra. Mr. Stern teaches theory at the Mannes College of Music in Manhattan, and Mr. Appleman is concertmaster of the Metropolitan Opera orchestra.

Also on the program is the Serenade for Strings in C major by Jacques Offenbach, whose father was cantor of the Cologne Synagogue in the mid-1800's; and Ernest Bloch's Concerto Grosso No. 2 for Strings. The two Bach pieces are Air from the Orchestral Suite No. 3 in D major and the Violin Concerto in A minor, performed by Mr. Appleman. Amy Lasswell is the guest conductor. Tickets cost $10. Call 238-3928 for reservations and driving directions.
