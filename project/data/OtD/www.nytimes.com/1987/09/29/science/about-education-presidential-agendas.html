ABOUT EDUCATION; Presidential Agendas

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: September 29, 1987

THE next President, said Senator Albert Gore Jr., a Democratic contender, ''should be known as an education President'' and he should use the ''bully pulpit'' to get support for education.

The only man in modern times to savor the designation of education President was Lyndon B. Johnson. He pushed through the Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965, which vastly increased Federal spending on the schools, and he presided over the creation of Project Head Start, a prekindergarten program that has dramatically helped poor children. He extended the Federal role and opened the Federal purse. There has been no comparable Presidential action since.

But wide public dissatisfaction and pressure for reform has made it unwise for any candidate to ignore education in the 1988 campaign. But so far none of the candidates has produced a bombshell of educational innovation or promise.

They agree that teaching must be improved; that basic skills and academic standards need shoring up; that successful schools should be rewarded, probably with Federal dollars, and that the rising cost of higher education must not be allowed to shut out able young people, especially members of minority groups.

Perhaps the only significantly new concern, shared by most of the candidates, is the desperate need for a network of facilities for high-quality day care and early childhood education -a dramatic extension of Head Start to 2- 3-, and 4-year-olds. Although much of the cost would have to be borne by the states, communities and parents, substantial Federal help will be needed, especially for children from poor families.

Individual candidates have stated some of their priorities. Among the Republicans:

* Vice President Bush has proposed a national college savings plan with tax advantages for parents who put money aside for their children's education. He wants children tested early and often to make sure they are learning.

* Senator Bob Dole of Kansas wants to focus on the quality of teaching and more rigorous standards in the education of teachers.

* Pete du Pont, the former Governor of Delaware, urges creation of a system in which parents would receive vouchers that they could use to pay for the education of their children in the public or private school of their choice. This proposal was originally the centerpiece of Reagan Administration's education program, but has long since been abandoned.

* Alexander M. Haig Jr. wants children to know more about the political system. He wants schools ''to teach virtues, not just values.''

* Representative Jack F. Kemp of upstate New York wants spending on education ''re-deployed'' for better returns in the classroom and urges help for educationally deprived children before they reach school age.

* The Rev. Pat Robertson wants to eliminate ''progressive education'' as advocated by John Dewey, which has tried to link education to the needs of a democratic society.

Among the Democrats:

* Bruce Babbitt, the former Governor of Arizona, wants a voucher system, supported by Federal and state Governments, to pay for child care. He would like the Federal Government to pay the entire cost of Medicaid and let the states use the savings for education.

* Gov. Michael S. Dukakis of Massachusetts has called for Federal support of educational research and development.

* Representative Richard A. Gephardt of Missouri would give Federal bonuses to states and individual schools that have dealt successfully with major problems, such as dropouts.

* Senator Gore, of Tennessee, calls for increased Federal aid to needy students and for a new program to provide literacy training to all welfare and food stamp recipients.

* The Rev. Jesse Jackson, who tells young people ''to put hope in your brains, not dope in your veins,'' has urged the Federal Government to scrutinize the colleges' hiring and promotion of women, members of minority groups and handicapped people who are faculty members.

* Senator Paul Simon of Illinois wants higher starting salaries for teachers, adult literacy programs, scholarships for top high school students who want to become teachers, more money for preschool education and for libraries.

It all sounds reasonable, except that the Federal Government's power is rather limited in dealing with many of the issues on which the candidates agree. Theodore R. Sizer, chairman of the department of education at Brown University and a leader in educational change, says that this is why education remains largely a ''rhetorical issue.''

Ernest L. Boyer, president of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, also suggests that unanimity among the candidates could be a problem: their speeches and platforms might become indistinguishable from each other. James B. Hunt Jr., a former Governor of North Carolina who is a leader in efforts to link the issues of education and the economy, has noted that it is not easy to make education a vote-getting issue. ''It isn't going to jump up there like a foreign policy crisis,'' he said.

Further, consensus does not bring out the voters. Controversy - even over phony issues - does.

There are potentially controversial education issues - specific proposals for lowering college costs or raising student subsidies; plans, at substantial costs, for an early childhood education network; or perhaps an entirely new blueprint for a National Endowment for Education, patterned on the successful endowments for the arts and the humanities.

Earlier this month, all the Democratic and two of the Republican candidates debated the issue at the University of North Carolina in Chapel Hill. There was much talk about a glorious vision of the schools by the year 2000; but it is easier to spell out distant goals than to tackle today's problems. Education's power to extract practical commitments from the campaign could get lost in high-minded agreement that gives no candidate an edge, turning voters' attention to other, more exciting issues.
