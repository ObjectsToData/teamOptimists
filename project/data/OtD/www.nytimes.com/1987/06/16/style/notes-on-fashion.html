NOTES ON FASHION

By MICHAEL GROSS
Published: June 16, 1987

The Metropolitan Museum of Art has announced that its Costume Institute will no longer present the annual blockbuster shows of historical fashion that have been a fixture in New York for 14 years.

The fund-raising dinner-dance that always accompanied the opening of the shows will continue, but the institute will mount large exhibitions only sporadically, with less ambitious presentations in between. The next big show, featuring fashions from the Napoleonic era, is scheduled for the fall of 1988, in conjunction with the newer fashion museum in Paris, the Musee des Arts de la Mode. Before that, the institute plans a show of items from its permanent collection, to run from next November through April, and a summer 1988 exhibition of clothes worn by the Miaos of China. Another exhibit will feature the wardrobe of the Duke of Windsor.

The museum said the reason for the change was ''the effects on the costumes of being kept on exhibition for long periods of time.'' But other factors might have been involved.

Many of the possibilities were detailed in ''Selling Culture'' by Deborah Silverman (Pantheon, $17.95), a book published late last year that attacked the institute's exhibitions. The author, a cultural historian, asserted, among many other things, that the shows had ''a fundamental inauthenticity, a reliance on fabrication, and a glaring disparity between symbolism and reality.'' She singled out the institute's special consultant and avatar, Diana Vreeland, for criticism.

Jean L. Druesedow, who heads the institute, said, ''The book brought certain things into focus, but it was never mentioned'' in discussing the change.

Mrs. Vreeland's shows have been among the museum's most popular and profitable attractions.

''The Costume Institute's time will be used in a way more characteristic of our 18 other curatorial departments,'' said Jon Ross, a spokesman for the museum. ''We are bringing the institute in line with what is doable in the museum world.''

Mrs. Vreeland, who is ailing, ''can't leave her apartment,'' Ms. Druesedow said, who added that she remains ''intellectually very active'' and has been ''informed from the very beginning'' of discussions about the changes. This year's Costume Institute benefit, to be held Dec. 7, will be a tribute to her. THE White House announced that

Betsy Bloomingdale and Bill Blass will be appointed to the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities. Asked why he thought he had been appointed, Mr. Blass replied, ''I don't know exactly.'' TUESDAY night, Edward S. Finkelstein, the chairman of Macy's, was to have received the first Man of the Year in Retailing award from the Berkeley School, a business college. But he didn't show up. His daughter-in-law had a baby in Hong Kong, where his son heads the Macy's office, so he flew there instead.

The night before, Ralph Lauren, the designer, was to have made his first public appearance since undergoing brain surgery. He didn't show up.

''He hasn't resumed a full schedule,'' said Buffy Birrittella, a vice president of Polo/Ralph Lauren. ''He's doing really, really well, but he wasn't up to being in a party situation with 1,500 people.'' She added that Mr. Lauren has been working, though, holding meetings at his Manhattan apartment and being interviewed for a profile in Esquire. NELL'S, a West 14th Street club, continues to attract the city's fly-by-night crowd, as well as celebrated visitors. Tuesday night, for example, Tony Bennett and Princess Stephanie of Monaco both put in appearances. Nell's will close for vacations for three weeks in August, and will reopen after Labor Day. ''We're going to make it even more shabby,'' promised Keith McNally, a co-owner.

Earlier Tuesday night, at a dinner at the New York Academy of Art on Lafayette Street, Nell Campbell, the namesake of Nell's, offered an entrance-making example for those seeking admittance to her establishment. Ms. Campbell arrived dressed in a hooped ''mini crini'' by Vivienne Westwood, a turquoise cummerbund and a ribbon-trimmed see-through top.

''Scuse me,'' she said as she breezed past the invitation checkers at the door. ''Am I barging in?''

The eclectic crowd also included the designers Tommy Hilfiger, Carolina Herrera and Stephen Sprouse; Hugh L. Carey and his wife, Evangeline Gouletas-Carey; Elizabeth Ray, and Peter Max, the artist, who said that beginning in October, his 1960's illustrations will be recycled on clothing, just in time for the 20th anniversary of the fall after the Summer of Love.

The dinner was a benefit for a scholarship fund, and was held in memory of Andy Warhol, who served on the academy's board. It was punctuated with musical performances in which four operatic sopranos - one in a black bustier, a short black leather skirt, frizzed hair, one knee-high lace stocking and a pink garter - alternated in classical and pop selections while posing in dramatic tableaux against columns and in windows.

Lynn von Furstenberg offered a succinct critique of the show. ''Hubba, hubba,'' she said.
