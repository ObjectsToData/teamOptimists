WHAT'S NEW IN COLLEGE RECRUITING; Law and Medicine: Easier Access

By JOHN LOFFLIN; John Lofflin, a journalism professor at Park College, writes about business in Kansas City, Mo.
Published: October 18, 1987

NOW may be the time to go to medical or law school. Applications are down significantly, and the schools are scrambling for candidates. In 1974, when medical school applications peaked, there were 2.8 applicants for each available slot; now there are just about 2 prospects for each seat. The peak for law schools was not until fall 1980, but the valley is just as deep. Applications to law schools declined about 17 percent between 1981 and 1985, according to William J. Kennish, consultant to the Law School Admissions Council, although they increased slightly in 1986.

Some professional schools are trying to attract nontraditional candidates. The Mount Sinai School of Medicine in New York has been trying to lure non-science majors. ''A broader-based humanities education may produce physicians who are better able to relate to patients as people,'' explained Dr. Barry D. Stimmel, academic dean at Mount Sinai. This year the school began an ambitious humanities recruiting program. Colleges were invited to enroll five humanities sophomores in a pre-med curriculum of minimum science with students spending summers at Mount Sinai. Admission to medical study is guaranteed if they maintain a B average.

Medical schools have sought minority students for two decades - with limited success - but that competition has really put the squeeze on predominantly black medical schools.

''We look on the opening of medical schools as very positive, but it did make things more competititive for us,'' said Dr. David Satcher, president of Meharry Medical School in Nashville, which is 85 percent black. Meharry's recruiting efforts begin early. The college has a summer program for freshmen and sophomores at feeder colleges, and even a program for selected high schools.

Over all, the number of medical school applicants has declined partly because of the high cost of a medical education and the increasing limits on doctors' incomes, Dr. Stimmel said. He added: ''In New York City it's not unusual to graduate owing $50,000. Government control and the rise of H.M.O.'s limit autonomy and income for physicians. There's been enough in the press that the message is finally getting across to medical school applicants.''

The effect of bad publicity is also felt by law school recruiters. ''We've been hurt by the notion bantered about that there are too many lawyers,'' grouses Suzy L. Golden, acting director of law school admissions at Golden Gate College. ''There probably is a glut, but if you're good, there are never too many.'' Golden Gate has cut the size of its entering class so it can afford to take only the better students. The entering class this fall at the urban San Francisco school is 130. In 1982 it was 300.

Schools like the University of Toledo law school are banking on increased scholarships to stabilize enrollment. The Ohio school of 600 offers 15 full and 20 partial grants to new students, double the grants given in 1982.

''One must be inventive,'' said Ms. Golden. ''Although the decline in applicants seems to be leveling off, we've all learned a lot. We've learned you've got to determine where your market is, who you are and what you have to offer.''
