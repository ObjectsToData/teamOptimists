Queens College, 50 Years Old, Seeks to Rebound

By DEIRDRE CARMODY
Published: October 11, 1987

There is a sense of energy and purpose these days on this incongruous campus of pink Spanish-mission-style buildings with red-tiled roofs deep in the heart of Flushing, as Queens College prepares to celebrate its 50th anniversary today.

The members of Mr. Finkelstein's Painting 2 class are on the lawn, squinting at their easels. A stream of South Asian, Chinese, Hispanic, Haitian, North African, Jamaican and other New Yorkers surges by on the way to classes. A nearly completed library incorporating pink and red building materials as if they were the college colors rises on the western end of the campus, visible evidence of a $175 million building program.

''This place is wonnnnnderful,'' said Dr. Shirley Strum Kenny, a Texan who was provost for the division of arts and humanities at the University of Maryland before she became president of Queens College two years ago.

The place has not always been wonderful, however. The city fiscal crisis of the mid-1970's forced the college to halt all hiring and building, and along with the other colleges of the City University, to charge tuition for the first time. The imposition of tuition in 1976 and the changing demographics of Queens -with far fewer people of college age -brought a steep decline in enrollment, from a high of 30,000 in 1975 to 15,000 in 1984. The student population is now 16,000, and tuition is $625 a semester. Shifts in Enrollment

''The decline in student enrollment defines one of the realities which face Queens College,'' a recent self-study of the college said.

The CUNY Chancellor, Dr. Joseph S. Murphy, who was president of Queens College from 1970 to 1977, said data he had seen suggested the decline in enrollment had bottomed out. He cited a similar situation at Brooklyn College, where the numbers have begun to turn around, and graduate enrollment at Queens, which has also begun to rise.

''Queens College is a very, very good liberal arts college,'' Dr. Murphy said. ''Not as strong as City College in physics and engineering. Not as strong as Hunter in English and biology.

''But if you look at all the colleges in the system, it's a very good little undergraduate college. The buildings have been taken care of. The infrastructure is there, and the faculty is excellent.''

Although the number of students is down, the student population has not lost its diversity. Queens College continues to be a conduit to the American way of life for a new wave of immigrants, much as it was for the Jewish immigrants who arrived in Queens 50 years ago. Today, the college estimates Asians make up 12 percent of the student body, blacks 11 percent, and Hispanic and Greek students 10 percent each. Problems of Next Century

''In some ways, what we will be doing in the next 50 years is what happened in the last 50 years,'' Dr. Kenny said in an interview in her 12th-floor office, from which the skyline of Manhattan looks like a faraway painting. ''It seems to me that the problems we are solving today are the problems that American education will be facing in the next century, when what we now call minorities will be a majority of the population.''

In an attempt to address the problems of bringing newcomers into the mainstream, the college held a seminar last summer for 35 teachers who work with Latin American students, to give the teachers a substantive program in Latin American culture. The college is also studying how to provide a framework to teach American literature to non-native students, many of whom have never heard of many features that are a natural part of American childhood.

Queens College has always been known for its top-flight faculty. In referring to the faculty in the context of the fiscal crisis, a report last year by an evaluating team for the Commission on Higher Education of the Middle States Association of Colleges and Schools spoke of the team's ''sense of wonderment, even awe,'' at ''the extraordinary quality, pride and commitment that have been preserved at Queens College under adverse and menacing conditions.'' Turnover of Faculty Members

The older faculty presents real problems for the college. By the mid-90's, it will have had a 30 or 40 percent turnover.

One group of faculty members was hired in the late 50's and early 60's, when the primary emphasis was on teaching. In the 60's and 70's, until the fiscal crisis put a virtual halt to hiring, another group of faculty members was hired with a much greater emphasis on their scholarship and the expectation they would be active in Ph.D. programs.

''In many departments, that disparity created some hard feelings, because as far as the reward system goes, it's generally easier to deal with people who publish a lot,'' said Prof. David H. Speidel, chairman of the geology department and a member of the University Senate, who arrived at the college in 1966.

What is needed to keep longtime teachers energized, he said, is an infusion of young people with new ideas.

About three years ago, Queens hired 40 new faculty members, the first infusion since the 70's. Dr. Kenny said she was intent on hiring good young faculty members. One of the main concerns is that the college see few minority-group teachers on the horizon.

''A lot depends on our enrollment, since our budget is enrollment driven,'' the head of the Academic Senate, Elizabeth S. Boylan, said. ''If enrollment goes up, we will be able to hire new people.''

Recently, Dr. Kenny has sent shock waves through the faculty by setting forth that it is she, and not the teachers, who run the college.
