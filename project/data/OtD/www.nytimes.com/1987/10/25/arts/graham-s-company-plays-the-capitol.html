Graham's Company Plays the Capitol

By EVA RESNIKOVA; Eva Resnikova writes on dance for The New Criterion and The New York Observer.
Published: October 25, 1987

THE MARTHA GRAHAM dance company season that ends tonight opened on Oct. 6 with a celebrity-studded gala benefit performance that looked both backward to the hard-won glory of its past and forward to its future. That future is dependent, Graham company sources say, on greatly increased funding, much of which the company is attempting to secure by bypassing the National Endowment for the Arts and appealing directly to Congress. A $4.1 million appropriation for the company has already passed the Senate. Though the appropriation must still be acted upon by the House, the Graham company's tactics have already sparked controversy in the arts community.

It is a future based on the premise that the Graham repertory - the company's raison d'etre - will be captured on videotape while the choreographer, now 93 years old, is still able to supervise its preservation. And, though the company's first concern is necessarily with its own continued existence as a repository for this choreographic treasure, there is the expectation that some of the works will eventually enrich the repertories of other dance companies. All of this, however, is dependent on the availability of funding in amounts that go far beyond the challenge grants meted out by Government institutions and outright gifts of corporate sponsors and wealthy individuals.

There were plenty of the latter in the audience the evening of the gala: The Graham company has been a magnet for the beau monde for years. What made this evening different was a pair of unlikely celebrities on stage. Appearing as guest artists in ''Appalachian Spring,'' Miss Graham's 1944 masterpiece of Americana, were the Soviet emigre ballet superstars and company directors Rudolf Nureyev and Mikhail Baryshnikov. For some time, Mr. Nureyev has expressed his desire to mount this work for his Paris Opera Ballet, but Miss Graham feels that the work is ill-suited to a French ballet company. Such is the mutual admiration between Miss Graham and Mr. Baryshnikov, however, that his American Ballet Theater could well become the first ballet company to perform a work by the matriarch of the American modern dance.

What happens to the Martha Graham Dance Company will in many respects serve as a test case for the future of other modern-dance troupes, especially those of second-generation moderns like Merce Cunningham and Paul Taylor.

In the 1930's and 40's, when Martha Graham was struggling to create a completely new indigenous American art form, it was assumed that the highly personal works of modern dancers could not survive their creators. Lincoln Kirstein, now the general director of the New York City Ballet, wrote of Miss Graham in 1934: ''Can her dancing be applied to others who are impervious to her personal appeal? Has any mode of dancing attached to a single name lasted longer than its inventor's theatrical life?'' Fifty years later, in an interview in The New Yorker, Mr. Kirstein called modern dance ''a kind of irrelevant heterodoxy'' and added, ''For the Government to back it seems to me a waste of money.''

Government funding institutions have seemed to share the belief that, at least as far as long-term financing is concerned, modern dance is a poor investment. In 1983, the National Endowment for the Arts rejected an application from the Martha Graham company for a $1 million challenge grant for projects intended to insure the continued existence of the company, its school and its repertory through the videotaping of ballets and the renovation of the company's East Side home.

Miss Graham, 89 years old at the time, said of the decision: ''I believe that they are afraid that when I die -I don't have a long life span - nothing will continue. I have every reason to believe that it will. The dances are in the custody of two people I trust and have trained for the future: Linda Hodes and Ron Protas,'' her company's associate artistic directors.

The following year the company received a $500,000 National Endowment challenge grant. Since then, it has addressed charges of lax management in the past and the absence of a detailed blueprint for the years ahead. It has also embarked upon an ambitious and innovative plan - one that is generally acknowledged to be unique in the American dance world - to provide funds that would help to insure its future.

According to this plan, the company would bypass the National Endowment and go directly to Congress for Federal funding as part of the Interior Appropriations Bill. It is the brainchild of the 34-year-old Richard E. Burke, who became president of the company's board of trustees last March. His approach is based on the assertion that Martha Graham's work is part of America's national heritage that is in danger of being lost and is therefore deserving of Government support for its protection.

Before coming to New York to take his place in the family business, American Electro Products. Mr. Burke spent a decade in Washington working for Senator Edward M. Kennedy, most recently as his chief aide.
