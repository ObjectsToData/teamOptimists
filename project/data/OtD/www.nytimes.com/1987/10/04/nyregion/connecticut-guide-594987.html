CONNECTICUT GUIDE


Published: October 4, 1987

YALE CONCERT Eleazar de Carvalho, the Brazilian conductor and composer, will celebrate his arrival at Yale University by conducting the Yale Philharmonia in a free concert at 8 P.M. Friday in Woolsey Hall. In his new post as professor of music and conductor in residence, Mr. Carvalho will teach advanced students and serve as conductor of the Philharmonia, succeeding Otto-Werner Mueller. His concert will replicate his first appearance in the United States with the Boston Symphony Orchestra in 1947. He will conduct William Schuman's Symphony for Strings, Arnold Schonberg's Chamber Symphony No. 1 (Op. 9), and Hector Berlioz's ''Symphonie Fantastique.'' A student of Serge Koussevitsky, Mr. Carvalho has taught several distinguished conductors, including Seiji Ozawa, Zubin Mehta, Claudio Abbado and Gustav Meier.

ANTIQUES SHOW

Eight historic house museums of the Connecticut Antiquarian and Landmarks Society, the state's largest and oldest preservation organization, are to benefit from the sales at the 21st Connecticut Antiques Show that will begin on Friday and continue through next Sunday at the State Armory in Hartford. Exhibitions by almost 100 dealers will be mounted in room vignettes, featuring furnishings and accessories from the 200-year period before 1840. No reproductions are allowed. Among the special collections are paintings, prints, porcelains - including Oriental export, Canton and Staffordshire - blown and patterned glass, early clocks, scientific and medical instruments, Shaker furniture, lighting devices, American pewter, oriental carpets and quilts. For those interested in architectural restoration, there is to be an exhibition by the 18th-Century Company of Durham, which specializes in antique flooring, paneling and other restoration activities. The show hours are from 5 to 10 P.M. on Friday; 1 to 9 P.M. Saturday and 1 to 6 P.M. next Sunday. The Armory is at the corner of Capitol Avenue and Broad Street. Admission is $5.

MARGARET WHITING SINGS

A concert by the singer Margaret Whiting at 8 P.M. Saturday is the first event of the new Music Theater of Connecticut. The company, based at the Roger Ludlowe High School on Unquowa Road in Fairfield, expects to present four productions a year, as well as operate a touring children's theater company and a school of theater arts. In selecting works for production, Kevin Connors, the artistic director, plans to tap little-known American musicals and brand-new productions. Opening the season in December will be an original version of ''A Christmas Carol.'' Tickets to Miss Whiting's concert are $25, and those wishing to attend a post-performance reception for the singer and the new music theater venture will be charged $50 and $100, tax deductible. Call 259-1198 for reservations.

'WEST END CLASSICS'

From 11 A.M. to 4 P.M. today, the Hartford Architecture Conservancy will hold its third annual house tour. The tour, ''West End Classics: Hartford's Turn-of-the-Century Houses,'' includes the boyhood home of Brendan Gill, a writer for The New Yorker, at 735 Prospect Avenue, and of Lillian W. Hart at 227 Girard Avenue, which was more recently owned by Evelyn V. Preston, whose estate provides the city with free annual outdoor concerts. The 1861 Gothic Revival home of Charles E. Perkins, who was Samuel Clemens's attorney, and the Tudor-style mansion of George W. Ellis, are on the tour of seven homes. Parking will be provided at the Greater Hartford Community College, 61 Woodland Street, where shuttle buses will take visitors to the tour bus. Admission is $10.

'BERNSTEIN' AUTHOR

Joan Peyser, the author of the book ''Bernstein: A Biography,'' is to speak Wednesday at the Westport Library's noon series. She will discuss her experiences in writing the book, which took 3 and a half years, during which she interviewed friends, family, colleagues and acquaintances of the composer-conductor. Miss Peyser has been the editor of Musical Quarterly, and the author of several other books, including a biography of the French conductor Pierre Boulez. In 1986, she received first prize in the humanities category from the Association of American Publishers for ''The Orchestra: Origins and Transformations.'' Miss Peyser has won four Ascap awards for excellence in writing about music. Her talk is free, and visitors may bring lunch.

NEW DEAL PHOTOGRAPHS

About three dozen photographs taken during the eight years in the 1930's in which the Farm Security Administration existed will be displayed at Wesleyan University in Middletown through Oct. 30 at the Davison Art Center. The photographs - including those of Walker Evans, Dorothea Lange, Russell Lee and Ben Shahn - were originally published in magazines and books and exhibited as proof of the need for New Deal legislation. Viewing hours are from noon to 4 P.M. Tuesday through Friday and 2 to 5 P.M. on weekends.

ITALIAN ART IN NEW HAVEN

Art from Italy is receiving much attention in New Haven. In addition to the Yale Art Gallery exhibition ''A Taste of Angels: Neapolitan Painting from 1650 to 1750,'' on view through Nov. 29, comes Artspace, with ''The Italian Artist,'' which includes works by 15 contemporary artists and architects from Italy and the United States. The display includes the work of prize-winning painters, designers and distinguished Italian photographers and architects of Milan and other Italian cities as well as work in many media by Italian-American artists. The exhibition may be seen from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday, and from 1 to 5 P.M. Sunday until Nov. 15. The gallery is at 44 Whitney Avenue. $90ELEANOR CHARLES
