Museum, With Just a Relic to Go On, Tries to Rebuild Lost Piece of History

Special to the New York Times
Published: August 16, 1987

Almost 230 years ago, British forces retreating on Lake George after a catastrophic defeat in the last French and Indian War scuttled their fleet of large troop-carrying rowboats before marching south.

Today, scores, perhaps hundreds, of these bateaus, which shuttled thousands of troops and tons of supplies in the French and Indian and Revolutionary Wars, are thought to lie buried in the mud on the bottom of lakes and inland waterways throughout the region. However, only the bottom part of a single bateau has ever been salvaged, and none are intact on dry land.

This summer the Basin Harbor Maritime Museum in Ferrisburg has set out to recapture this lost piece of maritime history. On Sept. 6 it will launch a reproduction of a bateau, built with hand tools that could have existed two centuries ago, volunteer labor and a $3,000 grant from the Vermont Council on the Humanities.

Like paleontologists trying to reconstruct a body from two or three bones, the builders, led by Arthur Cohn, the 38-year-old museum director, must document what they can and extrapolate the shape of the bateau from the relic housed at the Adirondack Museum in Blue Mountain Lake, N.Y. The rest becomes a historical guessing game. Recovery Is Too Costly

Mr. Cohn said the museum was building the reproduction rather than trying to recover an original because salvaging such old and fragile vessels, along with preservation and display, would be far more expensive.

''Too often,'' he said, ''they pull a boat out of the water and then try to decide what to do with it. A lot of history has been lost that way. A replica is not only cheaper to build, but kids can climb in it, people can ride in it.''

Mr. Cohn, who is also a professional skin diver, has done most of the historical research on the bateaus. Dexter Cooper, 32, a professional boat builder from Hartland, has volunteered both his Saturdays and his knowledge of wooden boat restoration. He is assisted by Clem Thompson, 28 years old, a diver and woodworker from Shelburne.

''Bateaus were about 34 feet at the waterline and carried 20 to 30 people,'' Mr. Cooper said. ''Those numbers are documented. They had flat bottoms and they were double-ended but not symmetrical. It's also been documented that they carried five oars and a paddle. One thing we don't know is whether they sat or stood to row them.''

Mr. Cohn said the bateaus fell out of use after the Revolution as bigger boats, powered by sails, were used for commerce on Lake Champlain and Lake George. A Deadly Miscalculation

Mr. Cohn believes the saga of the existing bateau relic began on Lake George in 1758. It was one of a fleet of 900 bateaus and 100 whale boats carrying 15,000 British troops to attack Fort Carillon, later made famous as Fort Ticonderoga, defended by 3,500 Frenchmen under the command of the Marquis de Montcalm.

In a catastrophic miscalculation, the British commander, James Abercromby, sent his best troops in a frontal assault on the fort. More than 2,000 of them died in 24 hours, and the British force retreated to the southern end of the lake.

''We believe the British sank their bateaus before marching south,'' Mr. Cohn said. ''That's the end of the story until 1960, when some local skin divers found five bateaus on the bottom of Lake George. John Gardner of Mystic Seaport salvaged one of them in 1960 and put it in the Adirondack Museum. That's the model for the one we're building.''

Resurrecting the boat-building techniques of two centuries ago has not proved difficult, Mr. Cooper said, because the techniques have not changed dramatically.

''Boat builders tend to be conservative people,'' he said. ''If you try something new and it fails, your customer drowns.'' Finding the Rare White Oak

In fact, the most difficult obstacle was finding the white oak needed to construct the frame and oars. In the virgin forests of the 1700's, white oak was plentiful and was prized for shipbuilding because it was strong and resisted rot, Mr. Cohn said, but by the Civil War little was left. After several weeks of searching, a logger found a small stand of white oak on the Vermont side of Lake Champlain.

Mr. Cohn's research has convinced him that the project has historical and educational value.

''This is the grandfather of troop transports,'' he said. ''It was the first design brought to North America by the European settlers. The Indians used dugout and birchbark canoes.

''This could be built from locally available materials, and it didn't require a specialized shipyard. We know they were sometimes built in two days. To store them over the winter, they would fill them with stones and leave them on the bottom of the lake to be preserved by the mud.'' Benedict Arnold's Request

Bateaus were often used as supply boats or guard boats to warships. Benedict Arnold carried a smaller version on his trip through the Maine wilderness in the fall of 1777. In a letter dated September 1776, Arnold implored Gen. Horatio Gates to send 1,500 men to help him defend Isle la Motte against a British attack.

''Twenty men to a bateau will be sufficient,'' Arnold wrote.

The project has attracted a local following of marine history buffs, many of whom will be invited to the scheduled launching of the bateau on the Sunday before Labor Day. It also has caught the eye of some of the guests at the Basin Harbor Club, on the same grounds as the museum.

Edward Pendergast of Canton, Ohio, who has made a lifelong study of the French and Indian Wars, was perched on a timber, reading aloud from an account from Abercromby's campaign as the volunteers worked. Taking a break from his reading, he looked up, admiring the piece of history that was taking shape before his eyes.

''I think this project is just great,'' he said, ''just great.''
