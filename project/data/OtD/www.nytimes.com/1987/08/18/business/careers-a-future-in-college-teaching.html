Careers; A Future In College Teaching

By Elizabeth M. Fowler
Published: August 18, 1987

THERE is a graying of America that can lead to good jobs at the nation's colleges for both young people and middle-aged career changers. In the next decade or so, about 40 percent of American college professors will be retiring, according to Karen Hiiemae, vice president for research and graduate studies at Syracuse University.

''Not only does college teaching offer personal satisfaction but also it is a wide open field,'' Dr. Hiiemae commented last week. ''There will be a sellers' market. It offers a brighter future now than in years.''

In such fields as business and engineering, middle-aged managers seeking alternate careers might find good teaching jobs by adding doctoral degrees, she said. For example, up to 27 percent of the professorships in certain engineering fields remain vacant because so many have been lured to industry by higher salaries. These are gaps that need to be filled even if nationwide college enrollment does not expand further.

After World War II, Dr. Hiiemae said, there was a boom in college attendance, partly because of the G.I. Bill of Rights. To meet the demand, new colleges were formed, mainly junior colleges.

Several institutions, including some of the newcomers, have become huge. The University of Massachusetts, once a state agricultural college in some fields near Amherst, Mass., with a few hundred students, has blossomed into a university that has more than 26,000 students on that campus alone.

There has been a growing need to train college teaching assistants, especially the numerous foreigners who come to this country for graduate work. Many hope to remain as teachers after obtaining their Ph.D. degrees.

Usually graduate students start their teaching careers as teaching assistants while they complete doctoral studies. They correct and grade papers, teach freshman courses and advise students.

Yet there have been problems. Unlike high school and elementary school teachers who take required courses in teaching and do practice teaching, most teaching asssistants at universities have had no guidance in how to give lectures or handle obstreperous students. They learn by the sink-or-swim method, with casual advice from professors they help.

Robert McClure, a Syracuse professor of political science, described the problem: ''The great tragedy in American higher education is that all of us have traditionally been thrown into classrooms with no preparation or support.'' This can be painful for professors as well as undergraduates, he said.

Foreign graduate students have an especially tough problem when they lack enough knowledge of English to give understandable lectures, much less the everyday idioms needed to deal with undergraduates.

Dr. Hiiemae hopes to change this by putting Syracuse's 300 teaching assistants through a formal program, one of the few in the nation. The University of California at San Diego has one for foreign graduate students, she said, and the University of Minnesota has been considering a program.

The Syracuse program begins this week with help for foreign graduate students, about 100 strong, in grappling with the English language. Next week they will be joined by the rest of the graduate teaching assistants in taking brief courses in ''Effective Lecturing,'' ''Your First Class,'' ''Leading a Discussion,'' ''Allocation of Time'' and ''Ethics, Plagiarism and Cheating.''

Teaching the teachers will be 25 ''experienced graduate students'' who will be paid $500 each, according to Dr. Hiiemae.

At Syracuse, graduate students hired as teaching assistants receive free tuition and an average stipend of $6,000. It can range from $4,790 to $8,000, with those specializing in humanities courses at the low end.

How do they survive? In big cities they probably need additional funds, but Joseph Janes, immediate past president of the graduate students' organization at Syracuse and now a research assistant to Dr. Hiiemae, said Syracuse students, ''if single and frugal,'' could manage on $6,000. Rents near campus run from $150 to $400 a month, and food may cost from $100 to $200 a month. Costs vary for books, transportation, clothes, health insurance and other items.
