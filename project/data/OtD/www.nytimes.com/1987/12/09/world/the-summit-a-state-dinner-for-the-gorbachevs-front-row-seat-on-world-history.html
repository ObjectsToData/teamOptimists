THE SUMMIT; A State Dinner for the Gorbachevs: Front-Row Seat on World History

By BARBARA GAMAREKIAN, Special to the New York Times
Published: December 9, 1987

President and Mrs. Reagan invited a cross-section of America - 126 stars of business, science, sports, politics and the arts - to the White House state dinner this evening in honor of Mikhail S. Gorbachev and his wife, Raisa.

It was the most coveted invitation in the capital in many a year, with celebrities and the other guests breaking previous engagements and, in the case of Mstislav Rostropovich, the Soviet emigre cellist and conductor, flying in from Europe to attend.

At 10 minutes after 7 a Russian-made Zil limousine made its way up the circular driveway to the South Portico, where the Soviet leader and his wife were met by President and Mrs. Reagan. Although it was a formal occasion, Mr. Gorbachev elected to wear a dark-blue shadow-striped suit with a wine-and-dark-blue striped tie. His wife wore a two-piece black brocaded gown with a narrow skirt that touched her ankles. At her neck and ears were pearls.

President Reagan was in black tie and Mrs. Reagan wore a beaded black gown with diamond drop earrings. Posed for Photos

Making their way up the marble staircase, the two couples posed for photographers before the grand staircase banked with flowers. On either side stood the color guard.

They then proceeded, to the sounds of ''Ruffles and Flourishes'' and ''Hail to the Chief,'' through the grand foyer to the East Room where they received their guests.

Limousines began drawing up to the East Gate of the White House at 6:30. The guests were greeted by harp and flute music as they made their way through the East Wing corridors.

''The only state dinner I have been to that can even begin to compare with this was when Queen Elizabeth came in 1976,'' said Lynne Cheney, head of the National Endowment for the Humanities, who with her husband, Representative Dick Cheney, Republican of Wyoming, was among the guests. Looking to the Future

''But that was looking backward, looking to our heritage,'' she added. ''This evening involves two superpowers and it is a night that is looking very much to the future.''

With all of its pomp and ceremony, a state dinner is always a special occasion. But tonight, in a White House decked with flowers and lit by sparkling chandeliers and candlelight, there was a sense of having a front-row seat for a moment of history.

The Rev. Billy Graham had just come from an hour-and-a-half talk with Mr. Gorbachev at the Soviet Embassy along with some 50 other Americans.

''It was the highlight of my time so far,'' said Mr. Graham. ''Mr. Gorbachev summed up what is happening in the Soviet Union, what he believes about the future. He painted a beautiful picture of the world in which we are all brothers.'' DiMaggio Wants an Autograph

And then there were those guests who had their own agenda.

Joe DiMaggio brought along a baseball he was hoping Mr. Gorbachev would autograph.

Zubin Mehta, conductor of the New York Philharmonic, said he wanted to invite Mr. Gorbachev to a concert in Moscow in June.

At dinner, Raisa Gorbachev sat on the President's right. On her left was Ambassador Vernon A. Walters, United States representative to the United Nations. Others at the table were Dr. Edward Teller, Donald E. Petersen, chairman of the Ford Motor Company, and Shirley Crowe, wife of the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Adm. William J. Crowe Jr. At an adjacent table, Mr. Gorbachev sat to Mrs. Reagan's right and Richard N. Perle, the former Assistant Secretary of Defense, sat to her left. Also at that table were Nancy Mehta; Dr. James H, Billington, the Librarian of Congress; Robert Strauss, a Washington lawyer; Cynthia Helms, wife of Richard M. Helms, a former Director of Central Intelligence, and Representative Cheney. An Exchange of Toasts

The President and Mr. Gorbachev toasted the signing in the afternoon of the treaty banning intermediate-range missiles, and offered their hopes for peace and continuing work in the fields of arms reduction.

''We cannot afford to rest,'' said Mr. Reagan. ''There is a great deal more to be done and time and history are marching on.''

In his toast Mr. Gorbachev replied, ''A boundless world stretches far and wide beyond the walls of this house and, may I say, you and I are accountable to it.''

During dinner, guests noticed that Mr. Reagan rarely spoke to Mrs. Gorbachev. Mr. Gorbachev, on the other hand, spent much of dinner in animated conversation with Mr. Perle, Dr. Billington and Representative Cheney. The Soviet leader even joked with Mr. Perle, who is known for his outspoken anti-Soviet views. Mr Gorbachev said that he had seen a television re-enactment of the summit meeting in Reykjavik, Iceland, on Monday night and that the actor who played Mr. Perle had lost some weight. Just 5 Weeks to Arrange It

Never has a state occasion - usually six months in the planning - been pulled together in such haste, from the selection five weeks ago of the guest list and the menu to the last-minute scurrying about.

The state dinner began at 7, a half-hour earlier than most. The Gorbachevs had requested an earlier start so that they could retire early.

In another departure from tradition, instead of gathering for coffee and conversation in the Blue, Red and Green Rooms after dinner, guests went directly to the East Room for a program of music by the pianist Van Cliburn, to permit Mr. and Mrs. Gorbachev to depart by 10 P.M.

The Gorbachevs wound up staying until 10:50.

Perhaps the most emotional moment of the evening came at the end, following the entertainment by Mr. Cliburn, when he was greeted with hugs and kisses by the Gorbachevs.

''It is a pity you don't have an orchestra and you could play Tchaikovsky,'' Mrs. Gorbachev said. Mr. Cliburn said that with her help perhaps he could provide an extra encore, and he sat back down at the piano and played ''Moscow Nights.'' The Gorbachevs joined Mr. Cliburn in singing the words, and by the second verse the entire Russian delegation in the audience had joined in.
