AND BEAR IN MIND (Editors' choices of other recent books of particular interest) COVERT ACTION: The Limits of Intervention in the Postwar World, by Gregory F. Treverton. (Basic Books, $19.95.) A historian and former senatorial staff member argues, with sharp narrative illustrations, that the bigger and more secret an operation is, the greater the likelihood of fiasco. STORIES FROM THE WARM ZONE AND SYDNEY STORIES, by Jessica Anderson. (Viking, $15.95.) This two-part collection by an Australian writer gives us, in uncluttered prose, tough, self-aware characters and dry, subtle dissections of family dynamics. SQUANDERING EDEN: Africa at the Edge, by Mort Rosenblum and Doug Williamson. (Harcourt Brace Jovanovich, $19.95.) The authors, a reporter and a naturalist, offer a simple thesis: Africa won't make it. Their proposals for shortening the odds are overwhelmed by their own gloomy statistics and anecdotes. STALIN IN THE BRONX: And Other Stories, by Suzanne Ruta. (Grove, $16.95.) Eleven stories, set in several countries, imbued with a sense of the fatal connectedness of private and political life. BOTTOMS UP! A Pathologist's Essays on Medicine and the Humanities, by William B. Ober. (Southern Illinois University, $19.95.) Brilliant essays on such related topics as infertility in the Bible, the psychosexuality of Rimbaud and the joys of spanking. THE THIRTIETH YEAR, by Ingeborg Bachmann. (Holmes & Meier, $19.95.) Nothing that terrifies or makes life impossible is omitted from these relentless stories, now reissued, by an Austrian writer of genius who died, possibly by suicide, in 1973. CIGARETTES, by Harry Mathews. (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, $17.95.) Paired in a series of dangerous liaisons, the people in the author's fourth novel are shrewdly invented and powerfully characterized.
