Review/Television; Testimonies to Injustice In 'Moyers: Facing Evil'

By JOHN CORRY
Published: March 28, 1988

It's hard to explore ideas on television; the medium moves too fast. Moreover, if it slows down, we grow bored. No matter how thoughtful television tries to be, it doesn't work unless it commands our attention. ''Moyers: Facing Evil'' is thoughtful; it commands our attention as well. In the least likely places, it is full of exhilaration.

The 90-minute program, on Channel 13 at 9 o'clock tonight, is the first in what Bill Moyers says will be ''an occasional series'' on public television, examining what Eric Sevareid once called ''news of the mind.'' What are our poets, philosophers and scholars saying?

More to the point here, can they say it effectively on television? The framework for Mr. Moyers's program is a three-day symposium held last fall at the Institute for the Humanities in Salado, Tex. Distinguished people sit around and talk. Sometimes they gather at cocktail parties. This, of course, does not seem rich for television.

Nonetheless, it is rich; the symposium, called ''Understanding Evil,'' does not always yield news of the mind, but it does offer stunning performances. The writer Maya Angelou reads a poem; the Holocaust scholar Raul Hilberg explains the mechanics of mass murder; the philosopher Philip Paul Hallie explores his own soul. Their passion compels us to listen. What happens, I think, is that the television program - brilliantly produced by David Grubin - transforms the symposium. Any symposium (or seminar, panel discussion or conference), no matter how distinguished its participants, has dread patches of boredom; Mr. Grubin and Joan Konner, the executive producer, edit them out. Flute music and outdoor scenes punctuate the film.

Meanwhile, the topic: understanding evil. Civilized people have wrestled with that since time began. What can be said that has not been said before? Not much, perhaps. With the exception of Mr. Hilberg, the people at the symposium talk more about themselves than they do about evil. Ego and self-affirmation are on display. Almost always, however, this is invested with intelligence and high purpose.

Ms. Angelou speaks of her life; she was raised by a disabled uncle in Arkansas. She commemorates him with one of her poems. She also recites a poem by Paul Dunbar and a song she wrote for Roberta Flack. Ms. Angelou distills the history of poor blacks.

At the conclusion, she turns her back on the audience, and when she turns around again she is crying. It is an actor's performance, meant to grip the people at the symposium. It does grip them, and it will grip a television audience, too. Ms. Angelou is a powerful presence.

Ms. Angelou is followed by Mr. Hallie. ''To my mind,'' he says, ''ethics is baloney.'' This does not sound promising, but then Mr. Hallie shares his life. He has agonized about the burned bodies and severed heads he saw in World War II; as a soldier, he took part in the slaughter.

Therefore, the paradox of Mr. Hallie's life: the ''continuing explosion in the middle of my mind.'' He is, he says, ''a decent murderer, a conscientious killer.' He is also ''someone who would do it again.'' History demanded that he kill other people.

Nevertheless, optimism upholds most of the discussion. The participants insist that good can come out of evil. At one point, they wave their arms cheerfully as they are led through the Chinese dance-exercise tai chi. Everyone, apparently, feels good.

But then there is Mr. Hilberg -dry, precise and aware of the murder of millions. The Holocaust didn't just happen, he says; it was intricately planned and carefully managed. German lawyers, soldiers and physicians supported a monstrous apparatus. The Nazi killers themselves weren't ''banal''; they were ''specialists'' in destruction.

Ms. Angelou asks Mr. Hilberg about the relation between ''victim and victimizer.'' Does it somehow contain the seed for redemption?

Mr. Hilberg doesn't seem to think so. ''It is one thing to forgive one's tormentor, but how do I forgive the killing of millions of people?'' he says. ''I have not been elected to do so.'' Americans, Mr. Hilberg tells Mr. Moyers later, are always hopeful; hope is part of their tradition. Even the academics at the symposium, he declares, sound like preachers. Mr. Moyers looks thoughtful, and wonders why we don't do more to stop evil. The question may not answered, but the program is an absolute triumph.
