Scholar Is Named as Director Of Woodrow Wilson Center

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 16, 1988

Charles Blitzer, head of the National Humanities Center in North Carolina for the last five years, was named today as director of the Woodrow Wilson International Center for Scholars, one of the nation's top scholarly positions.

Mr. Blitzer served earlier as an official of the Smithsonian Institution, so the new post will be bringing him back to Washington.

Although it is housed by the Smithsonian, the Wilson Center is separate from it, having been chartered by Congress at the urging of Hubert H. Humphrey, then Vice President, as a memorial to the 28th President. It receives funds from both the Government and private sources.

Mr. Blitzer, 60 years old, will succeed James H. Billington, who last fall became the Librarian of Congress. Mr. Blitzer served as assistant secretary for history and art at the Smithsonian from 1968 to 1983.

The center provides 50 research fellowships each year to people from the United States and abroad with backgrounds in the humanities and social sciences, as well as people from nonacademic careers like journalism, business, diplomacy and government. Background in Several Fields

Mr. Blitzer's background includes several of those careers, including that of government. He taught at Yale University from 1950 to 1961 and while there was elected to three terms as an alderman in New Haven. He was born in New York City and received his B.A. from Williams College and his Ph.D., in government, from Harvard.

He is a specialist in the history of political theory and is the co-author of ''The Age of Power'' with C. J. Friedrich, ''The Age of Kings,'' ''The Commonwealth of England'' and ''An Immortal Commonwealth.''

In announcing the appointment, the chairman of the center, William J. Baroody, said: ''Charles Blitzer is uniquely qualified to lead the Wilson Center into its next phase of mature development. He is well-positioned to build on the excellent scholarly institution that Jim Billington developed in his 14 years of inspired leadership.''

Mr. Blitzer helped in the formation of the center he will now head, working with Daniel Patrick Moynihan, now the Senator from New York, and Vice President Humphrey to write the legislation creating it.

''The two of us planned the whole exercise,'' Senator Moynihan said today of the role he shared with Mr. Blitzer. ''Charles Blitzer is one of the most eminent scholars and administrators in the nation.''

Senator Moynihan said that the form of the center was agreed to over breakfast, adding: ''We proposed an institution of learning that the 22d century will regard as having influenced the 21st. That's still our plan.''
