New Role for CUNY: Big Brother to Public Schools

By SAMUEL WEISS
Published: March 18, 1988

At 10 public schools in New York City, many educational decisions - including setting entrance requirements, shaping the curriculum and choosing the principal -are worked out with a branch of the City University.

The schools are the centerpiece of a growing effort by the Board of Education and the 21 branches of the CUNY system to work together to try to solve intractable school issues, including a high dropout rate and inadequate teacher education.

Most of the schools - one intermediate school and nine high schools - are on or near a college campus, and they have come to be known as campus schools. The students are often able to take college courses and use the college laboratories, libraries and athletic fields.

Other joint arrangements are being established around the country. In New York and other cities, businesses, civic groups, unions and executives have begun pushing for school improvements, sometimes by ''adopting'' a school or offering resources or specific programs.

Usually, colleges and universities have formed partnerships with school districts because they realized that in some ways their success depends on the quality of the local schools.

Many educators said no other initiative appeared as broad or potentially significant as the network of campus schools in New York. Board of Education officials said they hoped to expand the number over the next five years and to involve private colleges.

''I don't know of anything as comprehensive as what's been happening in New York,'' Dr. Harold Hodgkinson, a senior fellow at the Institute for Educational Leadership in Washington, said. ''It's certainly something other cities ought to be looking at.''

''It's an outstanding development that holds a great promise for excellence,'' the president of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, Dr. Ernest L. Boyer, said. ''Schools can't do the job alone. They need the help of outside institutions.'' Despite the successes of the efforts, creating campus schools has not been easy, educators said, because of the delicate negotiations required.

''College people and school people represent two different cultures,'' said Dr. Joseph Shenker, president of La Guardia Community College, which runs two campus schools. ''They have different structures, different expectations, different vocabularies.''

There have been disputes over jurisdiction, space on campus and financing. Some campus schools have been labeled ''elitist.''

In the most celebrated controversy, Townsend Harris High School was established four years ago over the opposition of the other 25 high schools in Queens. They were concerned that Townsend Harris would lure away their best students.

The schools jointly run by CUNY and the Board of Education range in size from 130 to 3,500 students. Although the schools are technically under the control of the board, which provides the budget, staff and students, each school is formally associated with a CUNY college, which has a broad responsibility to help structure and run the program.

''Historically speaking, the university has supplied the Board of Education with a majority of its teachers, while the city's schools have provided the City University with most of its students,'' the Chancellor of the university, Dr. Joseph S. Murphy, said. ''These schools demonstrate just how integrated our two institutions can become.'' Tough Requirements And Enticing Electives

The two campus schools run in conjunction with Brooklyn College, Midwood High School and the Brooklyn College Academy, illustrate the connections.

Midwood was a school in decline when it began its association with the college in 1984. Officials hoped to transform it to attract better students from throughout the city. Working with college faculty members and administrators, the curriculum was overhauled.

The effort has proved to be a success, with programs in the sciences and humanities attracting more than 7,000 applicants a year for a school of 2,700 students. The medical-science institute requires its 1,000 students to take the equivalent of five years of science courses in four years of high school and four years of mathematics.

The humanities program, with 700 students, requires students to take five years of English in the traditional four years, three years of a modern foreign language and two years of Latin.

The students in both programs are encouraged to take two to four college courses as electives in their junior and senior years. The features include an accelerated program in which students can earn their diplomas and bachelor's degrees in seven years, as well as a research-methods program that allows students to work on a project with a professor-mentor.

The chairman of the physical-sciences department at Midwood, David Kiefer, said the collaborative efforts provided students the chance to work with sophisticated equipment, earn college credit and obtain ''a more realistic view of what college life is really like.''

Alice Wei is one of three juniors working in the visual-research laboratory at Brooklyn College three afternoons a week on a color-perception experiment under the supervision of the head of the laboratory, Dr. Israel Abramov.
