LONG ISLAND JOURNAL


Published: January 24, 1988

'DANCIN' FEET' There is nothing static about either the name or the contents of the North Shore Dance Theater's latest production. It is called ''Dancin' Feet'' and that is what members of the energetic young, Huntington-based jazz dance company, under the direction of Sandi Bloomberg, a former Rockette, and Toni Wortman employ today at the Harborfields Public Library. Their dancing feet are the centerpiece of a program that ranges from American jazz to Afro-Caribbean rhythms, with a pause on Broadway for recognizable show-stoppers.

Naturally, given Ms. Bloomberg's background - another dancer-choreographer, Maryann Fiordalisi, is also an ex-Rockette - at some point in the program, the 15 dancers will face off in a kick-line rendition of ''All That Jazz.'' The free concert, which also invites participation from young audience members, is at 2:30 P.M. at the library, 31 Broadway in Greenlawn. NEW ORGANISTS

Although church organ music has strong ties to the old, there's plenty of new around these days; particularly when it comes to the people who perform that music. At least, that is what the concert planners at Christ Church in Oyster Bay think. And to prove it they have put together a four-part series, ''Keyboard Artists: The New Generation,'' to showcase the also-new Opus 30 organ recently created for Christ Church by a Canadian master builder, Hellmuth Wolff.

The series opens today with David Hurd, an American organist who won first prize at the 1977 International Congress of Organists and is now professor of church music and organist at the General Theological Seminary in New York as well as director of music at All Saints Episcopal Church in Manhattan.

In addition to traditional works by Buxtehude and J. S. Bach, Mr. Hurd will perform more contemporary pieces by Antoine Grunenwald and Guy Alain; his own ''Partita on the Hymn Tune, 'Detroit,' '' and improvise a composition on a theme suggested at the recital.

Future performers are Peggy Haas Howell, another American, on March 6; John Scott Whiteley, a British organist, April 10, and Ketil Haugsand, a Norwegian harpsichordist, May 22. The concert is at 4 P.M. at the church, 61 East Main Street. For ticket information call 922-6377. STILL CRYING FREEDOM

Donald Woods, one of the subjects of the film ''Cry Freedom,'' will be at the State University of New York at Stony Brook Tuesday in the Distinguished Lecture Series.

Mr. Woods, a writer and broadcaster, will talk about ''Apartheid and the Tragedy of South Africa.''

The speech is at 8 P.M. at the Fine Arts Center on the campus. There is no admission charge. MURDER, SHE SAYS

Having carved a career for herself out of a fascination with mayhem, E. J. Wagner, a crime historian, now wants to share her wealth of knowledge with as many of the curious as possible. Hence, ''Studies in Murder,'' a course at the Museum of Long Island Natural Sciences that will feature detailed analyses of the scientific evidence used to solve famous homicides.

Using experts from the office of the Suffolk County Medical Examiner and other guest lecturers, Ms. Wagner will present the historical and sociological backgrounds of several cases including Jack the Ripper, Lizzie Borden, Dr. Crippen, and the Lindbergh kidnapping, and then discuss the forensic matters relating to them.

The six-part course, at 7:30 P.M. Tuesdays - a seventh meeting will be a crime laboratory session -starts this week at the Museum on the campus of the State University at Stony Brook. To register call 632-8230. MUSICAL REVUE

Back from the dying (with some transfusions of emergency financial aid), Long Island Stage, on Tuesday, goes ''Back on the Town'' with ''A Cabaret for the Theater.''

Starring John Wallowitch, a Juilliard-trained pianist, composer and humorist, and Bertram Ross, a modern dancer-actor who was Martha Graham's partner and co-director for 25 years, the revue pays nostalgic tribute to the work of George and Ira Gershwin, Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Hart, Irving Berlin and others.

John Wilson wrote in The New York Times that the work was a ''mixture of the unexpected, the sentimental, the ridiculous, and even, depending on a listener's turn of mind, the sublime.''

The musical runs through Feb. 14 at the Hays Theater on the Rockville Centre campus of Molloy College and for ticket information call 546-4600. BROADHOLLOW EXPANDS

Meanwhile, convinced that there is ''a market out there'' for what they call ''affordable, accessible theater - musicals and comedies that give people what they want at prices they can pay,'' Patricia and Jerry Zaback open their third operation Friday; adding the Broadhollow Players at BayWay Arts in East Islip to the Broadhollow Players at the Studio Theater in Lindenhurst, and the Broadhollow Players at the Broadhollow Theater in Farmingdale, the flagship of what is now becoming a suburban theater empire.

The 250-seat playhouse, fashioned out of a recycled school and briefly the home base for the Other Vic Theater Company, begins its Broadhollow life with the musical ''Jerry's Girls,'' which just completed a run in Farmingdale.

However, future installments in the initial five-production, bargain-priced subscription series (with subscriptions starting at $20 and going up to $35) be freshly mounted versions of such box office favorites as ''Cheaters,'' ''The Second Time Around'' and ''A Funny Things Happened on the Way to the Forum.''

The BayWay is at 265 East Main Street. For ticket information call 845-PLAY. 'FIGARO' GETS MARRIED

Since into every music student's life ideally at least a little Mozart should fall, why not a big Mozart opera? Why not indeed, says Edgar Dittemore, chairman of Hofstra University's music department.

So, complete with orchestra, costumes and sets, the Hofstra University Opera Theater mounts a full production of ''The Marriage of Figaro'' Friday through next Sunday. The three-performance student effort, augmented by some now-professional alumni ringers, is at the John Cranford Adams Playhouse on the Hempstead campus. For ticket information call 5660-6644. GRAHAM ENSEMBLE

With her original movement techniques and her monumental body of work, Martha Graham has made one of the most significant contributions to the art of 20th-century dance. And to spread her artistry as far as possible - and provide young Graham dancers with a place to perform while they wait their turn to join the main company - in 1982 the Martha Graham Ensemble was formed under the direction of Yuriko, a former Graham principal dancer.

Dancing a trio of such standards as ''El Pentitente,'' ''Diversion of Angels'' and ''Acts of Lights,'' the 10-member troupe comes to Riverhead Saturday. The performance, under the auspices of the East End Arts and Humanities Council, is at 8 P.M. at the local high school on Harrison Avenue. For ticket information call 727-0900. 1/290BARBARA DELATINER
