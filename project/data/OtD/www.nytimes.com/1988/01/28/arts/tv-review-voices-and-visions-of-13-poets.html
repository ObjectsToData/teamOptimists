TV Review; 'Voices and Visions' Of 13 Poets

By JOHN J. O'CONNOR
Published: January 28, 1988

THE theory goes that television, in thrall to the visual image, is a spoiler of words.

Mesmerized by their television screens, people don't read anymore. Language is increasingly debased by poorly educated television personalities and unscrupulous advertisers. And so on, unto endless and often simple-minded tirades. Now, providing one kind of pointed response, public television is presenting an ambitious, indeed a unique, series of hourlong essays on that most incisive and demanding of word forms - poetry. ''Voices and Visions'' looks at the lives and works of 13 American poets. The series is simultaneously thoughtful and enthusiastic. It could send many of us back to reading poetry, which is in itself something of a lost art form in these modern times. The programs can be seen Tuesdays at 10 on Channel 21, Thursdays at 9 on Channel 13 and Sunday afternoons at 2 on Channel 31.

The executive producer for The New York Center for Visual History is Lawrence Pitkethly, born in Northern Ireland and formerly a journalist for the BBC. The center, a nonprofit film production and archive company, was founded in 1978 by Mr. Pitkethly and Anson Rabinbach, a historian. With Jill Janows and Robert Chapman as senior producers, ''Voices and Visions'' offers profiles of poets ranging from Walt Whitman to Marianne Moore, William Carlos Williams to Elizabeth Bishop. Some choices, most notably Ezra Pound and Sylvia Plath, may trigger reservations, but the overall collection is sound.

With major financing provided by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Annenberg/CPB Project, the series is perched, rather valiantly, on a substructure of educational tools and materials. There is a related college-credit ''telecourse,'' being offered in the New York area by the City University of New York. For purchase, there are video cassettes and two books: the anthology ''Modern American Poets: Their Voices and Visions,'' and the essay collection ''Voices and Visions: The Poet in America,'' edited by Helen Vendler. And, available free at public libraries, there is a viewer's guide written by Joseph Parisi, editor of Poetry magazine.

All of which leaves, of course, the programs themselves. Degrees of unevenness are perhaps inevitable, but the first two installments herald a series of unusual substance and distinction. The premiere, on Channel 13 tonight, looks at Robert Frost. Produced by Mr. Chapman and written by Margot Feldman, the profiles use fascinating archival material, some with interviews with Frost in the 15 years before his death in 1963, and illuminating commentaries from writers like Richard Poirier and William Pritchard and other poets like Richard Wilbur and Seamus Heaney.

Biography is deftly blended with reading from the poetry, several delivered by Frost himself. Brief dramatizations are employed, blessedly with a light hand, to illustrate some of the poems. And at times the words appear on screen, their formations and patterns creating a visual context for the poetry. The techniques work nicely. Meanwhile, Frost himself keeps coming into sharper focus.

Born in 1874, he wasn't even published until he was nearly 40. And that happened in England, where he had moved temporarily with his family. Once on his way, however, he quickly became the superstar of American poetry, delighted to appear in public, later on television, and play the genially crusty Yankee farmer. His darker side - Lionel Trilling called him a poet of terror - was generally well hidden but it gave his work the added dimension that made Frost more than merely a regional poet. And his common subject matter, we are reminded, was immersed in delicate inflections of speech.

Ezra Pound, subject of next week's essay, said he considered Frost ''an untutored child.'' Perhaps. But Alfred Kazin, the critic, has this to say about Pound: ''I think he was a damn fool - a literary peacock.'' That opinion stems from the record of Pound's activities in Italy during World War II, activities that would prompt the American Government to accuse him of treason and place him in a mental institution. Directed, written and produced by Mr. Pitkethly, ''Ezra Pound/American Odyssey'' manages to maintain a reasonable balance between Pound the fool and Pound the artist, whose influence on modern poetry and literature may stem less from his work than from his being a key influence in the lives of other writers.

''Voices and Visions'' is challenging and inspiring television. And, arriving at a point when the very purpose of public television is being debated once again (is the future to be reruns of ''Leave It to Beaver''?), the timing of the series couldn't be better.
