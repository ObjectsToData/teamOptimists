James Killian, 83, Science Adviser, Dies

By ERIC PACE
Published: January 31, 1988

James R. Killian Jr., the first Presidential assistant for science and technology, an architect of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, and a president of Massachusetts Institute of Technology, died Friday at his home in Cambridge, Mass. He was 83 years old.

A science administrator, not a scientist, Dr. Killian had been M.I.T.'s president for 10 years by 1957, when the Soviet Union launched Sputnik I, its first satellite, beating the United States into space. In worried response, President Eisenhower picked him as the first full-time Presidential science adviser, to serve as chief architect of the country's scientific programs.

As chairman of the President's Science Advisory Committee as well as Presidential assistant from 1957 to 1959, Dr. Killian, as the columnist Arthur Krock wrote in The New York Times, ''repaired a dangerous national deficiency by bringing science and technology into the inner circles of the government.'' 'A Brisk, Incisive Man'

Later, as chairman of the Carnegie Commission on Educational Television from 1965 to 1967 and chairman of the Corporation for Public Broadcasting in 1973 and 1974, Dr. Killian came to be considered the father of public broadcasting.

Dr. Killian's main responsibilities in the Eisenhower Administration were, as General Eisenhower put it at the time, ''helping me follow through on the program of scientific improvement of our defenses'' as well as expediting the United States' space program.

Once installed in Washington, Dr. Killian, a genial South Carolinian, soon showed himself to be, as Theodore H. White put it, ''a brisk, incisive man with the manner and dispatch of a brilliant surgeon.''

Moving briskly but coolly, Dr. Killian began laying the groundwork for the creation of NASA, which became what he defined as ''a civilian-directed and civilian-oriented space science and exploration program,'' providing ''research support for military aeronautics and space programs.'' It began operating in late 1958 and put the first American into space in 1961 and the first men on the Moon in 1969. In the Hurricane's Eye

In the months between the launching of Sputnik I and the creation of NASA, competition among the American armed forces and other government agencies for roles in the stepped-up space race became strident, as Dr. Killian noted tartly in his 1977 volume of memoirs, ''Sputnik, Scientists and Eisenhower,'' and ''under these conditions, the President's Science Advisory Committee found itself in the eye of a political hurricane.''

Dr. Killian had formed that influential body by taking over the Office of Defense Mobilization's Science Advisory Committee, putting it directly under the President, and beefing it up.

It was largely through the committee that Dr. Killian, smiling but tough-minded, moved in helping to shape what became NASA. Admirers said he was aided in that endeavor, and throughout his varied career, by a gift for persuading people with differing views to reach a consensus. Space Program Outlined

Dr. Killian and others on the committee - which had two dozen members, largely distinguished scientists - decided, as he put it in his book, ''that we should propose the outlines of our space program before we proposed an agency to manage it.''

He then named a four-member subcommmittee, headed by Edward Purcell, the Harvard physicist and Nobel laureate, to delineate a space program. The subcommittee's other members were Gen. James H. Doolittle, then chairman of the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics; Edwin Land, the inventor who founded the Polaroid Corporation, and Herbert F. York, a physicist at the University of California at Berkeley.

Stressing, in Dr. Killian's phrase, ''the importance of civilian exploration of space for scientific purposes,'' the subcommittee, as he wrote in his memoirs, gave General Eisenhower, ''without waffling or alternatives, a recommendation for one organization that in the wrap-up was the organization, with some appropriate modifications, that won approval from him and the Congress and that has managed our space program brilliantly'' - NASA. A Civilian Agency

On the ticklish question of how to set up the space organization, Dr. Killian wrote, a consensus emerged within his committee, at the Bureau of the Budget, and elsewhere in General Eisenhower's entourage, that the agency should be built around the National Advisory Council for Aeronautics, a civilian body established in 1915.

Dr. Killian talked the idea over with General Eisenhower and wrote that the President, ''as he explored possibilities with me, clearly leaned toward a civilian rather than a military agency.'' Dr. Killian then formally presented the recommendation to General Eisenhower, who approved it and had the necessary draft legislation prepared. In due course, after much debate on Capitol Hill, NASA was established by the National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958.

In July 1959, Dr. Killian resigned as science adviser, convinced, as he wrote later, ''that I had pretty well done the job that I, as a science administrator, had agreed to undertake.'' He also felt that the job should now go to a ''full-fledged scientist.''
