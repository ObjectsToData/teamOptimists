Call for a National Arts Commission

By JEREMY GERARD
Published: April 14, 1988

Declaring this to be ''a time of crisis in the state of the arts,'' the historian Arthur Schlesinger Jr. called last night for the next President to establish a national commission on the arts, whose mandate would be ''to examine the range of governmental activity affecting the arts, to propose a comprehensive arts agenda and to set goals for national arts policy in the 1990's.''

Dr. Schlesinger's speech at the National Academy of Sciences in Washington closed a day in which representatives of some 20 national arts organizations met with members of Congress to discuss major issues confronting artists today. His address was the first Nancy Hanks Lecture on the Arts and Public Policy. A copy of the lecture was received by The New York Times earlier this week.

''This seems to me to come at a propitious time for a re-evaluation of national arts policy,'' Dr. Schlesinger said in a recent interview at his office in the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, where he has been Albert Schweitzer Professor of the Humanities since 1966. ''We've had nearly a quarter of a century of experience with public support of the arts. I think an unnoticed revolution has taken place in these years, in which the endowments have become solidly established as national policy. The reason this has been so is that it represents a national consensus.'' The Purpose of the Lecture

Miss Hanks, who died in 1983, was the second head of the arts endowment, succeeding Roger L. Stevens in 1969 and remaining the chairwoman until 1977. Before taking over the endowment, she had run the American Council for the Arts, a national arts advocacy organization that sponsored yesterday's activities. The lecture in her honor, underwritten by the Gannett Foundation, was created to focus attention on public policy issues confronting artists and arts and educational institutions.

Early arts legislation ''had a predominantly Democratic flavor,'' Dr. Schlesinger said during the interview. ''As late as 1968, when they were voting on the reauthorization of the National Endowment for the Arts, the Republicans in the House voted against it two to one. Among the Republicans voting against it were such leaders of the Republican party in the House of Representatives as Gerald Ford, Robert Dole and George Bush. So that when the Republicans won the election that year, there was great concern about the future of the endowments. Nancy Hanks's great accomplishment was that after eight years, it was not only an executive-legislative partnership, it was also a bipartisan.''

In her first year, he added, she doubled the endowment's $12 million budget; by the time of her resignation, it had risen to $114 million. 'Financial Stringency'

The endowments even survived the attempt, in the early years of the Reagan Administration, to legislate them out of existence, Dr. Schlesinger said. But if they have won some measure of security in the Federal budget, he continued, the arts have not themselves been accorded the importance they merit. ''We're entering a time of financial stringency,'' he said, leaning back in a chair, his signature bow tie unknotted, a cigar in constant movement from hand to mouth to hand again. Surveying an office whose every square inch was taken up with piles of books, he wondered: ''What will happen to the public arts programs in an era where everyone is trying to cut the budget? The priority of the arts has been lowered somewhat.''

The members of Congress who spoke yesterday to the various arts groups shared that view, according to Bruce Cohen, a spokesman for the American Council for the Arts who attended the meetings. Senator Claiborne Pell, a Rhode Island Democrat who heads the Subcommittee on Education, Arts and Humanities, encouraged them to set up political action committees to ''support your friends on the Hill,'' Mr. Cohen said, while Representative Mary Rose Oakar, a Republican from Ohio who heads the Subcommittee on Libraries and Memorials, said that she would introduce legislation to create a Cabinet-level Secretary of Arts and Humanities.

The greatest task, Dr. Schlesinger argued in the Hanks lecture, lies in reversing the notion that the arts are a dispensable luxury. ''The United States will be measured in the eyes of posterity not by its economic power nor by its military might,'' he said, ''not by the territories it has annexed nor by the battles it has won, but by its character and achievement as a civilization.''
