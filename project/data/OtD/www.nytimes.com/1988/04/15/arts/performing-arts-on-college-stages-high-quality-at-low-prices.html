Performing Arts On College Stages: High Quality At Low Prices

By ANDREW L. YARROW
Published: April 15, 1988

BEYOND Lincoln Center and Broadway, City Center and Carnegie Hall exists a vast, yet little-known performing-arts circuit in New York where top musicians, dancers and actors can be seen almost every day at a fraction of mid-Manhattan prices.

Major orchestras and dance companies, up-and-coming recitalists, classic and new plays, and jazz and pop stars all can be found in this seemingly too-good-to-be-true network of theaters and concert halls on New York's dozens of college campuses. Recent on-campus performers included Pinchas Zukerman, the Tokyo String Quartet, Tito Puente and the Washington Ballet. And this weekend's lineup ranges from the Dance Theater of Harlem and the pop duo Ashford and Simpson, to the Brooklyn Philharmonic and a Samuel Beckett festival.

Indeed, from the giant performing-arts centers on several City University campuses to the intimate recital halls and small, spartan theaters of the Juilliard School or New York University, almost every college is a stage - at least between September and June. And virtually every performance - they number in the hundreds each month - is open to the public.

Contrary to many stereotypes, performing arts in academia go far beyond undergraduate plays and student recitals. Yet in sheer energy, enthusiasm and innovation, even those small-scale performances often make professional productions look positively humdrum. (Of course, these 21-year-olds may be the stars of tomorrow.) Many works that are daringly experimental, historically obscure or otherwise out of the professional-repertory mainstream frequently can be seen or heard only in college settings. And the informality and price - often free - of college performances are added attractions.

Sitting on folding chairs in a small theater or a functional auditorium may be part of the fun of attending some college productions, but such spaces are increasingly atypical of campus performing-arts venues. Modern, well-equipped theaters, like the Borough of Manhattan Community College Triplex or the Juilliard Theater, rival almost any commercial space for their technical amenities and comfort.

Like a number of the newer college theaters, according to William Spencer Reilly, the Triplex's executive director, ''the Triplex is an ideal place to develop new plays, because it's less expensive, yet a much better facility than most Off Broadway theaters.'' A handful of complexes on

City University campuses also serve as regional performing-arts centers where residents of the outer boroughs and suburbs can attend concerts and plays without trekking into Manhattan. These include the Brooklyn Center for the Performing Arts at Brooklyn College, a three-theater complex that was dedicated by Leontyne Price in 1953 and now is the setting for more than 300 performances a year. Colden Center for the Performing Arts at Queens College could rightfully be called the Lincoln Center of Long Island, since leading artists of every description perform in its concert hall and theater. Audiences from the Bronx and southern Westchester County find major performers in the four theaters of Lehman Center for the Performing Arts at Herbert Lehman College. And Aaron Davis Hall at City College provides a similar service for upper Manhattan residents, as does the College of Staten Island for inhabitants of New York's fastest-growing borough. According to Aaron M. Cohen, artistic director of Aaron Davis Hall, these centers were built for college, neighborhood and broader community audiences.

Because so many performers are drawn to New York, even many smaller schools are able to bring leading entertainers to their stages. Hostos Community College, for example, ''provides top-notch activities for residents of the South Bronx, for people who wouldn't, or couldn't afford, to go to Manhattan,'' said Wally Edgecombe, the college's director of culture and arts programming.

''Our theaters are geared to people who want to hear or see something interesting but don't want to go into the city,'' said Toby Hempel of Kingsborough Community College in the Manhattan Beach section of Brooklyn.

But since the caliber of performances is often so high - and the cost so low compared with mid-Manhattan prices - even Manhattanites might want to make the reverse cultural commute to many of these schools. From Beckett to Dance Brazil

As is true of almost every weekend during the academic year, this weekend's schedule gives a taste of the remarkable variety and extent of performing arts at New York colleges.

Theater lovers may choose between Beckett's ''Company'' at Lehman, Schnitzler's ''Ronde'' at N.Y.U., Turgenev's ''Month in the Country'' and Sam Shepard's ''Suicide in B Flat'' at Juilliard, Aristophanes' ''Clouds'' at Barnard College, and two Euripides plays - ''Electra'' at Pace University and ''Alcestis'' at Hunter College.

Musical offerings include the Brooklyn Philharmonic at Cooper Union, the Boys Choir of Harlem at Lehman, the Queens Symphony at Queens College and recitals galore at Juilliard, the Mannes College of Music and the Manhattan School of Music. Dance enthusiasts will find the Dance Theater of Harlem at City College, the Jazz Tap Ensemble with Harold Nicholas at Brooklyn College and Dance Brazil at the Borough of Manhattan Community College. And other events range from Ashford and Simpson at Queens College and Columbia University's Varsity Show (''The Bonfire of the Humanities'') to a concert at the Jewish Theological Seminary marking Israel's 40th anniversary.
