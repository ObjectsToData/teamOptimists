Alternative Movie Houses and Screening Rooms


Published: May 20, 1988

Here are some of the many places where movies can be seen in New York City other than in movie theaters. THE AMERICAN MUSEUM OF THE MOVING IMAGE, 34-12 36th Street, Astoria, Queens (718-784-4520). Screenings have been suspended until its new quarters open in September. When the museum's two small, new theaters open, it will present a film series called ''Glorious Technicolor'' and a video series, ''The Media and the Vietnam War.'' THE AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street (769-5650). Showings of ''Grand Canyon -The Hidden Secrets'' and ''Chronos'' in its large-screen Naturemax Theater, daily at 10:30 and 11:30 A.M., 12:30, 1:30, 2:30, 3:30 and 4:30 P.M. Tickets: $3.50; $2.75 for the elderly and $1.75 for children. Double features Fridays and Saturdays at 6 and 7:30 P.M. Tickets are $5.50; $4.50 for the elderly and $3 for children. ANTHOLOGY FILM ARCHIVES, (505-5181). Intends to open a new two-screen theater at 32-34 Second Avenue (near Second Street) in October for daily screenings of avant-garde and classic films. THE ASIA SOCIETY, 725 Park Avenue, at 70th Street (517-2742). Presents thematic series such as a recent one on Hollywood in Asia, as well as ones devoted to films of Asian countries. (It has even shown Sri Lankan and Vietnamese films.) Afternoon and evening screenings are held once or twice a week in the society's auditorium. The next major series will be in the fall, when Asian-made films from the Hong Kong Film Festival will be screened. THE BROOKLYN MUSEUM, 200 Eastern Parkway (718-638-5000, ext. 232). Presents about five annual feature and documentary film series in its third-floor lecture hall, as well as monthly Saturday morning children's films from November to May. The two current series are Films in the Humanities, with screenings Saturdays at 2 P.M. through June 4, and Architecture on Film, with screenings Sundays at 2 P.M. through May 29. Tomorrow, ''Do Not Enter: The Visa War Against Ideas,'' a 1986 documentary about artists denied visas to enter the United States, will be shown. ''Arata Isozaki,'' a film about the Japanese architect, will be shown Sunday. Films in both series are free with museum admission, which is $3; $1.50 for students; $1 for the elderly; free for members and children. THE BROOKLYN PUBLIC LIBRARY (718-780-7700). Presents features and documentaries for adults and children during afternoons, evenings and weekends in most of its 58 branches. ''With These Hands,'' a profile of eight folk artists, will be shown Sunday at 2:30 P.M. at the Greenpoint branch, Norman Avenue at Leonard Street (718-383-6692), and coming attractions this week include ''The Golden 20's'' at the Borough Park branch, 43d Street near 13th Avenue (Tuesday, 2 P.M.; 718-435-3375), and ''Lawrence of Arabia'' at the Windsor Terrace branch, East Fifth Street at Fort Hamilton Parkway (Thursday at 1:15 P.M.; 718-853-7265). Monthly calendars are available at each branch. CASA DE ESPANA, 314 East 39th Street (689-4232). Free screenings of Spanish films, with subtitles, from October through April on Saturdays and Tuesdays. THE CATHEDRAL OF ST. JOHN THE DIVINE, 1047 Amsterdam Avenue, at 112th Street (662-2133). Silent films will be shown during the summer, with accompaniment by Lee Erwin, an organist. The Lon Chaney version of ''Phantom of the Opera'' is scheduled June 8 through 10 at 8 P.M. Tickets From July 14 to Aug. 25, more organ-accompanied silent classics - including D. W. Griffith's ''Broken Blossoms'' and ''Intolerance,'' Fritz Lang's ''Metropolis'' and ''Thief of Bagdad'' with Douglas Fairbanks Sr. - will be shown every Thursday night at 8. Tickets are $5. CHARAS TEATRO LA TERRAZZA, 360 East 10th Street (982-0627). An organization called Films Charas has screened independent, Spanish-language, third-world and political films here since 1981. While raising money to build a 125-seat theater, Charas continues to show films Tuesday nights at 8. Tickets are $3; $1.50 for members. This Tuesday's program is ''An Evening of Latino Film,'' and on May 31, Michael Gray's ''Murder of Fred Hampton'' will be shown. COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, Broadway and 116th Street (280-3574). Frequent series at various locations on campus, including international films at Casa Italiana, Maison Francaise and Deutsches Haus. Columbia's summer-session series of movie classics runs May 31 through Aug. 11, with free outdoor screenings Thursday nights at 9 on Lewisohn Lawn (near the northwest corner of campus), and Tuesday evening shows in Earl Hall at 7 and 9:30. (Tickets are $3; free for Columbia students.) THE COLLECTIVE FOR LIVING CINEMA, 41 White Street (925-2111). Shows a mixture of rarely screened foreign movies, old Hollywood films, and new works by avant-garde film makers. The 120-seat theater has daily screenings, with tickets generally $5 for nonmembers. Children's programs, Saturdays at 11 A.M., are $1; $2 for adults. This weekend: Kembra Pfahler will present two new films tonight at 8; and two programs of third-world works by British film makers associated with the group Sankofa and the Black Audio Film Collective are scheduled tomorrow and Sunday at 8 P.M. THE FRENCH INSTITUTE/ALLIANCE FRANCAISE, 22 East 60th Street (355-6100). Shows French films with English subtitles on Tuesdays and Wednesdays, generally at 12:30, 3:15, 6 and 8:45 P.M., in its Tinker Auditorium. Next week's feature is Andre Techine's ''Rendez-vous.'' This Cine Club series, which runs every month except September, moves into the institute's new 400-seat Florence Gould Hall, at 55 East 59th Street, on June 8, after which screenings will be only on Wednesdays. The institute's summer series runs from June 8 through Aug. 31, opening with Marcel Carne's ''Portes de la Nuit'' (1946), and featuring a Marcel Pagnol retrospective in July and August. Tickets are $3.50; $2.50 for students and the elderly, and free to members. THE GOETHE HOUSE NEW YORK, 1014 Fifth Avenue, near 82d Street (744-8310). Shows German films in connection with exhibitions or lectures. Screenings are free, with tickets distributed an hour in advance. Several short films relating to the exhibition, ''Resistance, 1933-45,'' about German opposition to Nazism, will be shown tomorrow at 1 P.M. THE JAPAN SOCIETY, 333 East 47th Street (752-0824 or 752-3015). Presents about five yearly series devoted to particular themes or individual Japanese directors. Japanese Comedy, the current series, continues through June 17 with Friday screenings at 6:30 and 8:30 P.M. ''Tora-san Goes North,'' the 38th work in Yoji Yamada's series of comedies about a wandering street vendor named Tora-san, is scheduled tonight. Tickets are $6; $4.50 for members. THE JEWISH MUSEUM, 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92d Street (860-1863). Presents films in conjunction with exhibitions, as well as thematic series having to do with the Jewish experience. Coming films to be shown in connection with ''A Century of Ambivalence: The Jews of Russia and the Soviet Union'' are: ''The Partisans of Vilna'' on May 31 at 7 P.M.; ''The Fall of the Romanov Dynasty'' on June 5 at 4 P.M.; ''Krasnodar: The Trial of 1943'' on June 7 at 7 P.M., and ''The Fixer'' on June 12 at 4 P.M. Tickets are $6; $4 for students and the elderly. A festival of 20 recent American films and videos called ''A Contemporary Lens on Jewish Experience'' will be presented on seven Tuesdays and Wednesdays at 7 P.M. between June 1 and June 29. Each program will include discussions. Tickets are $5. THE KITCHEN, 512 West 19th Street (255-5793). Presents free video shows in its third-floor viewing room, Tuesdays through Saturdays from 1 to 6 P.M. Shows change each month, and visitors can stay as long as they wish. This month, ''Video in the Academy: Dead or Alive?'' includes 11 works by students and instructors at American universities. In June, political and social documentaries will be shown. THE METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART (570-3949 or 535-7710). Presents almost daily films and videotapes about particular artists or artworks in its Uris Center Auditorium or Grace Rainey Rogers Auditorium, as well as frequent series of feature films. Today and Sunday, for example, films about Rubens, Rembrandt and Vermeer will be screened at noon and 4 P.M. During July, the museum will show films of Bette Davis on Tuesday nights; ones by Billy Wilder on Wednesday nights, and about baseball on Thursdays. And in August, a series of features made in China will be screened. Art films and screenings in the China series are free with museum admission, which is $5; $2.50 for students and the elderly. (Tickets for films in the three July series are $5.) THE MILLENNIUM FILM WORKSHOP, 66 East Fourth Street (673-0090). This 22-year-old nonprofit media-arts center presents about 75 programs of primarily experimental, 16-millimeter films on weekends from October to mid-June, and offers classes in film making and film theory. Tickets are generally $4. Several Australian films - Corinne Cantrill's ''In This Life's Body'' (1984) and five shorts by Paul Winkler - will be shown today and tomorrow at 8 P.M. THE MUSEUM OF MODERN ART, 11 West 53d Street (708-9490). Presents one to four screenings of feature films every day the museum is open in its two theaters. The museum's many film series range from one devoted to early Frank Capra films, running through May 31, to a Russian series called Stories From Siberia in June and one on films of the performing arts, June 23 to July 10. Films are free with museum admission, which is $5 ($3.50 for students and $2 for the elderly), and tickets for these popular screenings are distributed at the lobby information desk after 11 A.M. for afternoon shows and after 1 P.M. for evening shows. This weekend's schedule of Capra films: ''Forbidden'' (1932), today at 1:30 P.M.; ''Platinum Blonde'' (1931), today at 4 P.M.; ''It Happened One Night'' (1934), today at 6:30 P.M.; ''Lady for a Day'' (1933), tomorrow at 12:30 P.M.; ''Dirigible'' (1931), tomorrow at 3 P.M.; ''American Madness'' (1932), tomorrow at 5:30 P.M.; ''That Certain Thing'' (1928), Sunday at 2:30 P.M., and ''So This Is Love'' (1928), Sunday at 5:30 P.M. Information: 708-9490. THE NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY. Most of the 81 branches in Manhattan, the Bronx and Staten Island show free films on weekdays and weekends. Offerings range from Hollywood features to documentaries to special children's programs. One of the most active film programs is at the Donnell Library, 20 West 53d Street (621-0618), where three current series in its 262-seat auditorium focus on cinematic views of Paris, behind the scenes in Hollywood and short films. This weekend, the 96th Street branch, at 112 East 96th Street (289-0908), will show ''Dodes Kaden'' tomorrow at 1:30 P.M., and the St. Agnes branch, 444 Amsterdam Avenue near 81st Street (877-4380), will show ''The Adventures of Robin Hood'' tomorrow at 2 P.M. The many films scheduled at various branches during the rest of May range from ''The 39 Steps'' and ''The Rules of the Game'' to ''Annie Hall'' and ''An American in Paris.'' Monthly systemwide calendars are available at every branch. NEW YORK UNIVERSITY. A two-day series of the best films made by students in the Tisch School of the Arts, Year-End Showcase, will be held tonight and tomorrow from 6 to 11:30 in Loeb Student Center, 566 La Guardia Place. Tickets are $5. Information: 998-1795. Throughout the school year, Maison Francaise, 16 Washington Mews, presents French films; this year's last screening is tonight at 7, and features Jean-Pierre Mocky's ''Cite de l'Indicible Peur'' (''City of Unspeakable Fear,'' 1964). Tickets are $1. Information: 998-8750. THE 92D STREET Y, 1395 Lexington Avenue (996-1100). Presents a number of annual film series that usually include post-screening discussions with directors, actors or other film professionals. The next series - with commentary by film scholars and critics - is The Forbidden Film. It begins June 2, and includes eight Thursday evenings of censored or controversial films and television programs, ranging from Mae West's ''She Done Him Wrong'' and John Schlesinger's ''Midnight Cowboy'' to a documentary on X-rated movies. Tickets are $8. (The series is $56.) THE PUBLIC THEATER, 425 Lafayette Street (598-7171 or 598-7150). Presents film series dedicated to works by individual directors or broadly grouped into particular themes, as well as limited runs of new films, six days a week. ''Tent of Miracles,'' a Brazilian film based on a novel by Jorge Amado and directed by Nelson Pereira dos Santos about a mulatto scholar who becomes a national hero, will be shown today through May 26 (no screenings on Mondays). Later shows through next Thursday are: ''The Record,'' a Swiss-German film about a television fanatic, and Clare Peploe's ''High Season.'' And a two-week survey of other recent and classic Brazilian films, The Return of Brazilian Cinema, begins May 27. (No films are screened Mondays.) Tickets are $5; $4 for students and the elderly. Documentaries are shown at no cost nearly every Saturday and Sunday at 2 P.M.; ''As If It Were Yesterday,'' about the World War II Belgian Resistance, will be shown through June 12. THE QUEENS MUSEUM, New York City Building in Flushing Meadows-Corona Park (718-592-2405). Films relating to current exhibitions are screened almost every Saturday, generally at 2 P.M. A four-week series of experimental documentary films, called New York, New York, begins tomorrow and continues through June 11. Tomorrow, four short films will be screened: Hilary Harris's ''Organism'' (1975), Frank Mouris's ''Coney'' (1975), Francis Thompson's ''New York, New York'' (1957), and D. A. Pennebaker's ''Daybreak Express'' (1953). May 28: ''Memoirs of a Movie Palace.'' June 4: ''Metropolitan Avenue.'' June 11: ''New York in the Twenties'' and ''City of Contrasts.'' Films are free with museum admission, which is a suggested $2 for adults; $1 for students, children and the elderly. THE QUEENS PUBLIC LIBRARY. Relatively recent and classic Hollywood features, travelogues and other documentaries are shown in its Central Library, 89-11 Merrick Boulevard, in Jamaica (718-990-0700), as well as at all 60 branches. Among the library's many free, and more or less regular, series are ones on Monday afternoons at Central Library; Wednesdays at 2 P.M. at the Richmond Hill branch, 118-14 Hillside Avenue (718-849-7150); Thursdays at 2:30 P.M. at the North Forest Park branch, 98-27 Metropolitan Avenue (718-261-5512); alternate Saturdays at the Glendale branch, 78-60 73d Place (718-821-4980), and every other Friday at the Hillcrest branch, 187-05 Union Turnpike in Flushing (718-454-2786). A film festival celebrating Irving Berlin's 100th birthday concludes at Central Library on Sunday at 1 P.M., with ''There's No Business Like Show Business.'' Schedules are available in each branch. THE WHITNEY MUSEUM OF AMERICAN ART, Madison Avenue and 75th Street (570-0537). Now showing 16 films and film excerpts made by Andy Warhol during the 1960's in its second-floor video gallery through June 5. Several daily screenings are presented, Tuesdays through Sundays. Today and tomorrow, ''My Hustler'' will be shown at 11:30 A.M., and ''The Chelsea Girls'' will be screened at 1:30 P.M.; on Sunday, the two films will be shown at 12:30 and 2:30 P.M. Tickets are free with museum admission, which is $4; $2 for the elderly, and students, children and members are free.
