Book Discloses That Reagan Planned To Kill National Endowment for Arts

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: May 15, 1988

The Reagan Administration planned to eliminate the National Endowment for the Arts when it came into office in 1981, but dropped the plan when its special task force on the arts and humanities discovered ''the needs involved and the benefits of past assistance,'' according to a forthcoming book by Livingston Biddle, who was chairman of the endowment at the time.

Mr. Biddle's book, ''Our Government and the Arts: A Perspective From the Inside,'' is scheduled to be published on May 27 by the American Council for the Arts, a nonprofit arts service organization in New York City.

Mr. Biddle, who had been appointed chairman of the endowment by President Carter and whose term in office extended 10 months into the Reagan Administration, writes that he first heard of the Administration's plan to eliminate the endowment altogether over a three-year period from friends in Congress. The news was confirmed, he states, when he met with Barnabas McHenry, who was soon to become vice chairman of the President's task force. Mr. McHenry ''indicated,'' according to Mr. Biddle, that some people in the coming Administration wanted the endowment to ''disappear.'' Heavy Persuasion

Last week Mr. McHenry confirmed Mr. Biddle's report. ''The transition team really did want to de-fund it,'' Mr. McHenry said. ''So we put a lot of people on the task force like Charlton Heston and Adolph Coors who were close to the President, and we all thought the task force did finally persuade him that it would be a terrible thing to stop the Federal support.''

Daniel J. Terra, President Reagan's ambassador-at-large for cultural affairs, said late last week: ''It's true there were some in the Administration then who would have liked to have seen the endowment reduced or done away with altogether. I came with a neutral mind, but then I took a very strong position in support of it, and gradually that became the consensus.''

Nevertheless, the Administration went ahead with a plan to cut the endowment's budget by 50 percent. In his book, Mr. Biddle wrote that the proposal was rolled back to only a 6 percent cutback, chiefly through the efforts of Representative Sidney Yates, the Chicago Democrat who is chairman of the House subcommittee that oversees the endowment's annual budget.

The book recounts Mr. Biddle's experiences in government and the arts, beginning with his helping Senator Claiborne Pell, Democrat of Rhode Island, to draft the original legislation for the endowment in 1963 and concluding with his battles with the new Reagan Administration in 1981. Praise With a Barb

Mr. Biddle praises Frank Hodsoll, the current chairman of the endowment, for his ''hard work'' and ''increase in understanding of the complicated endeavor involved.''

He also writes admiringly of his predecessor, Nancy Hanks, but suggests in the book that after two terms as chairman of the endowment she was ''enticed by notions of infallibility or irreplaceability.'' He writes that Miss Hanks expected to be appointed to a third term by President Carter even after Senator Pell had told her it was not possible. President Carter appointed Mr. Biddle instead.

He also jovially reports that on the 10th anniversary of the passage of the bill that created the endowment, Senators Hubert H. Humphrey and Jacob K. Javits became ''smitten by nostalgia'' and pictured themselves ''standing arm in arm in the well of the Senate'' after having ''jointly battled through the bill's approval, in all its precedent-setting and historic impact.''

It was, of course, not they but Senator Pell and Mr. Biddle who floor-managed the progress of the bill at that time, but, writes Mr. Biddle: ''How, under such circumstances, do you challenge a senior senator and a former Vice President? And do you really want to intrude on such memories?''
