New Yorkers Bask in Christmas Warmth

By MICHAEL T. KAUFMAN
Published: December 26, 1988

The children who received sleds for Christmas - presumably there still are children who get sleds instead of Nintendo games - must have been disappointed yesterday as fantasies of speeding down Dead Man's Hills dissolved in a 53-degree heatwave.

Still, despite the snowless skies and balmy winds, the essentials of the holiday remained as unavoidable as ''It's a Wonderful Life'' on television, as chestnut vendors on Fifth Avenue, as chestnuts in Christmas stories.

Communion and fellowship flowed in churches, in family groups and among strangers. There was a boom in charity, and simple civility also gained for the moment. People who normally ignored each other made eye contact and occasionally exchanged smiles. A woman on Broadway and 77th Street invited a woman she did not know to share her cab. In scores of churches, hospitals and community centers, the comfortable served the needy. People talked to each other on trains. Even runners in Central Park, normally taciturn ascetics, greeted each other yesterday morning as they trotted around the reservoir. Holiday for the Homeless

At many church services in the New York metropolitan region the story of the Nativity was linked to the shame of contemporary homelessness or class stratification.

At Trinity Church, on Broadway at Wall Street, the Rev. Daniel Paul Matthews told the 700 people who had come to the Christmas Eve service that it was wrong for people to isolate themselves.

''We tend to get so stratified into classes, into groups,'' he told the predominantly well-to-do congregants, some of whom had arrived in limousines. ''You and I want all the time to separate ourselves, to be apart from those who are different.''

During the holiday, volunteers sought out the destitute. As he does every night, Billy Batson, of the Coalition for the Homeless, took a van full of food to give to street dwellers. In addition to the food, he and his volunteer helpers gave out blankets and socks and gloves to nearly a thousand people.

Near Grand Central Terminal, an old woman accepted the blanket and socks but told a woman volunteer that she would not take the containier of milk. ''Look, honey,'' she said, ''I'll take the milk when my bank balance drops below $54,000, meanwhile you keep it.'' Everyone laughed.

Some of those on the line at the terminal were stocking up after having earlier been at the Grand Central Drop-In Center, which is run by the First Moravian Church. As a folk singer prepared to perform, Harold, a 35-year-old homeless man who would not give his last name, tearfully expressed his thanks. ''They took me off the street,'' he said. ''I'm not cold anymore. I'm not hungry anymore.''

Steve Riley, who is homeless and who is an organizer for a group that works with the homeless, was sitting nearby and recalling for some guests that Jesus was born in a manger because there was no room at the inn for Mary and Joseph. ''Christmas is the homeless holiday,'' he said.

At Allen Street near Delancey, a heavyset man named Jim was standing with three friends trying to make enough money for a bottle of muscatel by wiping the windshields of cars that stopped for the light. Jim, who said he had lived on the streets for six years, said he found the motorists less generous than in the past.

''Last year by this time we made about a hundred dollars, now we got just four dollars,'' he said. He was not complaining, he said. ''I'm out here because I want to be, and there are more places for us to eat,'' he added.

Jim had his lunch at the Bowery Mission, and he was thankful for it.

''But, you know, I think the drivers are now less prone to give us anything because of Mayor Koch and what he said about saying no to beggars and just giving to institutions,'' Jim said. He made a fruitless pass at a car and returned to the curbside conversation. ''I'm not blaming the Mayor. I don't need to make as much as I did last year, just enough for a bottle, and then maybe I could go to Times Square and see a movie,'' Jim said. ''I ain't seen a movie for six years.'' 'Why Not Kick the Mayor?'

The Mayor donned a chef's hat and spent about 15 minutes dishing out slices of turkey to some of the 1,300 people who lined up for a free dinner provided by the Sheraton Centre Hotel and Towers. He also spoke briefly to the crowd, telling them that the city would close the dilapidated hotels for the homeless within about 18 months.

Under the administration's plan, homeless families will be housed in temporary or permanent apartments with more services run by the city or nonprofit groups.

Some of the diners who had earlier participated in a memorial ceremony for a homeless woman who died in Grand Central on Christmas three years ago jeered the Mayor. He later told reporters he did not mind the boos.

''People have so many troubles, you can't blame them for wanting to let their frustrations out on others, and I'm the most visible symbol in this town,'' Mr. Koch said. ''You want to kick somebody, why not kick the Mayor?'' Balmy, Quiet Streets
