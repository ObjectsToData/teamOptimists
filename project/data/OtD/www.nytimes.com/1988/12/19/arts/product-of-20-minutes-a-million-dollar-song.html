Product of 20 Minutes: A Million-Dollar Song

By STEPHEN HOLDEN
Published: December 19, 1988

''I've always said it took me 20 years to find out how to write a song in 20 minutes,'' Bart Howard, the composer of ''Fly Me to the Moon,'' recalled the other day. ''The song just fell out of me. One publisher wanted me to change the lyric to 'take me to the moon.' Had I done that I don't know where I'd be today.''

Since it was introduced in 1954 under the title ''In Other Words,'' the song, which was officially renamed ''Fly Me to the Moon'' in 1963 at the suggestion of Peggy Lee, has earned Mr. Howard well over a million dollars in recording and performance royalties and enabled him to lead a life of ease.

This week the composer, who divides his time between a home in North Salem, N.Y., and a rented house in Palm Beach, Fla., will appear in a revue of more than 20 of his own songs at Jan Wallman's, 49 West 44th Street.

As a performer, Mr. Howard is a very late bloomer. Now in his early 70's, he had never sung in public until last June, when he took part in a program called ''American Cabaret.'' The program was the first of a four-part concert series organized by a former New York Times music critic, Allen Hughes, to benefit the Hammond Museum, a small museum for the arts and humanities in North Salem.

Last September Mr. Howard made his Manhattan debut at Jan Wallman's in a trial version of the show he will bring to the club for five evenings beginning tonight. To prepare, Mr. Howard has even taken singing lessons. Joining him will be Buddy Barnes, the pianist and singer, and K. C. Sullivan, an up-and-coming young singer and comedienne. In the Style of Cole Porter

Although Mr. Howard is known primarily for ''Fly Me to the Moon,'' for years he has been recognized by connoisseurs of pop as an exceptional craftsman of Porteresque songs that offer a sophisticated overview of love and life. ''Let Me Love You,'' ''It Was Worth It'' and ''The First Warm Day in May'' are among the more than half a dozen ballads by Mr. Howard that became staples of the late Mabel Mercer. Johnny Mathis also recorded several of his songs in the late 1950's and early 60's, and Frank Sinatra, who has recorded two versions of ''Fly Me to the Moon,'' included ''The Man in the Looking Glass,'' a song written for him by Mr. Howard, in his classic album ''September of My Years.''

Born in Burlington, Iowa, where he studied piano, Mr. Howard moved to New York in 1937 at the age of 23 and quickly became caught up in show business. When Mabel Mercer moved from England to America in 1938, they became immediate friends, and after World War II, which he spent as an Army musician, he became her accompanist for four years. From 1952 to 1960 he was the house pianist at the Blue Angel.

Mr. Howard's songwriting idol was always Cole Porter. ''Though we didn't know each other well, he was it for me,'' Mr. Howard said. ''Shortly after I arrived in New York my agent took me to meet him, and I played my paltry, dreadful little songs. But he told me I'd be successful once I learned to sing them myself.''

''Fly Me to the Moon'' has enjoyed a singular history. The first person to record it was Kaye Ballard, who put it on the flip side of ''Lazy Afternoon,'' her signature song from the show ''The Golden Apple.'' Over the next several years, dozens of other singers also recorded it, but it did not become a hit until 1963 with a bossa nova instrumental version by Joe Harnell and his orchestra that sold more than 500,000 copies. Gradually the song, which has now been recorded more than 300 times and in many languages, became a standard, earning its composer more than $50,000 a year and in some years more than twice that. Other Things to Do

In recent years, Mr. Howard, who moved out of New York in 1971, has virtually ceased songwriting and devoted his energy to remodeling houses.

''I don't think I missed a great deal by letting my career peter out,'' he reflected. ''A lot of people die if they can't keep on with their work. I'm fairly resourceful and just found other things to do.''

For this engagement, Mr. Howard returned to the piano to write two new songs. He will also perform ''Fly Me to the Moon.''

''I put the verb 'to fly' on the map,'' Mr. Howard said. ''After the song, everyone said, 'fly me here, fly me there.' But I'm not in the dictionary of quotations where I think I belong.''

Mr. Howard laughed softly. Like so many of his lyrics, his regret seemed tinged with a wry, quiet sense of humor.
