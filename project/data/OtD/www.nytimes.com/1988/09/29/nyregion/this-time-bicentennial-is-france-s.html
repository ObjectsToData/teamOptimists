This Time, Bicentennial Is France's

By RICHARD BERNSTEIN
Published: September 29, 1988

President Francois Mitterrand opened a yearlong celebration at New York University yesterday to mark the 200th anniversary of the French Revolution, noting that ''the message of 1789 has not faded.''

Speaking in French at the university's library after the Broadway cast of ''Les Miserables'' sang selections from the musical and after the French film director Louis Malle read from the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, the document that started the tumultuous and epochal event in 1789, Mr. Mitterrand said, ''Nothing better defines modern democracy than the synthesis, always to be reinvented, between liberty and equality.''

He said New York University's commemoration would help preserve the French Revolution's origins in the beliefs in human rights, most notably equality of all people before the law. Illuminating the Revolution

''It is our work to insure that these principles are enshrined in facts and do not remain at the level of talk,'' he said.

N.Y.U.'s yearlong program is intended, as a university brochure puts it, ''to illuminate the Revolution as it unfolded and to show how profoundly this event has influenced every dimension of human activity in modern times, from art to politics to social justice.''

And so, there will be conferences, public lectures, film series, special courses for N.Y.U. students, art exhibitions, concerts and many scholarly colloquia, the last no doubt intended to sift through the multiple meanings of the shattering episode that Edmund Burke, the 18th-century English philosopher, called ''the offspring of cold hearts and muddy understandings.''

The sponsor of the commemoration is the New York University Humanities Council. 'Rights of Man' Declaration

Among the other visitors expected are Francois Furet, considered the outstanding historian of the Revolution in France today, who is scheduled to give two lectures, one next month on the French Democratic heritage and another in April on the French Revolution and the Jews.

Robert Badinter, president of France's highest court, the Constitutional Council, is to speak in the spring on Justice and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, the document, proclaimed by the National Assembly in 1789, that is generally regarded as the most important and beneficial product of the revolutionary heritage.

Elie Wiesel, the Holocaust survivor and recipient of the Nobel Peace Prize, who was educated in France after being liberated from Auschwitz at the end of World War II, is to speak next spring on the philosophic implications in the 20th century of the Declaration of the Rights of Man.

Regis Debray, now an adviser to Mr. Mitterrand and the one-time friend of Che Guevara, the Latin American Communist revolutionary, is scheduled to speak in November on the Revolution and the Americas. E. P. Thompson, the professor of history at the University of Leeds in England, will give a lecture next April on the liberal response to Jacobinism, the term generally applied to the radical swing, led by such figures as Robespierre, that overtook the Revolution in its middle period. Films and Plays

Perfomances of two plays by Denis Diderot, the 18th-century Enlightenment philosopher, are scheduled for the fall: ''The Nephew of Rameau,'' in English, and ''Madame de la Carliere,'' in French, both to be staged by the City Stage Company Repertory Theater. An exhibition on caricature in the Revolutionary period, expected to travel to several places in the United States, is scheduled for early in the winter, and N.Y.U. will show a series of films that depict the Revolution, including those of the legendary director Jean Renoir.

The commemoration will be reflected in a similar program at the University of California at Los Angeles, and the National Endowment for the Humanities is sponsoring programs about the Revolution for secondary-school teachers across the country.

''The revolution was a world historical event as opposed to just a French event,'' said Thomas Bishop, chairman of N.Y.U.'s French department and an organizer of the commemoration.

Mr. Bishop said the invitation to Mr. Mitterrand was particularly appropriate in light of the French President's efforts to champion human rights. Fierce Debate in France

''It seems to me that the Rights of Man is the part of the heritage of the French Revolution that is getting more play in this country and more play in France, than any other part,'' Mr. Bishop said. ''Nothing has emerged from the French revolution that was more important than that.''

After his speech in the spacious atrium of the library, Mr. Mitterrand attended a dinner in his honor in a room in the library decorated with white-flecked red French roses, the emblem of France's Socialist Party, which currently leads the French Government, and, university officials said, Danielle Mitterrand's favorite flower.

Mr. Mitterrand came to the United States at a time of fierce debate in France about the meaning of the Revolution, an event that had numerous facets, good and bad.

There is no doubt, however, that the Revolution, which began in 1789 and ended in 1799, was a point of departure for the modern world - sharing that distinction perhaps with the American Revolution that preceded it and the British Industrial Revolution that gained momentum shortly afterward.

''Even the very notion of change itself,'' the N.Y.U. brochure says, ''the idea that a social and political world could be utterly transformed by acts of human will, owes its power if not its philosophical origin, to the example, for good or ill, of the Revolution in France.''
