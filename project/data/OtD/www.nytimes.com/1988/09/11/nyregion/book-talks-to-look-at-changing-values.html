Book Talks to Look at Changing Values

By SHARON L. BASS
Published: September 11, 1988

WHAT are some of the lasting effects of the 1960's? How have families changed - and stayed the same - over the last 50 years? Which Presidents since the Depression have had the greatest impact on American society?

Those are some of the questions 17 college professors will explore through biographies and autobiographies in a forthcoming library discussion series called ''American Lives: Reflections on Our Values and Ourselves.''

The discussions, to begin Wednesday at the Hamden Miller Library, will be held in 30 libraries around the state from the Wilton Library to the Acton Public Library in Old Saybrook to the East Hampton Public Library and the Oxford Public Library. The series will conclude May 3 at the Woodbridge Town Library.

Each library will present four of five topics, led by scholars, which examine the impact of the last 50 years of American life. The subjects include: ''The Imperial Presidency''; ''Families: Endurance in the Midst of Change''; ''Self-Portraits: Autobiographies of American Women''; ''The 1960's: Transformations in American Values,'' and ''Popular Culture: Stars, Artists and American Values.'' Change of Values

''The key part of our project is to show how values have changed over the last 50 years,'' said Barbara Rader, a project director for the Southern Connecticut Library Council, which is sponsoring the book talks. ''People bring to books their own predispositions and relate to things from their own lives, and that's what we hope these book discussions will bring about.''

Financed with a $156,000 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities, an Oct. 28 symposium at Wesleyan University in Middletown will highlight the series. The keynote speaker will be Justin Kaplan, the author of ''Walt Whitman: A Life'' and ''Mr. Clemens and Mark Twain.''

Some of the scholars who will lead discussions said they were drawn to the library series because of parallels between their lives and the ones they will explore.

Robert Stepto, a professor of English and Afro-American studies at Yale, grew up in the same poor, black neighborhood in Pittsburgh as Robby Wideman, the brother of John Edgar Wideman, who wrote the autobiography ''Brothers and Keepers,'' one of four books Mr. Stepto will discuss.

In his book, Mr. Wideman explores how two brothers with the same upbringing can turn out so differently - Robby, who became a criminal, is now serving time in prison, while Mr. Wideman is a college professor and writer. 'How Have I Failed?'

''Wideman's mother asks herself the same questions any parent does regardless of race or economic background,'' said Mr. Stepto. ''She wonders, 'How have I failed my son? Where did I go wrong?' '' Mr. Stepto's topic is ''Families: Endurance in the Midst of Change.''

Other books he will review are ''Growing Up,'' by Russell Baker; ''This House of Sky: Landscapes of a Western Mind,'' by Ivan Doig, and ''With a Daughter's Eye: A Memoir of Margaret Mead and Gregory Bateson,'' by Mary Catherine Bateson.

Mr. Stepto said he was also motivated to be a part of the series because ''I think this library council is important.''

''People don't read as much as they used to,'' he said, ''and programs like these bring people into the libraries.''

While ''these are timeless issues'' they are also ''time specific,'' Mr. Stepto said. For instance, he said, the authors that he will review describe what it is like being brought up in single-parent home, which is quite common today. However, they also had to deal with the ''particular pressures from their era, such as the Depression,'' he said. Paradoxes and Contradictions

Ronald Dufour, a history professor at Rhode Island College and a Cheshire resident, said he vividly remembers the 1960's, when he was a college student and a conscientious objector. ''I lived the paradoxes and the contradictions,'' he said.

He plans to relive them as he takes on ''The 1960's: Transformations in American Values'' and ''The Imperial Presidency'' during the library series.

''I hope to try to change people's conception of the 1960's,'' he said. ''Most people think of sex, drugs and rock and roll, but it was political activism, too. There was a desire to make American democracy work and a search for personal authenticity.''

''We are living in an era of liberal-bashing,'' he added. ''People look at the 60's as a bad time. However, much of what we value today comes out of the 60's.'' One of the major differences, he said, is that today, college students are Wall Street bound while two decades ago they were Peace Corps and graduate school bound. ''There was a real sense of authenticity then,'' he said.

Mr. Dufour will explore Presidents through such biographies as: ''Roosevelt: The Lion and the Fox,'' by James MacGregor Burns; ''JFK: The Presidency of John F. Kennedy,'' by Herbert S. Parmet, and ''Big Daddy From the Pedernales: Lyndon B. Johnson,'' by Paul K. Conkin. Contrasting Two Presidents

''We will discuss the concepts of leadership and myths,'' he said. ''What Americans conceive of their leaders and what they want from them. People are looking for Presidential leadership again.''

Mr. Dufour said he will contrast President Kennedy, ''who was a hungry politician and wanted to become President just for the sake of being President,'' with Franklin D. Roosevelt, ''who had an intense commitment to the people.''

Other books to be reviewed in the series include ''Memories of a Catholic Girlhood,'' by Mary McCarthy; ''Marilyn,'' by Gloria Steinem, and ''Woody Guthrie: A Life,'' by Joe Klein.

In addition, the Southern Connecticut Library Council will sponsor two photographic exhibits in conjunction with the biography series. From Jan. 1 through Jan. 28 a touring exhibit, ''War and Memory: In the Aftermath of Vietnam,'' will be shown at the Russell Library in Middletown. The exhibit is owned by the New England Foundation for the Arts.

A Smithsonian Institution traveling exhibit, ''Vietnam Veterans Memorial: A National Experience,'' will be on display at the Lyman Center for the Performing Arts at Southern Connecticut State University in New Haven from Jan. 28 to Feb. 26.

The book discussions are free and open to the public. It is not necessary to read the books beforehand. For schedule information, call the Library Council at 284-8535.
