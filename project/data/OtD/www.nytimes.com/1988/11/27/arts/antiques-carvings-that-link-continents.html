ANTIQUES; Carvings That Link Continents

By RITA REIF
Published: November 27, 1988

African ivories - exquisitely carved with exotic motifs, tribal scenes and images of the hunt - seem as startling today as when they were first brought to Europe 500 years ago. Popes, kings and artists, Albrecht Durer included, collected spoons, forks, saltcellars, dagger handles and hunting horns of the highly sophisticated type assembled in ''Africa and the Renaissance: Art in Ivory,'' at the Center for African Art, 54 East 68th Street. The exhibition, the first to focus on these rare objects, is to remain through April 9.

Trade in Afro-Portuguese ivories was brief, beginning 30 years after the Portuguese landed in West Africa in 1460 and lasting about a century. By 1504 import duties were paid in Lisbon for sizable numbers of these artifacts, carved for export by the Sapi people of Sierre Leone as early as the 1490's.

When the Sapis were uprooted by war about 1530, the Bini people of the area now known as Nigeria became the chief artisans of these ivories until about 1600 when they stopped making them. Although inexpensive at the time - a saltcellar, the most precious of these objects, cost less than a linen shirt - these ivories were prized by such European collectors as Cosimo de Medici of Florence and the Grand Duke of Tyrol, Ferdinand of Hapsburg.

The exhibition of 125 selections presents 70 of the 204 surviving Afro-Portuguese ivories - the first tangible evidence of cross-cultural contact between Europeans and black Africans beginning in the late 15th century. An additional 50 items dating from the 13th through the 16th centuries - European books, vessels, game boards and utensils as well as African Benin bronzes, textiles and stone sculpture - show similarities and differences between native African and European objects in the years when Afro-Portuguese ivories were made. ''At a time when most Westerners thought Africa barely existed, there were people living in courts and villages who produced a highly sophisticated art,'' said Susan Vogel, the center's executive director. ''We know from archeology that this was true even earlier. This work shows a kind of refined craftsmanship that we usually don't think of as African art. In sharp contrast, African wood sculpture is more concerned with power and force and expresses itself with bold unfinished looking forms.''

The most European aspect of these Afro-Portuguese ivories is their function - not their form or decoration. Leaflike spoons with insects crawling on the stalk-handles are pure African conceptions - far more organic in their shaping than anything known in Europe at the time. Saltcellars covered with images of people are only distant echoes of European pedestal-based chalices, awash with stylized flowers and classical ornament.

The exhibition includes loans from 29 museums and 17 private collections on several continents. Because of the fragile nature of these objects, most were hand delivered. ''We spent the last two weeks at the airport,'' Ms. Vogel said. ''We had no cargo shipments, only air tickets for the couriers and curators who brought these things in one by one - all are lap objects. The curator from Munich brought a spoon in the smallest crate I've ever seen, the size of a pencil box.''

The most compelling carved ivory vessel extant, a saltcellar topped with a scene depicting an executioner and his victims, is documented in the catalogue but as of early last week its presence in the show, as a loan from a museum in Rome, was not certain. e In this piece, six severed heads are arranged neatly in front of the executioner, an imperious man in a wide-brimmed hat who is identified as a chief. He is shown half kneeling, shield in one hand, ax in the other, ready to behead a seventh man who cowers before him.

Strangely, all eight of these boldly sculptured heads have the same bony, aristocratic face. The impact of this gruesome surrealist scene is undiminished by several repairs to the ax, the executioner's right hand and the head of the seventh victim. What is remarkable about this tour de force of craftsmanship, and all the others, is that they were carved from a single piece of ivory. From the beginning, these curiosities were often wrongly described as Turkish or Indian. In 1959 William Fagg, then keeper of African collections at the British Museum, identified these ivories fully and for the first time in his book ''Afro-Portuguese Ivories.'' Mr. Fagg and Ezio Bassani, an African art expert at Florence's International University of Art, wrote the scholarly illustrated catalogue of this show ($60 clothbound, $32 ).

The exhibition, which is financed by grants from the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, the New York State Council on the Arts and the Samuel H. Kress Foundation, will travel to Houston's Museum of Fine Arts in May. Always rare, these ivories show up infrequently in the market today, according to Bernard de Grunne, Sotheby's tribal art specialist. ''I was very excited to offer one in the last sale,'' he said, speaking of a fragment of a Sierre Leone hunting horn, carved in high relief in the early 16th century. The fragment was transformed in the late 17th century into a powder flask, which was sold for $13,200 at Sotheby's on May 10. That flask, owned by Carlo Monzino, a collector who lives in Lugano, Switzerland, is in the exhibition.

Another Afro-Portuguese hunting horn in the show was auctioned in 1981 at Sothbeby's in New York for $27,500. Formerly in the collections of Jay Leff and Nasli Heeramaneck, it is now owned by a New York collector who has not been identified.

A saltcellar that was auctioned for $13,800 in 1974 at Sotheby's in London would probably sell now, Mr. de Grunne said, for between $40,000 and $80,000.
