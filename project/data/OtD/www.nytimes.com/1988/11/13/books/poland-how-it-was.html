POLAND: HOW IT WAS

By JAN T. GROSS; Jan T. Gross teaches sociology at Emory University. He is the author of ''Revolution From Abroad: Soviet Conquest of Poland's Western Ukraine and Western Belorussia.''
Published: November 13, 1988

MY CENTURY The Odyssey of a Polish Intellectual. By Aleksander Wat. Edited and translated by Richard Lourie. Foreword by Czeslaw Milosz. Illustrated. 407 pp. Berkeley: University of California Press. THE JEWS IN POLISH CULTURE By Aleksander Hertz. Translated by Richard Lourie. Foreword by Czeslaw Milosz. 266 pp. Evanston, Ill.: Northwestern University Press. Cloth, $32.95. Paper, $14.95.

Such a fascinating book to read, this spoken memoir by Aleksander Wat! ''My Century'' is about resilience of human mind and character, about dilemmas of intellectuals in the 20th century, about evil, about historical regressiveness of Communism and the cult of monarchy in the Soviet Union, about the sophistication of the Russian intelligentsia and Russia's barbarism and its people's belief in the occult, about social atomization caused by mendacity and terror, about religiosity, about friendship. This is but a truncated list of subjects weaving through conversations between Aleksander Wat and Czeslaw Milosz, which were taped in Berkeley, Calif., in 1964 and 1965. To be exact, the book is a series of Wat's monologues, which Mr. Milosz occasionally gently points in one direction or another.

Aleksander Wat was a poet, and ''My Century'' is a work of art - a rare thing for a book that is basically a sequence of reflections. And yet this text distinctly demands active reading. We are drawn into the conversation and thus made to join the creative process.

Wat's book may be read as a spiritual biography of a generation of European intellectuals who found themselves confronted by the ''accursed problems'' of the first half of the 20th century: ''The alternative - fascism or communism - was false, but it fitted the reality.'' Let us be clear about what was at issue then. For intellectuals from a Jewish background, of whom Wat was one, Fascism was not a viable option, of course. Nor were intellectuals necessarily drawn to the radicalism of the left. But the intellectual climate of these years, together with the political experience accumulated since the First World War, dictated that one must choose Communist allegiance in order to oppose Fascism effectively.

Along with many brilliant and socially minded young men and women of his generation, Wat, who was born in Warsaw in 1900 and went on to study philosophy and write poetry, was caught in the trap. He was too much of an esthete to become a Marxist: ''My relation to Communism,'' he writes, ''was a little like Simone Weil's to Catholicism. I was afraid of infecting it. I could feel the burden I carried within myself as a literary man, a poet - the burden of the old capitalist bourgeois decadent Adam.'' Nevertheless, he was, as he put it, ''a mad fanatic, a sectarian.'' In 1928 he founded an influential Marxist periodical, The Literary Monthly. A highbrow magazine attracting brilliant contributors, it was a front for Communist Party propaganda. Wat was a most willing fellow traveler, selflessly laboring on behalf of the cause of which he deemed himself unworthy: ''I did the work of ten men at The Literary Monthly.''

In a few years his infatuation with Communism began to wane. Yet he could not become an anti-Communist in inter-war Poland, where ''everything was moving toward fascism.'' But the obligatory discourse of the radical left, full of cliches and slogans, was paralyzing. His creative ability was stifled and he stopped writing poetry. Wat did not recover spiritually and intellectually until he was imprisoned by the Communist authorities in Lvov, in 1949. (As a result of its non-aggression pact and the subsequent Friendship and Frontier Treaty with Nazi Germany, the Soviet Union occupied the eastern half of Poland in September 1939. Aleksander Wat, just like many other Communist sympathizers and throngs of ordinary people, trekked eastward that autumn to run away from the Germans. The policies of the Soviet administration in the occupied territories soon proved devastating. The Soviets deported from this area about one and a quarter million people, and executed perhaps as many as one hundred thousand. Anybody could be arrested then; Wat was imprisoned together with a group of Communist writers.) ''It was in a communist prison that I came fully to my senses and from then on, in prison, in exile, and in communist Poland, I never allowed myself to forget my basic duty - to pay, to pay for those two or three years of moral insanity.''

Wat's education about Communism, then, came in prison. He met there people from all walks of life, since all of Russia was in jail at the time. His memoir is a tapestry of conversations, character sketches and reflections drawn from the experience. His vast memory and gifts as a raconteur (which, he believes, saved his life in jail, where he earned the respect of the ferocious criminal prisoners to whom he recounted the plots of great novels for hours), combined with a superior intelligence and a state of spiritual mobilization, have produced coherent and captivating insights into a totalitarian society spawned in the middle of the 20th century.
