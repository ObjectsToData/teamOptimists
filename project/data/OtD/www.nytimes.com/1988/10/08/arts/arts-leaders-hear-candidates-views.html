Arts Leaders Hear Candidates' Views

By WILLIAM H. HONAN, Special to the New York Times
Published: October 8, 1988

Spokesmen for Vice President Bush and Gov. Michael S. Dukakis clashed sharply here today over which Presidential candidate would do more for the arts.

The occasion was the final session of a three-day National Arts Convention, which drew more than 300 artists and cultural leaders from across the country. Sponsored by the American Council for the Arts, a service organization that helps arts groups raise money and in marketing and management, the convention featured talks by the playwright Edward Albee and the Mexican poet Octavio Paz, and lively panel discussions on ''Artists' Rights'' and ''Anticipating the 1990's.'' But Presidential politics stole the show.

''The Reagan-Bush Administration has been the most hostile and disdainful of the two national endowments since their inception,'' said Anne Hawley, representing the Governor of Massachusetts. ''When Michael Dukakis becomes President, we can look forward to a new era of greatness in the arts.''

''When you think about who to elect as President, think about how your choice will affect your donors,'' said Lois Burke Shepard, who spoke for the Vice President. ''Think about it: is your attendance up? Are you living at a time when you don't need to worry about inflation or energy bills?'' Private vs. Public Sector

It was, in a sense, the campaign in miniature, with Mr. Bush's champions minimizing the role of the Federal Government and stressing the importance of arts support from the private sector, and Gov. Dukakis's admirers concentrating on how the next President must exercise compelling leadership and develop imaginative new programs.

The main difference in arts policy between Governor Dukakis and Vice President Bush, said Ms. Hawley, who is executive director of the Massachusetts Council on the Arts and Humanities and a longtime friend and neighbor of the Governor, can be seen in the way Governor Dukakis ''has made arts policy integral to the work of many state agencies, believing that the expression of imaginative life is not confined only to the concert halls and museums.''

''Our agency works with the Department of Public Works on bridge design, with bonding authorities on designs of buildings they finance, and encourages these bonding authorities to finance construction of arts facilities,'' she explained.

Mrs. Shepard, who has known Mr. Bush since 1978 and is director of the Institute of Museum Services, a Federal agency that provides operating support for museums, read a message from the Vice President. ''I am pleased to be running on the strong Republican arts and humanities platform which reflects my commitment,'' the Vice President's statement declared. ''You concerned citizens, arts professionals and friends of American culture have my highest respect . . . I particularly salute the corporations sponsoring this event. Administration Sought Cuts

Ms. Hawley held Vice President Bush accountable for the record of the Reagan Administration, which came into office in 1981 asking for a 50 percent cut in the budgets of both the National Endowment for the Arts and its sister agency for Humanities. After that effort was rebuffed by Congress, the Reagan Administration sought a sustained but never increasing level of financial support for the endowments.

''What has happened in the past seven years has been injurious to the arts,'' said Ms. Hawley. ''Governor Dukakis would not be assaulting the nonprofit sector. He would be in partnership with it.''

Asked in an interview whether or not Vice President Bush had supported President Reagan's effort in 1981 to cut the budgets of the arts and humanities endowments, Mrs. Shepard, said: ''Back in 1981 the Vice President believed that the national economy was in such bad shape that drastic measures were necessary. Now, with the G.N.P. where it is, and with a revitalized economy, modest increases are not in jeopardy.'' She said the Vice President believed ''that the Federal Government has a role to play in supporting cultural life, but that Federal funds should never be dominant and should motivate private investment.''

Governor Dukakis's policy paper distributed here, titled ''A Presidency That Will Celebrate the Arts,'' pledges to ''end the annual assault on the National Endowment for the Arts budget.'' Bush Declared Blanket Support

Mrs. Shepard indicated that Mr. Bush's support of certain controversial provisions of the Tax Reform Act of 1986 was no longer absolute. Last May, the Vice President declared his blanket support of the act despite complaints from art museums that the removal of tax benefits from the donation of appreciated property was discouraging donations. Today, however, Mrs. Shepard said, Mr. Bush ''is very aware of the fragile economy of the nonprofits, and he would look closely at any such unintended consequences of the law.''

''There still is no reliable evidence that this law is having such unintended consequences as have been claimed,'' Mrs. Shepard said, ''but if, in fact, that is the case, there are certain things that are not closed doors.''

Ms. Hawley responded in an interview after the meeting: ''This is another tax break for the most affluent members of society. Much more important is the provision in the Tax Reform Act that does not allow non-itemizers to deduct gifts. That affects 70 percent of the taxpayers in America, and we need incentives to encourage them, as well as the more affluent, to give.'' 'An Amazing Consensus'

Milton Rhodes, president of the American Council for the Arts, which sponsored the conference, said at its conclusion: ''The intellectual dialogue has been raised.'' He added that the conference had been a success not only because it clarified ''two distinctly different views of how the arts should be supported,'' but because ''there is now an amazing consensus that arts education is the primary focus of the field today to meet the challenge of dwindling audiences, lack of teachers and the other problems we face.''

In another closing statement, Eugene C. Dorsey, president of the Gannett Foundation and chairman of the American Council for the Arts, said: ''Somehow, through the splintered maze of conflicting ideologies, we must look for ways to bridge differences so that we do not alienate the supporters of arts nor neglect issues critical to artists.''

At the final session of the conference, Schuyler Chapin, dean emeritus of the School of the Arts at Columbia University and the chairman of the bipartisan Independent Committee on Arts Policy, reported that after months of research the committee had five recommendations for the next President of the United States.

The President should, Mr. Chapin declared, address legislative, legal and tax issues that adversely affect artists and arts institutions, endorse the importance of the arts in education, promote greater access to the arts, help insure the financial stability of cultural institutions and support international cultural exchange.
