EDUCATION; Lessons

By Edward B. Fiske
Published: October 12, 1988

If anyone has any doubts that boys are different from girls, just ask a college registrar. Girls get better grades than boys in high school and go on to college in greater numbers. But boys get higher scores on standardized tests - a matter of some economic significance since tests scores are used to award thousands of competitive academic scholarships.

Of the $23 million in National Merit Scholarships handed out last year, $15 million went to boys and $8 million to girls. Candidates are chosen from a group of finalists, 64 percent of them male, who do outstandingly well on the Preliminary Scholastic Aptitude Test in the spring of their junior year in high school.

This anomaly - girls being better students but boys getting the scholarships - has attracted a growing amount of attention.

Two years ago the New York State Education Department, which had been using college entrance examination scores to hand out $40 million in state scholarships, altered its formulas to include grades as well as test scores. The change had the desired effect, eliminating the boys' edge in Regents Scholarships entirely and reducing their share of Empire State Scholarships to 62 percent from 72.

But the new formula also produced some nasty side effects. Some schools began inflating the grades of their best students so that more of them could cash in on the state scholarships, which are worth as much as $10,000 over a college career. So last week it was back to the drawing board.

State officials decided to return to the old system of using the national test scores that virtually no one likes until the state can come up with a new test more closely tied to New York State curriculums. The assumption is that, if the test reflects what students actually study, the differences between the scores of boys and girls will be narrowed.

Educators and others give many theories about why boys score higher on tests. One is that tests are biased.

''Boys do better on questions about sports and mechanical things, while girls do better on questions involving relationships or the humanities,'' said Sarah Stockwell, a spokeswoman for the advocacy group FairTest. ''But there are more of the former than the latter. When's the last time you saw a knitting analogy on a Scholastic Aptitude Test?''

Donald M. Stewart, president of the College Board, which sponsors the S.A.T., calls such allegations nonsense. ''We use complicated statistical procedures to spot bias when we are pretesting questions,'' he said ''and if we find it we throw out the question.''

The flip side of the test bias argument is that high school grading favors women. There is well-documented evidence at the elementary school level that teachers, most of whom are women, look more favorably on girls than boys. Sugar and spice comes closer to the prevailing ideal of primary classroom demeanor than snails and puppy dog tails, and some educators suggest that this phenomenon has a counterpart at the high school level.

''It may be that girls are less rebellious and more ready to do homework and other assignments that contribute to academic achievement,'' said Robert Solomon, a vice president of the Educational Testing Service.

Finally, there is the argument that boys and girls learn different things in American schools. Boys take more math and science courses, while girls spend more time with social studies, literature and the arts.

Girls may work harder and get better grades, the argument goes, but the kind of knowledge boys are picking up pays off when it comes time to open up the test booklet with all the little squares. Do college admissions tests make value judgments about what kinds of knowledge are the most important? ''Sure they do,'' answers Mr. Stewart.

So the underlying issue involves not only fairness to girls who would like a better chance at merit scholarships but also some questions about fundamental academic values.

Does America want an educational system operating under a definition of ''academic merit'' that says that math and science are more worthy than literature and the arts?

If the answer is yes, then the sex bias question ought to be recast to ask school officials why they permit girls to take so few of the really important courses. If the answer is no, then the criteria for awarding merit scholarships - whether academic transcripts or test scores - ought to be broadened to reflect a more inclusive concept of merit.

Any large-scale scholarship competition will involve compromises. Standardized tests may be crude, for example, but they are economical and, at least on the surface, impartial. Mr. Solomon of the testing service said critics might be expecting too much from programs like the National Merit Scholarships. ''They don't claim to identify all talented kids,'' he said. ''It's an effort to identify some talented kids.''

The problem is that too many of these kids are of one sex, and that is a compromise that fewer and fewer people seem to be willing to make.
