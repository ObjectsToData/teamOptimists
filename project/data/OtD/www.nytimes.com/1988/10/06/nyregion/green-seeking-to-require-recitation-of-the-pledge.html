Green Seeking to Require Recitation of the Pledge

By NEIL A. LEWIS
Published: October 6, 1988

The New York City Schools Chancellor told the Board of Education yesterday that he believed it was important for all public schools in the city to begin the day with a recitation of the Pledge of Allegiance.

''I want it known on the public record that the Pledge of Allegiance is a requirement,'' Chancellor Richard R. Green said at a board meeting. He said that he did not mean schools should be monitored to insure that they comply and he stressed that students and teachers were free to decline to participate.

But Dr. Green was startled when a member of the board, Gwendolyn C. Baker of Manhattan, objected and said she would vote against formalizing the policy next month.

State law requires that schools have a program to provide for the pledge, but allows students to decline to participate. City schools adhere to the program. But, officials said, it was followed unevenly. Dr. Green's proposal would merely formalize the requirement. Eye on Election

Dr. Green said he was raising the issue because his office had received inquiries about the policy as a result of the pledge's becoming an issue in the Presidential campaign. Vice President Bush has criticized Gov. Michael S. Dukakis for his veto of a measure passed by the Massachusetts Legislature requiring teachers to lead their students in reciting the pledge. Mr. Dukakis said he did so on the basis of an advisory opinion from the state's highest court that the measure was unconstitutional.

Dr. Green also announced, in what he described as his personal opinion, that he would not ask the board to approve the position formally until after the Nov. 8 election, to avoid entanglement with politics. ''I expect we'll have a consensus then,'' he said, only to hear Ms. Baker say, ''No you won't.''

She said later that she did not believe the pledge should be compulsory. ''They should simply provide a flag in every class and leave it up to students and teachers to say it or opt out,'' Ms. Baker said. If recitation is conducted as it is now, she said, a child might attract undue attention by declining to participate.

Dr. Green also said he was hoping to demonstrate improvements at two zoned high schools, George Washington in Manhattan and Erasmus Hall in Brooklyn. He said afterward that was part of his effort to improve the zoned schools because of the perception that there were two classes of high school education in the city, special schools, to which students apply for admission, and the zoned schools, which must accept students who live in their neighborhoods.

Washington will receive $800,000 to help make it a center for the largely Hispanic surrounding area of Washington Heights. Among the possibilities are to keep the school open until 10 o'clock nightly and offer adult education and other programs. Erasmus is being divided into ''subschools'' featuring the humanities, science and mathematics, business and the arts, in an effort to make it more appealing to students.
