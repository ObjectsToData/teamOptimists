WESTCHESTER GUIDE


Published: October 9, 1988

EYEING THE EAGLE The American bald eagle as fine art, political symbol and collectible is to be celebrated with more than 150 items on exhibition in ''The American Eagle: Spirit and Symbol 1782-1882,'' at the Katonah Gallery today through Dec. 31.

Images of the bird appear in scrimshaw, as the carved prow of ''The Empress of China,'' the first American ship to sail from New York to Canton; on the splats of a Duncan Phyfe chair made for DeWitt Clinton, the former New York State Governor; in numerous paintings and sculptures; on the emblem commissioned for the inauguration of George Washington; on coins, tavern signs, gravestone rubbings, advertisements, butter molds, quilts, weathervanes, woodstoves, porcelains, chamber pots and various items for the military, including powder horns, drums, buttons, and buckles.

Several related events will begin with a parade and performance by two fife and drums corps at 2 P.M. today on the Katonah Village Green. Sixty members of Waterbury's Mattatuck Drum Band, founded in 1767, will be joined by the West Point Color Guard from the First Battalion of the First Infantry Regiment.

At 1 P.M. Saturday Bill Robinson, a wildlife lecturer will conduct a family program on ''The World of Eagles'' with live eagles and other birds of prey. At 3 P.M. on Oct. 23 Theodore C. Sorensen, former adviser to President Kennedy; David Garth, a media consultant; and Lester M. Crystal, executive producer of the McNeil/Lehrer Newshour, will participate in a forum titled ''The Electing of a President: From Broadside to Broadcast.'' Additional lectures and a musical program are scheduled through early November.

The gallery is at 28 Bedford Road in Katonah and is open from 1 to 5 P.M. Tuesday, Thursday and Sunday and from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Saturday. Call 232-9555 for more information. SIMPLY QUAKERS

Mary Ellen Singsen, a member of the Scarsdale Quaker Meeting, will give a talk about the Quakers of Quaker Ridge at 3 P.M. today in the Scarsdale Historical Society's building at 937 Post Road. Her lecture will cover the beginnings of the religious sect in the 18th century and a 26-year split that began in 1828 between orthodox Quakers and Hicksites, a splinter group formed by Elias Hicks of Long Island, who believed that many Quakers were drifting too far from their original ideas.

The building in which she will speak is a 19th-century Quaker meeting house that fell into disuse, was moved from another site and reconstructed by the historical society in 1976 as a museum. Quaker Meeting members from the Scarsdale-Mamaroneck-New Rochelle area actually meet in a building built in 1949 on Popham Road.

Ms. Singsen will note that in the early part of this century Quaker farms in the area were bought by New Yorkers as summer homes, and the religion waned as its adherents moved away. But since the late 1940's interest in Quakerism has revived. ''We have a lot of young families now,'' Ms. Singsen said, attributing the growth in membership to reactions against World War II and the Vietnam War, the humanitarian and educational work of the American Friends Service Committee, and ''the very simple base of the religion, which makes it attractive to all kinds of people,'' she said. Admission is free. ELMER HADER, PAINTER

The name of Elmer Hader is well known to connoisseurs of children's books, but he is little known as a painter. A cache of 250 of his paintings, discovered in his attic by relatives who inherited his house at Grandview-on-Hudson, inspired an exhibition opening today at the Hopper House Art Center in Nyack.

Hader died in 1973. His nephew, the Broadway producer Richard Horner, was unable to sell the collection to galleries in New York because of the relatively small output, poor condition of the works and the anonymity of their creator. Beman Galleries in Nyack took the paintings, had them restored and is now exhibiting a selection at Hopper House to raise funds for the museum.

''We think they are an undiscovered treasure,'' said Lynn Beman, owner of the gallery. ''His style is post-Impressionist - realistic, bold in color and form. Some are done on canvas, some on board.'' A 25-by-30-inch oil on board, titled ''Hudson River Winter,'' is $17,500 and is the largest and most expensive piece in the show. Other paintings averaging 18 by 24 inches in size, range in price from $975 to about $5,000.

As a writer and illustrator of 300 children's books, Hader won several prices, including a Caldecott Award in 1942 for ''The Big Snow.'' Most of his fine art was done from 1912 to 1921. When his baby died in 1923 he stopped painting and devoted himself to children's books until the mid-1940's, when he returned to painting. He never exhibited his work.

Hopper House, the restored boyhood home of the painter Edward Hopper, is open from 1 to 5 P.M. weekends at 82 North Broadway. Call 358-0774 for more information. SEA SOUNDINGS

Why the sea is rising and how Long Island Sound and the 50 communities along its shores may be affected is the subject of a conference at the State University of New York at Purchase from 9 A.M. to 3:30 P.M. Saturday. The conference, organized by the Long Island Sound Task Force of the Oceanic Society in Stamford, will bring together a variety of experts as panelists. It will be held in the theater of the Humanities Building. Participants include Dr. K. O. Emery of the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute; James Titus of the United States Environmental Protection Agency; Steven Gill of the National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Service in Maryland; George Wisker, senior environmental analyst at Connecticut's State Department of Coastal Management; Dr.Vivien Gornitz of the Goddard Institute for Space Studies, and George Stafford, director of coastal resources programs for the New York State Department of State.

The conference is open to the public for a fee of $25, which includes lunch and a wine and cheese reception afterward. Students and the elderly will be admitted for $15. For more information or to register call (203) 327-9786. Registration fees will also be accepted at the door.
