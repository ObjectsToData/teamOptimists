U.S. Agency Cuts Aid to New York Public Library

By HERBERT MITGANG
Published: June 9, 1988

The National Endowment for the Humanities, reversing a 16-year partnership with the New York Public Library, has drastically reduced its financing in 1989 for the activities of the research libraries - the heart of the institution - and says it may continue to do so in the future.

The action by the endowment is seen by library officials as a blow to the institution's continuing and expanding activities. They say it raises both financial and philosophical questions about the relationship between the New York library's role as a national resource and the Federal Government's attitude toward research and scholarship in the library world.

''The loss of this vital support jeopardizes our ability to maintain the scope of our national services, including acquisitions, cataloguing and preservation,'' said Dr. Vartan Gregorian, the library president. ''Unless additional Federal revenues are provided, this decision will signal the end of an era of exemplary partnership in which the Government and private sector have shared responsibility for rebuilding and sustaining this institution.''

The endowment has cut its contribution to the research libraries from $2 million a year over the past three years - on a matching basis of one Federal dollar for every $3 from the public - to $1 million, for one year only, on a matching basis of one Federal dollar for every $4 from the public. Budget Deficit Projected

The operating budget of the research libraries has increased from $42 million in 1987 to a projected $50 million in 1989. The increase is largely due to the cost of books and other library materials, which have risen by 50 percent over the past two years because of inflation and the devaluation of the dollar. A budget deficit of $3.6 million is projected for next year.

In the last few days, the dispute has increased tensions between the principals in New York and Washington. A polite, but heated, exchange of letters between Dr. Gregorian and Lynne V. Cheney, chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, is filled with recriminations.

''I never thought our success would be our liability,'' Dr. Gregorian said yesterday. Dr. Gregorian maintains that the New York Public Library is ''unique'' - a national and international resource of knowledge and information, second only to the Library of Congress, that should not be judged by ordinary standards for Federal financing.

''Every N.E.H. chairman in the last 16 years has recognized that we are unique,'' he said. ''The most important thing is access; we are the only library in the country that is free and accessible to all scholars. Even university libraries have user fees.'' Grants Outside the Rules

Dr. Cheney said the New York Public Library, up to now, has been receiving ''anomalous'' grants outside the rules and guidelines that are used for all other institutions applying for endowment funds.

''The library has become a viable, even growing institution,'' she said, ''one that has had increasing success in recent years achieving both public and private support. The library has also become a decidedly competitive institution.''

During the last seven years of the dynamic Gregorian regime, success in fund raising, from private, foundation and public sources, and success in turning the library into a more open and even trendy social institution, have called attention to itsscholarly resources as well as to its after-hours activities. There are more lectures, exhibitions, meetings, literary events and black-tie parties (whose participants are often wealthy benefactors).

The new visibility apparently has left an impression - including within the ranks of the National Endowment of the Humanities - that the New York Public Library can shake the money tree on its own whenever an extra million or two is needed. 'You Can Raise Money Better'

Dr. Gregorian said that he was recently told, admiringly, by Dr. Cheney, ''You can raise money better than we can.''

Earlier this year, he said, Dr. Cheney asked him, ''When are you going to end your dependency on N.E.H.?''

Dr. Gregorian replied: ''We cannot be considered dependent on you because our mission is the same as yours - both of us are in the humanities. If we didn't exist, you would have to invent us. We are the library of last resort. Cutting us back goes against everything that the Administration has said about self-sufficiency and privatization.''

The dispute came to a head in the last two weeks. On May 26, 1988, Dr. Gregorian wrote Dr. Cheney a letter that, in part, goes:

''A bipartisan list of chairmen and council members have agreed since 1972 that the unique situation and services of the N.Y.P.L. merited a unique form of support and a unique relationship. Now, that support will be ended in spite of the fact that no questions have been raised about the continuing value of the national services of the N.Y.P.L. I am shocked and dismayed at the decision itself, and I also must protest the way the decision was made. It is sad that our apparent success is being held against us.'' 'You Are Not Well-Off'

In a letter dated June 1, 1988, Dr. Cheney, in part, responded:

''Although I understand the disappointment that engendered your letter of May 26, I feel obligated to note, for the record, that in that letter you misquote me and inaccurately describe the National Endowment for the Humanities' consideration of the N.Y.P.L. application. The perilous financial condition of the library that originally justified the Endowment's treating N.Y.P.L. differently from all other applicants no longer prevails. Of course, you are not 'well-off' (a description I have never offered), but the library has become a viable, even growing institution, one that has had increasing success in recent years in achieving both public and private support.''

Dr. Gregorian commented yesterday: ''No matter how you slice it, we requested $6 million over three years and we got only $1 million for one year. So we're being cut 50 percent. The only recurring money comes from N.E.H. and, with that halved, our margin of excellence is in danger.''
