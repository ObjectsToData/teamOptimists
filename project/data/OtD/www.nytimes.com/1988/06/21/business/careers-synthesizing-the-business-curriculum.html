Careers; Synthesizing The Business Curriculum

By Elizabeth M. Fowler
Published: June 21, 1988

FOR three days last week 70 college presidents, deans and professors with liberal arts or business backgrounds discussed how undergraduate schools of business could be improved to meet the needs of the next century, especially on the international and cultural fronts.

The American Assembly of Collegiate Schools of Business, which includes graduate schools among its members, and the Association of American Colleges sponsored the meeting. Peat Marwick Main & Company, the accounting firm, underwrote most of the expenses and provided its training center in Montvale, N.J., as a meeting place.

Among the movers and shakers were David H. Blake, dean of the graduate school of management at Rutgers University; Robert G. Hawkins, dean of the school of management at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, and Richard J. Wood, president of Earlham College.

Undergraduate business schools have been criticized for turning out specialists without the broad liberal education wanted by corporations and with little international understanding. Conversely, most liberal arts students are said to graduate with little business knowledge.

''How many in this room can read the financial pages?'' asked Albert A. Anderson, a Babson College professor of philosophy, waving a newspaper during a vigorous group discussion. Fewer than half the 20 participants raised hands.

Among the questions raised were these: Should business students be offered business history and should it be required? Can foreign-language proficiency be measured? What about international courses? Should courses in ethics be required?

Such questions encouraged lively discussion. ''Our students are not required to take a language, but more of them are,'' one professor said, arguing that making language courses mandatory was not necessary.

One speaker, Susan Strange, professor of international relations at the London School of Economics, emphasized the need for more international studies, offering precise suggestions. Recently, she said, she began noticing a perceptible loosening of ''walls separating economics from politics and business.'' Then she said, ''But there is a long way to go yet.''

International relations, which developed out of diplomatic history, is ''a distinct field of study from international business and international economics,'' and this has confused students, according to Mrs. Strange. She suggested that all three fields be brought together in a course called international political economy.

She called for a fresh look at banking courses, saying they need more global orientation, and added: ''If this sounds as though the curriculum is in danger of overloading, I would say that these days of easy access to current information make it less necessary for students to know every last detail. They need only know where to find out.''

A number of universities used the conference to disclose new programs:

* Indiana University: a pilot program, with 25 students, combining liberal arts and business courses.

* Babson College, with its management orientation: workshops to bring language and culture into closer contact with business courses.

* Ball State University: an interdisciplinary major in international business with a required language minor.

* Clemson University: a bachelor's program in ''language and international trade,'' a double major.

* Eastern Michigan University: new courses in international accounting, finance, marketing and transportation and logistics.

* University of Hawaii: reintroduction of a foreign-language requirement and plans for a doctorate in international management.

* University of Kansas: a requirement that business majors take two years of a language, four courses in foreign area studies or a combination, emanating from a close relationship since 1978 between the humanities and business faculties.

* University of Southern California: a ''truly multidisciplinary faculty,'' teaching Asian and other area studies to personnel of the McDonnell Douglas Corporation, the Ingersoll Rand Company, the General Electric Company and others.

* University of Toledo: ''systematic internationalization'' of business courses and a new one, ''Cultural Dimensions of International Business.''

In discussion groups, the participants aired ideas about courses in technology, science history, business history, negotiation and productivity.

The three-day meeting was regarded as a pioneering step toward curriculum improvement.

''We see the coming of age of business schools at this meeting,'' said James C. Manley, professor of social sciences at California State Polytechnic University.
