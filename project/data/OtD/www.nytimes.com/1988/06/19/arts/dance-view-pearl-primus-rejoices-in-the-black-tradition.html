DANCE VIEW; Pearl Primus Rejoices in the Black Tradition

By Anna Kisselgoff
Published: June 19, 1988

''MY CAREER HAS BEEN A quest, a search for roots,'' Pearl Primus, the black dance pioneer, has written. It is not a statement of belief one would necessarily find among others in the dance world. The special contribution that black choreographers and dancers have made to modern dance receives both honor and scrutiny this week at the American Dance Festival in a program entitled ''The Black Tradition in American Modern Dance.'' The three-year project aims at reviving ''lost'' or little-performed works and recording them on videotape, while also fostering scholarship through seminars and the publication of a collection of essays.

The initial phase will culminate Thursday through Saturday when the festival, based at Duke University in Durham, N.C., presents the first seven dances that have been re-staged, under the choreographers' direction, for dancers from four different companies. Next Sunday at Duke, the festival will sponsor the last of the discussions that have taken place since June 15 at various colleges in North Carolina.

The subject of that panel is the past, present and future of ''The Black Presence in American Modern Dance,'' and nobody in this series would seem to embody that presence so symbolically as Pearl Primus.

Consistently ranked with Katherine Dunham for her pioneering work during the 1940's in developing a consciousness of the black heritage in American dance, Miss Primus retains the distinct profile that first catapulted her to fame through her own powerful and emotional dancing.

Three of her celebrated early solos from 1943, all social-protest works, will be danced in ''The Black Tradition'' programs: ''The Negro Speaks of Rivers,'' ''Strange Fruit'' and ''Hard Times Blues.'' Because Miss Primus was the first, after Asadata Dafora and his company, to delve deeply into African culture and its dances, her work is identified today mainly with African traditions.

Yet, by her own account she was very much a product of early modern dance that grew out of the 1930's in the United States: trained mainly at the New Dance Group but also studying with Martha Graham, Doris Humphrey and Hanya Holm. Graham dancers like Sophie Maslow and Jane Dudley were her mentors at the New Dance Group. Like them, she was a concert dancer, eschewing the revue format favored by Katherine Dunham. One of the points made in essays in the festival's ''Humanities'' series (directed by Gerald E. Myers and Stephanie Reinhart, funded by the Ford Foundation and the National Endowment for the Humanities) is that choreographers who draw upon the black experience should be defined as creative artists within American modern dance, not as exponents of an ethnic form. (Other choreographers on the festival program are Donald McKayle, Eleo Promare and Talley Beatty.) It is against this background that Miss Primus's work must be viewed. She absorbed what are now mainstream modern-dance techniques from her teachers. Speaking of this training, she said the other day, ''My insides thoroughly enjoyed it.''

This is the quintessential Primus. The 68-year-old dancer, choreographer and Ph.D. in anthropology (from New York University) is much honored (the latest honorary doctorate was from Spelman College last month). In an interview from Amherst College, where she is a Five-College professor, she sounded as wise, authoritative and exuberant as ever.

Born in Trinidad, where her African grandfather was a member of the Ashanti people, she was raised in New York City and graduated from Hunter College. When she began doing African dances in the 1940's, she encountered resistance from black audiences who, she said, ''had been taught,'' to be ashamed of their heritage as ''primitive.'' Today, the tables are turned. African-inspired dance groups (and very few are authentic in Miss Primus's view) are spread across the nation, but now, she said, ''I am not aware of too many social protest dances.''

Hence, when she has rehearsed Kim Y. Bears and Warren P. Miller Jr., the two dancers from the Philadanco troupe who will perform her solos at the festival, she has felt compelled, as Miss Primus put it, ''to bring to them some of the social environment in which these dances were created. It is not just a matter of lifting a leg, it's the why.''

''Strange Fruit,'' for instance, is concerned with ''a deep psychological thing, and I came out of that period of dance,'' she added. Based on the famous poem by Lewis Allen, a white author, the solo does not contain his image of ''black bodies'' hanging from the trees. Instead, Miss Primus took the daring step of identifying herself with a white woman in a lynch mob. The solo is danced by this woman.

''The dance begins as the last person begins to leave the lynching ground,'' Miss Primus said, ''and the horror of what she has seen grips her, and she has do a smooth, fast roll away from that burning flesh. The hurt and anger that hurled me to the ground in that solo were translated into an anger that took me into the air in 'Hard Times Blues.' ''
