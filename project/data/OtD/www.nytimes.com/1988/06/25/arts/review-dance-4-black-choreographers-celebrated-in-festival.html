Review/Dance; 4 Black Choreographers Celebrated in Festival

By JENNIFER DUNNING, Special to the New York Times
Published: June 25, 1988

The culminating event in the first year of ''The Black Tradition in American Modern Dance,'' a three-year project by the American Dance Festival, took place on Thursday night in Page Auditorium at Duke University. Dances by four black American choreographers were performed by four companies chosen and overseen by each choreographer.

''Congo Tango Palace,'' an excerpt from Talley Beatty's ''Come and Get the Beauty of It Hot,'' is a powerful and sleek evocation of life lived at an urgent run. The setting of the piece, performed to music by Miles Davis, is a dance hall through which life surges in diagonal runs and spinning turns, in jazz dance that is cool and hot. Mr. Beatty has stylized the specific here. And the last moments of the work epitomize the duality of the choreography and the lives, as a double line of dancers stretches across the stage. The women are taut columns, the men lithe matadors who disappear, leaving the women stretched up in a mute cry into the night. ''Congo Tango Palace'' was danced, with punch but little crispness, by the Joel Hall Dancers.

Eleo Pomare's 1967 ''Desenamoradas'' was inspired by ''The House of Bernarda Alba,'' a play by Federico Garcia Lorca that examines the frustrations of five sisters confined to their tyrannical mother's manless, soul-killing household. And the Dayton Contemporary Dance Company captured all the coiled and seething, repressed violence of Mr. Pomare's powerful physicalized theater.

''Las Desenamoradas,'' performed to music by John Coltrane, is both the most economical and psychically cluttered of dances. There seems to be no room for the dancers to move in, yet there is no stopping those terrible bodies until a last break for freedom into almost lyrical death.

Debbie L'Blunden was terrifying as the mother and Shonna Yvette Hickman's young love had the bruised fragrance of a gardenia, in a standout cast that also included Dawn Wood, Sherri Williams, Karen Moon, Debra Walton, Eric J. Miles, Blaine Evans, Michael L. Groomes and Calvin Norris Yong.

Another program highlight was Donald McKayle's ''Games,'' strongly performed by the Chuck Davis African-American Dance Ensemble. ''Games,'' too, is economical, but it is filled with detail. Set to children's game chants and innovatively incorporating elements of games, the 1951 piece seems at first simply a charming and potent evocation of urban children's street life and of their fluidly imaginative play.

Almost imperceptibly, however, Mr. McKayle establishes the fear and danger underlying these games. In the end, a little girl is killed and a little boy begins to fathom his terrible powerlessness. Brenda Hayes was unforgettable as the victim, a spunky bundle of elbows and knees with a twanging sing-song to her voice and mischief in her heart. Sherone Price was as searing as the tough little boy with a newly acquired knife that will not save him or his friends. L. D. Burris was a poignantly vulnerable child in search of himself, and Ava McFarlane was the big tough girl revealed as an imp and a baby.

Robert and Gloria Bailey were the fine singers, sensitively weaving their presences through the piece from their tenement window. The superb cast also included Thaddeus Bennett, Toni K. Hall and James K. Green 3d.

These dances by Mr. Beatty, Mr. Pomare and Mr. McKayle are American classics. The three solos by Pearl Primus and the dance by Mr. McKayle that completed the program are also classics, but they looked a little lifeless as performed here. A hard act to follow, Miss Primus narrated the solos with a characteristic mix of powerful dignity and passion. There was nothing wrong with the dancing of the solos created for her, but the performers may need to develop a little less purely intellectual involvement with the dances.

One did get a sense of the huge world incorporated in the small worlds of Miss Primus's 1943 ''The Negro Speaks of Rivers'' and the poem by Langston Hughes that inspired it. The solo, danced by Kim Y. Bears to music by Sarah Malament, is filled with eddying surges, skipping and hints of desperate supplication that do bring to mind a powerful, sometimes playful river and the artifacts of civilization created beside it at great human cost.

The striking premise of Miss Primus's 1943 ''Strange Fruit'' is that the soloist is a white woman who has just witnessed a lynching and drawn back, in horror. Miss Bears did create a sense of fear in her small-stepped, urgent runs and falls, but there is little sense of horror here, even in the sudden last gesture of revulsion. And one needed Miss Primus's introductory remarks about protest to fathom that ''Hard Times Blues,'' a 1943 solo set to a song sung by Josh White, was a protest against sharecropping and not a springing, almost exuberantly scatter-footed celebration of the life force. The solo was strongly danced by Warren T. Miller.

Mr. McKayle draws in gestures from daily life - the work of a chain gang - in ''Rainbow 'Round My Shoulder'' as imaginatively and clearly as he does in ''Games.'' There are similar moments of transcendent emotion, particularly in dances for the woman the prisoners dream of. But the Dayton company presented the work simply as dance or choreography, in a peformance of remarkable emotional restraint.

The project, supported by the Ford Foundation and the National Endowment for the Humanities, includes seminars on dance by black choreographers, archival recordings of their works and the publication of ''The Black Tradition in American Modern Dance,'' an illuminating - and disturbing - collection of essays on the history, nature and contributions of black dance. It is available through the American Dance Festival.
