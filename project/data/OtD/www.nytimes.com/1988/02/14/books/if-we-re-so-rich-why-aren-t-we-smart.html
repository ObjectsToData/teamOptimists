IF WE'RE SO RICH, WHY AREN'T WE SMART?

Andrew Hacker teaches political science at Queens College. He is currently writing a book on race and class in America.
Published: February 14, 1988

MONEY AND CLASS IN AMERICA Notes and Observations on Our Civil Religion. By Lewis H. Lapham. 244 pp. New York: Weidenfeld & Nicolson. $18.95.

Not only are the rich getting richer: more Americans are taking home more money than at any time in our history. At last count, according to the Internal Revenue Service, some 58,419 taxpayers declared incomes exceeding $500,000, compared with 4,560 a dozen years ago. After accounting for inflation, households banking over $1 million per year have expanded fourfold. Also using constant dollars, the Census reports that even the $75,000 bracket has come close to doubling since 1975. Shouldn't this be cause for rejoicing? After all, widespread affluence has always been America's dream. Lewis Lapham thinks not. In ''Money and Class in America,'' he argues that most of us get and spend too much for our own and the nation's good. The book, which he calls ''a speculative essay,'' is acerbic in tone; hyperbolic when not hit-and-run; withal, an amusing and provocative tract.

As Mr. Lapham sees it, all those bloated ''salaries reflect neither individual merit nor the workings of a market.'' Here I tend to agree. Not so long ago, I visited a company that had just given its top executives some very ample bonuses. I asked the chairman if all were star performers who might otherwise be lured away. He granted that while only one or two deserved that designation, he had included the rest so as ''not to hurt their feelings.'' Moreover, as Mr. Lapham notes, this ''widespread affluence is something new,'' caused largely by the upsurge in professional and managerial positions, where compensation has a tenuous connection with value added or productivity.

Mr. Lapham's chief targets are the very rich, who fall into several categories. First are heirs of large family fortunes, ranging from old-money scions to second-generation Texans. Typically, they abuse their children, driving them to ''alcoholism, suicide, drug addiction, insanity and despair.'' Next come self-made millionaires, a group that also includes athletes and entertainers. In Mr. Lapham's view, people who amass new wealth ''find it difficult to avoid becoming criminals -if not in practice, then in the attitude of contempt with which they habitually regard their fellow human beings.'' Last are corporation chairmen and presidents, who award themselves seven-figure salaries and bonuses at the apex of their careers. Mr. Lapham dismisses them as ''boyish'' bureaucrats, ''not clever enough to steal anything important.''

Much of the material for ''Money and Class'' derives from personal observation. The author, a long-time editor of Harper's magazine, attended Hotchkiss and the Yale-within-Yale of secret societies. He also seems to spend a fair dollop of his time dining and otherwise consorting with the very rich. Hence we are quoted remarks overheard at exclusive clubs or during ''an expensive dinner party in Dallas.'' Upwards of half his anecdotes are sited in the Hamptons, including one about a woman who raced down from her veranda, bent on liberating the mackerel some fishermen were hauling in. Ecology has become a theory of the leisure class.

I should make clear that all this rumbling about the rich does not flow from the left. On the contrary, Mr. Lapham takes the aristocratic view that wealth must justify itself by sustaining ''the architecture of civilization.'' Or, as an earlier observer, Alexis de Tocqueville, put it somewhat more elegantly, ''to refine the habits, embellish the manners, and cultivate the arts.'' In fact, ''Money and Class'' is essentially an update of Tocqueville's ''Democracy in America,'' which Mr. Lapham quotes approvingly. He echoes the comment Tocqueville made a century and a half ago, that ''though there are rich men, the class of rich men does not exist: for these rich individuals have no feelings or purposes, no traditions or hopes in common.'' Even if they occasionally endow a museum or underwrite the ballet, few holders of personal and corporate wealth can claim a historic and philosophical vision.

Thus ''the glorious name of profit'' has become our ''civil religion.'' Lacking the guidance of an aristocratic class, we have created a landscape where ''all the hotels and office buildings and suburbs look the same'' and a culture that ''cares as much about the humanities as it cares about the color of the rain in Tashkent.'' Or was it this way from the start? For, as Mr. Lapham sees it, ''the gentlemen who wrote the Constitution in the summer of 1787 had it in mind to make their world safe for commerce.'' And they meant that ambience to suffuse the society.

So Mr. Lapham's quarrel is not just with the low sights of the very rich but with democracy itself. Hence his lament that ''no matter what their income, a depressing number of Americans believe that if only they had twice as much, they would inherit the estate of happiness.'' Surely this is understandable. Currently, the Census tells us, the typical American family makes $29,460. Are they so wrong to aspire to $58,920? Money brings comfort and convenience, freedom and security, respect and recognition, sometimes even happiness. It also serves as a scorecard to tell how well we are doing. In accumulation and consumption, America still leads the world, even if we are living beyond our means. At the same time, it should be acknowledged that our purchases probably show more taste than in earlier eras. Indeed, we are less materialistic, spending more on experiences - like opera tickets and dining out - than physical possessions. It is hard to imagine a pleasant Sunday afternoon that doesn't end up with some kind of cash outlay. Of course, most of us would like to feel there can be more to life than getting and spending. The problem is that we aren't quite sure what the alternatives are.
