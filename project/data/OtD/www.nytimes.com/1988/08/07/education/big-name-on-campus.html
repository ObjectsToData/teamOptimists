Big Name on Campus

By Lisa W. Foderaro; Lisa W. Foderaro is a reporter at The New York Times's Westchester bureau.
Published: August 7, 1988

THEIR NAMES leap off the pages of course catalogues. Registering for their classes inspires the competitive, camping-out-on-the-ticket-line spirit usually reserved for a Bruce Springsteen concert.

They are the celebrity professors, and increasingly they are bringing a touch of Hollywood, the White House and Wall Street to college campuses, shining a Klieg light of real-world experience on the ivory tower. Most of them do not possess Ph.D.'s; some have never stood at a classroom lectern. But what they lack in degrees, they make up for in experience.

Last year at Columbia University, for instance, the playwright and director David Mamet taught directing to graduate students in the film division, while Asher B. Edelman, the corporate raider, taught the art of takeovers at the business school. This coming academic year, Paul Volcker is sure to fill the classroom at Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School of Public and International Affairs; Garret FitzGerald, the former Prime Minister of Ireland, will preside over the Irish Studies Program at Boston College, and Jimmy Carter will continue his interdisciplinary performance at Emory University in Atlanta. What Names Can Do for a College

While some educators question the commitment to teaching and extent of knowledge in formal disciplines possessed by these celebrity instructors, others feel that those who have reached the top echelons of their fields serve three helpful purposes on college campuses: enhancing the institution's image, electrifying the campus and inspiring students.

''It would probably be a mistake if the whole curriculum were taught by stars,'' said the Rev. William B. Neenan, academic vice president of Boston College. ''But as a complement to the regular course offerings, they are marvelous.''

Even some celebrity professors who have put in long years toiling in the groves of academe applaud the addition of accomplished outsiders on the faculty. Carl Sagan, the professor of astronomy and space science at Cornell University, who has been teaching for 20 years, said: ''It's desirable to have people of many different backgrounds at a university. This idea of an elite group, which can be reached only by passing a specific set of degrees, exams and tests, is a little narrow. By all means such people should be brought in when we're trying to teach about the depth and breadth of the real world.'' Mr. Sagan's 13-part television series, ''Cosmos,'' was first aired in 1980 and has since reached 300 million viewers in 60 countries.

Celebrity instructors can also roil the academic waters. One example of such a storm can be found in Mr. Edelman's course, ''Corporate Raiding: The Art of War,'' offered last fall at Columbia University's School of Business. For the final exam, the 48-year-old entrepreneur asked students to pinpoint a company worth acquiring and explain why. But controversy erupted over a $100,000 finder's fee offered by Mr. Edelman to the student whose analysis prompted him to take over the corporation. The university forced him to rescind the offer, and the dean of the business school made a special trip to the class of 15 students to explain the decision.

Mr. Edelman, who taught the same course - without offering the finder's fee - the following semester, is still bitter about the episode. ''Columbia agreed to the finder's fee five weeks before I started the course and reneged on it five weeks into it,'' he said in a recent interview.

The story attracted immense media attention, and, whether good or bad, Columbia received publicity from the affair, which is arguably better than none. Indeed, officials at other universities are candid about the public-relations benefits that can be garnered, not necessarily from controversy in their halls, but from celebrity. ''Every university prizes its Nobel laureates, its Pulitzer Prize winners and its public personalities,'' said Frank H. T. Rhodes, president of Cornell University.

There are universities that scoff at the notion of jazzing up a curriculum with household names. Their faculties may be laden with Nobel laureates, but not in headline-grabbing fields - molecular biology, say, as opposed to world peace. Many first-class schools, too, possess professors with towering reputations in their particular academic areas but whose books are not destined for the best-seller list.

Stanford University is such an institution. ''We look for individuals whose names don't necessarily scream at you, people who are not media heroes,'' said Bob Beyers, a spokesman for the university. ''What matters to us is how much they've kept up on the edge of knowledge and how well they teach.''

This is precisely what worries some of the colleagues of famous instructors. Noncelebrities on the faculty feel that a strong base of knowledge is sometimes lacking in these newcomers, and dangerously so. David Lewin, a professor in Columbia's Business School, who openly objected to Mr. Edelman's style and questioned his contribution to the students, criticized the trend in general.
