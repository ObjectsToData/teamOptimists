WHAT'S DOING IN:; ASPEN

By LAURA BISHOP; LAURA BISHOP is on the staff of The New York Times bureau in Denver.
Published: August 7, 1988

In summer Aspen's intellectual aspirations are as lofty as the surrounding Rockies. At this time of year the town's reputation as a glitzy ski resort is temporarily eclipsed by its image as a cultural center.

The music and art lovers who flock here are following a long tradition. Intellectual stimulation was part of the original plan for Aspen; Walter Paepcke, one of the resort's founders, organized a Goethe Bicentennial Celebration as early as 1949. That event provided the foundation for the Aspen Music Festival, which continues to draw visitors today.

In contrast to Aspen's frenzied winter pace, the town slows down somewhat in summer and fall and takes on an informal tone. Outdoor cafes are popular gathering places, and during the Music Festival, students give free performances on street corners.

Recent restorations of vintage homes, hotels and the Wheeler Opera House point up the Victorian charm that lends yet another dimension to the resort. The architecture dates to the 1880's, when Aspen was a booming mining town. A lot of the new homes echo the original two-story Victorian designs, with pointed roofs and scrolled woodwork.

Not all the resort's pleasures are cerebral. There are plenty of opportunities for hiking, river rafting, horseback riding and jeep trips into the mountains. Early fall brings crisp days and the painting of designs on the mountains by the changing colors of the aspen trees. Peak foliage generally starts in mid-September.

Aspen's upscale shopping and fine dining come at a cost. Be prepared for steep prices. The Arts

As part of the Aspen Music Festival, which continues through Aug. 22, an Aspen Jazz Ensemble concert on Aug. 13 will feature the trumpeter Vincent DiMartino. The pianist Toshiko Akiyoshi will perform Aug. 20. Both concerts are at 8:30 P.M. in the Aspen Music Tent (Third and Gillespie Streets) and cost $15. A jazz benefit for the Aspen Music School Scholarship Fund will be held Aug. 22 at 8:30 P.M. in the Tent. Tickets cost $30 to $150.

Festival concerts are also held at the Paepcke Auditorium (behind the Tent) and the Wheeler Opera House (320 East Hyman). Tickets are $8 to $22. The flutist James Galway performs tomorrow through Wednesday and the pianist Misha Dichter tomorrow and Tuesday. Performance times vary.

The Wheeler Opera House will be the setting tomorrow night for the last opera of the season, Verdi's ''Falstaff,'' performed by the Aspen Opera Theater Center. The Aspen Resort Association's Visitors Center is also situated in the opera house, which is celebrating its centennial next year.

The box office at the Tent is open an hour prior to each performance. For more information call 303-925-3258. The Festival also has a box office in Snowmass, 10 miles from Aspen. Situated on the Village Mall, it is open 9 A.M. to 5 P.M. daily; call 303-923-5400.

The concert information line number is 303-925-9042. Tickets may be ordered by phone with a Visa, MasterCard, or American Express. There is a service charge of $3 an order.

The Snowmass/Aspen Repertory Theater (105 Village Square off Snowmass Mall; 303-923-3773) presents ''I'm Not Rappaport,'' ''The Nerd'' and ''Tom Jones'' through Aug. 25. Performances are Tuesday through Sunday at 8 P.M. Admission is $18. There are also some matinees, at $10, and children's matinees, $5.

The Ballet/Aspen Dance Festival, which ends on Saturday, features the David Parsons Company Wednesday through Saturday at Aspen High School off Maroon Creek Road at 8 P.M. Tickets are $4, $10 and $24. For more information call 303-925-2750.

The Aspen Art Museum (590 North Mill Street; 303-925-8050) is sponsoring its second biennial exhibit of outdoor sculpture in a variety of sites through Sept. 25. Works may be seen on the grounds of the museum, near the Music Festival Tent and at the Music School Campus on Castle Creek Road.

''Latitudes: Focus on Chicago'' will include painting and sculpture by artists from the Chicago area through Sept. 11. Also through Sept. 11 is a show of work by artists who have been awarded fellowships by the Colorado Council on the Arts and Humanities. The next exhibit, Sept. 22 to Oct. 30, is ''Images in Stone: Two Centuries of Artists' Lithographs,'' organized by the Blaffer Gallery of the University of Houston, which will display 150 works by Cezanne, Degas, Goya, Matisse, Picasso and others. The museum is open noon to 6 P.M. Tuesday through Sunday, and until 8 P.M. on Thursday. Admission is $2.

The tenth annual Aspen Filmfest will be held Oct. 5 to 9. Most showings will be at the Isis Theater, 406 East Hopkins Street, and at the Wheeler Opera House. The focus of the event this year will be independent films. Tickets are $6; call 303-925-6882. The Outdoors

The snow-capped Maroon Bells, rising to an elevation of 14,014 feet, are among the most photographed peaks in Colorado. Against this backdrop sits beautiful Maroon Lake. The area is noted for its wildflowers and its varying moods in different seasons.

A bus to the Maroon Bells leaves twice an hour from the Aspen Highlands parking lot 9 A.M. to 5 P.M. through Sept. 5. Tickets purchased in the parking lot cost $2.50. You may drive to the Maroon Bells (11 miles southwest of Aspen at the end of Maroon Creek Road) before 8:30 A.M. and after 5 P.M. For more information call 303-925-8484.
