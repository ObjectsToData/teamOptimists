Collegeville Journal; Will Success Spoil a Spiritual Haven?

By ANNE M. MATTHEWS, Special to the New York Times
Published: August 18, 1988

For more than a century, St. John's Abbey has been known for its remote north woods peace. But this summer it is better known for a chronic shortage of parking.

In the second week of August, 1,370 visitors checked in or out of guest quarters at St. John's Abbey, the largest of North America's Benedictine communities. Hundreds more roamed the monastery's forested grounds daily or swam and sailed on Lake Sagatagan in the shadow of the abbey's glass and concrete church.

''This place used to be dead in summer,'' said the Rev. Wilfrid Theisen, a St. John's University physics instructor, as he watched a dozen cars fruitlessly circle the packed main parking lot. ''Now, it's like a Hyatt.''

True to the abbey's 132-year history of embracing innovation, the 250 monks at St. John's have gone into the special-events business. From May 25 to Aug. 30, nearly 10,500 visitors, up from 7,000 two years ago, will have been housed and fed at the monastery or in the dormitories of its surrounding 2,400-acre St. John's University campus, 70 miles northwest of Minneapolis.

By the time summer is over 94 groups will have met at the abbey. The meetings include family reunions and a basketball camp, and the groups range from Narcotics Anonymous to the National Endowment for the Humanities and the National Conference of Catholic Bishops.

''Bringing in groups makes sense in terms of our role as a major local employer,'' said the Rev. James Tingerthal, the special events director at St. John's. ''We grossed $550,000 in 1987, with most flowing back into a depressed rural economy. But it's also a widely effective pairing of traditional Benedictine hospitality and modern outreach.''

St. John's was founded by Bavarian monks in 1856 to serve the German settlers then filling the upper Midwest, and it has long served the Roman Catholic Church as a testing ground for change. Its early 20th century liturgical reform movement was a major impetus for Vatican II.

The decision to bring the world to the abbey, and vice versa, is partly financial. St. John's endowment now stands at $30 million, up from $2 million in the 1960's.

But a deeper issue is the direction of Benedictine life for the 21st century. The order credits its survival over nearly 1,500 years to careful interpretation of St. Benedict's Rule, a slim volume of management advice describing a balanced existence of work and prayer, and commanding welcome for all strangers.

St. Benedict, however, offered few concrete suggestions for an abbey with its own exit on an interstate highway. Deciding where up-to-date hospitality becomes over-aggressive marketing is stirring a debate here.

A few issues do transcend the centuries. Almost daily in the summertime, travelers turn off Interstate 94 and ask for monastery lodging, in good medieval fashion, which St. John's provides. Dormitory rooms cost $11 a person; breakfast is $3.25.

But what of the recent 20-minute promotional video for St. John's University, criticized by some monks as ''too Hollywood,'' with its dramatic helicopter shots of the abbey and the campus? Many here contend that a few more such promotional moves will turn St. John's into a monastic theme park, endangering its basic function as spiritual sanctuary.

Special events and conferences are not the abbey's only attractions. Inquiries about retreats, or brief private stays, are up sharply as well, so much so that the Rev. Jerome Theisen, St. John's abbot since 1979, wants more year-round guest quarters.

''Retreatants actually live with our community, which is unusual elsewhere,'' the 58-year-old abbot said. ''We get people from all states and countries, Buddhists and Lutherans, women and men. Many simply crave time to walk, read and reflect.''

For modern monks, though, the prospects of a life of contemplation in the north woods are dimming.

The lay visitor heading to St. John's for a tranquil timeout may meet many of its monks only at the airport. More than one-third of St. John's members now work outside the abbey walls, learning to pray in a jetliner at 35,000 feet.

''The world is moving a lot faster,'' Abbot Jerome said. ''Well, so are we.''

At the special events office, the phones continue to ring. ''Seven hundred Mormon youths want to come,'' said Father Tingerthal, scanning the latest printouts. ''And the financial planners are booking through 1992. So, Nancy, where do we put them?'' Nancy Delles, his 21-year-old student worker supervisor, laughs.

''My Peace Corps interview is next week,'' she says. ''With luck, I'm heading to Africa, maybe Asia, for a quiet life.''
