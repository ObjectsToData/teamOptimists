Fairness Was Issue in Ending Public Library's Special Status


Published: July 13, 1988

To the Editor:

''Baffling Blow to the Library'' (editorial, June 25) asks: ''Who will help in the unglamorous but ever more costly tasks of cataloguing or preserving crumbling books?'' The answer is the National Endowment for the Humanities. Every year, millions of dollars of Federal funds are awarded by the endowment's preservation program to institutions across the country for exactly these important purposes; and Lynne V. Cheney, the endowment's chairman, has encouraged the New York Public Library to apply for these grants.

True, institutions must compete for preservation funds, and the National Endowment for the Humanities receives more applications than it can fund. But any objective observer would expect the New York Public Library, with its record of excellence, to do very well.

You should not be baffled by the recent vote of the National Council on the Humanities regarding the library. The council - appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate to advise the chairman on all awards made by the endowment - raised questions three years ago about continuing special treatment for the New York Public Library. Large grants -nearly $25 million over 16 years -awarded to the library have been anomalous in two major respects: they were awarded without competition, and they provided for general operating support, rather than funds for specific projects or endowment.

The National Endowment for the Humanities had, since 1972, been willing to give the library special treatment because its perilous financial condition threatened the survival of a precious national institution. Under Vartan Gregorian's able leadership, however, the library has been transformed into a viable, fast-growing institution, well able to succeed without special treatment. Indeed, in fiscal year 1988 the library will receive almost $1 million through regular National Endowment for the Humanities grant categories.

The question before the council came down to this: Was it fair for any one institution, no matter how worthy, to be exempt from the usual competition and receive preferential treatment - not to speak of one-seventieth of the endowment's annual budget - especially when there were no longer compelling reasons for doing so? The National Council on the Humanities voted overwhelmingly that it was not. We decided that the New York Public Library henceforth must apply for endowment funding through regular grant categories. Any other determination would have violated the principle of fairness - to all institutions in New York and throughout the country.

Surely, the principle of fairness has to be paramount when public dollars are at stake. LEON R. KASS, Vice Chairman National Council on the Humanities Chicago, June 30, 1988
