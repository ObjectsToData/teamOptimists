Constitution: Looking Back and Ahead

By LISA W. FODERARO
Published: July 27, 1988

There were none of the vitriolic outbursts that marked the first constitutional convention held here 200 years ago. Nor was the question at hand quite as awesome as the one then: whether to ratify the United States Constitution.

But the spirit of the 187 New Yorkers gathered here today would have been reassuring to the Federalists who won the day on July 26, 1788, when New York State ratified the Constitution by a 30-to-27 vote. For these New Yorkers, ranging in age from 12 to 80, commemorated that historic vote with a convention of their own, dedicated to the thorny constitutional issues of their day.

The 187 delegates, who were chosen through an essay contest or appointed by their local elected officials, came to this onetime state capital, where the Constitution was ratified, to engage in self-government. They were, for the most part, common folk - high-school students, retired people, lawyers, elected officials, teachers, carpenters and correction officers. 'A Creative Exercise'

They used the ''Critical Choices'' convention, sponsored by the New York State Bicentennial Commission, to examine the constitutional areas of right to privacy, freedom of expression, equal rights and economic opportunity, governance and local home rule, and criminal justice. And they drafted, debated and voted on 60 resolutions within those categories, from AIDS testing and housing for the homeless to abortion, gun control and censorship of high school newspapers.

''It's a creative exercise,'' said Carlos Hortas, a delegate from Manhattan and the dean of humanities and the arts at Hunter College. ''I think a lot of the people here who had been blase about government might leave with a renewed sense of activism and political involvement.''

Daniel Abbis, a 15-year-old from Rome, N.Y., who was selected because of his essay on homelessness, said: ''This might be the beginning of something big for me. I like politics. I don't think government gets the respect it deserves. I don't think I'll be the President or a senator. Probably a mayor.''

''We were chosen instead of firecrackers for the celebration,'' said Giles Erickson, a delegate from Rochester and a self-described teacher, carpenter and playwright. ''But we are not as easy as firecrackers. We want to talk. I just wish we had more time to debate the resolutions. Another three or four weeks, at least.''

''I was very impressed by the caliber of intelligence,'' said Sol Wachtler, the Chief Judge of New York's highest court, the State Court of Appeals, who as the Bicentennial Commission chairman for New York chose reflection over revelry. ''There's no way you can focus in on this very interesting time in our history simply by watching a parade.'' A Few Volumes

Judge Wachtler, one of whose predecessors as the state's chief judge was the Federalist John Jay himself, indicated that there was a good chance that some resolutions, which will be submitted to legislative leaders, Governor Cuomo and the two major candidates for President on Sept. 16, Citizenship Day, could become law.

If the weighty debates were courtesy of the state, the city of Poughkeepsie provided the pageantry. There was a parade on Saturday, fireworks on Sunday, a laser-light extravaganza on the facade of the post office Monday night. And there was a fitting performance for budding delegates: the political satirist Mark Russell, with his trademark star-spangled piano, lobbed one-liners about the United States system and its caretakers to a howling audience. 'Devotion' to Constitution

This morning, after a plenary session of all the delegates, each one signed a parchment to reaffirm ''their support for and devotion to'' the Constitution.

Shortly before noon, in the Dutchess County Courthouse, Governor Cuomo extolled the United States Constitution ''as a vision of justice and reason for the world'' and defended the 65 delegates to the original ratifying convention. Indeed, it took six weeks of debate before a majority approved the Constitution, partly because it did not contain the provisions that a few years later would form the Bill of Rights.

The scope and tone of the ratifying convention was more sweeping and dramatic than the one that concluded today. At one point, history has it, a reticent delegate named Thomas Treadwell, an anti-Federalist from Long Island, jumped from his chair and proclaimed to a Federalist: ''You, sir, are driving a dagger into the bosom of liberty.'' Steady and Reasoned

At this convention, the talk was steady and reasoned. What emotion existed came mostly from young adults. A recent high school graduate railed against a resolution to censor school-financed student publications. A man in his 20's, citing the Sedition and Espionage Acts, rebuked a man who had argued that a resolution guaranteeing the right to nonviolent dissent was unnecessary since it was already covered by the First Amendment to the Constitution.

And a 15-year-old from Harlem, TaShawn Hinton, a sophomore at the Charles Evans Hughes High School for the Humanities in the Chelsea section, forcefully persuaded her fellow delegates to adopt a resolution stating that ''equal-quality education that enables children and adults to lead lives as productive citizens is an inalienable right of all persons.''

''This convention gave me a sense of pride,'' said Miss Hinton, who sits on the youth advisory committee in the office of the Manhattan Borough President, David N. Dinkins.

''I never knew I had so many rights,'' she said. ''I'm proud that the youth came out for this. We are the future. In this room, we might have the next President of the United States. Or the next Congresswoman.''

She pointed at herself and smiled a modest smile.
