WESTCHESTER GUIDE


Published: July 10, 1988

FOCUS ON LYNDHURST

Renata Rainer, the Viennese photographer who has lived in the county for more than 20 years, has spent much of the last decade photographing Lyndhurst, the Tarrytown estate that became a property of the National Trust for Historic Preservation in 1961. Fifty of her black-and-white images of the Gothic mansion are on exhibition at the Hudson River Museum in Yonkers through Oct. 30.

Primarily a landscape photographer, Ms. Rainer made an odyssey through Lyndhurst's 67 acres that began with the gardens, greenhouses, lawns and century-old trees, then turned to the buttresses, porches and bay windows of the mansion's exterior, finally penetrating the vaulted rooms inside.

The house was designed by Alexander Jackson Davis in 1838 for William Paulding, Mayor of New York City, who called it Paulding Manor. William Merritt, a wealthy merchant who bought it in 1864, renamed it Lyndhurst. After his death in 1873 the estate was sold to the railroad tycoon Jay Gould, whose daughter Anna, Duchess of Talleyrand-Perigord, bequeathed it to the National Trust.

The exhibition, titled ''Motif: Lyndhurst, Photography by Renata Rainer,'' may be seen from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Wednesdays through Saturdays, noon to 5 P.M. Sundays and Thursdays until 9 P.M. A public reception is scheduled from 3 to 5 P.M. next Sunday. The museum is at 511 Warburton Avenue. Admission is $2 for adults, $1.50 for children and the elderly. Call 963-4550 for more information.

Ms. Rainer, who teaches at Marymount College in Tarrytown and lectures at the State University of New York at Purchase, will give a talk on ''100 Years of Landscaping Photography'' in her capacity as lecturer for the New York Council for the Humanities at 10 A.M. Saturday in the Katonah Gallery, at 28 Bedford Road in Katonah. Admission is free. The gallery phone number is 232-9555. A DAY ON A FARM

A day on a Croton farm is planned by the Teatown Lake Reservation from 10 A.M. to mid-afternoon Saturday. Participants will meet at Teatown and car-pool to Cliffdale Farm, where the farm manager, John Bukovsky, will lead a tour of the gardens, orchards and livestock, including new spring lambs, chickens, horses and guinea hens.

A Teatown naturalist, Todd Baldwin, will then lead a nature walk through wildflowers and specimen plants in the field and among the hedgerows. ''The farm is run as a gentleman's farm by a wealthy owner who does not use it as his primary source of income,'' Mr. Baldwin said. ''They cut their own hay for the horses and manage it as a self-sufficient operation,'' he said. Participants must register in advance and take a picnic lunch. The fee is $3. Call 762-2912 to obtain more information and to register. EARLY DANCING

The Ananda Dancers of New York City will perform and teach a mini-workshop in 18th- and early 19th-century dances at 2 P.M. today on the lawn of the Van Cortlandt Manor in Croton-on-Hudson.

The 10-year-old troupe of early dance specialists led by Bhala Jones will demonstrate ''Graie's Inn Masque;'' ''Argeers,'' which Ms. Jones believes is a corruption of a place name; ''Flowers of Edinburgh,'' and ''The Irish Washerwoman,'' which has endured in its original form through more than two centuries. The titles identify not only the dances but the tunes, to be played on the pipe and tabor by Ben Harms.

''The tabor is a drum played with one hand, and the pipe is a one-handed recorder played with the other hand,'' said Mr. Harms, a classical percussionist. ''It's a sort of one-man-band combination of rhythm and melody that's about 1,000 years old. It is still played in rural parts of England, France and Spain for dancing.''

Ms. Jones will be joined by three adults and two children in her performance. ''The principal difference between early European dancing and Colonial American dancing is the number of people,'' she said. ''In Europe there were very few dances done in large groups before the 18th century. A few individuals or couples danced while everyone else watched. The dancers formed what is called a Longways Set - lined up behind each other facing the Presence; the place where the king or highly placed personage was seated. In America everybody got up and danced together.'' She will teach visitors ''The Ribbon Dance,'' ''La Belle Catherine'' and the ''Virginia Reel,'' among others.

To reach Van Cortlandt Manor take the Croton Point Exit off Route 9 and make two right turns to the entrance. Admission is $5 for adults, $4.50 for the elderly and $3 for students and children 6 and older. The fee includes tours of the manor house. Call 631-8200 for more information.
