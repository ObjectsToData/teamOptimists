Frohnmayer Concedes Arts Agency Faces a Lag in Public Confidence

By WILLIAM H. HONAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: July 31, 1990

John E. Frohnmayer, the chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts, today acknowledged that both Congress and a substantial section of the American people have lost faith in his agency, but he insisted that new legislation or guidelines were not necessary to correct the situation.

Mr. Frohnmayer was testifying before an independent commission established by Congress last fall to review the grant-making procedures of the arts endowment. It was the first time he spoke in public since late June, when his decision to deny grants to four performance artists whose applications had been favored by a peer panel touched off a storm of protest from diverse arts groups.

''One of the problems that we currently face with both Congress and a fairly large segment of the people is that they have lost confidence in our ability to make good judgments,'' Mr. Frohnmayer told the commission during an hour and a half of testimony in a conference room in the old Post Office Building, where the endowment has its headquarters. ''Whether that's justified or not, it is a reality. And we must, in my view, do a far better job of publicizing the positive things that we do to help regenerate that very positive image that ought to be in all the citizens and in Congress when they think of the arts endowment and the things that it does.''

'We Have the Tools Now'

Responding to questions by Peter N. Kyros Jr., a commission member who served as chairman of a White House committee to coordinate arts support during the Carter Administration, Mr. Frohnmayer said he was opposed to any further legislation because ''we have the tools now'' and all problems in the agency could be dealt with by administrative means.

Pressed by Mr. Kyros, Mr. Frohnmayer said:

''There is a kind of statutory language that I believe would be both constitutionally firm and perhaps would give additional guidance, and that is language which says that if public money has been used for something that's obscene, then the arts endowment will get that money back. I think that language is sensible. I also think we can already do that with administrative procedures now in place.''

This statement, a spokesman for the endowment later said, does not represent any change in Mr. Frohnmayer's opposition to restrictions on the agency imposed last fall by a House-Senate conference committee.

The endowment has been under attack by some members of Congress and conservative religious and political organizations for having supported art that they consider obscene or blasphemous.

In his testimony today, Mr. Frohnmayer also indicated that he would increasingly use the chairman's prerogative to block grant recommendations for projects likely to offend the public.

''I think we cannot look strictly at artistic excellence in a vacuum,'' he told the commission, ''but we have to look at it as how it's going to play with the audience that we're charged with serving - which is the people.''

In the written statement Mr. Frohnmayer submitted to the commission, he said that at the next meeting of the National Council on the Arts, which begins Aug. 2, he would return for reconsideration one application that the council had rejected. He did not specify which application and was not questioned on the matter.

Not 'Some Sort of Sacrilege'

Defending his right to overrule peer-panel recommendations, he said in a statement before being questioned, ''It should not be considered some sort of sacrilege when either the chair or the National Council differs with a panel.'' The National Council on the Arts is a 23-member, Presidentially appointed body that advises the chairman.

When asked by John T. Agresto, the president of St. John's College in Santa Fe, N.M., who served as deputy chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities under William Bennett from 1985 to 1989, whether he thought public financing should not be used to support ''ideologically driven or racist art,'' Mr. Frohnmayer replied:

''I think it's real difficult to make a sweeping statement of principle like that. I don't think anybody's for racism, or for promoting it, but one of the real strengths of this country is the breadth that the First Amendment gives us. I simply believe that individual judgment is preferable to written standards.''

Mr. Frohnmayer also told the commission that he did not believe there should be a difference in standards for public and private support of the arts.

The 12-member independent commission, which was created by Congress as part of a compromise to drop the most severe restrictions on how the arts endowment might use Federal money, is also considering whether the standard for publicly supported art should be different from that for privately financed art.

Congress Delays Debate

Last week the House and the Senate postponed until September their floor debates on the future of the endowment. With this delay, attention is now focused on the independent commission appointed by President Bush to examine the endowment's standards and procedures. The commission is expected to complete a report in time for the next Congressional vote. If no sweeping changes are recommended, Congressmen may find it easier politically to vote for keeping the endowment substantially intact.

Ideologically broad, the commission has as its co-chairmen John Brademas, the president of New York University, who served in the House as a Democrat from Indiana from 1959 to 1981, and Leonard Garment, a Washington lawyer who was a special consultant to President Nixon from 1969 to 1974.

Since its first meeting on June 6, the commission has questioned officials of the endowment, arts professionals who have served as members of the endowment's grant-making peer panels and officials of various Federal agencies that use peer panels to make grants and others.
