Pledge for Arts Grants Is Modified

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: July 21, 1990

Recipients of joint grants from the Rockefeller Foundation and the National Endowment for the Arts may modify the pledge they are now required to make, according to instructions mailed to them by the foundation yesterday.

The recipients are required to sign an anti-obscenity proviso and a certificate of compliance. Under the terms of an agreement reached by the foundation and the arts endowment on July 5, they may if they wish add the words ''to the extent that such terms and conditions, and requirement to accept them, are lawful under the Constitution and the laws of the United States.''

A letter accompanying the instructions signed by Alberta Arthurs, director of the arts and humanities program at the Rockefeller Foundation, states the foundation's belief that the current obscenity ban is ''unconstitutional, unfair and dangerous to artists'' but adds, ''We believe the modified language provides a measure of protection for these shared grantees.''

Prof. Kathleen M. Sullivan of the Harvard Law School said: ''It's like saying I'm going to kill you except to the extent that murder is against the law. All the expression of good intent in the world cannot change the fact that the courts will have to decide whether Congress's restrictions as enforced by the N.E.A. are constitutional.''

An 'Impossible Position'

The Rockefeller Foundation indicated exasperation with even the modified pledge. In a statement, Frank Karel, the foundation's vice president for communications, said artists and arts organizations ''are being required to sign a certificate that puts them in the impossible position of guessing how some faceless Federal bureaucrat, at some unknown future point, will judge the content of their art, often art they have yet to create.''

''We believe this is an unconstitutional requirement made worse by new guidelines that are threatening in their specifics and even more menacing where they are vague,'' the statement continued. ''The N.E.A has agreed to a new certificate procedure for our joint grantees that provides them with a measure of protection. We are sending them this news in a special mailing, hoping it will enable the N.E.A., the Rockefeller Foundation, and our joint grantees to continue to work together.''

This year the Rockefeller Foundation will give a total of about $7.5 million in grants to some 400 people working in the arts. That sum is part of a total of $90 million given by the foundation in a variety of fields, notably minority affairs and third-world development.

The Rockefeller Foundation's agreement with the arts endowment, first reported by The Washington Post yesterday, was disclosed in a friend-of-the-court brief filed Thursday in behalf of the New School for Social Research. The New School filed suit in Federal District Court on May 23 challenging the constitutionality of the restriction on arts endowment grants.

The anti-obscenity restriction in the endowment's 1990 appropriations act prohibits the agency from supporting art enterprises that might be considered obscene as defined by the Supreme Court in the 1973 Miller v. California decision. In part, that decision defined as obscene any explicitly sexual work that did not also possess serious literary, artistic, political or scientific merit. Grant recipients have been required to sign a pledge of compliance.

Some Refuse Grants

A number of artists and arts organizations have refused to accept endowment grants as a form of protest, and others have expressed anger about the restriction and the written pledge of compliance. The choreographer Bella Lewitzky has also filed suit challenging the constitutionality of the pledge and has refused an endowment grant.

Congress is expected to debate the issue this month when it considers legislation to extend the life of the endowment.

Asked whether all those receiving arts endowment grants would be able to add the modifying words to the pledges they are required to make, Katherine M. Christie, a spokeswoman for the endowment, said: ''The agreement made with the Rockefeller Foundation applies only to recipients of joint grants from the foundation and the arts endowment. It does not apply to the vast majority of arts endowment grant recipients who receive only public money.''
