Congressional Panel Opposes Legislation To Limit Arts Grants

By BARBARA GAMAREKIAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: September 12, 1990

The commission's report manages to address what opponents of restrictions see as the central issue - insuring artistic freedom - while demanding public accountability for the way tax dollars are spent. While the commission explicitly spurns Federal censorship and language that would legislate what is acceptable art, it suggests that Congress include in arts legislation cautionary language about the responsibility inherent in spending Federal money. The standard for publicly financed art must go beyond the standard for privately financed art, the commission asserts, and entails considerations that go beyond artistic excellence.

The endowment's chairman, John E. Frohnmayer, refused to comment on the report, but he has repeatedly resisted demands that he scrap the pledge.

Less Power for Peer Panels

The commission recommended additional measures that would appreciably weaken the role of the endowment's grant-making peer panels, placing more authority in the hands of the chairman and the National Council of the Arts, the endowment's advisory body. Until now, the peer-panel recommendations have almost always been approved by the council and the chairman.

The commmission was established as part of a compromise worked out by a House and Senate conference committee that rejected a proposal by Senator Jesse Helms, Republican of North Carolina, that strict moral standards be imposed on endowment grants. The action followed months of Congressional controversy over the endowment's support of the photographic works of Robert Mapplethorpe and Andres Serrano, which some legislators called blasphemous and obscene.

In presenting the study, the commission's co-chairmen, John Brademas, president of New York University, and Leonard Garment, a Washington lawyer who was a White House aide in the Nixon Administration, said they hoped it would serve as a catalyst in shaping a consensus in Congress and break the current impasse on Capitol Hill, where the endowment is up for legislative renewal.

''We've tried to produce a set of recommendations that on one hand is respectful of freedom of expression and on the other hand the stewardship of Congress,'' Mr. Brademas said.

The endowment has been under a yearlong attack by some members of Congress and conservative religious and political groups for having supported art they consider obscene. The legislation that established the 25-year-old agency expressly prohibits Government interference in artistic decision-making, but now many legislators are calling for Federal restrictions on the kind of grants the endowment can make. The debate over legislation to extend the life of the agency was postponed until Congress's return this week from summer recess.

Reaction From the Hill

The commission's 94-page report received mixed reviews on Capitol Hill. Representative Pat Williams, Democrat of Montana, who heads the subcommittee that has oversight over the endowment, said that the recommendations ''may very well provide Congress with the building blocks for consensus, but there is no doubt that the issue of obscenity has to be dealt with, not only thoughtfully but also recognizing that many members of the House and Senate have a firm eye on Nov. 6, Election Day.''

His Republican counterpart on the committee, Representative E. Thomas Coleman of Missouri, who has called for increasing the allotment the endowment gives to states from 20 percent to 60 percent, said the commission's report had not changed his thinking. The report recommends maintaining the present formula.

Larry Hart, communications director for Representative Dana Rohrabacher, a California Republican and a leader of forces in the House most closely allied with the endowment's opponents, said that Mr. Rohrabacher found the commission's report predictable.

''His reaction is that he is not surprised by the report because he was aware that when this commission was appointed, it was really designed to provide political cover to fund the N.E.A. without any restrictions,'' Mr. Hart said.

'Sharply Divided Views'

The commission, which was appointed by the White House and the Congressional leadership, represented a broad ideological spectrum. ''There were very sharply divided views and very strong feelings,'' Mr. Garment said. ''We fought over virtually every word and were happy to arrive at a set of unanimous recommendations. But we feel the endowment is an American treasure very much worth preserving.''

The commission, which was mandated last October, was scheduled to report in April, but it did not meet until early June because of delays in the Presidential appointment process. Since then the commission has held six days of public hearings, taking testimony from academics, lawyers and arts advocates.

In addressing the question of obscenity, the commission affirmed that ''freedom of expression is essential to the arts,'' but it pointed out that ''obscenity is not protected speech and that the National Endowment for the Arts is prohibited from funding the production of works which are obscene or otherwise illegal.''

In lieu of restrictive language, the commission suggested a preamble to the endowment's authorizing legislation making it clear that ''the arts and the humanities belong to all the people of the United States,'' but also notes that Federal arts programs should ''be sensitive to the nature of public sponsorship.''

Throughout the report, the commission referred to the endowment's peer panels as ''advisory panels'' and proposed that such panels recommend more grants than it can afford to give, thereby giving the chairman and the council a genuine choice in awarding grants.

Reshaping the Panels

Among other grant-making changes, it called for an expansion of panel membership to people knowledgeable about the arts but not earning their living from them. It also recommended that panelists not be permitted to serve on a panel considering applications from organizations with which they are affiliated.

Some arts advocates expressed unhappiness with the commission's report. ''I think it was hastily done and doesn't address a number of issues,'' said Jeffrey Chester, lobbyist for the National Campaign for Freedom of Expression and the California Confederation of the Arts. ''A critical problem is the dilution of the power of the peer panels by removing the panel's right to name an exact number of awardees and the dollar amount. It allows the chair to cherry-pick over those who might get grants. It creates an arts czar.''

Theresa Elmore Behrendt, Tuxedo Park, N.Y.

Marcia Laing Golden, Goodland, Kans.

Kay Goodwin, Ripley, W.Va.

Peter N. Kyros Jr., Westlake Village, Calif.

Charles K. McWhorter, New York.

Rosalind Wyman, Los Angeles.
