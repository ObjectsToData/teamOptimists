LONG ISLAND GUIDE


Published: September 23, 1990

FALL FESTIVALS

Fall fairs dominate the scene today - and well they should, for it is that time of the year already.

Among the events is the eighth annual Long Island Harvest Festival in Riverhead. Appropriately, it celebrates the abundance of local crops, and it does so with samples and sales of produce, seafood and wines, demonstrations of, and lectures on, pastimes like gardening, beekeeping and horsemanship and musical entertainment.

The festival will be held from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. on the grounds of Suffolk Community College's Eastern Campus. Parking is $3 a car.

At Clark Garden in Albertson from noon to 4 P.M., there will also be sessions on beekeeping, children's activities that include the Long Island Game Farm's Petting Zoo and music from the No Frills Band, but the emphasis will be on gardening, including rose care, flower drying and herb demonstrations. Clark Garden is on I.U. Willets Road. Admission: 621-7568. Everything nautical figures in Captree State Park's ''Captree Carnival'' from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. There will be demonstrations of scuba diving and fish filleting, crab races, a clam-shucking derby, free boat rides, seafaring exhibitions and, of course, seafood noshing. Everything is free, except for what you buy. The Brookhaven Fall Fair is not necessarily seasonal, nor is it today. Rather, this five-day event from Wednesday through next Sunday -it is sponsored by the Committee for the Respect of Old Glory - celebrates the flag with a carnival midway, a petting zoo, a 1950's dance band revue, crafts exhibitions, food and, on Saturday at dusk, ''Red, White and Blue'' fireworks by Grucci.

This all takes place from 6 to 10 P.M. Wednesday and Thursday, 6 P.M. to midnight Friday, noon to midnight Saturday and 2 to 9 P.M. next Sunday at Suffolk Meadows Race Track in Yaphank. It is free, except for what you buy.

MUSIC UNDER THE STARS

While some music fans frequently see stars when they listen to their favorites, everybody who gathers at the Vanderbilt Planetarium's Sky Theater tonight will. Not only stars, but also all kinds of other visual elements.

The occasion is the season's first ''Music Under the Dome'' concert, starting at 7:30 P.M. While the pianist Lawrence Schubert plays ''The Romantic Composers: Brahms, Chopin, Liszt, Scubert and Schumann,'' the cameras and projectors that normally take viewers through the universe will concentrate on appropriate, mind-expanding and music-enhancing visuals.

The planetarium is on Little Neck Road in Centerport. Tickets: 262-7800.

SOVIET CINEMA

With Glasnost having transformed the Russian film industry, material once considered taboo is now permissible. For better or worse, crime, sexuality and political dissent are subjects explored by Soviet filmmakers.

To see how far they have come, the Humanities Institute at the State University of New York at Stony Brook is joining forces with the Greater Port Jefferson Arts Council to present a series of recent films from the Soviet Union.

''Soviet Cinema: A Selection of the Recent Best'' gets under way tomorrow with ''Oblomov,'' Nikita Mikhalkov's prize-winning saga about a man with a reasonable mind who cannot decide whether or not to get out of bed.

The series will also showcase ''Little Vera'' on Oct. 1, ''Scarecrow'' on Oct. 22 and ''My Friend Ivan Lapshin'' on Nov. 5. A lecture, ''Glasnost in Soviet Film,'' will be offered on Oct. 29.

The films, in color and with subtitles, will be shown at 8 P.M. at Theater Three on Main Street, Port Jefferson. Tickets: 928-9100.

VISITING WRITERS

With its Visiting Writers Program, which begins its 11th year Tuesday, the State University of New York at Farmingdale shares some literary wealth with the community at large.

Opening the series at 9:35 A.M. in the Loft Lounge of Roosevelt Hall is Conleth O'Connor, the Irish- born poet, playwright and novelist who recently was elected to Aosdana, the prestigeous Irish society of artists.

Mr. O'Connor, like all the other visitors - they include Joy Harjo, a Creek writer, and the poet-crtic Hayden Carruth - will read from his own works.

The sessions are free. For a complete schedule, call 420-2031.

BANNED BOOKS WEEK

It's that time of the year again - Banned Books Week - and, naturally, instead of banning books, the Merrick Library is among those celebrating such books and one's right to read whatever one so chooses.

In addition to an exhibition, ''Censorship in Schools and Libraries,'' which will be on display through September, the library has scheduled the following two special events:

''The Fabric of Freedom: Voices and Views,'' a one-woman show by Shirley Blanc Romaine based on works and speeches by John Steinbeck, Lillian Hellman, the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and Sojourner Truth.

* ''Freedom of Speech and Censorship,'' a lecture by Burt Neuborne of New York University's School of Law.

The performance is at 1 P.M. Tuesday and the talk at 8 P.M. Wednesday, and both are free. The library is at 2279 Merrick Avenue.

BACK TO THE PAST

For many, the good old days are those in the 1950's and 60's when doo wop and its practitioners dominated the pop music scene. The genre is in the throes of revival these days, thanks in part to Dick Fox, who brings his ''Autumn Night Doo Wop Extravaganza'' into the Westbury Music Fair Saturday.

Sharing the bill, which will be emceed by J. J. Flash, are names and voices from the era, such as the Drifters, Fred Parris and the Five Satins, the Del Vikings, Vito Picone and the Elegants and the Sensations.

The concert is at 7:30 P.M. at the Music Fair, which is on Brush Hollow Road. Tickets: 334-0800. $90BARBARA DELATINER
