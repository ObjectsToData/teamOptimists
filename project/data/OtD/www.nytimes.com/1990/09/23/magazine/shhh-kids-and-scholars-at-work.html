Shhh! Kids and Scholars at Work

By Bruce Weber; Bruce Weber is an editor of this magazine.
Published: September 23, 1990

Margaret Hetley, the head librarian at the Hunts Point branch of the New York Public Library in the South Bronx, is having a typical afternoon. It's quiet, subdued - a couple of men in leather jackets killing time, reading magazines at a corner table; a woman in a nurse's uniform perusing the library's community service guide; a young mother with an infant in a stroller browsing in the neatly kept stacks - until about 3:30 P.M., when the local schoolkids begin filing in.

Hetley, a slight, bespectacled woman in her 40's, wearing jeans and running shoes and the weary, amused, authoritative expression of librarians everywhere, is showing off the branch's well-used collection of 10,000 titles in Spanish - including computer texts and auto-repair manuals as well as volumes of Spanish and Latin American literature and the complete works of Agatha Christie - when the hectic part of her day begins. A pre-teen interrupts: Where are the books on George Washington? Then there's a query from a baffled youngster desperate for what he thinks is a math text. ''That sounds like computers,'' Hetley says. ''You don't know? Honeychild, it's your homework.''

During a brief respite, Hetley takes a visitor to the reading room on the second floor, noting that the roof leaks and, along the way, pointing out on the stairway the building's ''intercom'' system - a coffee can filled with washers, suspended on a rope, that is rattled to call employees to the phone. Upstairs, she stops in front of a computer to chat with the high-school student in charge of teaching younger children how to use it. But her attention is diverted again. ''Don't run,'' she calls out, adding with a sigh: ''This is a recording.''

The scene at Hunts Point is expressive of the mundane yet indispensable social role played by the whole of the New York Public Library system, one of the nation's grand institutions, encompassing four nonlending research libraries and 82 branches spread out over the Bronx, Manhattan and Staten Island. (Brooklyn and Queens have independent library systems.) ''It is entirely a service organization,'' says Andrew Heiskell, for the last decade the chairman of the board of trustees, and its dual mandate - to acquire and to provide - is both the library's strength and the bane of its existence. Its 46 million separate items - books, manuscripts, journals, recordings - constitute the second-largest collection, after the Library of Congress, in the United States. And its usership - from the city's students, for many of whom the branches function as their public-school libraries, to Ph.D. scholars; from lawyers and investment bankers, who take advantage of the library's vast stores of public records from around the world, to the homeless who find solace in its quietude and warmth; from reporters (who invaded the library's map room for street maps of Baghdad and Kuwait) to novelists like E. L. Doctorow and Toni Morrison - is wider and more diverse than that of any other like institution in the world.

But in an era of proliferating information, maintaining its breadth of services and depth of resources is an ever-escalating struggle. The New York Public Library is in much the same predicament as the yeoman-spirited Hetley; for all it has and for all it does, more is always in demand.

This is a particularly pivotal moment in N.Y.P.L. history. Having completed a $307 million fund-raising campaign last December, the culmination of a decade of rejuvenation, the library has rebounded famously from veritable ruin. The campaign provided for 200,000 items, acquired but inaccessible, to be catalogued and shelved. A huge conservation effort, to save crumbling volumes or, in the case of lost causes, to preserve what was in them on microfilm, was initiated. The library's showcase, the central research facility at 42d Street and Fifth Avenue, has been largely restored to its original Beaux Arts splendor. Behind it, underneath Bryant Park, a $20 million expansion will be ready next April - one of four new facilities scheduled to open next year. The resurgence among the city's corporate and private donors of library chic is the legacy, more or less, of Vartan Gregorian, who departed last spring after eight years as president of the institution to become president of Brown University.

But by now, with the exception of $30 million that bumped the library's endowment to $180 million, the campaign money is nearly spent, leaving the place - and its new president, the Rev. Timothy S. Healy - confronting cutbacks in vital city support and a budget that calls for $19 million in private donations for fiscal year 1991. ''We're broke,'' Andrew Heiskell says cheerfully. ''With a little more muscle than we had. We can arm-wrestle a bit with City Hall. But we're broke.''

Timothy Stafford Healy is a native New Yorker, a Jesuit who came to the library after 13 years as president of Georgetown University in Washington. Now president of an institution with more than 3,000 employees and a budget this year of $132 million, he defines his job this way: ''I am the library spokesman, its principal fund-raiser and administrative head. But once you've said that, you've got a whole lot of spaghetti on your plate.''
