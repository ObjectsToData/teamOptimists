Bluff in Dominica Yields Columbus's First Colony

By JOHN NOBLE WILFORD
Published: November 27, 1990

ON a low limestone bluff lapped by turquoise waters, archeologists are scraping away five centuries of decay and covering loam to get at the ruins of the first known European colony established in the Americas, the place Christopher Columbus in 1494 described as a "well-situated rock" and, with extravagant hopes, christened La Isabela.

Archeologists have identified the stone foundations of the settlement's main structures: a large storehouse, watch tower and sentry posts, a powder house, hospital, church and the house of Columbus. The cemetery has yielded skeletons with their hands crossed over their chests in Christian burial.

They have also found the quarry half a mile away where colonists cut stone for the fort, an oven where lead was melted for pottery glaze or molded into lombard shot used in cannons, and a kiln where colonists fired ceramics, the first Old World manufacturing in America. Buried in the mound at the site of Columbus's house were fragments of earthenware plates and bowls he probably ate from, roofing tiles that sheltered him and the rim of what very likely was a chamber pot. Columbus's very own?

Digging at the storehouse site, archeologists accidentally released thousands of evanescent drops of mercury that glittered in sunlight as they floated away. With the look of a triumphant alchemist on his face, Dr. Jose M. Cruxent, a Venezuelan archeologist, cupped a brownish glob of the thickly liquid mercury in his hand and uttered the intoxicating word, "Gold." This was some of the mercury the Spaniards had brought for separating from the mineral matrix the pure gold they had hoped to find in abundance.

For more than three years, Dr. Cruxent has directed archeologists and residents of this isolated fishing village in the first systematic exploration of La Isabela. Excavations are being expanded with the addition of archeology teams from the University of Florida under the leadership of Dr. Kathleen Deagan, curator of anthropology at the Florida Museum of Natural History at Gainesville.

They are trying to reconstruct the outlines of the first Spanish capital in the Indies in time for the 500th anniversary of Columbus's voyage of discovery in 1492. Their ultimate aim is to learn more about how the 1,500 sailors, soldiers and settlers accompanying Columbus on his second expedition in 1493 managed in their strange surroundings at La Isabela and why their colony collapsed and was abandoned in less than five years.

"Isabela is the gem of Columbus archeology," said Dr. Deagan. "It lets us look at how the Spaniards first began to cope with the American environment and began to develop many of their concepts and methods of colonization." Origin of Slave Trade

Here the Spaniards provided the New World with the first intentional introduction of plants and animals, the first masonry houses and the first European canals. Here, too, Columbus's lieutenants led troops across the plain and into the hills to plunder and pillage, ending any hope there might have been for peaceful relations between these reunited branches of humanity. From here, a year later, Columbus loaded 500 Taino Indians into four caravels and shipped them back to Spain, inaugurating the transatlantic slave trade.

Dr. Michael V. Gannon, a historian and director of the Institute for Early Contact Period Studies at the University of Florida, said: "The town provides the first example we have of a sustained contact between Europeans and Native Americans. It was the initial experience, if you will, of a process that changed the political, economic, social and religious world of the 16th century and thereafter."

The excavations at La Isabela highlight the growing role of archeology in the study of Columbus and the period of initial contact between European and American cultures. Except for the rare discovery of a new archive or an insight gleaned from improved translations, historians have learned just about all they can from written documents of the time and are looking to archeology to fill some of the gaps in understanding this crucial period.

Archeologists, for example, are aiding Columbus scholars by searching for physical evidence of exactly where he first landed in the New World, what his ships were like and where and how his men put down their first roots on American soil.

Dr. Charles A. Hoffman, an archeologist at Northern Arizona University, digs each year in the Bahamas at San Salvador Island, searching for confirming evidence that this, and not some other contending island, is where Columbus made his historic landfall on Oct. 12, 1492. He said he had found buried there more Spanish coins, beads, ceramics and a belt buckle from the Columbus period.

"If it wasn't Columbus who was here, it was somebody shortly thereafter," Dr. Hoffman said. "Perhaps Ponce de Leon."

Expeditions from Texas A & M University's Institute of Nautical Archeology and Ships of Discovery, an archeology foundation, are in the field this fall, trying to find and recover sunken remains of Columbus ships in Jamaica and Panama.
