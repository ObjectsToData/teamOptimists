Campus Life: Mills; Still for Women Only, With New Curriculum


Published: November 18, 1990

A half-year after Mills College reversed its decision to admit men for the first time in its 138-year history, faculty members have begun a plan to revitalize the liberal-arts curriculum and thus make ends meet by attracting 225 more female students in the next five years.

The plan centers on a pilot program of interdisciplinary classes for freshmen and sophomores.

Marilyn Chandler, the English department head, who is teaching one of the classes, said Mills's experiment was part of a national trend. "A lot of schools are going in the direction of interdisciplinary, cross-cultural and ethnic studies," she said, adding that recent writings, like Allan Bloom's "Closing of the American Mind," have raised questions about what educated people should know.

The college's acting president, Virginia Smith, said: "If we think about how people use liberal-arts education, it is rarely used within the confines of any one discipline. Life isn't so neatly divided."

Ms. Smith, the former president of Vassar College, replaced Mary Metz, who resigned in July, saying she could no longer provide the strong leadership that she felt Mills would need during the next five years. Her nine-year tenure had ended in a student and faculty uproar over a May 3 decision by the trustees, supported by Ms. Metz, to make Mills coeducational. Enrollment Goal

In reversing their decision on May 18, the trustees set various goals for fund raising and alumnae participation as well as enrollment -- to 1,000 -- for the college to meet by 1995. The proposed curriculum changes are intended to help attain those goals.

The freshman seminars -- "Women at Work in America," "Ecological Policy and Public Catastrophe" and "Understanding Revolutions" -- and the sophomore core course, "Critical Perspectives on Human Problems," are taught by teams of professors from two different disciplines. In "Understanding Revolutions," for example, students are asked to research and define a revolution from both a historical and a political point of view. "Ecological Policy" draws from biology and government.

Although many colleges offer freshman seminars, Ms. Chandler said, the sophomore course is more unusual. Ms. Chandler and Bruce Pavlik, a biology professor who also teaches the course, said they were trying to provoke thought and to question assumptions about subjects like space, time and knowledge systems.

During the unit on space, for instance, professors from different disciplines lecture weekly on spatial concepts, maps, urban planning, indoor and outdoor space in photography and ancient peoples' sacred space, Ms. Chandler said. Different Perspectives

The students will learn "how to step back and think rather broadly about the assumptions surrounding an issue," using these different perspectives to help them, Ms. Chandler said. In her planning, she talked to core class directors at Princeton University and at Occidental College in Los Angeles, which has an integrated humanities program for freshmen and sophomores.

The faculty voted to start the pilot courses last April, when it also voted on a motion to remain a women's college. The professors and students in the classes will turn in evaluations at the end of this semester that the full faculty will use to decide whether to continue the classes.

Nancy Thornborrow, an economics professor who helped develop the program, said the idea was for first-year students to complete a semester-long seminar and move on to a two-semester sophomore core course, eventually choosing an interdisciplinary major. Staff Turnover

Because of staff turnover and less than adequate advertising, the pilot courses "didn't get the kind of student response we expected," Ms. Chandler admitted. They also were not "bracketed as new" in the registration course packet given to students or listed in the course catalogue. Professors sent freshmen information on the classes during the summer, but most of the interest came from juniors and seniors who were allowed to enroll because of the low number of freshmen and sophomores.

Elin Caughey, a freshman from Indianapolis who is an art major, said that the seminar concept reminded her of humanities classes in high school and that she liked having two professors for a seminar.

Salome Portugao, a freshman from San Francisco who is majoring in education, is taking two seminars. "It's neat getting two different perspectives," she said.
