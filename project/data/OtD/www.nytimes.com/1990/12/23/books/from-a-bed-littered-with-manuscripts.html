From a Bed Littered With Manuscripts

By Peter Brooks; Peter Brooks is a professor of French and comparative literature and director of the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale University.
Published: December 23, 1990

PROUST A Biography. By Ronald Hayman. Illustrated. 564 pp. New York: An Edward Burlingame Book/ HarperCollins Publishers. $29.95.

WRITING a biography of Marcel Proust might well seem a temptation to be resisted. It's not so much that "Remembrance of Things Past" is the most autobiographical of novels but that in a deep sense it is anti-autobiographical, about the discontinuities between life and art. Proust himself spoke eloquently of why a biographical approach to a writer's work misses the point. "A book is the product of a different self from the one we manifest in our habits, our social life and our vices," he wrote in his demolition of the 19th-century French critic Sainte-Beuve, who was more interested in scrutinizing an author's character than in reading his works.

But Proust's life exercises a certain fascination precisely because of its ambiguous relation to the monumental work it produced, and some 30 years following George D. Painter's two-volume biography (recently updated), Ronald Hayman has done it again, this time in one highly readable stout volume. Mr. Hayman's version is a success, not only because he is consistently entertaining, well informed and instructive, but also because he doesn't attempt overly much to bridge the mysteries that separate Proust's life from "Remembrance of Things Past." Mr. Painter's biography is more "literary," in that he is forever citing from the novel, attempting to show how people and incidents in Proust's life served as models for characters and episodes, but the result is often a blurring of the margins between reality and fiction. Mr. Hayman has the great merit of leaving his readers freer to intuit the connections between life and art. And he has deftly gathered all the most recent Proust scholarship and woven it into a lively narrative.

"At the end of 1906," he writes, "no one could have predicted the thirty-five-year-old Proust would become a great novelist." This is just right: Proust's life has no predictive relation to his great work. It is only through the retrospective lens of the work that the life is redeemed. For its first 35 years, the life is largely a record of social frivolities, illness and the cultivation of an esthetic sensibility that expressed itself in often exquisite but distinctly minor literary endeavors. Dressing for dinner, selecting a gift for the mistress of some salon, seeking the company of glamorous young men appeared to be Proust's main occupations when he arose from bed, usually at the end of the afternoon. Proust seemed on the way to becoming a dandy and esthete, like Charles Haas or Robert de Montesquiou, who in their fictional transformations as Charles Swann and the Baron de Charlus become examples of men who took the easy option of preferring to cultivate their social personae rather than submitting to the discipline of work. Andre Gide may almost be pardoned for advising the Gallimard publishing house to reject "Swann's Way" because he thought Proust merely "a snob, a socialite amateur."

What Proust would discover following his mother's death in 1905 is precisely the discipline of art; he overcame that "weakness of will" that his doctor father had detected in him in a diagnosis he was only too happy to accept. Weakness and illness, particularly when they could be used to prolong his dependency on his mother, were a solution to life's demands. And the constant asthma attacks, whatever their cause, were real enough. What no biography can ever record -- and Mr. Hayman tactfully makes little attempt to do so -- is the inner story of how Proust transformed this situation, how he turned illness into the isolation of the man with work to do and, from 1908 to his death in 1922, wrote "Remembrance of Things Past."

The death of Proust's mother appears to have forced him to re-create the lost affective bond through fiction, and more and more he would substitute imaginary conversations with friends, fictive dialogues with reality, for lived experience. From his bed littered with manuscripts, he unfolded scenarios of desire animated by an unappeasable lack, of fulfillments that could come no more. He learned to discipline subjectivity. If Franz Kafka claimed he entered literature when he learned to say "he" rather than "I," Proust seems to have followed the reverse path (as one of the best Proustian critics, Gerard Genette, has pointed out). The early, unfinished "Jean Santeuil" used third-person narration, but remained much too close to the record of Proust's own life. The narrator of "Remembrance of Things Past" speaks as "I," but it is an "I" that is not identical with the novelist, that indeed judges people like the novelist severely. It is an "I" that has become impersonal in the service of art.

If his mother's death freed Proust to turn inward in self-discovery, it also, Mr. Hayman notes, freed him to pursue homosexual relationships with less fear of detection -- though never with an open declaration of his sexual preferences. Proust notoriously transformed homosexual into heterosexual love in his fiction, and much has been written about how Alfred Agostinelli, his chauffeur, secretary and live-in companion, became the Albertine of the novel. What is less often seen is how this kind of transformation mirrors the reversals that structure the novel. The common French term of Proust's time for homosexuality, inversion, signals all the changes that are wrought as we discover that several prominent characters -- Robert de Saint-Loup, the Baron de Charlus, the Prince de Guermantes -- lead hidden sexual lives. Reversals of first impressions are crucial in the education of the fictive protagonist, Marcel; they are the very principle of his learning that society, friendship and love are not as they appear, and that the true life lies elsewhere: in inwardness, in art.
