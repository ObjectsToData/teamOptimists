Advisory Council Rejects Restriction on Arts Endowment's Grants

By WILLIAM H. HONAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: August 6, 1990

The advisory council to the National Endowment for the Arts today refused to bar support for any project that ''advocates or promotes a particular political, ideological, religious or partisan point of view.''

The restriction, advanced by Dr. Jacob Neusner, a professor of religious studies at the University of South Florida at Tampa, was defeated by a vote of 10 to 2, with 1 abstention.

Dr. Neusner, who has become something of a gadfly to John E. Frohnmayer, the chairman of the arts endowment, moved for a number of prohibitions that he said were in the guidelines of the National Endowment for the Humanities.

Phyllis Berney, an arts patron from Wisconsin, supported Dr. Neusner's motion because, she said, ''we'd better be listening to what Congress has to say.''

Bob Johnson, a Republican state senator from Florida who is a member of the council appointed by President Bush, agreed. ''We have a duty to give Congress a sign that we don't need a hearing aid,''he said.

Dr. Neusner and Ms. Berney were the two council members who voted for the motion. Dr. Joseph Epstein, a professor of English at Northwestern University, abstained.

A Commitment to 'Quality'

Mr. Frohnmayer, who served as chairman of the meeting of the National Council on the Arts, held in the Old Post Office building, said he ''strongly opposed'' the restriction because it represented ''the highest degree of insensitivity to the fact that we have an obligation to use our good sense.''

By a vote of 12 to 1, the council adopted a proposal by Mr. Frohnmayer reaffirming the agency's commitment ''to bring quality art to the American people'' by fair procedures, careful analysis and an opposition to obscenity.

Mr. Frohnmayer said he had proposed his substitute motion because Dr. Neusner had ''thrown down the gauntlet'' and suggested that ''we are not players in the discussion'' of whether Congress should restrict the grant-making of the endowment.

This fall Congress will debate whether to extend the life of the endowment.

The three-day council meeting ended today; it was the first that was open to the public.

Still No Grants for 2 Performers

Yesterday the council declined, on technical grounds, to recommend grants to two performance artists, Karen Finley and Holly Hughes. The performance artists were among four whose applications were rejected by Mr. Frohnmayer last month. The performance artists had been opposed by endowment critics on the grounds that their work is offensive or obscene, or that the Government should not finance work that could be construed as obscene.

By declining to act on the performance-art grants, the advisory council enabled Mr. Frohnmayer to avoid making an immediate decision on the politically charged grants before a divided Congress debates endowment procedures.

The council postponed consideration of five experimental-arts grants because of a possible conflict of interest involving the peer panels that reviewed the applications. The endowment's legal counsel will study whether the endowment's conflict-of-interest rule had inadvertently been violated by the panel recommending the grants, and then the grants will be reconsidered by a reconstituted peer panel with that report in mind. The council would reconsider the applications at its next meeting, in November, after the expected debate in Congress on debate the endowment's future.

Mr. Johnson said in an interview that, given the mood of Congress, it would have been ''suicidal'' for the council to have recommended those grants.

A Pair of Victories

Liberal members of the council took pleasure in the large majority that favored urging Mr. Frohnmayer to eliminate an anti-obscenity pledge that grant recipients have been asked to sign.

They also counted as a victory the unanimous support for restoring two previously rejected grants for the Institute for Contemporary Art in Philadelphia, which they felt had been unfairly penalized by the council at its meeting in Winston-Salem, N.C., last May.

''The council voted against those grants in May simply because the I.C.A. did the Mapplethorpe show,'' said Wendy Luers, a writer, foundation executive and council member. She was referring to an exhibition of photographs by Robert Mapplethorpe, organized by the Institute for Contemporary Art and supported in part by an endowment grant. Some in Congress considered the show obscene.

New York State Senator Roy Goodman, a Republican from Manhattan, said in an interview that he thought the council had grown in the last three days ''from a rubber stamp to an effective operational overseer.''

He cited as examples of the council's new strength the overwhelming majority against the anti-obscenity pledge, and various reforms in the peer-panel system for which, he said, the council has been ''the driving force.''

Both liberals and conservatives on the council expressed concern that 13 of the 23 members of the council reached the end of their six-year terms at the conclusion of this meeting. They wondered if the new appointments, all of which require F.B.I. clearance, will be made in time for the November meeting, and they wondered what ideological slant the new appointees might have.
