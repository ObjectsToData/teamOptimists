STAGE VIEW; Meet Richard III, A Contemporary In All That's Evil

By CARILYN G. HEILBRUN; Carolyn G. Heilbrun is the Avalon Foundation Professor in the Humanities at Columbia University. Her most recent book is ''Hamlet's Mother and Other Women'' (Columbia University Press).
Published: August 12, 1990

''Richard III,'' Shakespeare's first enduring drama, is an extraordinary rendition of the price exacted when what is considered appropriate to manly behavior shades first into violence and finally into acts of ultimate brutality.

Richard III is Shakespeare's most ebullient embodiment of evil, and like all usurpers he apparently believes that the moral order he would destroy to seize power can be restored after he succeeds. Instead, as happens in the aftermath of naked aggression, it rises up to destroy him. No obstacle stalls his drive to power. Lies, deceptions, cruelties, murders - all are justified because they will gain him the crown.

Richard offers his deformity as an excuse - ''I, that am curtail'ed of this fair proportion,/ Cheated of feature by dissembling nature,/ Deform'd, unfinish'd, sent before my time/ Into this breathing world, scarce half made up'' - but this motive is rarely portrayed as the true cause of his brutal actions. As he kills, destroying women and children as well as those men who stand between him and the throne, Richard's demon is sheer masculine ambition. The result of his actions is the moral devastation a society suffers when ''manly'' dominance, extreme machismo, is raised to an ideal.

The appalling consequences of Richard's lust for power are obvious enough. What has been less noticed is the extent to which his behavior rests on contempt for women and, indeed, requires that they be suppressed as moral agents. The women in this play, confined within appropriate ''femininity,'' are powerless and have no being beyond the position of the men they marry or bear.

Shakespeare's Richard III must not, by the way, be confused with the historical king of the same name, who may not have been a villain at all. The case in his favor has been made most cogently by Josephine Tey in her novel ''The Daughter of Time.'' Whether or not the historical Richard resembled Shakespeare's, the player-villain is the portrait of a man obsessively overinvested in the rights of manhood and the enactment of machismo.

The Shakespeare in the Park production of ''Richard III,'' now in previews and opening officially on Thursday, serves as a reminder that Shakespeare's Richard remains a remarkably contemporary figure: the rapacious manipulator, enticing us with sweet words, ferocious scheming and moral indifference, he is a hero sanctified in the decade just passed. Directed by Robin Phillips, the play's cast includes Denzel Washington in the title role, Mary Alice as Queen Margaret, Jesse Bernstein as the Duke of York, and Daniel Davis, Virginia Downing, Jonathan Fried, Jenny Nichols, Nancy Palk and Sam Tsoutsouvas.

Film critics have noted that the heroes of some recent movies are devoted to killing as though it were a badge of masculinity; they hardly excuse their violence. The difference between Richard and a hero portrayed by Arnold Schwarzenegger, who embodies today's most honored qualities of machismo, is barely perceptible. For these men there seems no ideal of heroism, no heroic deed or end to which they aspire. ''Richard III'' tells us why: In that genre there is no heroism, which must always be enacted in the service of a cause larger than self-aggrandizement or ambition or even survival. Instead, there is only the manly protagonist's need to prevail, to control or destroy.

Was this what Shakespeare, in his first continuously successful play, wished to intrigue us with? For we must recognize that there is something attractive in the vibrancy and arrogance of pushy, careless, obsessive men. They fascinate us in the root sense of that word: they hold us spellbound, they mesmerize us with their effrontery. As Marilyn French has written: '' 'Richard III' would be dreary and mechanical without Richard's joyous liberated breaking of every moral delicacy, every moral sanction. He acts with impunity as, if truth be told, we would all like to act.'' Yet Ms. French also notes that ''almost the entire first scene is given over to the disparagement of women.''

And, indeed, a look at the women in the play tells us much. Margaret, the old queen and mother of the murdered Henry VI, was brought into this drama by Shakespeare despite history and chronology. By the third scene in Act I, she is cursing the evil times and Richard, their embodiment; at the play's end, all her curses have been fulfilled. Perhaps she stands, in what Virginia Woolf called the androgynous mind of Shakespeare, for the cry of powerless women who have, since Cassandra, predicted, all unheeded, the inevitable disaster that follows male rampage. Cassandra's fate was to be right but not believed; Margaret was left out of Laurence Olivier's 1956 film version of ''Richard III,'' and without her prophesies of doom, the focus shifted to the ''hero's'' murder-strewn career. Margaret was also left out of the 1979 stage production on Broadway, which starred Al Pacino as Richard. These Margaret-less intepretations were apparently following the precedent set by Colley Cibber, who rewrote the play in 1700 to reinforce the audience's delight in Richard's overweening ambition.
