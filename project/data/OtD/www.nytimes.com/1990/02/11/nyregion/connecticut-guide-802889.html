CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: February 11, 1990

BLUEBIRDS

The Connecticut Bluebird Working Group believes that most residents of the state have not seen a bluebird within the past 30 years. The group's seven members are attempting to lure the birds back, and one of them, David O. Hill of Guilford, will give a free talk titled ''Bluebirds,'' illustrated with slides and a film, at 3 P.M. today in the art gallery of Jorgensen Auditorium on UConn's Storrs campus.

The group says bluebirds are desperate for nesting sites because so many of the open meadows they once inhabited have been taken over by shopping centers, industrial parks and housing developments. In addition, the importation of house sparrows and starlings from Europe has further threatened the bluebird's survival by destroying its eggs and competing for its food.

Mr. Hill will point out the need for human assistance in building bluebird nest boxes, installing them in appropriate places and monitoring them. Measurements must be exact to allow the birds in and keep predators out.

Because of such assistance, the birds are reappearing in West Hartford, New Haven and a few other places in the state. The group also serves as adviser to the Connecticut Department of Environmental Protection's project aimed at saving the eastern bluebird.

GEORGE WASHINGTON

David Spicer, the minister of music at the First Church of Christ in Wethersfield, has written a play commemorating a visit to the town by George Washington in 1789. It will be performed in costume, with music, at 7:30 P.M. today in the church. Robert J. Lurtsema, the popular Boston radio personality, will be the narrator, and Paul Anderson, a singer who bears a strong resemblance to the first President, will portray him.

A cast of 100 singers will perform music of Washington's time, re-creating a concert given in his honor when he visited Wethersfield to confer with the Comte de Rochambeau on the final stages of the American Revolution.

''An American Patchwork,'' as Mr. Spicer has named his work, will feature selections by the early American composer William Billings, excerpts from Handel's ''Judas Maccabeus,'' and the ''Magnificat'' of Carl Pachelbel, a son of the German composer Johann Pachelbel, whose family emigrated to America in the early 1700's after fleeing a French invasion of their homeland.

Several choirs, a recorder consort, the Col. John Chester Fife and Drum Corps and the Ars Musica Madrigal Group from Newington High School are among the participants. In addition, a small contingent of dragoons, which originated in Wethersfield in 1776 but is now based in Boston, will fire a musket volley outdoors.

Suggested donation is $6, students $3, and there will be limited child care available. The church address is 250 Main Street, Exit 26 off Interstate 91. Call 563-7759 for more information.

JEWISH MYSTICISM

B'nai B'rith Hillel Foundation at Yale has organized an unusual series of free lectures called ''A Different World: Dimensions of Jewish Mysticism'' beginning Wednesday and continuing weekly at 7 P.M. in Dwight Hall on campus until May 2.

Angels and demons, unicorns and beasts, magic, the afterlife, women, and comparative religion are among the topics to be discussed by scholars in various disciplines from the United States and abroad.

The first program, ''Chaos and the Embrace of Infinity: The Vision of Abraham Isaac Kook'' will be conducted by Yehuda Gellman, professor of philosophy at Ben Gurion University in Israel. ''Mathematics and the Divine Blueprint of Creation'' is the topic April 2, with Stan Tenen, an inventor, and ''Disembodied Souls and Celestial Halls: A Magical Mystery Tour of the Afterlife,'' with Simcha Raphael, a psychologist, is scheduled on April 19.

A free symposium, ''On the Possibility of Jewish Mystical Experience,'' will be held at 8 P.M. March 26 in the Whitney Humanities Center Auditorium on Wall Street, and a display of books and rare manuscripts on Jewish mysticism may be seen at the Sterling Memorial Library at 120 High Street until Feb. 22. Call 432-1134 for more information.

ART AND POLITICS

Nineteen artists from as many Connecticut towns have contributed to an exhibition, ''Art and Politics: A Contemporary Response,'' opening at the Museum of American Political Life at the University of Hartford from Thursday to April 2.

Two- and three-dimensional works focus on such issues as women's rights, nuclear destruction, pollution, and politics viewed as entertainment. Portraits of Presidents John F. Kennedy and Ronald Reagan are also in the show. Most of the pieces were created especially for this exhibition.

''We are really a history museum, not an art museum,'' said the director, Edward Sullivan, who is a professor of history at the university. ''I happen to believe that artists have something to tell us about politics that we don't hear otherwise.''

The museum opened in April with a permanent display of politically oriented art, ''some of it so-so, some of it very good,'' said Mr. Sullivan. ''We are not interested so much in well-known artists as in art that relates to political history.''

Museum hours are 10 A.M. to 6 P.M. weekdays, 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Saturdays, 1 to 5 P.M. Sundays, and Thursday evenings until 8 P.M. The museum, which is in a wing of the university library, is at 200 Bloomfield Avenue in West Hartford. For more information call 243-4089.

GRAPHICS BY PAINTERS

''Second State: The New Painter-Printmaker,'' on view at the Davison Art Center of Wesleyan University until March 9, features graphics by established artists who are primarily painters. Mimmo Paladino, Susan Rothenberg, David Salle, Eric Fischl, Louisa Chase, Jennifer Bartlett and Richard Bosman have used a wide variety of techniques including drypoint, woodcut, etching and aquatint. While the individuality of each artist is apparent, the bold manner in which they have executed these works is described by the gallery as a unifying element, evocative of German Expressionism and American Abstract Expressionism.

The prints were assembled from the Davison's own collections, and from the Yale and Mount Holyoke art museums. The art center is at 301 High Street in Middletown, open from noon to 4 P.M. Tuesday to Friday, and from 2 to 5 P.M. weekends. Call 347-9411 for more information.
