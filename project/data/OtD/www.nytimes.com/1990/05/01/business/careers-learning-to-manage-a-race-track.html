Careers; Learning To Manage A Race Track

By Elizabeth M. Fowler
Published: May 1, 1990

''THE racing world will be here this week, and we will be busy taping interviews to be used in class,'' Dr. Robert Lawrence, a professor at the University of Louisville, said the other day. He heads its race track management program. Then, on Saturday he and many students are off to the races - the Kentucky Derby at nearby Churchill Downs.

The Kentucky Legislature has allotted a percentage of betting money for the program, which began in the fall of 1987 in the business school. ''We're getting about $600,000 out of the Kentucky betting dollar yearly, but perhaps it has slipped a little this year.'' he said last week. ''It goes up and down with parimutuel results.''

Being a horse lover is not enough to qualify a student for the program, Dr. Lawrence said, explaining that the industry needs better-trained personnel in accounting, financial management, marketing, computer science and public relations.

Dr. Lawrence said the program also conducted ''research and offers service seminars.'' One recent seminar was held to train race track stewards and another was for veterinarians.

The program has produced only four graduates, but about 60 are in the pipeline. Students are required to take two years of humanities and business courses before they can choose the major at the start of their junior year. For the final two years they take courses with titles like ''Introductory Horse Science,'' ''Legal Environment of the Equine Industry,'' ''Race Track Management'' and ''Breeding and Syndication Finance.'' They also do internships. The degree: bachelor of business administration with an equine major.

''I think the race track management field offers a good opportunity for younger people with the right training,'' Dr. Lawrence said, even though he acknowledges that ''racing has been losing out to casino and other types of gambling'' with regard to the share of the gambling dollar.

Dr. Lawrence cited the latest figures, compiled for 1987, that put racing and the non-racing show horse business at ''a very conservative total of $15 billion.''

''Racing will survive despite doom-and-gloom thinking,'' he said. ''It needs creative and forward-looking managers. Our graduates will have an impact.''

Dr. Lawrence plans to add an international equine course in the fall. In August he will attend a gaming and wagering conference in London to discuss the program. ''The British do not have such training,'' he said.

Although Britain and France have long been noted for famous horse races, Dr. Lawrence said the enthusiasm for racing in Australia and New Zealand was even greater. ''Australians bet 10 times as much money on horses as do Americans on a per-capita basis,'' he said.

Dr. Lawrence is searching for a few good professors. ''We have a position open for an economist,'' he said. ''It is hard to find Ph.D.'s with experience in the horse industry.''

Dr. Lawrence is an experienced horseman. At the time the university found him he was a professor of agricultural economics at the University of Maryland. A native of Calgary, Alberta, he graduated from the University of Oklahoma with a geology major and then added an M.B.A. Later, he received a doctorate from Texas A & M. There are many programs for horse enthusiasts, mostly stressing animal science, but the most notable competitor to emphasize management aspects is the University of Arizona, in Tucson.

''Don't call that competition,'' Dr. Lawrence said. ''Call them colleagues.''

Arizona's program is in its 17th year, and Dr. Lawrence does not know why Kentucky, the horse racing and breeding center of the nation, started one so much later.

The Arizona program has graduated more than 160 people, including some middle-aged career changers, said Lonny T. Powell, its coordinator and director. He holds a four-day symposium annually. Last December it was attended by more than 1,000 racing executives, giving students a chance to mingle.

Mr. Powell said starting salaries for his graduates were about $20,000, depending on the region of the country, but went up quite rapidly, with some soon earning $60,000 and more.

Meanwhile, the four graduates of the Louisville program have had plenty of job opportunities. One accepted a post at the Pimlico race track in Baltimore, another took a job with the Golden Gate Fields track in Oakland, Calif., and a third joined an Ohio bloodstock agency. The fourth has not yet decided between two breeding farms.
