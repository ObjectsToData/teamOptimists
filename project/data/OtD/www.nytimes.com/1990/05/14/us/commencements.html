COMMENCEMENTS


Published: May 14, 1990

American University

In his first public address in the United States, Alexander Dubcek, the leader of Czechoslovakia's National Assembly, said yesterday that the ''wide and profound'' changes taking place in his country could succeed only if the United States and the Soviet Union achieved success in further negotiations to curtail arms buildups.

In a spirited address before 5,200 people at American University's commencement in Washington, Mr. Dubcek, speaking through an interpreter, said, ''Our dreams of our role in Europe are contingent on the ending of the cold war.'' Mr. Dubcek, the Communist who led Czechoslovakia's liberalization movement of 1968 only to be ousted by Soviet troops, said the movement 22 years ago was ''the source of ideas'' that resulted in recent changes in Eastern Europe.

''But today's changes are more wide and more profound,'' he said. ''Our transition to democracy should be smooth, gradual, cultured, civilized, in every sense of the word. Our attempts to humanize should be the moral compass for us all.''

Mr. Dubcek, who had lived in political obscurity until recently, arrived in the United States on Friday for a four-day visit. After arriving, he met with Congressional leaders and Secretary of State James A. Baker 3d.

He agreed to receive an Honorary Doctorate of Laws and speak at American University at the request of his old friend Arnost Lustig, a Czechoslovak novelist who teaches literature at the university.

George Washington University

In his last public address before leaving his post in the United States, Ambassador Yuri V. Dubinin of the Soviet Union said that traditional approaches to relations between the United States and the Soviet Union were ''no longer adequate.''

Mr. Dubinin told graduates of George Washington University's Elliott School of International Affairs that there was ''a need for new thinking'' between the superpowers.

''Over the years our best political minds thought of ways to deceive or defeat the enemy,'' he said. ''Now we are learning to think about each other not as adversaries but as partners.''

Mr. Dubinin, who is to leave Washington to become Ambassador to France, predicted that the spring meeting to begin in two weeks between President Bush and President Mikhail S. Gorbachev would become ''a major step in the process of bringing our two nations closer together.''

Mr. Dubinin received an honorary Doctor of Public Service degree along with James H. Billington, the Librarian of Congress, Lynne A. Cheney, chairwoman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, and George J. Mitchell of Maine, the Senate Democratic leader.

Butler University

As it emerges from communism and seeks ways to forge its own political future, Hungary must also establish ties with its neighbors while maintaining good relations with the Soviet Union, Hungary's interim president told a commencement audience at Butler University in Indianapolis yesterday.

''All that's happened in Hungary is absolutely and finally irreversible,'' said Hungary's President, Arpad Goencz, an author and leader of the Liberal League of Free Democrats. ''The strength of Hungarian democracy will take place as the result of very harsh debate between political parties.

''We want to make a good connection to the Soviet Union,'' he said in a speech delivered in English. He added, ''I believe that success of the Hungarian democracy will depend on its own and not on the fate of Mr. Gorbachev.''

Mr. Goencz, 68 years old, was chosen to lead Hungary on May 2 in the country's first free elections in at least four decades. He plans to meet with President Bush and Congress.

Mr. Goencz, a lawyer and author, was invited to receive an honorary degree and to speak at the commencement by his friend Dr. Janos Horvath, a former member of Parliament and an economics professor at Butler.
