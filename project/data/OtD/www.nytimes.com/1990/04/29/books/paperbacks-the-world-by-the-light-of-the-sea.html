PAPERBACKS; THE WORLD BY THE LIGHT OF THE SEA

By WILLIAM H. GASS; William H. Gass, the David May Distinguished University Professor in the Humanities at Washington University in St. Louis, is the author of ''Habitations of the Word'' and other books.
Published: April 29, 1990

PLAYING WITH WATER

Passion and Solitude on a Philippine Island.

By James Hamilton-Paterson.

282 pp. New York:

New Amsterdam Books. Paper, $11.95. Cloth, $19.95.

A great many of our classic tales of travel and adventure (as bookstores customarily combine them) are ventures into the interior, explorations of the landscape of the self, even while they are advertised as treks across Antarctica or Arabia, assaults on snowy unreachable peaks, cockroachy crawls into the cracks of the earth, voyages alone around the world or those hunts that test the Hemingway of the heart - to mention some of our more popular ordeals. As the authors (English more often than not) become the drinking buddies of some Sardinians, get used to life in the harem, go native in Naples or learn a number of delicious ways to prepare ants and grubs, and as the facts about local flora and fauna pile up and the catalogue of curious customs grows too heavy and thick for the post; he and she soon find their secret souls reflected in the shards of an ancient civilization; they are compelled to recognize the ambiguities of their desires as they are mimicked by the luxuriant tangles of the jungle or discover their moods mirrored in the movements of a morose ocean. And tests of character are passed the way similar ones were failed in boarding school or while on holiday with the family.

Outback, beach, bush, open boat or hut of reeds: there, eventually, long suppressed feelings are released and honest ones formed; nature stands clear of man for a change; solitude is achieved and meditation becomes almost an occupation. The format is familiar. The risks such adventurers run are the same as the flaws that will weaken their account: sentimentality, nostalgia, misapprehension, self-absorption, a romantic patronizing of every animal and plant that reaches the pen, lying and overlooking - a lot of macho derring-do that vainly disguises the profound fearfulness inspiring it.

James Hamilton-Paterson is an Englishman, as I said he'd almost have to be, a middle-class university graduate who, by dint of eccentricity, disappointment and some misanthropy, has become a dedicated traveler, diver, writer and fisherman. He can contrive a spear from a length of power cable and a barb from a bent nail. He can rethatch his hut's roof if he needs to, construct a rack to sun-dry his catch, or concoct a poultice to cover a coral scratch or other wounds. He is an experienced journalist, too, and understands the problematic character of facts, the dangers inherent in their collection, the difficulties of their objective depiction. He does not tell us how his name came by its hyphen, but it suits the compound world he likes to live in. Sea light illuminates him better than a bulb, and he can hunt for hours beneath a hundred feet of layered ocean: groupers, rays, eels, rabbitfish, squid, shark.

Mr. Hamilton-Paterson is also an author of stories, some for children; he can tell a tale so that the point behind the telling is as sharp as his underwater weapon. Solitude loves him and serves him as a wife might. The text of ''Playing With Water'' contains a mother, but barely, and a sibling so scanted we barely learn her name - Jane. Father, however, is a figure like a distant mountain: low in the landscape, yet with a reality that's as imposing as his son's name. A screenwriter off and on, Mr. Hamilton-Paterson is additionally a dramatist who can compose dialogue with ear, economy and precision, and he has a heart that hears the sounds of nature's intimacies with humility and discretion. Finally, he is a poet as well, with two volumes published before this one - an account of his frequent sojourns in the Philippines that unites his several skills in a prose shaped to shame most present-day poetry. With this prose, Mr. Hamilton-Paterson creates a thoughtfully pronounced and richly lyrical meditation on what might be concisely though abstractly characterized as the relation of consciousness to its manifold conditions. The result is a work of such genuine commitment, balanced perception and responsive passion that it will certainly be condemned to become a classic.

Three locations fix the fundamental figure of this Philippine island life. There is, first of all, Manila, the corrupt, stricken, crowded capital, squeezed by Ferdinand Marcos's fist into a dry pulp, yet holding itself out to the people as if it were as alluringly red and round as Eden's first fruit. Then, at one end of the base beneath the dubious apex, there lies the little fishing community of Kansulay, marginal in every way (equally on the edge of water, land and life), whose residents are depicted here with the sort of fearless affection that strives to get them right. Finally, on the other end of the base line of the triangle and facing the village of Sabay - he cautions us that Kansulay and Sabay are composites of real places, not imaginary, but ''passed through an imagination'' - across a mile-wide strait, there is the tiny uninhabited and waterless island of Tiwarik, which poses on a brief strand of crushed coral it can call a beach, but otherwise ''rises from gurgling boulders more or less vertically up volcanic cliffs of black rock. From one quarter there is a steep sweep of coarse tall cogon [thatching] grass up to the forest which caps the peak. Seen from the strait on a breezy day the sunlight goes running up and up through this wild grassfield. It is the same effect as with young hair and similarly afflicts me,'' our author confesses, ''with deepest melancholy, affection and pleasure.''
