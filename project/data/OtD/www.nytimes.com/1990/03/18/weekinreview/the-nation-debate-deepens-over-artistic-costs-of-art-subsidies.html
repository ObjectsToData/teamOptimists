THE NATION; Debate Deepens Over Artistic Costs Of Art Subsidies

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: March 18, 1990

ON the eve of their annual descent on Congress to seek Federal support for the arts, the artists and institutions that make up America's loosely knit cultural lobby are fractious and divided as never before. At issue is whether to accept restrictions like those passed last year on Federal financing for ''obscene'' art as a necessary evil or as a challenge to be vigorously confronted.

The cause of the infighting is the so-called Yates compromise, a legislative looking glass that allowed all who peered into it last fall to see themselves as at least partially victorious. Crafted by Representative Sidney R. Yates, a Chicago Democrat and champion of the arts, the compromise helped assuage senators outraged by the now famous Mapplethorpe photography exhibit by prohibiting the use of Government money for works deemed obscene. But it also said that obscenity was to be ruled out only if devoid of ''serious literary, artistic, political or scientific value.''

The compromise won the support of restriction-minded members of Congress like Senator James A. McClure, an Idaho Republican who figured half a loaf was better than none, as well as freedom-of-expression advocates like Anne G. Murphy, executive director of the American Arts Alliance, the largest arts lobbying organization in Washington. Ms. Murphy spoke for many last year when she said she could ''live with the compromise'' because it ''only restated the present law of the land'' on obscenity and was a way of mollifying critics of the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities, the principal Government agencies for supporting artists and writers.

The first crack in the seemingly smooth facade appeared last October when Milton Rhodes, president of the American Council for the Arts, the oldest cultural advocacy group in the country, denounced the compromise as ''very dangerous.'' Grass-roots opposition gathered force the next month when John E. Frohnmayer, chairman of the arts endowment, canceled a $10,000 grant for an art exhibition about AIDS at the Artists Space gallery in Manhattan. Mr. Frohnmayer quickly reversed himself, but Susan Wyatt, director of the gallery, said, ''Our case showed people what the impact of the language in the Yates compromise could be.'' The endowment further riled artists by requiring signed assurances that they would not use their grants to create ''obscene'' works.

It was not long before militant artists began demanding that leaders of the arts establishment withdraw their support of the compromise and insist that no such restriction be included in the legislation drafted this year to reauthorize the endowments.

Well aware of the changing sentiment of their constituents, the arts leaders did a prompt about-face. ''A lot of people have had time to step back and reflect on this,'' said Ms. Murphy, adding that she now believes the Yates compromise has had an ''extremely chilling'' effect.

''One can support different positions in different contexts,'' said Edward H. Able, executive director of the American Association of Museums, who now opposes any restrictive language.

Even so, many artists feel inadequately represented in Washington and have set up new and more militant organizations. This division is expected to be manifest on Tuesday, ''Arts Advocacy Day,'' when the traditional arts organizations marshal their troops at the Mayflower Hotel before fanning out to visit senators and representatives, while the artists themselves, under banners like the Arts Coalition for Freedom of Expression, the National Association of Artists' Organizations and the Creative Coalition, hold a rally at the Capitol.

The same day, the Taxpayers for Accountability in Government, a coalition of 15 conservative groups whose leaders say they are offended by some endowment-sponsored art, are scheduled to hold a news conference. The Rev. Lou Sheldon, chairman of the Traditional Values Coalition, said these groups, which previously favored tougher restrictions against obscenity, will now call for ''defunding'' the arts endowment.

With this opposition in mind, some in the arts establishment are apprehensive about the new militancy.

''The Yates compromise was entirely statesmanlike, carefully worked out, and it preserved freedom of expression,'' said Livingston Biddle, who was chairman of the arts endowment during the Carter Administration. ''I don't think that now is the time to stir up the hornets again.''

Leonard Garment, who as a special consultant to President Nixon promoted substantial increases in the endowment's budget, said: ''I hope the arts advocates will keep three points in mind: that the distinction between private funding and public monies is a real one, not a semantic cop-out; that democratic politics depends upon negotiation and compromise; and that a refusal to make any concession to the realities of politics can be very hurtful to public support for the arts. The role of the N.E.A. is to advance cultural excellence, not to test the outer limits of expression permitted by American law and politics.''
