WHERE EAST MEETS WEST - TO BOOGIE!

By WILLIAM H. GASS; William H. Gass is the David May Distinguished University Professor in the Humanities at Washington University in St. Louis.
Published: March 4, 1990

Dynasty Hao is a restaurant that has recently opened in Tokyo. Its interior has been designed to resemble a bare-beamed SoHo loft, and its sound system speaks riffy American jazz. The food, however, is spicy Taiwanese: pork dumplings, sauteed pumpkin with garlic, minced chicken in lettuce. The restaurant is the second of this name to be opened in Tokyo by Kiddieland, an upscale Japanese toy store. To find most places in Tokyo requires elaborate directions since locations aren't numbered - there are no addresses - but you can get your bearings from the Famous Amos shop near nearby, and the Mexican restaurant, La Jolla, which is just across the street. Young mothers naturally frequent Dynasty Hao after purchasing Cabbage Patch dolls and Babar books at Kiddieland. I'm told that their favorite drink is Perrier flavored with a Japanese plum brandy. The restaurant's liquor list spells it ''Perie'' but Perrier is what it is. Misspellings are part of the fun. So is incomprehensibility. (A place in Nagoya calls itself Cafe L'emoi. I don't know whether the sign is trying to advertise the Me Cafe, or whether it really means to call itself Cafe Flutter - better yet, Cafe Anxiety. Perhaps, to the Japanese, it only says French.) Minced pigeon is a popular dish in many of Tokyo's Chinese restaurants, although the bird is sometimes surreptitiously replaced by chicken. However, if your food is Taiwanese, your surroundings American, your management Japanese, your owner a toy store with a California-style name, then it scarcely matters what has been minced and hidden in the hay: what you are eating is pidgin.

Neither bird nor word, pidgin is a hash made mostly by the media, and served on the street like hot pate dogs and Irish bagels. We are creating a life that's like such a language. Why not? A pidgin culture is no doubt the culture of the future.

I call it pidgin in honor of all those wonderfully twisted tongues that are the consequence of an often abrupt collision between cultures - initially the speech of sailors, stevedores, servants, whores, missionaries, merchants and later of anyone else who wanted to pick up some loose change from the wreck. Pidgin is a subordinate lingo almost entirely commercial in origin and aim. It begins as a loose mix of two or more languages with the dominant partner pidginized, or reduced to speakability, by the presumably weaker one (the pidginator, I suppose), hence those colorful, indicative names: Tourist Turkish, Sandalwood English, Chinook jargon, Plantation Pidgin, Miskito Coast Creole, Pitcairnese, Ngatik Men's Language, Sango, Taki-Taki, Town Bemba and the like. The result seems (deceptively) like shanty or shack talk, oral in origin, built of whatever is handy and floating along in the speech stream, often of similar sounds if not similar senses.

Pidgin cultures are as visual as pidgin languages are oral. Such cultures are secondary overlays of an original, of course, and while they reveal the presence of desire, they conceal its customary body. Pidgin culture is - to repeat - commercial, as unstable as its markets, rootless (at first), simplified, crude, repetitive, blunt. Apart from its business use, pidgin culture's considerable appeal is this: its icons have no history; they belong to an exiting, shameless Now; they can be appreciated and adapted with ease, abandoned without qualms; and to teen-agers, pidgin culture seems about as old as they are.

It is to the teeming cities of Japan we must turn if we want to experience pidgin culture in its purest manifestation. Gertrude Stein once said, with her customary shrewdness, that the United States was the oldest country in the world because it had been in the 20th century longest. Now the same sort of thing, concerning the next century, can be said of Japan, which will be the leader in what is to come.

Of course the excitement and success of a city like Tokyo depends on skillful management, the traditional Japanese values of work and respect, the individual citizen's inner checks, society's collective aims, as well as pidgin culture's zizzy superficialities and (the other futuristic factor) the resident mastery of an international high-tech style and mentality. Japanese cities are also wealthy, expensive as any, full of people busy at two jobs, working by the light of both sun and moon, constructing and staffing innumerable new shops and cafes, offices and arcades, factories and hotels. A world of steel, glass and concrete has replaced the former one of wood and rice paper, but the Japanese still do not think of their buildings as immortal. Everything needs renewal, even if it merely means that an emptied cup will be refilled from the same pot of tea.

The Japanese tend to conceive of interiors first, and construct from the inside out, so the last thing they worry about is a building's facade. This neglect has made a shambles of many a street. Moreover, city lots have been cut up in odd ways and into strange shapes. Trapezoidal structures rise from these haphazard cracks like weeds, and symmetry is a stranger. Cities aren't zoned, the fortunes of various regions rise and fall like tides, and neighborhoods expand or contract like something alive.
