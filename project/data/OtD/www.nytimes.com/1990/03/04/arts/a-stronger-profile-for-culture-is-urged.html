A Stronger Profile For Culture Is Urged

By BARBARA GAMAREKIAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: March 4, 1990

As the governors of the nation met here this week for the National Governors Conference, a dozen state cultural advisers gathered at the Library of Congress to engage in a little networking.

The mood was upbeat as the cultural representatives told of new political power coming from state legislators' recognition that an enhanced quality of life brings economic benefits to their districts and their states, not only in the form of tourism but also in business opportunities.

''Twenty-five years ago, state arts and humanities councils were just coming into being,'' said Sondra Myers, cultural adviser to Gov. Robert P. Casey, Democrat of Pennsylvania, who organized the meeting on Tuesday. ''But today, the breadth and depth of the cultural presence in government have grown tremendously, and I thought it important to develop a greater sense of solidarity and collegiality.''

The advisers met with James H. Billington, the Librarian of Congress, and Representative Mary Rose Oakar, Democrat of Ohio, who has introduced a bill calling for a cabinet-level office for the arts and humanities. Those attending discussed their programs and methods to attract the support of legislators for cultural projects.

Joining them were several national cultural leaders, including Milton Rhodes, president of the American Council on the Arts; Jonathan Katz, director of the National Assembly of State Arts Agencies, and John Hammer, of the National Humanities Alliance.

'An Attack on Creativity'

''This kind of a meeting bringing the arts and the humanities together is unusual,'' said Jamil Zainaldin, president of the Federation of State Humanities Councils, before the forum.

''Events of the past year with Mapplethorpe have made us aware that an attack on the arts is an attack on creativity, and we can't let the arts take that beating,'' he said about the furor last summer over an exhibition of photographs by Robert Mapplethorpe and the ensuing Congressional effort to curtail governmental financing of artwork deemed obscene. ''The specter of Congressional regulation of creativity has helped to bring us together for some common conversation.''

Patric Dorsey, secretary of the Department of Cultural Resources of North Carolina, said: ''Industry looks very closely at the environment of a community before they decide to move into it. Now legislators are asking, 'What are you doing in my county; in my district?' ''

Ms. Myers said: ''I used to be ignored by the lowest staff person on a legislator's staff. These days, I don't know one legislator who doesn't have a theater they want to reclaim for restoration.''

On Securing Attention

Pat Clubb of the Texas Commission on the Arts said she discovered that doors were opened to her in the state legislature when the arts were linked to public policy and social issues. ''Most legislators think in terms of broader agendas than just the arts,'' she said. ''So you get their attention by talking about the drop-out rate and what kinds of arts education programs you can put into schools to alleviate dropouts, and you talk about rural arts programs, about minority participation.''

Joan Baldridge, director of the Department of Arkansas Heritage, said: ''We keep in constant contact with state legislators. We inform them of every program and how it helps them in their district. Giving that information to your state legislators is vital: it enables them to share in your success.''

The participants heartily endorsed Representative Oakar's proposal for the creation of a position called Secretary of the Arts and Humanities. She urged their support in lining up co-sponsors for the bill, which she introduced in January.

'Where Is Our Revolution?'

''There is nothing like having someone right in the room with you around that cabinet table when you are discussing national priorities,'' she said.

She mentioned that she had just returned from Eastern Europe. ''When we asked, 'What do you want from us?' their priorities amazed me,'' she said. ''They want cultural exchanges, artist exchanges, Peace Corps volunteers to teach English. I must confess, I was a little jealous. I had to ask myself: 'Where is our revolution? What are we doing in our quest for loftier things?' ''

Mr. Billington said: ''We are living in a world where increasingly we have to live by our wits rather than our strength. More and more people are playing the arts-and-humanities game.''
