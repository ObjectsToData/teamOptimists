This Discussion Series Is One for the Books

By CAROLYN BATTISTA
Published: March 4, 1990

STARTING this month, public libraries around the state will be offering free book-discussion series in the largest such project ever organized in Connecticut. The project, Connecticut Reading Connections, supported by a Federal grant, will bring 240 individual programs to 60 communities.

Each participating library will offer of four discussions on a single theme, like ''Being Ethnic, Becoming American'' and ''The Mystery Novel as a Reflection of Its Times.'' The libraries will stock extra copies of the books being discussed, but people may participate in the programs without reading them.

The Southern Connecticut Library Council received a $194,540 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities to organize the Reading Connections project. Among the 20 libraries offering programs this spring, the Phoebe Griffin Noyes-Old Lyme Library will start first, on Thursday, with the series ''Myths in Marriage.'' Another 20 libraries will offer programs in the fall, with 20 more presenting discussions the following spring.

''We're answering a need for this new type of library service,'' said Barbara Rader, director of the program, who noted the success of the council's earlier book discussion series. ''People like to come and discuss good books,'' especially in informal programs to which participants can bring their own experiences, she said.

'Wonderful Group'

The discussion leaders are faculty members from colleges and universities in Connecticut, some of whom have presented similar programs in recent years.

Fred Pfeil, who teaches English at Trinity College in Hartford, has led discussions on several American biographies at the H. C. Hull Library in Clinton. The participants, he said, were ''a wonderful group of about 20, mostly retired, who showed up faithfully.''

''They had expected me to come in and lecture and would have been happy enough with that,'' said Professor Pfeil. ''But I made introductory remarks, then told them I wanted to hear what they thought. They loved that. They became kind of a club. I'm still missing them, and I learned a lot.''

He said he always came prepared with questions to get participants thinking and plans to do the same when he begins a series on March 14 at the Windsor Public Library. The series,''Being Ethnic, Becoming American,'' will look at authors like James Baldwin and N. Scott Momaday.

Professor Pfeil said he will raise questions like: ''What does the protagonist want to keep from the culture that he or she comes from? Or want to give up? Or want to take from American culture? Are there ways in which we all have to become American? Do we want to do it?''

Serious Mystery Fans

Robin Winks, a professor of history at Yale University, will lead ''The Mystery Novel as a Reflection of Its Times'' at the West Haven Public Library, beginning on April 9. At earlier programs, he said, people who were seriously interested in mystery fiction were pleased to talk with one another. ''They're accustomed to people in our culture not taking it seriously,'' he said.

Jordon Pecile, a teacher of humanities at the United States Coast Guard Academy, will lead discussions on ''The Family in Contemporary American Literature'' at the Thompson Public Library starting on March 19 and will offer a series on the mystery novel at the Acton Public Library in Old Saybrook starting on March 23.

Helaine Dauphinais, library director in Thompson, said Mr. Pecile's previous discussions there proved popular.

Connecticut Reading Connections will also be offered this spring at public libraries in Avon, Bristol, Hamden, Lyme, Milford, Morris, Newington, Newtown, Old Lyme, Old Saybrook, Seymour, Shelton, Stafford, Stratford, Tolland, Wallingford and Westbrook.

Programs are scheduled for next fall in Amity, Branford, East Lyme, Ellington, Essex, Fairfield, Farmington, Greenwich, Groton, Middletown, New Britain, New Milford, Norwalk, Portland, Simsbury, Southbury, Thomaston, Washington, Westbrook and Woodbridge.

Spring 1991 programs will be in Bolton, Coventry, Cheshire, Darien, Durham, East Hampton, Granby, Guilford, Kent, Ledyard, Madison, Naugatuck, Norwich, Oxford, Redding, Sherman, Stamford and Waterbury.

Additional programs will be conduced in the fall at the Amity Regional Senior High School Library and at next spring at the North Branford High School Library.

More information is available from the Southern Connecticut Library Council, 60 North Main Street, Wallingford, Conn. 06492 and from participating libraries.
