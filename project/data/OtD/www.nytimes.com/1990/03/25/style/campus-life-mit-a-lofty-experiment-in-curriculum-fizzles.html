Campus Life: M.I.T.; A Lofty Experiment In Curriculum Fizzles


Published: March 25, 1990

Three years ago, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology's Committee on Curricula approved a 12-subject experimental program titled ''Human Contexts of Science and Technology.'' It was an attempt to make undergraduates more aware of the impact of science and engineering on society.

Now, M.I.T. administrators concede that they offered a curriculum and virtually no one came.

With two exceptions, attendance has been sparse at the 12 interdisciplinary classes offered at various times during the last five semesters on subjects dealing with ethics and social responsibility.

Only ''AIDS: Scientific Challenge and Human Challenge'' and ''Engineers, Scientists and Public Controversies'' proved popular enough to continue, with an average of about 25 enrollees, school officials said. But two other courses - ''Automation, Robotics and Unemployment'' and ''Life and Institutions of Science'' -were canceled, said Margaret S. Richardson, assistant dean for curriculum support. After the present semester there will be no Human Contexts courses, she said.

Ms. Richardson heads the newly formed Context Support Office, which is to replace the formal courses in ethics and social issues with less formal workshops and colloquiums, research projects and lectures.

'Nobody's Shedding Tears'

Context is now being co-directed by Lawrence M. Lidsky, a professor in the nuclear engineering department, and Merritt Roe Smith, a professor of the history of technology. ''Nobody's shedding tears over the loss of Context subjects,'' Mr. Lidsky said.

The reason for the anemic enrollments is straightforward: Students will not voluntarily devote time to courses that do not satisfy requirements for graduation.

''What it comes down to is 'Can you offer credit that counts towards a student's degree?' '' said Lacinda R. Hummel, a junior biology major from Bloomington, Ill., who sat as a student representative on the review group.

The idea for a formal program on the scientist's social responsibility came to life through the investigations of two working groups, one from the School of Humanities and Social Science, the other from the School of Engineering. The groups devised 12 courses, both seminars and lectures.

In its July 1989 report, the Context Review Group gave several reasons for the Context Initiative, as it has become known. These included making students and instructors aware of the contexts - cultural, political, economic, environmental and ethical -within which the practice of science and engineering takes place.

''We are likely to be more effective in our professional work when we are aware of the way in which social values influence the work we usually think of as purely disciplinary,'' the report said. It added that scientists and engineers have the responsibility ''to be aware of the potential social effects of their work, so that they will be able to inform the society they serve of the likely consequences of new technologies.''

Redesigned Biology Course

Despite the initiative's failure as formal curriculum, it did prompt several professors to include ethical and social considerations in their otherwise technical classroom discussions. For example, Paul T. Matsudaira and Brent H. Cochran, biology professors, have redesigned the introductory course, moving its emphasis away from molecular biology to a more general view that includes population genetics, physiology and ecology.

One approach is to assign students weekly readings from publications like Science magazine and The Economist. These are discussed at recitations, whose function has traditionally been limited to expounding on material covered in lectures.

'' 'Context' is another name for real life,'' said Edward F. Crawley, an aeronautics and astronautics professor who teaches a departmental course called unified engineering. Nowadays, he begins class a few minutes early with discussions of the social and political aspects of engineering: how changes in the Soviet Union will influence the careers of students in the class; the politics of the space program; the ethical implications of compromises in design.

''Students are very interested,'' he said. ''They find it unusual that professors are willing to talk about things other than solid mechanics.''
