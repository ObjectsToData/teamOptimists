Vacancies Hamper Agencies for Arts

Ashley Smith/Times-News

Rocco Landesman, center, former arts endowment chairman, with Representative Mike Simpson, right, in Idaho.

By PATRICIA COHEN
Published: August 7, 2013

The week before the Broadway producer Rocco Landesman was scheduled to deliver his first Congressional testimony as chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts, he traveled 2,000 miles from Washington to Jerome, Idaho, to take in a production of “Othello” at a high school. Sitting next to him that spring day in 2010 was his host, Representative Mike Simpson, the top Republican on the committee that controls the endowment’s financing.

This kind of face-to-face encounter is at the core of the chairman’s job. But now, as the White House and Congress prepare for a September showdown over the budget that could mean severe cuts for the federal arts agency, advocates are fretting over the absence of their strongest champion — a permanent chairman.

More than eight months have elapsed since Mr. Landesman announced that he was stepping down from the nation’s top arts post. The only time a search has taken longer was in 2002, after the conductor and composer Michael P. Hammond died after just six days in office. Yet the hunt for a replacement — who must be nominated by the president and confirmed by Congress — seems to have stalled, according to people with knowledge of the search, who asked to remain unnamed because of the sensitivity of the subject.

“It shouldn’t take this long to find a cultural leader,” said Robert Lynch, president and chief executive of Americans for the Arts, a lobbying group.

The endowment’s sister agency, the National Endowment for the Humanities, has also been without a permanent director since May, when its chairman, Jim Leach, resigned. Both endowments have acting chairwomen, Joan Shigekawa at arts and Carole Watson at humanities, who have drawn high marks, but experts say even the most able manager cannot replace a permanent chief who has the president and Congress’s seal of approval.

“The N.E.A. and N.E.H. need chairpersons, because they need people to be working with Congress, the White House and the public to build support,” said Dana Gioia, the poet who led the arts endowment from 2002 to 2008. Without confirmed chairmen, the endowments are forced to “operate with a disability.”

“That’s just Washingtonian common sense,” he added.

The endowments, both created in 1965 — one to nurture and preserve the arts, the other to promote humanities research and education — have always represented a minuscule portion of federal spending. Nonetheless, in some smaller states, endowment money can account for a third to a half of the arts funds, and nationally the chairman is a cheerleader for the cultural and economic benefits of the arts. “The power of the N.E.A. is not the amount of money it has,” said Mr. Lynch of the Americans for Arts, but in the “bully pulpit” it provides.

For decades, though, supporters say the endowments have come to resemble orphaned dogs who beg for table scraps but often get kicked instead. They have recovered from their darkest hours in the 1980s, when President Ronald Reagan tried to eliminate both agencies, and in the 1990s, when budgets and programs were lacerated during the height of the culture wars. But there remains a vocal minority of Republicans who treat them as luxuries the nation cannot afford.

In 2011, for example, the Republican presidential hopeful Mitt Romney pledged to slash the endowments’ financing, while the Alaska governor and former vice presidential candidate Sarah Palin labeled them “frivolous,” and called for them to be put on “the chopping block.”

Last month, in the face of crushing federal deficits, the House Appropriations Committee, which includes Mr. Landesman’s fellow theatergoer in Idaho, Representative Simpson, proposed halving the $154.5 million budget for each endowment that President Obama had requested for the 2014 fiscal year.

Mr. Simpson did not return repeated phone calls and e-mails to his office.

Politicians and advocates acknowledge that focusing on the endowments is difficult when Washington is consumed with a budget stalemate, the secret surveillance of Americans, the choice of a new Federal Reserve chairman, an immigration bill and a backlog of judicial nominees who need Congressional approval.

“Frankly, the whole government is stalled,” said Representative Louise M. Slaughter, a Democrat from New York’s northwestern tier and a chairwoman of the Congressional Arts Caucus. “We can’t get anything done.”

Ms. Slaughter said she does not think the lack of a permanent chairman would necessarily hurt the arts or humanities endowments during budget negotiations, saying that there are representatives from both parties who understand their importance.

She, like other endowment cheerleaders, emphasized both the economic and educational returns that come from investing in the arts. Mr. Landesman, for instance, has frequently noted that each dollar spent by the arts endowment generates $8 of additional investment and $26 of economic activity in the community. Mr. Lynch traces the creation of art districts in Miami and the annual Art Basel fair there to an infusion of endowment seed money.

Arts advocates, in particular, expressed frustration with the delay in filling the $165,300-a-year job at the arts endowment. “People have been expressing interest in this position for many months, and I just don’t know why there hasn’t been movement,” Jonathan Katz, chief executive of the National Assembly of State Arts Agencies, said. “Programmatically, the agency tends to drift until you have a chairman coming in, because nobody wants to commit the agency to something that might not be the new chairman’s priority.”

The violinist Aaron P. Dworkin, who founded the Sphinx Organization in Detroit to promote diversity in classical music, confirmed that he was considered for the post after Mr. Landesman announced his departure. But he said he was happy to be continuing with his work at Sphinx, “where I think I can have the best impact.”

Mr. Dworkin, who is a member of the National Council on the Arts, the panel that advises the arts endowment chairman, said that possible candidates are being considered, but that he did not know who they were or if there was anyone moving along the pipeline. At the council’s last meeting in June, the update on the search contained no new information.

Some arts advocates said they had pushed for Caroline Kennedy, but the president had other plans for her. Two weeks ago, she was nominated to be ambassador of Japan.

An administration spokesman said the White House does not comment on personnel decisions.

William Ferris, who steered the humanities endowment during the Clinton administration, said he hopes to hear an announcement about nominees soon. The case for support “needs to be put on a personal level,” he said, “and the chairperson does it best.”
