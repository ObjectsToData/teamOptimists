A Childhood Home, and Poets’ Muse

Jim Wilson/The New York Times

Julie Carlson, shown at her house in Northern California, bought her childhood home in Wellfleet, Mass., and on its porch she came up with “Remodelista,” a digital guide to remodeling.

By PENELOPE GREEN
Published: October 30, 2013

Julie Carlson’s childhood home, a modest but elegant, mid-19th century white clapboard near the main street of Wellfleet, Mass. — also known as the “seacoast of Bohemia,” as Mary McCarthy slyly described the town — has been evoked so many times in the poems of so many late-20th-century poets it deserves its own anthology. Patricia Goedicke imagined its “white curtains like loaves of bread.” Deborah Garrison wrote how “The porch that needs painting peels its white skin in the sun.” Cynthia Zarin decried its penetrating cold in the winter, and “the knocking boiler’s burned old heart.”

When Ms. Carlson took over the house, buying out her sister after their mother died, it played muse to her, too. Seven years ago, it was on its porch that she cooked up Remodelista, a digital guide to home design and remodeling, with her business partner and friend, Francesca Connolly — another child of Wellfleet. On the occasion of “Remodelista,” the book, out next month from Artisan, we spoke to Ms. Carlson, who lives full time in San Francisco. Her new book is a lovely and extremely useful portfolio of houses, rooms and products, which will no doubt serve as an inspiration and as a source book for would-be redecorators and remodelers; also, Julianne Moore has written the introduction. Still, we would rather discuss Ms. Carlson’s summer home, and her childhood there, which fascinates us. (The interview has been edited and condensed. A longer version is posted at nytimes.com/home.)

Her mother, Jocelyn Carlson Baltzell, was a saloniste who was once fired from her job as associate director at the New York Institute for the Humanities at New York University for inviting Hillary Rodham Clinton to one of that association’s lunches, which were gatherings for intellectuals. Her stepfather, E. Digby Baltzell, was a professor at the University of Pennsylvania, famous for coining the acronym WASP. Ms. Carlson’s father was a Choate-and-Yale-educated commercial fisherman. Her mother ran the Wellfleet house like a boardinghouse and salon, Ms. Carlson said, renting rooms in the summer to musicians, artists and poets to make ends meet. (Clearly poets were well-represented among the boarders.) She was anti-remodeling, her daughter said, but adventurous — even reckless — in her decorating, buying an Arne Jacobsen faucet when, as Ms. Carlson said, “we could hardly pay our phone bill” and recovering all the furniture in acid green.

Q. I’m sorry to say we will not be discussing your book, which is terrific, by the way. Can you paint a picture of the Wellfleet house?

A. That’s O.K., I’ve been talking about the book a lot lately. The house had belonged to my great-uncle, and my mother was always obsessed with it. When he died, she bought it and we moved there when I was 11. It was very bleak in the winter. There was a really interesting mix of characters in the town, all those intellectuals, of course, and the year-rounders. I think Mary McCarthy described it as a place with not one but two town idiots. My parents were larger than life and we had this crazy Bohemian upbringing where there was never any money and this constant stream of people coming through the house. “The Ice Storm,” but set in Cape Cod. There was intermittent money, fishing money. Francesca’s parents and my parents were best friends. Two threads that infused the aesthetic of Remodelista were the modernism of the cottages on Wellfleet juxtaposed with the beauty of the old houses there. It was unusual to have architecture be so front and center in a New England town. Now, Francesca and I both have these respectable lives compared to our parents.

“The Ice Storm” if only in temperature.

The house was very cold in the winter. When my mother bought it, I remember her saying, “We don’t remodel.” There was no heat on the third floor, where my sister and I had our bedrooms. We still have the same heating system today that Cynthia Zarin wrote about.

How did you keep warm?

We had lots of quilts. And not enough outlets but light bulbs hanging from the ceiling and lots of extension cords snaking around. Remodelista is maybe kind of a reaction. In the summer my mother would rent out rooms. One summer she rented to four cellists. Two in the little studio house and two in the main house. All day long it was cello music.

What an amazing childhood.

It was kind of fantastic in retrospect, but, of course, at the time all I wanted was to live in a normal, bourgeois house. My mother was always under the influence of whomever was staying with us. The pop artist Peter Gee mapped out the colors in the living room, the pumpkin walls and the bright blue ceiling. One friend of hers decided it was hopeless to maintain a lawn, so they got bags and bags of marble chips and spread them over the grass. It looked horrible, and we spent the next decade picking out pieces of gravel. One of my mother’s catchphrases, if something looked too perfect or fixed up, she would fret that it was looking “too much like Chatham.”

You said your mother was hurt that her plumbing had been maligned by Cynthia Zarin.

She wasn’t hurt so much as annoyed. I think she was being protective. She was so proud of the house, and it felt like having your child insulted for having bad teeth, or something like that.

And your father, what was it like having him gone so much?

He wasn’t gone for more than a few weeks at a time but I remember when “The Perfect Storm” came out, reliving how dangerous and scary it all was. I have a memory of talking to him on a ship-to-shore radio, and my mother pacing about. They had a rocky marriage, and divorced after 20 years. He was the heroic adventurer, and she was very phobic. They were badly matched.

Shouldn’t you be writing a memoir next?

My sister is.
