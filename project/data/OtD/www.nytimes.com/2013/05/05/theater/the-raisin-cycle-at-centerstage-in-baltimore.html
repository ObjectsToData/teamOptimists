For Sons of ‘Raisin,’ a Back-to-Back Duel

Matt Roth for The New York Times

Kwame Kwei-Armah, the artistic director of CenterStage in Baltimore.

By ROB WEINERT-KENDT
Published: May 2, 2013

BALTIMORE — When Kwame Kwei-Armah took the job of artistic director at this city’s biggest theater, CenterStage, in the summer of 2011, he started hearing a drumbeat about one play in particular.

“People kept saying, ‘Are you doing “Clybourne Park”? You’ve gotta do “Clybourne Park”!’ ” said Mr. Kwei-Armah, 46, a tall Briton of African-Grenadian descent who alternates easily between glad-handing charm and utter earnestness. The only hitch was that Mr. Kwei-Armah had mixed feelings about Bruce Norris’s tetchy, impolitic play about race and real estate, which took the 2011 Pulitzer Prize for drama and has since become one of the most-produced pieces in the United States.

“I find ‘Clybourne Park’ to be a brilliant play, all that we want a modern play to be —a magnificent catalyst for a debate,” Mr. Kwei-Armah said over dinner at an Afghan restaurant in Mount Vernon, the historic neighborhood where CenterStage has made its home since 1974. “However — and I don’t think Bruce set out to do this — but connotationally, the play says that whites build and blacks destroy.”

That’s one way to sum up the play’s arc. In following the fortunes of a Chicago house over 50 years, “Clybourne Park” does take as a given the all-too-typical history of white flight and ensuing blight. It is also peppered throughout with frank, profane talk about race and class, and in the second act it devolves into a volley of pointedly offensive jokes — some of which, Mr. Kwei-Armah admitted, he felt he could easily have been the butt of.

Mr. Norris’s play is indeed now running at CenterStage through June 16, without caveats but with an addendum: at the curtain call, a slide projected at the top of the proscenium reads, “To Be Continued ... ‘Beneatha’s Place.’ ” That’s the title of a new play by Mr. Kwei-Armah that the theater is producing in repertory with “Clybourne,” with roughly the same cast and the same director, Derrick Sanders. Performances begin on Wednesday.

The two are being staged under the umbrella title “The Raisin Cycle,” as both are inspired by Lorraine Hansberry’s 1959 classic, “A Raisin in the Sun.” (Other theaters have paired “Raisin” and “Clybourne.”) The house in Mr. Norris’s play is the same one that the Younger family of “Raisin” scrounge and save to move to, while Mr. Kwei-Armah’s play traces two “Raisin” characters on a different path: Beneatha Younger, the family’s resident intellectual, and her Nigerian fiancé, Asagai, who travel to his home country on the eve of its independence from British colonial rule.

In short, while some artistic directors might put a controversial play into context for their audiences with a program note or a post-show talkback, Mr. Kwei-Armah has put his reputation on the line with an ambitious new work that, although it doesn’t take on “Clybourne” directly, will invite inevitable comparisons.

“It’s madness,” he confessed. “I’m getting in there with this Goliath, and I set up the ring —I put the ropes in. What was I thinking?”

Reached by e-mail, Mr. Norris conceded that “there’s a lot of — totally justifiable — sensitivity around a white guy writing about race, money and real estate, so I’m not surprised if people occasionally get prickly.” But he insisted that “Clybourne” is “as much about class as it is about race,” and that the play’s portrait of decline mirrors the reality that falling property values inarguably lower a neighborhood’s median income.

“Poorer neighborhoods, be they white, black, brown, or whatever, look different from rich neighborhoods,” he added. “It’s about money, not about race — except indirectly.” (He said he wasn’t sure whether he’d get to Baltimore to see “Beneatha’s Place.”)

Mr. Kwei-Armah has set his first act in Beneatha’s adopted African home in 1959 and the second act in the same home decades later, after she has emerged as a dean of African-American studies at a California university. The disputed territory has shifted from property to history, as Beneatha must vie with a white humanities faculty over the question of who owns black studies, and even whether race should be studied at all.

Mr. Kwei-Armah can afford to make this producing gamble partly because the theater he inherited is in solid shape. With two midsize spaces and an inviting lobby in a capacious former Jesuit high school building, CenterStage has a budget of $7 million and a subscriber base that is rallying after a slow decline, according to its managing director, Stephen Richard.

The previous artistic director, Irene Lewis, had crusaded to diversify the audience and the programming, which involved, for example, bringing in Mr. Kwei-Armah from London to stage his play “Elmina’s Kitchen” in 2005 and commissioning “Let There Be Love” from him in 2010.

“I looked around Baltimore and then looked at the audience we had, and they all looked like me,” said Ms. Lewis, who is white. “I decided that a third of the season would be African-American work. A lot of subscribers dropped off the train, but over time those plays became our biggest
sellers.”

Ms. Lewis was less excited about being the public face of the theater. “I’m demure,” she said. “The rehearsal room is my home.” Mr. Kwei-Armah, by contrast, is both comfortable in the spotlight and a relentless multitasker. In Britain, he starred in a long-running TV drama, “Casualty,” released a pop album and held posts at the National Theater and with BBC radio. In 2010, he directed an international black arts festival in Senegal.

Since taking the helm of CenterStage, he has directed plays including “The Whipping Man” and “The Mountaintop” there, as well as Dominique Morisseau’s “Detroit ‘67” at New York’s Public Theater. This summer, Mr. Kwei-Armah will direct Naomi Wallace’s “Liquid Plain” at the Oregon Shakespeare Festival. (He will remount it at CenterStage in 2014).

On the day he spoke to The Times, Mr. Kwei-Armah had rehearsals for “Beneatha’s Place,” a meeting with donors and a post-show talkback; he said he’d been up till 4 a.m. the previous night doing rewrites. Mr. Richard, the theater’s managing director, called his colleague’s energy “superhuman” and stressed that Mr. Kwei-Armah’s crammed schedule is not merely a social calendar.

“The thing that’s impressive is that Kwame has the capacity to do things that are collective activities, like meetings and rehearsals, and then transition seamlessly to activities that are entirely solitary, like writing,” he said.

Mr. Kwei-Armah may have a knack for the job’s social aspects, and he said he was invigorated by the challenges and potential of Baltimore, a majority-black city that has, like the neighborhood in “Clybourne Park,” faced its own cycles of decline and rebirth. And if “Beneatha’s Place” is his first stab at writing American characters, it’s unlikely to be the last. “The thing that I like best,” he said, “ is being a playwright.”
