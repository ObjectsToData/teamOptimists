Spare Times for Feb. 15-21

By ANNE MANCUSO
Published: February 14, 2013

Around Town

Follow @nytimesarts for arts and entertainment news.

Museums and Sites

Morris-Jumel Mansion: Chocolate Lecture and Tasting (Saturday) How advertisements for chocolate over the years have been as coveted as the products they pitch is the subject of a talk — with samplings — by Carol Ward, a historian and deputy director of the museum. The event will include a historical display of prints dating to the 1800s. From 3 to 5 p.m., 65 Jumel Terrace, at Sylvan Terrace, Washington Heights, (212) 923-8008, morrisjumel.org; $25, $20 for members.

Mount Vernon Hotel Museum and Garden: Washington’s Birthday Ball (Sunday) Visitors to the museum can party like it’s the 19th century with traditional country dancing demonstrated by costumed dancers, as well as music, museum tours and refreshments. The party celebrating the 281st anniversary of George Washington’s birth — it’s actually next Friday — will go on from 1 to 3 p.m. 421 East 61st Street, at York Avenue, Manhattan, mvhm.org; $15, $10 for members and children under 12. Reservations are recommended: (212) 838-6878.

El Museo del Barrio: ‘Super Sabado: Carnaval!’ (Saturday) Part of the Free Third Saturdays series, this daylong celebration of the approaching Easter holiday will feature music, storytelling, dance and art workshops. From 11 a.m. to 6 p.m., 1230 Fifth Avenue, at 104th Street, East Harlem, (212) 831-7272, elmuseo.org.

Museum of Jewish Heritage: ‘Through Soviet Jewish Eyes: Photography, War, and the Holocaust’ (through April 7) This exhibition of more than 50 rare images, which chronicled the daily hardships that Soviet Jews endured during World War II, is based on David Shneer’s book of the same title. Mr. Shneer is also the co-curator, with Lisa Tamiris Becker, of this exhibition. 36 Battery Place, Lower Manhattan, (646) 437-4202, mjhnyc.org; $12, $10 for 65+, $7 for students; free for members and children 12 and younger. Museum hours are Sunday through Tuesday and Thursday from 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; Wednesday from 10 a.m. to 8 p.m.; and Friday from 10 a.m. to 3 p.m.

New York Transit Museum: ‘Meet Miss Subways’ and ‘ElectriCity: Powering New York’s Rails’ (continuing) Two exhibitions are on display. “Meet Miss Subways,” which looks at the beauty contest and the lives of former Miss Subways, up through March 25; and “ElectriCity: Powering New York’s Rails,” a historical display of items from the museum collection, including switches and circuit breakers, that illustrates how electricity powers the subway system. Tuesdays through Fridays from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m., Saturdays and Sundays from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m. Boerum Place and Schermerhorn Street, Brooklyn Heights, (718) 694-1600, mta.info/mta/museum; $7, $5 for those ages 2 to 17 and 62+, free for members and for 62+ on Wednesdays.

Gardens

Queens Botanical Garden: Backyard Bird Count and Lunar New Year Celebration (Saturday) You can make a day of it at the garden with these two events. Volunteers are needed for the annual bird count at 10:30 a.m., sponsored by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, Bird Studies Canada and the National Audubon Society. Data about species and migration patterns is gleaned from the count, which is free but requires registration: (718) 886-3800, Ext. 213. An outdoor lantern-making workshop to usher in the Year of the Snake will run from 1 to 3 p.m. 43-50 Main Street, at Dahlia Avenue, Flushing, Queens, (718) 886-3800, queensbotanical.org; $2; parking, $5.

Events

‘Jackson Heights Trilogy’ (Friday through Sunday and Wednesday) This three-play repertory focusing on the diverse Queens neighborhood of Jackson Heights continues at 777 Theater, 777 Eighth Avenue, at 47th Street. Each play comprises work by five or more playwrights and features a multiethnic cast, reflecting the cultural diversity of the neighborhood. On the program are “167 Tongues” (Saturday at noon); “You Are Now the Owner of This Suitcase” (Saturday at 4 p.m., Sunday at 2 p.m. and Wednesday at 7:30 p.m.); and “Jackson Heights Jam” (Friday at 8 p.m., Saturday at 8 p.m. and Sunday at 5 p.m.) (800) 838-3006, theatre167.org; $18.

Brooklyn Navy Yard Center at BLDG 92: ‘Reflections on Rosie’ (Friday through Sunday, Wednesday and Thursday) The exhibition, “Reflections on Rosie,” contains the stories of women who worked at the Brooklyn Navy Yard during World War II and the female artists who now work in studios in the Center. The display, also sponsored by the Brooklyn Historical Society, includes oral histories and multimedia presentations and will be on view through May. Open Wednesdays through Sundays from noon to 6 p.m. Brooklyn Navy Yard Center at BLDG 92, 63 Flushing Avenue, at Carlton Avenue, (718) 907-5992, bldg92.org; free admission to exhibition.

Grand Central Terminal: ‘Grand By Design: A Centennial Celebration of Grand Central Terminal’ (through March 15) For anyone who ever heard a parent yell “What is this, Grand Central Station?” the terminal has been more than just a place to catch a train. Its history and impact on the city as both a transportation hub and a cultural icon are the focus of this exhibition of photographs and artifacts in celebration of its 100th anniversary. Hours are daily, 8 a.m. to 10 p.m. 42nd Street, between Lexington and Fifth Avenues, grandcentralterminal.com; free.

Green-Wood Artist Tour at the Metropolitan Museum of Art (Friday) Sponsored by the Green-Wood Historic Fund, this tour focuses on work by some of the noted artists who are buried at the historic Brooklyn cemetery, including George Bellows, Louis Comfort Tiffany and William Merritt Chase. The tour, which begins at 10 a.m., will be led by curators and historians, including Lisa M. Messinger, a co-curator of an exhibition on George Bellows, that will be up through Monday, and Jeff Richman, the cemetery’s historian. Metropolitan Museum of Art, (718) 210-3080, green-wood.com/toursevents; $125, $110 for members; $119 and $104 for 65+; the fee includes lunch.

Lunar New Year Celebrations (Saturday and Sunday) The Year of the Snake will be welcomed with parades, lion dances and festivals. On Saturday the annual parade sponsored by the Flushing Chinese Business Association — the 18th — will begin at 11 a.m. at Union Street and 37th Avenue in Flushing, Queens, and will end at 39th Avenue and Main Street; fcbainc.org. On Sunday the festivities in Chinatown — the 14th annual celebration sponsored by the Better Chinatown Society — begin at 1 p.m., with a parade at Mott and Hester Streets that will make its way to Broome and Grand Streets, where it will join a festival of music, dance and martial arts in Sara D. Roosevelt Park (Chrystie Street between Delancey and Broome Streets, Lower East Side). More information is at betterchinatown.com.

Malaysian Celebration in Bryant Park (Wednesday and Thursday) Some of the spicy dishes on sale at this celebration of Malaysian cuisine and culture may help take the edge off a frosty day. Sponsored by the Malaysian External Trade Development Corporation, the event features cooking demonstrations, dance performances and a food market. Dishes will range from $4 to $8. From 11:30 a.m. to 2:30 p.m. and 5:30 to 9 p.m., Avenue of the Americas, at 40th Street, (212) 768-4242, bryantpark.org, malaysiakitchennyc.com.

‘Milt Hinton: Inside Jazz’ (through March 4) The acclaimed jazz bassist, who died in 2000 at 90, was also an accomplished photographer who began taking informal photographs of musicians at recording studios and other locations beginning in the mid-1930s. Thirty of his photographs — part of a collection with thousands of images — are on view in the Living Room Gallery of St. Peter’s Lutheran Church, Lexington Avenue, at 54th Street. Among those whose portraits are featured are Cab Calloway, Dizzy Gillespie, Billie Holiday and Thelonious Monk. The free exhibition is the first of two events at the church — noted for its jazz vesper services and its devotion to jazz and jazz musicians — in celebration of Hinton’s life and music. On March 3, “Prez Fest 2013,” dedicated to the musician, will feature a documentary screening, a panel discussion and performances. More information is available at (212) 935-2200; saintpeters.org/events. The exhibition can be viewed during church hours, daily from 8 a.m. to 9 p.m.

My Favorite Enemy in Concert (Friday) A band of international musicians will do something that their respective political leaders often find hard to do — play together in harmony. My Favorite Enemy, made up of American, Israeli, Jordanian, Palestinian and Norwegian musicians, sings in English, Arabic and Hebrew. The band’s performance at Congregation Rodeph Sholom, will be its first in New York City; it made its United States debut this month in Washington and has appeared in the Middle East and Norway. Members of the congregation will also perform with the band during the 6 p.m. shabbat service, which is free and open to the public. 7 West 83rd Street, (646) 454-3030, rodephsholom.org.

Plays Commemorating Black History Month (Friday through Sunday) This series, featuring theatrical works that highlight noteworthy figures in black history, continues with “He Who Endures,” a play by Bill Harris about Frederick Douglass, the social reformer and abolitionist, at the National Black Theater, 2031 Fifth Avenue, at 125th Street. A discussion on the influence of Douglass and John Brown on the abolitionist movement will follow the Sunday matinee and feature a panel composed of Louis A. DeCaro Jr., an assistant professor of history at Alliance Theological Seminary and the author of “John Brown: The Man Who Lived”; Larry Lawrence, chairman of the John Brown Historical Society; and Herb Boyd, a journalist and educator. The last in the series, a play about W.E.B. Du Bois, is scheduled for Feb. 22 to 24. The plays are presented by the National Black Touring Circuit and Voza Rivers/New Heritage Theater Group. Friday and Saturday at 7:30 p.m., Sunday at 3 p.m., (212) 279-4200, tinyurl.com/be965dh; $20.

‘Seis del Sur: Dispatches From Home by Six Nuyorican Photographers’ (through March 8) The South Bronx of the 1980s is the subject of this photography show at the Bronx Documentary Center. Among the photographers whose works are on display are two staff members of The New York Times: Ángel Franco, a photographer, and David Gonzalez, a reporter for the Metropolitan desk. The other photographers are Joe Conzo, Jr., Ricky Flores, Edwin Pagán and Francisco Molina Reyes II. Thursdays and Fridays from 4 to 7 p.m., Saturdays and Sundays from 1 to 5 p.m., 614 Courtlandt Avenue, at 151st Street, (347) 332-6962, bronxdoc.org; free.

‘Suddenly Last Summer’ Performance and Talk-back (Saturday) This series of talks with the director and cast following the 8 p.m. performance of this Tennessee Williams play continues for the next two Saturdays at the Alexander Technique Center for Performance and Development, 330 West 38th Street. Austin Pendleton, the play’s director, is also featured in the cast. brownpapertickets.com/event/312074; $15, $10 for students.

Spoken Word

Lecture on Outsider Art (Tuesday) The history and religious significance of art produced by those outside the mainstream art world — specifically artists with autism or mental illness — will be discussed by Mark Lilla, an author and professor of humanities at Columbia. A specialist in intellectual history, Mr. Lilla’s most recent book is “The Stillborn God: Religion, Politics, and the Modern West” (Knopf, 2007). At 7 p.m., SVA Theater, 333 West 23rd Street, Chelsea, (212) 592-2980, sva.edu; free.

New York University’s Creative Writing Program: Readings by Manuel Gonzales and A.M. Homes (Friday) The two writers will read from their recent books at 5 p.m. Ms. Homes, an alumna of New York University’s writing program, will read from “May We Be Forgiven,” released by Viking last fall. Manuel Gonzales’s “Miniature Wife and Other Stories” was released by Riverhead last month. Lillian Vernon Creative Writers House, 58 West 10th Street, Greenwich Village, (212) 998-8816, cwp.fas.nyu.edu; free.

92nd Street Y: Conversations with Vera Wang and Peter Yarrow (Tuesday) It may be difficult to choose between these two talks, but there is no wrong decision. At 8 p.m. in the Kaufmann Concert Hall, Ms. Wang will talk about her career in the fashion industry with Fern Mallis, host of the radio show “Fashion Insiders With Fern Mallis” on Sirius XM. The conversation will also be streamed live on 92y.org/livecast. At 8:15 p.m. in Buttenwieser Hall, Mr. Yarrow will talk about his life as a musician and a member of Peter, Paul & Mary. John Platt, a disc jockey and program director at WFUV, the Fordham University radio station, will lead the discussion. 1395 Lexington Avenue, (212) 415-5500, 92y.org; $29 to $60, $15 for those 35 and younger.

Karen Russell at the Greenlight Bookstore (Monday) Ms. Russell, the author of “Swamplandia!,” will talk about her new short story collection, “Vampires in the Lemon Grove,” released by Knopf this month. At 7:30 p.m., 686 Fulton Street, at South Portland Avenue, Fort Greene, Brooklyn, (718) 246-0200, greenlightbookstore.com; free.

Symphony Space: Selected Shorts with Lorrie Moore and Sherman Alexie (Wednesday) The two writers will introduce and discuss their most recent books — Ms. Moore’s “Gate at the Stairs” and Mr. Alexie’s “Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian” — which will be given dramatic readings by actors including Cynthia Nixon, Amber Tamblyn and Jill Eikenberry. At 7:30 p.m., 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street, (212) 864-5400, symphonyspace.org; $28, $24 for members, $15 for those 30 and younger.

‘The Worlds of Ntozake Shange’ (Friday) This free event focusing on the writing of Ms. Shange, author of the 1976 Broadway play “For Colored Girls Who Have Considered Suicide/When the Rainbow Is Enuf,” continues with discussions about her writing and the arts of the African diaspora, sponsored by the Africana Studies Program at Barnard College. Ms. Shange, a Barnard alumna, will attend the day’s events. From 10 a.m. to 6 p.m., Barnard College, Diana Center, 3009 Broadway, between 116th and 120th Streets, Morningside Heights, (646) 854-6146, barnard.edu.

Walking Tours

‘Union Square: Crossroads of New York’ (Saturday) A tour focusing on the area’s political history meets at 2 p.m. by the statue of Lincoln in Union Square Park, near the 16th Street transverse. Sponsored by the Union Square Partnership. (212) 517-1826, unionsquarenyc.org; free.

Washington’s Birthday Walking Tours (Saturday through Monday) Tours focusing on the city’s connection to presidents of the past and the not-so-distant past are the big draw this weekend. Ghosts of New York is offering two walks in Lower Manhattan with George Washington as the linchpin: a “Presidential Promenade” on Sunday at 2 p.m., with stories about Andrew Jackson, Calvin Coolidge, Richard M. Nixon and others, and a search for Washington’s ghost along with those of Walt Whitman, Captain Kidd and Edgar Allan Poe on Monday at 2 p.m. Each meets at the Starbucks at 38 Park Row, at Spruce Street, and is $30 in person or $25 with credit card payment online; reservations, (646) 493-7092; ghostsofny.com. The professional haunts of George Washington and other figures of the Revolutionary War are the focus of a tour on Monday at 1 p.m. led by Big Onion Walking Tour; it will meet at City Hall Park, at Broadway and Murray Street; the fee is $20, $15 for students and 63+; information: (212) 439-1090, bigonion.com. And, if you want to see where presidents and presidential hopefuls hoisted an ale or two, a tour of historic pubs and taverns of Union Square and Gramercy Park will be led by NYC Discovery Tours on Saturday at 5 p.m.; the fee is $20, not including drinks. For reservations and meeting place: (212) 465-3331. ANNE MANCUSO
