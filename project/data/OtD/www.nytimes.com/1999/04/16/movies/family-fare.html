FAMILY FARE

By Laurel Graeber
Published: April 16, 1999

From White Cells To Weimaraners

It's not often that you see a film whose heroine is a beautiful white blood cell. Or animation inspired by Kafka. But these works and more will be offered this weekend in the BAMkids Film Festival, presented at the Brooklyn Academy of Music.

''The idea of combining the new and the known drove all the choices,'' said Jayme Koszyn, the academy's director of education and humanities. ''We wanted to include imaginative vintage films that kids of this generation might not have seen.''

The festival will screen ''National Velvet'' (1944) tomorrow and ''The Prince and the Pauper'' (1937) on Sunday. Its new selections will include ''Chasing the Kidneystone,'' a Norwegian film about a little boy who magically enters his ill grandfather's body, and ''My Friend Joe,'' an American and Irish feature in which a young circus performer (Schuyler Fisk, the daughter of the actress Sissy Spacek) disguises herself as a boy. There's even a combination of new and old: ''The Wind in the Willows,'' a Monty Python version of a classic tale.

''I'm hoping we'll open new worlds for kids,'' said Ms. Koszyn, who was inspired by the Chicago International Children's Film Festival, the co-producer of the BAMkids event. Organized by Nicole Dreiske, who works for the Chicago group, the Brooklyn festival will show 29 movies from 18 countries, including one directed to 4- to 8-year-olds: ''Tic Tac,'' a Spanish film about time.

When foreign films are shown, actors will read the subtitles aloud. ''Kids who speak the film's language will get headsets to hear just the soundtrack,'' Ms. Koszyn said.

The festival will conclude on Sunday with a special guest appearance after ''The Hardly Boys,'' a detective spoof. ''Not only are they not the Hardy Boys, but they're dogs and they're girls,'' Ms. Koszyn said.

You don't need to be a sleuth to guess the identity of the director: it's William Wegman, who will arrive with two Weimaraners: Batty, a starlet, and Chip.

BAMkids Film Festival, tomorrow and Sunday, starting at 10 A.M., at the BAM Rose Cinemas, 30 Lafayette Avenue, Fort Greene, (718) 623-4100. Tickets: $5 per program; $8.50 for two programs on the same day; (718) 623-2770.

Solving Mysteries

The detective work that takes place in a library is usually pretty sedate: Just where is that elusive information? Tomorrow, however, at least one book-filled institution will play host to investigators other than scholars. In ''Mysteries at the Morgan,'' a family day at the Morgan Library, literary sleuths like Sherlock Holmes and his sidekick, Watson, will study clues instead of words.

Why offer a mystery program? Elementary, my dear reader. The activities relate directly to two of the Morgan's exhibitions: ''Detectives, Private Eyes and Spies,'' a group of early editions by crime writers, and ''Poe: The Ardent Imagination,'' which focuses on the originator of the detective genre, Edgar Allan Poe.

''The whole afternoon is hosted by a number of characters,'' said Rebecca Gantwerk, the library's public programs coordinator. Mrs. Peacock, for example, from the board game Clue, will lead children on a treasure hunt to search for hidden animal images in the Morgan's collections. Later, the actors portraying the characters will present ''The Case of the Missing Page,'' a play in which ''the kids will have to help solve the mystery of an illuminated manuscript,'' Ms. Gantwerk said.

Other highlights will include a workshop to make magnifying glasses and disguises, a costume room in which to don ''detective duds'' and ''cops-and-robbers'' silent films, with piano accompaniment. And, yes, refreshments for hungry gumshoes.

''Mysteries at the Morgan,'' tomorrow from 2 to 5 P.M. at the Morgan Library, 29 East 36th Street, Manhattan, (212) 685-0008, Ext. 426. Free with suggested admission: adults, $7; students and the elderly, $5; ages 12 and under, free.

How Pip Made It

Young theater fans are probably much less familiar with Philip Pirrip, better known as Pip, than with his fellow orphan and Dickensian creation, Oliver Twist. Hardly a season goes by without a dramatization of Oliver's life, with or without singing and dancing pickpockets.

But now the Heights Players, a Brooklyn community troupe, is redressing that imbalance with ''Great Expectations,'' an adaptation by Robert Johanson of Dickens's novel.

The play chronicles Pip's rise from a poor boy, dazzled by wealth and privilege, to a well-off young man who learns that his world is not what it seems. With its rueful ending and universe of moral relativism, ''Great Expectations'' is not a show you'd expect to see at the Theater at Madison Square Garden anytime soon. Yet it offers its own bittersweet rewards, which the cast, directed by Bill Wood, richly delivers.

Jason Roos makes a convincing adult Pip, who is both narrator and leading man. Growing up in the home of his sister (Rachel Millett) and her husband, Joe (Patrick Holohan), Pip is taken under the wing of Miss Havisham (Suzanne Hall-Fritsch), a rich eccentric. Longing to be a gentleman able to woo Miss Havisham's ward, Estella (Shana Scudder), Pip wins the help of an anonymous benefactor, whose identity is the central mystery -- and irony -- of Dickens's tale.

Because of its dense plot, the play may be difficult for children under 8. But older ones will have their own expectations more than fulfilled.

''Great Expectations,'' tonight and tomorrow at 8 P.M. and Sunday at 2 P.M., at the Heights Players, 26 Willow Place, Brooklyn Heights, (718) 237-2752. Tickets: adults, $10; children and the elderly, $8.
