After Flood of Donations, 15 More Students Are Awarded Times Scholarships

By MONTE WILLIAMS
Published: April 7, 1999

In the month since The New York Times published an article about six New York City high school students who won Times scholarships for high achievement in the face of overwhelming obstacles, private donors have contributed more than $150,000 -- enough to send 15 more scholarship finalists to college.

Dozens of readers have written and called the newspaper, asking how they could give more help to the six original recipients and also pay for scholarships for the other finalists, who had persevered in the face of similar odds.

To absorb the contributions, The Times has set up The New York Times College Scholarship Fund, a charitable vehicle for people seeking to make tax-deductible contributions for students chosen by the scholarship program.

Most of the contributors have pledged to continue their donations through four years of college, if the recipients maintain good grades. The scholarships range from $5,000 to $12,000 a year.

''The response is remarkable,'' said Arthur Sulzberger Jr., publisher of The Times and chairman of the Times Company. ''It's stunning and heartwarming. We knew we could only finance a handful of the winners, but now many more will get the benefit of going to a good college.

''One of the great joys of The New York Times,'' he added, ''is that the audience is very responsive to what appears in the paper.''

Since the article appeared, on March 3, readers have called to offer not only money but also advice, shopping trips, weekend getaways and even psychotherapy for the original recipients. All of the students excelled academically despite having to contend with extreme poverty, and in some cases with homelessness, drug-addicted parents and memories of fleeing their home countries because of political or religious persecution.

The State University of New York Maritime College, in the Bronx, offered a full scholarship to one of the finalists.

Among the contributors was Timothy Mellon, co-owner of Guilford Transportation Industries, a rail freight and airline company in New England, who gave $72,000, more than any other donor.

Steven Klinsky, a general partner of Forstmann Little & Company, a Manhattan investment firm, and his wife, Maureen Sherry, donated $24,000. ''We read the article,'' said Mr. Klinsky, ''and thought, 'What a tremendous group of teen-agers!' But there were similar students and there was not enough to help every one of them. That led to the decision to help.''

George Knox, an executive at Philip Morris, and his wife, Gail, gave $12,000. ''We read the article in The Times, and we were struck by the courage and the perseverance of these kids,'' Mr. Knox said. ''We had been giving to schools. But we saw this as a way to personalize our gift.''

John Tremaine gave $12,000 from the Emily Hall Tremaine Foundation, a family foundation based in Meriden, Conn. Mr. Tremaine and his wife, Susan, run a lighting design firm and a lighting manufacturing business. ''I believe in George Bush's thousand points of light,'' Mr. Tremaine explained. ''We can help the less fortunate succeed.''

Some donors chose to remain anonymous. Others included Dr. William Cohen, a retired internist who practiced in Waterbury, Conn. Dr. Cohen gave $5,000. ''When I was young I was the recipient of a college scholarship, and it changed my life,'' he said. ''I was never as desperately poor as these kids, but I was poor enough to know I couldn't afford to go to school outside the city.''

Sue-ann Friedman and her husband, Michael Finkelstein, vice chairman of SBS Broadcasting, also gave $12,000. The couple have six children, all but one in college or professional schools.

''We are surrounded by needy kids,'' Ms. Friedman said, laughing.

She added that she had been particularly moved by the story of Elizabeth Murray, who will graduate at the top of her class after only two years in high school. During most of those two years, she was homeless, living with friends or on the street. Now she has been accepted at New York University and is on the waiting list for admission to Harvard.

''It can't be easy to go to Harvard and then go home to live in the hallway of a Bronx apartment,'' Ms. Friedman said.

In addition to Miss Murray, from the Humanities Preparatory Academy in Greenwich Village, the original scholarship recipients were QiQi Cheng from Newtown High School in Queens; Denise De Las Nueces from Cathedral High School on the East Side of Manhattan; Mirela Miraj from Grover Cleveland High School in Queens; Anahad O'Connor from the High School for Leadership and Public Service in lower Manhattan, and LeTicia Williams from Martin Van Buren High School in Queens.
