Teeming Isolation

By Wendy Steiner
Published: May 9, 1999

SEA BATTLES ON DRY LAND

Essays.

By Harold Brodkey.

452 pp. New York:

Metropolitan Books/Henry Holt & Company. $30.

TO be a writer means positioning yourself among strangers, and readers are left to judge the skill of the maneuver. Sometimes the mastery is immediate and exquisite. Ezra Pound shifts ''the apparition of these faces in the crowd'' into ''petals on a wet, black bough'' in the blink of a line break. But other writers stall at the gate. In ''Sea Battles on Dry Land,'' Harold Brodkey mires his essays, profiles and reviews in bogs of sympathy -- or the peculiar variety he calls ''Manhattan empathy . . . almost semibrotherhood.''

Brodkey's version of ''In a Station of the Metro'' is a 1987 New Yorker essay, ''The Subway at Christmas,'' in which blank-eyed commuters suddenly come together: ''We were a party, or were like a party in the sense of being a group of knowing people linked for a moment and enclosed with one another in a place that is not home and noticing one another but, of course, not smiling or addressing one another.'' Swept up in such experiences of ''almost semibrotherhood,'' Brodkey never questions whether his brothers share his thoughts. In another New Yorker piece, he is looking out of his high-rise at construction workers across the street. When the Mets score in a playoff game, he senses a cheer rising up all over the city: ''Invisible and scattered shouters, midair, we're all up here at the cr me-frache level of the skyline, suspendees . . . companions in New York unlikelihood.'' These scenes of unwonted -- and unverified -- solidarity are Brodkey's epiphanies, redeeming the emotional minimalism of Manhattan existence with intimations of community.

If being a New Yorker means living a life among strangers, Brodkey, who died in 1996, just as often presents himself as the friend, the relative, the lover of us all. To go by an essay called ''Family,'' his contains so many halfs and ex's and in-laws that ''six degrees of separation'' is a hermit's state by comparison. ''I believe we are nearly everybody,'' he decides. And for those days when the Brodkey family is not quite the family of man, Brodkey the reader has whole libraries of lovers. ''Reading a good book is not much different from a love affair,'' he remarks in another essay, ''complete with shyness and odd assertions of power and of independence.''

Anonymous in crowds or suspended in skyscrapers, New Yorkers know that belonging is the supreme value. But belonging may entail hard, undemocratic exclusions. Thus against the oceanic yearnings of Brodkey the universal lover we hear the superb desperation of Brodkey the coterie man, dedicated to the belief that the coteries he belongs to are the best. They're the best because they're ''real'' -- and they're real because New York is the only real city. Brodkey should know, because he came (like Eliot, Fitzgerald, Hemingway) from the Midwest. ''New York was scary for a while for a hick like me,'' he confesses in an essay about Frank O'Hara. But then O'Hara ''asked if I wanted to see artists, the real people of the real New York.'' The advantages of this realism were immediate. ''New York was the capital of American sexuality,'' the one place where you could have sex ''with some degree of sophistication.'' Though Brodkey thinks he did not succumb to O'Hara (apparently it was hard to be sure), he does seem to have accepted the poet's view of the city. ''Frank believed that the bohemian Village housed almost the entire truth quotient in American life.'' The inhabitants of many Bohemian villages probably believe the same thing, but then they lack the benefit of O'Hara's sophistication.

Brodkey deplores the condescension of politicians to the little people, but still likes his friends big. Perhaps he learned to negotiate this contradiction from his editor at The New Yorker, William Shawn, whom he celebrates in a 1993 New York Observer essay for his ''promise of a certain egalitarian superiority if you lived right and were sensitive.'' Well yes, ''egalitarian superiority'' is the ticket, rather like democratic exceptionalism, or ''Manhattan empathy,'' for that matter. Those like Woody Allen (the subject of a previously unpublished 1992 essay) who are egalitarianly superior, sometimes get into unfortunate scrapes with their adoptive daughters, for ''certain sorts of behavior are within the range of the normal for a movie star and not for us.'' But maybe this ''us'' is really ''you.'' Brodkey understands both stardom and adoption. ''Being adopted is a more romantic situation than being a blood child. . . . The love isn't based on genetic duty. Instead, choice and exoticism, foreignness, are there from the start. . . . Emotions are acted out in good performances. Or in weak ones.''

An adopted child and a New Yorker by adoption, Brodkey feels the exoticism of strangers, public figures, images. ''A close-up, like a glamour photo,'' he writes in an essay called ''American Movies and Sex and Nazis and Moral Questions,'' ''is an image of desolate isolation which elicits infatuation.'' His profiles of movie and television stars -- Woody Allen, Marlon Brando, Carol Burnett -- are extraordinary exhortations to adore the isolated. If, Brodkey tells us, Allen is following Hemingway in ''prettifying'' aspects of his masculinity, then ''you can feel from your own experiences and with your own powers of sympathetic projection the polarities of Allen's sexual reality in relation to women.'' If we cannot, we know at least that Brodkey can, and that is the point. His empathy is star-quality. After all, he learned his masculinity from Brando. With Burnett, he bullies her until she echoes his point of view. ''At times,'' he exults, ''she spoke in the sentence structure and inflections that I use.''

This collection does contain some very good sentences -- Winchell's idea of a story was Jezebel in five lines'' -- but it also has some that are unspeakable: ''A car simply is too weak and too complex to be a good symbol, since neither does it plow, and it does not weep either.'' Brodkey's philosophizing alternates between deconstruction-rivaling nonsense and delusional pieties: ''The purpose of what I do is a more equitable and flexible language and a just world, of course; an increase in the spirit of charity among us; greater happiness for a widening number of people; and an enlargement of one's sense of amusement and interest in life.'' After such knowledge, what forgiveness? But there we are -- unsympathetic readers. Brodkey would forgive. He had Manhattan empathy.
