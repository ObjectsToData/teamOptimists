Choice of Clinton to Give Humanities Lecture Meets Resistance

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY
Published: September 21, 1999

Since 1972, the National Endowment for the Humanities has selected a leading scholar to give its annual Jefferson Lecture on the Humanities. Past recipients of the honor have included Lionel Trilling, Robert Penn Warren, John Hope Franklin, Sidney Hook, Toni Morrison, Gwendolyn Brooks, Gertrude Himmelfarb and Barbara Tuchman.

Today, the chairman of the endowment, William R. Ferris, said the lecturer in 2000 would be President Clinton. Scholars were not happy.

John Hammer, the director of the National Humanities Alliance, said he was concerned that the choice of Mr. Clinton, who appointed Mr. Ferris chairman, would politicize the endowment, a Federal agency. Mr. Clinton is the first President to be asked to deliver the lecture.

''The list of lecturers until now has been very distinguished, without regard to politics,'' Mr. Hammer said.

John H. D'Arms, president of the American Council of Learned Societies, told The Chronicle of Higher Education, which first reported the selection of Mr. Clinton on its Web page today, that he was also concerned.

''At a time when one is very careful about trying to depoliticize the humanities and the arts in the United States in the eyes of Congress,'' Mr. D'Arms told The Chronicle, a ''speech by President Clinton might have the reverse effect.''

Mr. Ferris defended the choice. ''You take a little heat when you make a change,'' he said in an interview.

The purpose of inviting Mr. Clinton, he said, was to begin a tradition in which all Presidents would deliver a Jefferson Lecture.

''We are expanding the endowment to reach the whole country,'' he said. ''We're opening all the doors and windows. Having the President reflect on his or her relationship to the history of the nation is part of that.''

Because history is one of the endowment's main components, the endowment has helped to finance many Presidential history projects, Mr. Ferris said.

When asked if what he was doing would be considered an injection of politics, Mr. Ferris replied: ''In a way it is. We're a political reality. We depend on Congress and the White House for our very existence.''

But any politicizing, Mr. Ferris insisted, was bipartisan because the Clinton invitation would set a precedent to be followed in future Administrations, regardless of party.

One former chairman of the endowment, William J. Bennett, said the invitation to Mr. Clinton was not only a politicization of the endowment, but a partisan one as well.

''This is horrible, corrupt, but it fits the pattern of this Administration,'' said Mr. Bennett, a conservative Republican who served in the Reagan Administration.

''Bipartisan?'' Mr. Bennett asked. ''I don't think so. The Clinton-appointed chairman picks the guy who picks him to give the lecture, a guy who has no qualifications, and calls it bipartisan and broadening.''

Mr. Bennett added, ''Clinton has not only been corrupted; he has corrupted all of those around him, and this is an example of that.''

Mr. Ferris said of Mr. Bennett's criticism, ''It's short-sighted to couch this as a partisan issue.''

''This is going to create a historical record,'' he said. ''We will have a bipartisan record.''

Mr. Ferris said Mr. Clinton, a friend, had not answered the invitation by this evening but that he expected the President to accept. The White House did not respond to a call seeking comment. The lecture is given in the spring in Washington.

When asked how Mr. Clinton could be regarded as a scholar, Mr. Ferris said: ''The endowment's founding language calls for bringing the humanities to the American people. No matter how visible the lecturer is, very few Americans have ever heard of the Jefferson Lecture. This will bring it to every American. People will become aware of what the humanities are. It will broaden the humanities and touch the lives of all citizens.''

Mr. Ferris acknowledged that President Clinton, whose advanced degree is in law, was not a scholar in the traditional sense, adding, ''But he is in the Jeffersonian sense that every citizen has the right to express their thoughts about the nature of democracy.''

Asked whether the typical President would not simply assign the speech-writing task to an aide, Mr. Ferris said he thought each President would write every humanities speech, seeing it as an opportunity to express his philosophy.

Mr. Hammer, the director of the National Humanities Alliance, said: ''I understand they are hoping it will provide more publicity. I imagine that's true.''
