BOOKS OF THE TIMES; A Lossless Fractal Algorithm? Some Other Time

By CHRISTOPHER LEHMANN-HAUPT
Published: November 18, 1999

TIMELINE

By Michael Crichton

Illustrated. 450 pages. Alfred A. Knopf. $26.95.

An elderly man is found wandering alone in the Arizona desert, suffering from mysterious physical defects and babbling incoherently about ''quantum foam.'' As he lies dying in a hospital, it's learned that he possesses a detailed ground plan of a 14th-century French monastery and that he works for a New Mexico-based company that specializes in quantum technology.

The company is run by a brilliant but supremely arrogant physicist who has already made a fortune developing ''new new'' technology and is impatient to rush to completion a project he is backing to reconstruct a medieval community in the Dordogne section of France. When he pressures the team of Yale historians researching the site, its members are astonished to learn that the ground plan found in the possession of the babbling man in the desert contains more information about the monastery than they have come up with.

After questioning how the company knows so much, the professor who heads the Yale team is flown to New Mexico for further briefing. A day or so later the professor's assistants dig up at the Dordogne site some 650-year-old parchments wrapped in oilskin. Among them is a message in the professor's hand, written in ink that also proves to be six centuries old. It says, ''Help me'' and is dated 4/7/1357.

So begins ''Timeline,'' Michael Crichton's latest exercise in exploiting technological possibility and imagining its Frankenstein's-monster consequences, in this case the horror of traveling back in time and getting caught in the middle of the Hundred Years' War.

As with Mr. Crichton's previous thrillers, like ''The Andromeda Strain'' and ''Jurassic Park,'' the neatest thing about the plot is the ingenious way he gets it started. According to one perfectly plausible view of quantum reality, a character in ''Timeline'' explains, an infinite number of universes impinge on one another, each containing a version of reality, some of them earlier versions of ours.

So what is thought of as time travel is actually space travel, a sort of faxing of a human being into a universe containing a past version of our own. Any cracks in this logic are filled by Mr. Crichton with sentences like ''To be precise, we use quantum technology to manipulate an orthogonal multiverse coordinate change,'' or ''We compress it'' -- that is, the information about the human form being sent -- ''by using a lossless fractal algorithm.'' O.K., so we believe it.

And as is usual with Mr. Crichton's thrillers, the story doesn't quite live up to the idea that gets it rolling. Three of the professor's grad students are faxed over to fish him out of the 14th century. The machinery breaks down. The Yalies have to run a dangerous obstacle course before they can come back, featuring warring English and French armies, jousting knights, plague-infested rats and all the other fun things that prompted Barbara Tuchman, the historian, to pronounce the 14th century calamitous in her book ''A Distant Mirror.''

In ''Timeline'' plenty of action takes place, the pages begin to turn themselves, but everything seems a little helter-skelter.

Still, Mr. Crichton makes a number of imaginative points along the way. When the time-space travelers first arrive in 14th-century Dordogne, one of them is struck by the fact that he doesn't hear anything. ''That's right,'' a more experienced character explains. ''There's no ambient noise here: no radio or TV, no airplanes, no machinery, no passing cars. In the 20th century, we're so accustomed to hearing sound all the time, the silence feels creepy.''

Later, impressed by the fury of two knights practicing swordplay, another character reflects that he had ''imagined a slower fighting style'' only ''from an unconscious assumption that men in the past were weaker or slower or less imaginative than he was, as a modern man.'' Again and again Mr. Crichton's story makes the point that the past doesn't think of itself as being the past.

Elsewhere, another character grows increasingly impressed by the emptiness of his graduate-school debates over ''whether formalist criteria outweighed objectivist criteria, whether derivationist doctrine concealed normative commitment.'' This relates to the bright side of the arrogant company president's ambitions, which is to apply technology to the humanities and make it possible to practice a discipline called experimental history.

Alas, his plans go awry when his probes of the past reveal that Lincoln delivered the Gettysburg Address in a voice that sounds ''like Betty Boop'' and Washington, crossing the Delaware, was ''huddled in the back of the boat,'' not good advertisements for what the company is trying to sell.

Mr. Crichton himself ends up with a problem in advertising. In his concluding acknowledgments he writes of the medieval period: ''An age that was once thought to be static, brutal and benighted is now understood as dynamic and swiftly changing: an age where knowledge was sought and valued; where universities were born, and learning fostered; where technology was enthusiastically advanced; where social relations were in flux; where trade was international; where the general level of violence was often less deadly than it is today.''

This is contrary to the view of a number of books listed in his extensive bibliography, not least among them Ms. Tuchman's aforementioned ''Distant Mirror: The Calamitous 14th Century,'' which was written in part to reassure its readers that things in the 20th century were not uniquely awful.

Most emphatically is it at odds with Mr. Crichton's own cheerily nihilistic portrait of the 14th century, where life is not only nasty and brutish but also as short as that of a condemned man with his head on the chopping block.
