AN EPITAPH FOR THE 20th CENTURY; A Time of Energy And Enterprise And E-Everything

By REED ABELSON
Published: December 20, 1999

When eight well-known executives were asked to sum up the most important characteristics of the last 100 years in an epitaph for the 20th century, they invariably talked about the powerful impact of technology in all its myriad forms: the airplane, television, computers and the Internet.

''Before this century, science did not matter to the average person,'' said Franklin D. Raines, the chairman and chief executive of Fannie Mae, the world's largest nonbank financial services company. ''In this century, science is changing our lives fundamentally.''

But as much as these business leaders, who hailed from entertainment and financial services and industry, praised the huge advances in technology over the last 100 years, they expressed concern about how technology could be used and the double-edged nature of change.

The dark side of the passing century was hinted at by the comic creator Stan Lee's rendition of Mongorr, a despot eager to infiltrate our world to take advantage of the new technology. But, in as epic terms as any battle waged between superheroes, Robert E. Rubin, the former Treasury secretary, also talked about the stark contrasts of the last 100 years: the huge advances made and the devastation wrought by the ability of people to hurt one another. ''Human nature has remained a constant,'' he concluded.

Many of the executives were also quick to note the substantial divide that exists between those who have benefited from the prosperity of the last 100 years and those who have not. ''The haves and have-nots are getting further apart,'' said Lawrence A. Bossidy, the chairman of Honeywell, the industrial giant created by the recent merger of Honeywell, the maker of electronic controls, and AlliedSignal, a diversified manufacturer.

As the chairman of Stan Lee Media, a publicly held company based in Encino, Calif., Mr. Lee is participating directly in the revolution being created by the Internet. ''I've been in comics, radio, television, animation, movies and now the Internet,'' said Mr. Lee, who is one of the people responsible for such memorable superheroes as Spider-Man and the Fantastic Four and plans to bring a new generation of different superheroes and villains to the Internet in short animated episodes.

Television and the Internet, in particular, have achieved enormous global reach, Mr. Lee says. People who once lived in isolation are exposed as never before to what is going on in the rest of the world. ''There are no hicks anymore,'' he said.

But Mr. Lee is genuinely worried about how this technology is used. Like Mongorr, one of Mr. Lee's new characters, there are those who may use the Internet to harness hatred. ''I'm hoping that the closer we come together, the more that will fade away,'' he said.

When Mr. Bossidy of Honeywell thinks about the 20th century, he ticks off the numerous advances in electronics: the cathode ray tube in televisions, the microchip and the various iterations of computers that allow work to be done rapidly, leading to huge improvements in productivity.

The advances in medicine -- the cure of diseases like scarlet fever and new treatments for heart disease and cancer -- are also striking, Mr. Bossidy said. ''People feel better; they don't just live longer,'' he said.

But the role of the corporation has also undergone significant change, he argued, as companies have become much more efficient. ''Corporations used to be run exclusively by dictators,'' said Mr. Bossidy, who spent more than three decades at General Electric before joining AlliedSignal, where he became chief executive in 1991. ''Now you have to lead and be prepared to serve,'' he said, and employees have the freedom to contribute.

Increasingly, governments and other organizations will rely on a corporate model to improve how they operate, according to Mr. Bossidy. He also believes that companies will be asked more and more to address social issues like education, not in privatizing them but in marshaling resources.

But Mr. Bossidy was wary of trying to guess what other innovations the next century might bring. Just as few foresaw the Internet, ''most of the changes in the 21st century will not be predicted,'' he argued.

Mr. Rubin, who is as well known on Wall Street as he is in Washington, takes an even-handed approach in assessing the century. Once a top official at Goldman Sachs, he is now the chairman of the executive committee of the board for Citigroup, the nation's largest financial services company. ''It has been a period of tremendous advance,'' Mr. Rubin said, noting how much of the global population has benefited from technology. But he is quick to point out the last century's episodes of war and violence as well as the use of technology for mass destruction.

Globalization has also remarkably changed how we live, he noted. ''I think we are far more interdependent today than we were 100 years ago,'' economically, environmentally and geopolitically, Mr. Rubin said. He, too, is concerned about the gulf between those who are enjoying this century's tremendous prosperity and those who are not but understand what they are missing.
