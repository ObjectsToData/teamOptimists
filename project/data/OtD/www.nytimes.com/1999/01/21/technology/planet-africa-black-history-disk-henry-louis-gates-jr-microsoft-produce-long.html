Planet Africa: Black History on Disk; Henry Louis Gates Jr. and Microsoft Produce A Long-Awaited, Much-Debated Encyclopedia

By MICHEL MARRIOTT
Published: January 21, 1999

IN 1995, Henry Louis Gates Jr., a Harvard professor and perhaps the most widely recognized African-American scholar today, cast himself in the role of fast-talking encyclopedia salesman. Dr. Gates, who had begun dreaming of creating a comprehensive encyclopedia of the black world two decades earlier, found himself in New York presenting his proposal to executives from the giant publishing company Random House. But before he could finish his pitch, the company's chief executive, Alberto Vitale, asked him if the encyclopedia could be done as a multimedia CD-ROM, rather than in the conventional print-only manner.

''Of course,'' was Dr. Gates's response. The only hitch: he had no idea what a CD-ROM was.

But Dr. Gates is a famously quick study. And the result is the Encarta Africana, a new electronic encyclopedia that attempts to distill the black experience into two honey-hued CD-ROM disks.

The disks -- crammed with 2.5 million words and more than 2,500 pictures, maps, graphs, sounds and video clips on practically all things of African descent -- reverberate with their subject. A mouse click can send users, even those with modestly powered personal computers, on a cinematic voyage along the Swahili coast, or permit them to retrace centuries of slave routes between the ''dark continent'' and the New World, or simply watch, in stunningly stark video clips, haunting scenes of civil rights marchers being attacked with fire hoses and police dogs. A keystroke can call up a 1929 performance of ''Saint Louis Blues'' belted out by Bessie Smith, or samples of Duke Ellington's elegant redesign of jazz, or the Temptations crooning in their Motown glory years in the 1960's or sounds from hip-hop's humble birth in the Bronx.

''We have finally a technology that can explore and explain the expressive features -- music and dance, art and cinema, literature and story-telling -- that are the heart of cultural achievements of people of African descent,'' Dr. Gates says in a three-minute video clip in the encyclopedia.

There are other CD-ROM collections of some of the sounds, words and deeds of blacks, like last year's Encyclopedia Britannica Profiles Black History. But most of them have been very limited in scope and imagination. By contrast, Encarta Africana, officially released Monday by Microsoft to mark the celebration of Martin Luther King Jr.'s birth, had from its inception the audacious task of chronicling black history and culture stretching back to the first Africans and, for that matter, the first humans, some four million years ago.

Almost for its ambition alone, the project has attracted an enormous amount of attention and some controversy. For Dr. Gates and his co-editor, Kwame Anthony Appiah, a Harvard colleague, the struggle to create the encyclopedia had taken many paths. Some led to dead ends and crushing disappointments. Others met with debate over whether the project was taking an Afrocentric or Eurocentric perspective, and over who actually had the rights to the idea of a black encyclopedia, long a recurring dream among some African-American intellectuals.

In the process of creating the Encarta Africana, Dr. Gates and Dr. Appiah called on more than 400 scholars to research and write more than 3,000 articles that could appeal to everyone from grade-school students to graduate-school students. Dr. Gates also persuaded noted figures like Wole Soyinka, the Nigerian-born recipient of the Nobel Prize in literature, and Secretary General Kofi Annan of the United Nations, to participate in the project. Dr. Gates even called upon his friend Michael Jordon, recently of the N.B.A., to preview the encyclopedia. Mr. Jordan's endorsement -- ''a reference work which every family should own'' -- appears on the encyclopedia's packaging.

Borrowing some of the most popular features from Microsoft's Encarta series, Africana was designed, its makers say, to show as much as tell. Some officials at Microsoft have been describing the encyclopedia, which costs $49.95 after a $20 mail-in rebate that expires at the end of this year, as a groundbreaking marriage of content and technology. One feature, for example, offers users six virtual tours that include the slave castles of Goree, off the coast of Senegal, West Africa, and the Egyptian ruins at Medinet Habu, using 360-degree photographs that rotate with the nudge of a computer mouse.

''The majority of people in the United States are not going to have a chance to make it over to Africa, so we really try to bring you the sights and sounds in a stimulating format,'' said Evette Gee, a Microsoft project manager for Encarta Africana.

The earliest dream of a comprehensive black encyclopedia belonged neither to Professor Gates nor to Bill Gates, but to a man whose life ended long before the advent of personal computers, the Internet and CD-ROM's. In 1909, W.E.B. Du Bois, who was the first black person to earn a Ph.D. from Harvard University and who went on to become an influential American intellectual, writer, social scientist and Pan-Africanist, called for the creation of a black encyclopedia. He said he wanted this work, which he called the Encyclopedia Africana, to tell the complete saga of Africa and its diaspora in Europe, Asia, the Caribbean and the Americas.
