PUBLIC LIVES

By JAMES BARRON with Lawrie Mifflin
Published: January 22, 1999

Weighing Anchor

She went from a daily morning shift to a once-a-week slot on Saturday evenings, playing the trouper without complaint, but PAULA ZAHN has had enough. She is quitting CBS News to become an anchor on the Fox News cable channel.

Did she make the switch because she was not chosen as a correspondent on the new CBS News magazine show ''60 Minutes II?''

''No,'' said her agent, RICHARD LEIBNER. ''With three young children, she was not interested in a job that called for the travel commitment '60 II' would have required.'' (Neither he, CBS nor Fox could find Ms. Zahn for comment yesterday.)

She goes from being the Saturday-evening anchor of the ''CBS Evening News'' to a cable network that reaches far fewer households than CBS. But she will be on twice a day: during the day, at a time not yet chosen, and again from 7 to 8 P.M.

''Very few women have ever been given the dinnertime, hard-news broadcast slot,'' Mr. Leibner said. ''And it's an opportunity to broadcast news twice a day, five days a week, and she has missed that since she came off mornings.''

She spent six years as co-anchor of ''CBS Morning News,'' until the show was given a make-over in mid-1996 and she and HARRY SMITH received new assignments -- she to the Saturday night anchor spot on ''CBS Evening News.'' By coincidence, Mr. Smith left CBS News last week to become anchor of the ''Biography'' series on another cable network, A&E.

Medical Matinee

KATHLEEN CHALFANT usually appears in ''Wit'' -- a play about a professor battling cancer and chemotherapy while reveling in the sonnets of John Donne -- at the Union Square Theater on East 17th Street. But on Tuesday, she and the other members of the cast headed uptown to perform at Cornell University's Weill College of Medicine on York Avenue.

''We were all very nervous doing it for a roomful of doctors and medical students,'' Ms. Chalfant said. ''But we got laughs we don't ordinarily get. We got medical student laughs -- they laughed at things that medical students would find funny that other people don't. There was a huge guffaw at the beginning of the grand rounds scene. They recognized the situation.''

DEBRA GILLERS, the associate dean of academic affairs, said the performance was part of a new series of seminars on humanities and medicine. She also said that Ms. Chalfant had initially asked why the doctors and doctors-in-training should not simply go to the theater. ''My thought was, when you bring it into their world and they see the people responsible for their world -- the faculty, with whom they interact in a very narrow world -- the relevance is more immediate.''

Indeed. ''One of the physicians came up and said, 'I'm surprised you didn't quote the John Donne poem about C.P.R.' '' Ms. Chalfant said. ''I went, 'Huh?' '' He was apparently referring to the sonnet that begins, ''Batter my heart, three-person'd God.''

Painful Memories

Invited to a $500-a-person dinner that raises money for baseball players who did not have million-dollar contracts and do not have million-dollar pensions, JIM PALMER said yes first and asked the question later.

''I was taught by Cal Ripken Sr., and also by the Orioles: if you live in a community, be involved,'' he said. ''Baseball is a community, so this dinner is important. I said, 'By the way, who are you honoring?' ''

The answer was the 1969 Mets, who went from 100-to-1 odds during spring training to World Series champions for the first time in the team's eight-year history.

''I said, 'That's like inviting me to New York to go the dentist and filling the cavities without Novocaine,'' said Mr. Palmer, who pitched Game 3 at Shea Stadium and lost to the likes of TOM SEAVER, JERRY KOOSMAN and TOMMIE AGEE, all of whom attended the dinner on Tuesday at the Marriott Marquis Hotel in Manhattan.

Reciprocation

RICHARD J. SCHWARTZ, an investor who was the chief executive of Jonathan Logan until he negotiated the sale of the company in 1985, is the new chairman of the New York State Council on the Arts. He has been a member of the council's board and a trustee of Lincoln Center and the Westchester Council for the Arts, but his biography did not mention something that worked in his favor in Albany, where there is a long tradition of naming financial backers to honorary posts: He is a major donor to Republican committees and candidates.

So was his predecessor as chairman of the arts council, EARLE I. MACK, who gave $10,000 to Gov. George E. Pataki's campaign last year.
