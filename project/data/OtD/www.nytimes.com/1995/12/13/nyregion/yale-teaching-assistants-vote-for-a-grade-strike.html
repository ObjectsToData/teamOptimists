Yale Teaching Assistants Vote for a Grade Strike

By DAVIDSON GOLDIN
Published: December 13, 1995

An organization of Yale's graduate teaching assistants has voted not to submit grades for this term, raising the possibility that some undergraduates will not receive grades on time and that some seniors might have incomplete transcripts with their graduate school applications.

Yale officials stressed that they would not tolerate a grade strike, and did not rule out the possibility of seeking to expel teaching assistants who withhold grades.

The group, the Graduate Employees Students Organization, has been seeking to become a labor union for graduate students in the humanities and social sciences since 1990. It wants higher teaching stipends, lower health care costs, smaller class sections and their own grievance procedure for complaints against the university. Graduate students normally receive stipends of $4,970 for each semester of undergraduate teaching, and 90 percent of them receive fellowships for full Yale tuition.

As at most large research universities, graduate students at Yale teach and grade undergraduates in almost every discipline. While professors handle most smaller courses, generally known as seminars, graduate students have the main responsibility for teaching discussion sections and grading students enrolled in large lecture courses. In some cases, graduate students teach their own courses.

Because of heavy workloads, the organization said, graduate students spend 25 hours a week on teaching, rather than the 17 1/2 hours the stipend was intended to compensate. "Yale has grown to depend on us for a lot of work," said Thomas Thurston, a Ph.D. candidate in American Studies from Albuquerque, N.M., "and that's where we're in a position of strength."

But Yale has been firm in refusing to negotiate. "The graduate school provides fellowships and stipends, and the students are supposed to grade and teach," said Jonas Zdanys, associate dean of the graduate school. "We don't intend to compensate every single nickel and dime of their cost of living."

It was not clear this week how many students would be affected by a grade strike. Classes ended last week, and final examinations begin Friday.

Professors and administrators said they would do all they could to make sure students received grades. Students said that in some classes, professors had asked them to complete forms listing their grades from earlier tests and papers.

The graduate group is seeking to formally represent Yale's 1,500 graduate students in the humanities and social sciences, 389 of whom are teaching assistants this semester. The organization has threatened a full strike next semester, and leaders of the two unions representing Yale's 3,600 clerical, maintenance and technical workers said they expected to honor any picket line set up by the group. A strike by those unions in 1984 virtually shut the campus down for 10 weeks.

On campus here this week, there seemed to be little support for the grade strike.

"I find it unfortunate, lamentable and completely inappropriate," said Mary Miller, the chairwoman of the art history department. "These graduate students have a responsibility to the undergraduates, and teaching is part of their training as future faculty."

Ms. Miller said that graduate students from her department who participated in the grade strike would probably not be hired again as teaching assistants.

A dozen students studying for final exams in Cross Campus Library were unanimously unsympathetic to the planned grade strike.

"A lot of people sacrifice a great deal to come to Yale, whether it's financially or the amount of work they do," said Darin Johnson, a junior from Neighborville, Ill., who is majoring in political science and economics. "It seems unfair that after all these efforts, that you may not be able to get grades at the end of the semester."
