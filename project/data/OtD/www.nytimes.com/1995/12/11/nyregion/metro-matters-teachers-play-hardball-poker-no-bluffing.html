Metro Matters;Teachers Play Hardball Poker (No Bluffing)

By JOYCE PURNICK
Published: December 11, 1995

AND the moral is: Never underestimate a New Yorker's talent for outpoliticking the politicians.

Why, for the first time in 35 years, did members of the United Federation of Teachers reject a deal negotiated and defended by their leaders? Because they figured they had nothing to lose. And maybe a lot to gain.

A signed contract would have stalled teacher salary increases for two years, anyway. So why not wait it out, they figured? Especially since two years from now both Mayor Rudolph W. Giuliani and Sandra Feldman, the president of the United Federation of Teachers, will be facing re-election.

"They'll be under more pressure then," explained Louis Howort, a teacher at the High School of the Humanities, in the Chelsea section of Manhattan. To Mr. Howort, the union chapter chairman at his school, the contract failed for an obvious reason: "Teachers can sit back and wait two years and break even. Can they?" he asked -- referring to Ms. Feldman and Mr. Giuliani.

Many other teachers at Humanities said much the same last Friday. The school, on West 18th Street, seemed a good place to examine post-vote sentiment, since about 80 percent of the school's teachers voted no, according to Mr. Howort. Citywide, teachers voted 56 to 44 percent against the deal.

THE result caught many by surprise because outsiders judged the five-year settlement to be quite handsome. It included a no-layoff pledge for three years, an 11 percent salary increase (13 percent including benefits) with payouts starting in the third year; relief from cafeteria and hallway duty; a top salary of $70,000, and no significant concessions or "productivity savings," despite the Mayor's pledge to make unions more efficient.

The contract was so far from meeting the standards Mr. Giuliani had set that it drew criticism from the ever-frowning Citizens Budget Commission, from editorial boards, and from this and other columns. But to teachers, the avoidance of "productivity savings" has merely a theoretical ring, while "wage freeze" is Economics 101: the rejected contract meant no new increase for two years, and even after five, New York's teachers would still earn less than those in most neighboring communities.

It was clearly that freeze -- and not the impending raise in the Mayor's own salary -- that killed the contract. Ms. Feldman said Mr. Giuliani's support for his own raise and pay increases for managers and other elected officials "tipped it." But many teachers at Humanities insisted they would have rejected the deal anyway, and complained louder about Ms. Feldman's U.F.T. salary of $156,963. Let Mr. Giuliani and others get their raises, they said. But they want theirs, too, and never mind the city's poverty pleas.

"I AM 64 and I cannot retire until I am 150, not with that contract," complained Hilda Aronson, language coordinator at the High School for the Humanities.

Other teachers also said it would have been unfair to defer 5 percent of every new teacher's salary for four years, and considered the no-layoff pledge an empty gift; with 20,000 new students turning up every year, why would anyone be laid off? Even the relief from cafeteria duty was not appreciated; it was packaged with new authority for principals to tell teachers how to use their free "professional" periods. And since few teachers believe that the Mayor's plan to have welfare recipients patrol cafeterias will ever succeed, they figured they would be risking more than they gained.

The same might be said of the majority's vote last week. Given the Mayor's inordinate fondness for getting his way, the teachers may be risking more than they're counting on. For now, Mr. Giuliani refuses to reopen negotiations. But the rebellious teachers on West 18th Street are convinced that they have the initiative. Their union has 68,000 teachers, and many live and vote in the city. They predict that with his re-election bid looming, Mr. Giuliani will have to listen to them. So will Ms. Feldman.

Several disgruntled teachers consider the union president too chummy with the Mayor, too willing to greet his education cuts with only mild complaints.

Not so, Ms. Feldman has steadily maintained. In any case, she and the Mayor have been exchanging fairly nasty comments since last week. And in a noticeable change in tone, Ms. Feldman's statement about the contract rejection referred to "horrendous" and "horrible" conditions in the schools -- which presumably were horrendous and horrible before last week.

That's politics. So is Mr. Howort's prediction that the teachers will eventually obtain a more attractive deal. "It's because of the election. That is the political reality."

Any New Yorker knows that. Maybe, by now, even the politicians.
