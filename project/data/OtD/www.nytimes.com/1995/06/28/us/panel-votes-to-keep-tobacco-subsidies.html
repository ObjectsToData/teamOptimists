Panel Votes to Keep Tobacco Subsidies

By JERRY GRAY
Published: June 28, 1995

Led by a bipartisan bloc of Southern representatives, the House Appropriations Committee today defeated an attempt to end the 50-year-old Federal Government subsidy to tobacco growers.

But in another move that cut as deeply across party lines, the committee by a surprisingly wide margin approved an amendment to extend a moratorium on new offshore oil drilling.

That vote reversed a subcommittee decision to leave the extension out of the legislation, which would have allowed the 13-year-old moratorium to end. The ban applies to much of the Pacific Coast, the Florida Gulf Coast, and Alaskan and New England coastal waters; the delegations of those states led the effort to extend it.

"The risks and potential hazards to the environment outweighed the economic benefits of new explorations," said Representative Frank Riggs, a Republican from California.

The committee, which has 32 Republicans and 24 Democrats, held firm on scores of cuts in Federal spending on science, the environment, and arts and humanities programs.

By unanimous voice votes, the committee approved appropriations bills that cover the Interior and Agriculture departments.

The full House of Representatives and the Senate must still vote on the appropriations bills, and losers in both fights vowed that they would find the means to bring the issues back up again.

Legislation within the Interior bill would eliminate the Bureau of Mines, the agency responsible for mine safety; make a 40 percent cut in energy-conservation programs, and strip the National Park Service of most of its money for land acquisition.

This bill would also cut the appropriation to the National Endowment for the Arts to $82 million from $134 million.

The tobacco-subsidy amendment failed on a vote of 17 yeas and 30 nays during the committee's consideration of the Agriculture appropriations bill for 1996.

The debate began like a whisper but quickly rose to a storm.

"Mr. Chairman, I have an amendment, and it is controversial," said Representative Richard J. Durbin of Illinois, sponsor of the tobacco amendment and the ranking Democrat on the Appropriations subcommittee on agriculture.

Mr. Durbin's statement brought laughs from some committee members, and grumblings from others who represent tobacco-growing states.

"Tobacco is unlike any other crop subsidized by the United States Department of Agriculture," Mr. Durbin said. "Tobacco, when used as directed, will kill you.

"Why, in God's name, when we are talking about cutting Medicaid and education, are we still subsidizing the production and the manufacture of tobacco?"

By Washington standards, the Federal money that tobacco farmers receive is tiny -- currently $49 million, Mr. Durbin said, and much of that is offset by nearly $33 million in annual assessments that the Government collects from tobacco growers.

But the fight over tobacco portends an even bigger battle over Government subsidies for sugar, peanuts and other agricultural products. Representatives from agricultural-based states were quick to mount defenses today.

"It's an economic argument we are having here," said Representative W. G. (Bill) Hefner, Democrat of North Carolina. "It's not going to stop anybody from smoking or taking up the habit."

Representative Harold Rogers, Republican of Kentucky, provided the crossfire.

"If you adopt this amendment, it is the small farmer who you are going to hurt," he said. "We are not here talking about a health issue today. We are talking about whether small farmers are going to be able to grow tobacco."

The fight to extend the ban on drilling was driven largely by environmentalists fearful of oil spills and landowners who objected to more offshore rigs, which they feel would degrade their seaside vistas and devalue their property.

All 23 members of the Florida delegation and 28 of the 54 Californians in the House support the ban, as do the Governors of California, Florida and Alaska. Together they form a political front that is likely to withstand any attacks on the House floor.

"We've never busted that bloc before," said Robert L. Livingston, Republican of Louisiana, chairman of the Appropriations Committee, who temporarily surrendered his gavel to lead the fight against extending the moratorium. .

But he was not ready to concede defeat.

"We may take it up on the floor," he told reporters after the vote. "We have to take a look at the numbers."
