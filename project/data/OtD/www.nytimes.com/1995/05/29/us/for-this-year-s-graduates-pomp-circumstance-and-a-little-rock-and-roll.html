For This Year's Graduates: Pomp, Circumstance and a Little Rock-and-Roll

By JAMES BARRON
Published: May 29, 1995

First Michael Bolton received an honorary degree from the University of Connecticut. Then Barbra Streisand collected one from Brandeis University. Neil Diamond got one from New York University -- the very school he dropped out of 35 years ago.

All four members of the Emerson String Quartet took home honorary doctorates from Middlebury College in Vermont yesterday. And the trumpeter Wynton Marsalis expects to claim honorary degrees from Princeton University and Hunter College this week to go with the ones he picked up last week at Yale University and the Manhattan School of Music.

At college commencements, this is the year of the musician -- particularly the top-of-the-chart singer.

Of course, celebrities have long been staples of commencements. They draw crowds and, some college administrators hope, donations, if not from the celebrities, then from wide-eyed graduates who eventually strike it rich. But university officials and sociologists who keep track of such things say that musicians are in particular demand this year because politicians are out as commencement speakers -- too controversial. Lawyers and doctors are also out -- too stodgy. So are megabucks chief executives -- too unpopular among students who cannot find entry-level jobs.

And so from the Claremont Graduate School in California, which gave the jazz trumpeter and songwriter Quincy Jones an honorary doctorate, to Moravian College in Bethlehem, Pa., which bestowed one on the pianist Gary Graffman, college after college has honored people who are famous for strumming, plucking or blowing instruments, or waving batons, or singing to sold-out crowds.

And many are rock musicians who do not spend their lives keeping time to staid, classical warhorses like the "Pomp and Circumstance" march.

Even nonperformers who received honorary degrees have invoked rock musical idols.

Under the velvet-trimmed academic robes of Bennett R. Cohen and Jerry Greenfield, the Ben & Jerry's ice cream moguls, who were honored at Southampton College of Long Island University last week, were Grateful Dead T-shirts.

"The graduates are looking at the future and seeing it's going to look a lot like the present: they're still going to be hanging out, and their lives aren't going to be changing much," said Gerald Celente, the author of "Trend Tracking" (Warner Books). "So they're going to want to see the people who are important in their lives now. You're in a dormitory, music is going on all the time. That's who they would rather be like, instead of TV anchors or business people."

Not only that, but musicians do not have to deliver a soon-forgotten stem-winder to restless graduates waiting to toss their mortarboards in the air and leave the campus for the last time. Mr. Diamond, for example, set one condition for accepting the degree on May 18 from N.Y.U.: no big speech.

"He said, 'I'm not a speaker, I'm a performer; if it's all right, could I sing instead?' " recalled Virgil Renzulli, the university's spokesman.

The university said yes. But a funny thing happened on Mr. Diamond's way to the podium: he decided to give a speech anyway. It was a brief one. He confessed that he had never told his mother he never graduated.

As a premed student on a fencing scholarship, he left six months before his own commencement in 1960 to become a $50-a-week songwriter. That said, he slung a guitar over his shoulder and belted out "Louie, Louie."

Some musicians tried improvising, riffing at a microphone instead of a keyboard.

"I admit to you in all honesty that I haven't got the faintest idea of what I'm going to say," Daniel Barenboim, the music director of the Chicago Symphony, told graduates at the Juilliard School, which awarded him a doctorate on May 19. He then told a bad joke about English conductors.

Sight read a speech?

Some tried that, though it seems to work better with sheet music in a recording studio. Mr. Bolton, for example, skipped over a page in his prepared text.

Other musicians discovered the commencement speaker's perennial frustration: so much wisdom to impart, but so little time in which to impart it.

"To try to talk to the young people who will run the future of our society -- in 10 minutes -- is a little like trying to put a cantaloupe in a Coke bottle," Mr. Jones said at Claremont.

But graduates were thrilled, even if the musicians only talked instead of played or sang.

"Neil Diamond being there really added something to the spirit of graduation," said Mark Malyszko, of Brooklyn, who graduated from N.Y.U. "It was great. When we went to commencement, we didn't expect to get a concert, too."

A big-name performer can also surprise the graduates' parents.

"On an occasion that's intergenerational, as commencement is," said Peter N. Stearns, the dean of the college of the humanities and social sciences at Carnegie-Mellon University in Pittsburgh and the author of "American Cool" (New York University Press, 1994), "music is an ingredient that allows some shared communication."

But, as with perennially popular nonmusicians like Ted Koppel and Cokie Roberts of ABC News and the pediatrician T. Berry Brazelton, what college administrators look for is marquee value.

"Barbra Streisand," said Robert Walker, a founder of the American Program Bureau, an agency in Boston that has booked everyone from former President Ronald Reagan to the former Surgeon General, Joycelyn Elders. "Great name, but don't you think there are so many more people who deserve an honorary degree than Barbra Streisand?"

Still, they fill seats.

Claremont, where Mr. Jones shared the stage with Robert Redford, had a standing-room-only crowd for commencement in a 2,500-seat auditorium.

"We've never been over capacity before," said John V. German, the university's spokesman. "It's the power of the names."
