    Spare Times for Children for Jan. 17-23

    By LAUREL GRAEBERJAN. 16, 2014

      ‘Still Awake Still!’

      Poor Miss Ivory Tinklefinger. She just can’t keep a lid on things.

      First, her grand piano: The top keeps popping open to reveal — well, more on that later. Then, her emotions: She tries to keep them properly contained, but before long, they’re bursting forth. And, finally, the audience: Miss Tinklefinger has been sent to make the children at her show fall asleep, a task that’s doomed to failure.

      This fictional diva presides over “Still Awake Still!,” at the New Victory Theater this weekend. Created and directed by Jessica Wilson, who wrote the script with Jim Lasko and the Australian troupe Jump Leads, the show is billed as “cabaret for kids.” That’s true enough, but “cabaret” doesn’t begin to convey its giddy spirit, which should grow even more manic in a theater filled with children. (I saw a dress rehearsal with giggling adults.)

      The action begins sedately: After a man’s voice on a loudspeaker introduces Miss Tinklefinger as “the Queen of Sleep,” she begins to play and croon a lovely lullaby. (The songs are from the Australian book-and-CD package “I’m Still Awake, Still!,” with music by Sue Johnson and lyrics by Elizabeth Honey; Chopin, Beethoven and ragtime surface, too.) But no one snoozes. This rattles Miss Tinklefinger, but not half as much as the big, furry bear legs and paws that occasionally pop out from beneath her piano’s lid. Soon a guy, Bass Boy (Dan Witton), jumps from the bear’s lair with, yes, a bass. Another fellow, Rhythm Boy (Renato Vacirca), follows.

      So much for sleep. The Boys — Mr. Witton and Mr. Vacirca are terrific physical comedians — are about as soporific as Robin Williams doing stand-up on amphetamines. They jazz up not only the music, but also the proceedings, and, eventually, Miss Tinklefinger. This costs the lady her job and leads her to banish them back to the piano’s interior. (But you can’t keep a good rhythm section down.) And that Pandora’s box of an instrument keeps spewing; half the fun is seeing what pops out next.

      I won’t reveal the contents, except to say that they’re worthy of a teeming unconscious. Miss Tinklefinger, who finally learns to embrace the adventure, may not sing anyone to sleep, but she does unleash some extraordinary dreams.

      (Saturday and Sunday at 11 a.m. and 3 p.m.; Monday at 11 a.m.; 209 West 42nd Street, Manhattan, 646-223-3010, newvictory.org; $14 to $38; $9 to $25 for members.) LAUREL GRAEBER

       

      ‘All Aboard With Thomas and Friends’ (Friday through Monday) In winter, the New York Botanical Garden becomes as much a train station as a plant-filled paradise. Although the Holiday Train Show is now over, the garden is continuing to offer “All Aboard With Thomas & Friends,” a mini-performance featuring Thomas the Tank Engine and Driver Sam, of storybook and television fame. Children can help the characters as they decorate the train station for a big surprise party and pose for photos with Thomas at the end of the singalong show. (Through Jan. 26.) At 11 a.m. and 1:30 p.m., with an additional performance at 3:30 p.m. on Saturday, Sunday and Martin Luther King’s Birthday. (The garden is normally closed on Mondays but is open for the holiday.) At Ross Hall, Bronx River Parkway (Exit 7W) and Fordham Road, Bedford Park, the Bronx, (718) 817-8777, nybg.org. Free with an all-garden pass: $20; $18 for students and 65+; $10 for ages 2 to 12; free for members and under 2. Advance purchase is recommended for “All Aboard” shows; those buying online must choose the tickets that specify mini-performances and reserve a time. Some shows are sold out.

      ‘The Amazing Max and the Box of Interesting Things’ (Sunday and Monday) Max Darwin, a.k.a. the Amazing Max, may come across like the M.C. at a raucous bar mitzvah party, but he does know his craft. In this hourlong magic performance, back onstage for another six-week run, he performs feats like mind reading, making objects disappear and then reappear in unexpected places, and sending coins raining from various parts of the body. The show includes lots of audience participation. (Through March 1.) At 1 p.m., Manhattan Movement & Arts Center, 248 West 60th Street, (212) 352-3101, theamazingmax.com; $34.50 to $49.50; premium seats, at $59.50, include 25-minute preshow access to the Magic Lounge, where children will have a magic lesson and receive a magic trick to take home.

      ‘Angelina Ballerina the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) That winsome white mouse who does all her scurrying in toeshoes has closed her holiday show, but now she’s back in her original adventure. Vital Theater Company’s musical adaptation draws on the books by Katharine Holabird and Helen Craig and the PBS series “Angelina Ballerina the Next Steps.” The show, by Susan DiLallo and Ben Morss, features Angelina and her fellow students doing modern dance, the Irish jig and hip-hop, as well as ballet. (Through April 27.) At 1:30 and 3:30 p.m., McGinn/Cazale Theater, 2162 Broadway, at 76th Street, fourth floor, (212) 579-0528, vitaltheatre.org; $39.50; $59.50 for premium seats.

      ‘Archaeology Zone: Discovering Treasures From Playgrounds to Palaces’ (Friday, Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Children will step into the shoes of an explorer like Indiana Jones in this permanent exhibition at the Jewish Museum, but the adventures will be purely scholarly. Still, there is plenty of excitement in analyzing artifacts like a jar handle, a clay jug and a bangle and figuring out the purpose behind ancient pieces like a Greek helmet and a bull-shaped vessel. This interactive show, for ages 3 to 10, also includes a recreated room from the Ottoman period (about 1900), where young archaeologists can dress in costume. From 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; until 4 p.m. on Fridays and until 8 p.m. on Thursdays; open this Wednesday and Jan. 29. 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street, (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org. Free with admission: $15; $12 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for under 18 and members; pay what you wish on Thursdays from 5 to 8 p.m. The museum is closed on Martin Luther King’s Birthday.

      ‘Beauty and the Beast’ (Saturday and Sunday) This isn’t Broadway, but it does have lots of singing. An operatic version of the fairy tale, composed by Vittorio Giannini, it’s part of the Opera for Kids series at Dicapo Opera Theater. And it’s opera even 4-year-olds can appreciate: one act, one hour, sung entirely in English, with a question-and-answer session afterward. At 11 a.m., Dicapo Opera Theater, St. Jean Baptiste Church, 184 East 76th Street, Manhattan, (212) 868-4444, dicapo.com; $20; $12 for children.

      Community Conversations: Kids Conversation About Service and the Legacy of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. (Monday) The Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. was a great orator, and in this program, sponsored by the New York Council for the Humanities, children in elementary and middle school will be able to use his words to help shape ideas of their own. Aided by a facilitator, they will delve into “Martin’s Big Words,” a picture-book biography of Dr. King, written by Doreen Rappaport and illustrated by Bryan Collier, that draws on his speeches. Then they will discuss the themes of freedom, service and democracy. From 1 to 3 p.m., Lefferts Historic House, Prospect Park, the Willink entrance, Flatbush Avenue and Empire Boulevard, Brooklyn, (718) 789-2822, prospectpark.org; free.

      Eco-Cruises: Winter Seals and Waterbirds of New York Harbor (Sunday) A lot of tourists visit New York in the winter, but some of the most interesting won’t be found in the crowds at Times Square. These two-hour cruises from New York City Audubon focus on birds that migrate to this region at this time of year, like many ducks and geese. They also visit the harbor seals that can be found around Governors, Hoffman and Swinburne Islands. (This season several seal behavior researchers will be part of the tours.) Gabriel Willow, a naturalist and storyteller, narrates the adventures, conducted via New York Water Taxi. Warm clothing and binoculars are advised. (Through March 9.) Sundays at noon (boarding is at 11:50 a.m.), Pier 16, South Street Seaport, 89 South Street, near Fulton Street, Lower Manhattan, (212) 742-1969, nywatertaxi.com/tours/audubon-winter; $35; $25 for ages 3 through 12; free for under 3.

      ‘Fancy Nancy the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) One of the arts’ youngest divas is back onstage at the McGinn/Cazale Theater. This musical from Vital Theater Company focuses on a little girl who wants everything to be fancy — especially herself. Based on the book series written by Jane O’Connor and illustrated by Robin Preiss Glasser, the production explores the predicament that ensues when Nancy finds out that she hasn’t been cast as the glamorous mermaid she’d hoped to be in the coming dance show. With a book by Susan DiLallo, a score by Danny Abosch, and lyrics by both, the musical humorously follows Nancy’s attempts to cope with a most unfancy role: that of a tree. (Through Jan. 26.) At 11 a.m., 2162 Broadway, at 76th Street, fourth floor, (212) 579-0528, vitaltheatre.org; $39.50; $59.50 for premium seats; $20 for unreserved tickets purchased at the theater 20 minutes before showtime.

      Film Forum Jr.: ‘Babe’ (Sunday) Why can’t a pig herd sheep? It certainly beats ending up on a platter with an apple in your mouth. In Chris Noonan’s 1995 film, part of Film Forum Jr., a series introducing classic cinema to children, the title character, a little pig, proves his worth. He’s surrounded by lots of other cute animals, both real and fabricated. (Jim Henson’s Creature Shop was involved.) (Series runs through March 23.) At 11 a.m., Film Forum, 209 West Houston Street, west of Avenue of the Americas, South Village, (212) 727-8110, filmforum.org; $7.50.

      ‘The Gazillion Bubble Show: The Next Generation’ (Friday through Sunday) Children love bubbles, and this interactive show promises not just a gazillion but also some of the largest ever blown, along with light effects and lasers. The stars are the members of the Yang family: Fan and Ana Yang and their son Deni and others, who rotate as M.C.’s for the production. Audience members may even find themselves in bubbles of their own. (The run is open-ended.) Friday at 7 p.m.; Saturday at 11 a.m. and 2 and 4:30 p.m.; Sunday at noon and 3 p.m.; New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, gazillionbubbleshow.com; $55 to $75; lap seats for ages 2 and under are $20, cash only, at the box office.

      ‘The Gift of Winter’ (Saturday through Monday) Does the title of this show sound like an oxymoron? It doesn’t have to. Based on a children’s book by John Leach and Jean Rankin, “The Gift of Winter,” a musical revival from Tada! Youth Theater, deals with two boys from a town that’s determined to plead its case against the season at the Ministry of Winter. Written by Michael Slade (book), David Evans (music) and Faye Greenberg (lyrics), the production doesn’t deal with the banishment of the cold but with the origins of a redeeming feature (to children, at least): snow. (Through Feb. 9.) Saturday at 7 p.m. (sold out); Sunday at 2 and 4 p.m.; Monday at noon and 2 p.m.; Tada! Theater, 15 West 28th Street, Manhattan, (212) 252-1619, Ext. 5, tadatheater.com; $20; $8 for 16 and under; $25 and $15 for premium seats.

      ‘The Greatest Pirate Story (N)ever Told!’ (Saturday) How do you describe a show whose script changes with every performance? A few reliable statements: Smooching, sword fighting and water spraying will occur. “Aaarr!” will be heard often. And, most important, it will provide more laughs than there are bones in Davy Jones’s locker. So it goes in this hourlong family musical, created by Christopher Leidenfrost, in which a pirate crew is cursed by a sea witch and has to perform a Broadway-style spectacle to get its treasure. Young audience members help write the scenes, in the style of the game Mad Libs. (The run is open-ended.) At 11 a.m., Snapple Theater Center, 210 West 50th Street, Manhattan, (212) 921-7862, greatestpiratestory.com; $39 to $99. (The $39 tickets are available only by calling or visiting the box office.)

      iLuminate (Friday through Monday, and Wednesday and Thursday) At this hourlong Off Broadway production, it isn’t the names that go up in lights. Founded by Miral Kotb, a dancer and software engineer, the troupe iLuminate uses wireless programs to light up the bodies of its performers, turning them into glowing silhouettes that perform in total darkness. Using music, dance and virtually no dialogue, the company tells the story of Jacob, an artist whose magic paintbrush, which turns his creations into living beings, is stolen for evil ends. The results tend to favor technology over art, but it’s still fun to watch these young dancers shine. (The run is open-ended.) Friday and Thursday at 8 p.m.; Saturday at 2:30 and 8 p.m.; Sunday at 3 and 7 p.m.; Monday and Wednesday at 7 p.m.; New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com; $49.99 and $69.99; $26 for rush and student tickets, available at the box office only.

      Jazz for Kids (Sunday) Every Sunday during most of the school year Jazz Standard presents not just jazz for kids, but also jazz by kids: the featured players at its Sunday brunches are the members of the Jazz Standard Youth Orchestra, a group of talented young people ages 11 to 18. In this series, which is celebrating its 12th year, the musicians play new arrangements of big-band classics while listeners enjoy barbecue offerings that include a children’s menu. (Through May 18.) Doors open at 1 p.m.; shows are from 2 to 3 p.m.; 116 East 27th Street, Manhattan, (212) 576-2232, discoverjazz.org/upcoming. Free; in lieu of a cover charge, guests make a $5 donation, which benefits the Jazz Standard Discovery Program, an initiative that brings musical performances to city schools.

      Kids ‘N Comedy: ‘Fresh Start Show’ (Sunday) The class clown gets applause, not detention, at this series, in which tween and teenage comics perform stand-up. Here they offer a show focusing on the new year and all those resolutions — the ones everyone is already breaking. The material is recommended for children 9 and older, but there’s no need to worry about appropriateness (profanity is banned) or knock-knock jokes (these dudes are sophisticated). Reservations required. At 1 p.m., Gotham Comedy Club, 208 West 23rd Street, Chelsea, (212) 877-6115, kidsncomedy.com; $15, with a one-item (soda or food) minimum.

      Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Weekend at the Brooklyn Children’s Museum (Saturday through Monday) “I have a dream” was the theme of one of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.’s most historic speeches, and in this three-day celebration at the Brooklyn Children’s Museum, young visitors will add their dreams to his. On Saturday and Sunday, from noon to 3 p.m., they can write or draw their visions of the future on paper clouds that will decorate a birthday window for Dr. King. At 11:30 a.m. on those days, they are invited to take part in “Let’s March,” a program to watch films of the civil rights movement’s 1963 March on Washington and to create a poster dedicated to its ideals before marching through the museum themselves. Other Saturday and Sunday activities include “Let’s Join Hands,” a workshop at 2:30 p.m. for ages 5 and younger that will explore Dr. King’s values through stories, songs and a craft project. And on Monday only, at 1:30 p.m., the Berean Community Drumline, an organization for teenagers in the Crown Heights neighborhood, will celebrate black history with percussion and dance. At 145 Brooklyn Avenue, at St. Marks Avenue, Crown Heights, (718) 735-4400, brooklynkids.org. Free with admission: $9; free for members and under 1.

      Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Weekend at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan (Saturday through Monday) A mosaic, a memoir and a musical performance will be among the touchstones used to celebrate Dr. King’s legacy at the museum. Each day, from 10 a.m. to 4:45 p.m., young visitors can use recycled materials to create a mosaic of Dr. King’s portrait, while at 10 a.m. and 1 p.m., the tiniest among them — those 4 and younger — can decorate a temporary installation devoted to his ideals. At 2 and 3:30 p.m., in the program “Heroic Heroines: Ruby Bridges,” those 5 and older can learn about a child who was herself an emblem of the civil rights struggle: Ruby, who at the age of 6 was the first black student in an all-white New Orleans school in 1960. Her book, “Ruby Bridges Goes to School: My True Story,” will be at the center of the discussion. And on Monday only, at 3 and 4 p.m., the National Jazz Museum in Harlem’s All-Stars Band will honor Dr. King’s life with a concert. At the Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street, (212) 721-1223, cmom.org. Free with admission: $11; $7 for 65+; free for members and under 1.

      ‘The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe’ (Saturday) If the children of the Pevensie family could travel to the magical land of Narnia through something as simple as a wardrobe, why shouldn’t two hard-working actors be able to play them and all the characters found there? That’s what Abigail Taylor-Sansom and Dan Wilson are attempting in the Off Broadway Family Theater’s first production, le Clanché du Rand’s adaptation of C. S. Lewis’s classic novel. (The run is open-ended.) At 11 a.m., St. Luke’s Theater, 308 West 46th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, narniaoffbroadway.com; $35.

      ‘Make It/Take It’ (Monday) You don’t have to wait until summer to take advantage of the offerings of Curious Jane, a summer camp for girls interested in science and design. On Martin Luther King’s Birthday, the organization is hosting a daylong “Make It/Take It,” with activities for girls ages 6 to 8 and 9 to 11. They’ll spend the hours immersed in crafts, which can be holiday-themed or whatever they like: headbands, charm bracelets, masks. Curious Jane will provide all art materials and snacks; participants should take along a nut-free lunch and water bottle. From 9:30 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., Old Stone House, 336 Third Street, between Fourth and Fifth Avenues, Park Slope, Brooklyn, mitibrooklynmlk.bpt.me; $75 for the day; $65 each when registering siblings; $10 additional for early drop-off (starting at 8:45 a.m.). Space is limited, and registration is required.

      ‘Monday Night Magic’ (Monday) This show bills itself as the longest-running Off Broadway entertainment of its kind. (It opened in 1997.) Every Monday a different group of magicians appears (or disappears, as the case may be), offering illusions, sleight of hand and an occasional thrill like knife throwing. The lineup planned for this Monday is Jen Slaw, Harrison Greenbaum and Jeff Moche. At 8 p.m., Players Theater, 115 Macdougal Street, at Minetta Lane, Greenwich Village, (800) 551-4767, hollywoodpop.com/mondaynightmagic; $66.50 to $72.50; $10 discounts available on phone orders with the codes HPG-Prem or HPG-VIP.

      ‘Piggy Nation the Musical!’ (Sunday) Sammy Hamhock, the little pig at the center of this show for ages 3 to 10, doesn’t mind being a pig, but he’s disturbed when he sees somebody acting like one. With a script and lyrics by Richard Rosser, who adapted his own children’s book “Piggy Nation: A Day at Work With Dad,” and a score by Alec Wells, this 75-minute musical follows Sammy as he accompanies his father on Piggy Patrol: that’s when you get a ticket for hoglike behavior. (The run is open-ended.) At noon, the Jerry Orbach Theater, Snapple Theater Center, 210 West 50th Street, Manhattan, (212) 921-7862, piggynationnyc.com; $30 to $60 (premium seats).

      Recess Monkey (Saturday) The three guys in this band are elementary school teachers who are obviously interested in playing (and in play). Hailing from Seattle, they offer tunes that are said to be Beatles-influenced, even though the subjects — sleepovers, monsters, loose teeth — are not. This concert, part of the Just Kidding series at Symphony Space, celebrates their new album, “Deep Sea Diver,” with lots of tunes about the ocean. At 11 a.m., Leonard Nimoy Thalia Theater, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street, (212) 864-5400, symphonyspace.org/family; $20; $15 for children; $17 and $13 for members.

      ‘Red Grooms’ New York City’ (Friday through Thursday) The painter and sculptor Red Grooms may soon acquire a whole new set of fans: New Yorkers under 10. His bright, fanciful and often mixed-media work makes up an unusual exhibition at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan. The show, “Red Grooms’ New York City,” consists of eight pieces highlighting both Mr. Grooms’s creative process and the city itself, like “Rockefeller Center” (1995), which he calls a “sculpto-pictorama.” Daily art workshops at 10 a.m. explore themes related to the show. (Through May.) Hours: 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; to 7 p.m. on Saturdays. Open on Martin Luther King’s Birthday. Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street, (212) 721-1223, cmom.org. Free with admission: $11; $7 for 65+; free for members and under 1.

      ‘Tweet’ (Friday through Monday, and Wednesday and Thursday) This show has nothing to do with digital communication, though most of those represented in its exhibits would have no problem confining their messages to fewer than 140 characters. “Tweet,” at the Children’s Museum of the Arts, is all about birds, displaying artwork that ranges from Vik Muniz’s digital photograph of a large-scale hummingbird, made from scrap metal, to John James Audubon’s detailed prints. New York City Audubon has also been offering bird calling workshops at the museum in conjunction with the show, and the final ones are on Thursday, from 3 to 6 p.m. (They run roughly every half-hour, first come first served.) Children will learn to identify and imitate bird calls, and even create their own bird-song recordings. (Exhibition closes on Jan. 26.) At 103 Charlton Street, between Hudson and Greenwich Streets, South Village, (212) 274-0986, cmany.org. Museum hours: Fridays and Thursdays from noon to 6 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Mondays and Wednesdays from noon to 5 p.m. Free with admission: $11; free for 65+, under 1 and members.

      ‘A Voyage Through Jewish History’ (Sunday through Thursday) This journey involves not just miles, but years. In this new permanent interactive exhibition at the Jewish Children’s Museum, young visitors can travel from Abraham and Sarah’s tent to the Western Wall in contemporary Jerusalem and beyond. Activities along the way include giving water to Rebecca’s camels, playing the strings on David’s harp (a high-tech version with beams of light that emit sounds when struck) and taking part in the exodus from Egypt. Museum hours: 10 a.m. to 4 p.m.; until 5:30 p.m. on Sundays; 792 Eastern Parkway, at Kingston Avenue, Crown Heights, Brooklyn, (718) 907-8833, jcm.museum; $13; $10 for 65+; free for under 2.

      YA Lit at 92Y: Laurie Halse Anderson With Sarah Harrison Smith (Tuesday) An author who has specialized in presenting the past from a young person’s point of view — her protagonists have included teenage slaves in the Revolutionary War era — Laurie Halse Anderson turns to more recent history in her latest young-adult novel, “The Impossible Knife of Memory.” It focuses on Hayley, a high school senior, whose single father, Andy, is tormented by his combat experiences in the Iraq war. Ms. Anderson, whose own father had post-traumatic stress disorder, will discuss the novel with Sarah Harrison Smith, children’s book editor of The New York Times Book Review, and sign books afterward. At 7:30 p.m., Warburg Lounge, 92nd Street Y, 1395 Lexington Avenue, (212) 415-5500, 92y.org; $29; $15 for ages 35 and younger. LAUREL GRAEBER
