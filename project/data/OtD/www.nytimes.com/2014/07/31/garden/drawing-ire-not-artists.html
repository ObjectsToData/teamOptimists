    Drawing Ire, Not Artists

    JULY 30, 2014

    Inside
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      House Proud

      By PENELOPE GREEN

      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      STAMFORD, Conn. — The setting was pastoral, eight gently rolling acres on a water-lily-flecked lake with two dainty islands. Overlooking it was a modestly elegant Tudor-style house built in the 1930s, with a brood of 19 Rhode Island Red chickens, a colony of honeybees and a burly but amiable German shepherd named Brady.

      To Michelle Slater, a 39-year-old academic of independent means, it was just the right spot to enact her longtime vision of an arts retreat, a sanctuary where scholars, musicians and fine artists could attend weeklong workshops punctuated by nearly vegan meals made with produce from her vegetable garden, sunrise yoga and meditation sessions she would lead herself, and lectures by luminaries in the humanities held at a local museum.

      Ms. Slater, who plays the cello and has a doctorate in German and Romance languages, as well as two master’s degrees, has written articles on Derrida, run study-abroad programs, been a Woodrow Wilson fellow and taught French. Last winter, after convening a board of academics in various disciplines, including her Ph.D. thesis adviser from Johns Hopkins, she invited historians, artists and musicians to create programs in chamber music, narrative nonfiction and mixed media, among other things. She teamed up with the Stamford Museum & Nature Center for the lecture series. She put up an official-looking website, the Mayapple Center for the Arts and Humanities, replete with program descriptions and a lofty mission statement, and crowned it with a logo featuring two conjoined trees on a maroon background inspired, she said, by the color of her favorite Hermès scarf, and bearing the motto “Studia Artis et Humanitatis.”

      “I thought, if I manifest the vision, it will flourish,” Ms. Slater said.

      But what has flourished instead is a virulent case of Nimbyism, inflamed by angry neighbors who say they support the arts but do not want a commercial enterprise in their North Stamford neighborhood. At the same time, the anticipated rush of gentle scholars eager to partake of Ms. Slater’s programs has yet to materialize, though she booked professors through August. To drum up attendance, she advertised “half-scholarships” for her $1,200-a-week workshops. When that didn’t work, Ms. Slater said, she ended up calling on friends and friends of friends to flesh out each week’s program as her guests.

      It turns out, it’s not so easy to turn your home into the American Academy.

      Last week, there were bright yellow “Stop Mayapple Center!” signs lining Mayapple Road, where Ms. Slater lives and from which she derived her retreat’s name. John J. O’Connor, 42, an artist who teaches at Sarah Lawrence, was leading a workshop called “Confusing the Controller,” which had something to do with making art after throwing yourself off balance.

      Arthur Zorn, 60, an artist and musician from Barre, Vt., where Ms. Slater’s father lives; Melissa Fernholz, 40, an artist from Bowie, Tex., and Ms. Slater’s best friend from boarding school; and Rina Dweck, 37, a mixed-media artist from Brooklyn, had been in residence since Sunday evening and by Wednesday were already deeply bonded. They had watched an Andy Goldsworthy documentary, brought by Mr. O’Connor, in Ms. Slater’s screening room, had stayed up all night drawing together (though Ms. Slater, who has Lyme disease, had gone to bed early) and had been up at sunrise for yoga on the front lawn. (This reporter joined in the latter activity, and during a particularly exuberant move, Brady head-butted her posterior.) They had given each other pornography nicknames, pairing the name of a pet with the name of a street they grew up on (“Don’t tell Michelle,” they begged). And they had made some interesting work as well.

      The sunroom was littered with Mr. Zorn’s charcoal sketches, including one that bore the digestive imprint of a chicken, an interference that delighted Mr. Zorn, an impish fellow with a taste for performance. Mr. O’Connor said he was amused to see Mr. Zorn rubbing extra charcoal on his already-smudged pink cheeks to amplify his dishevelment. Mr. Zorn said he had stifled the impulse to vandalize the “Stop Mayapple” signs by adding the word “by” after “Stop.”

      Ms. Slater said, “Arthur has the heart of a child, which is why he has attended every one of the workshops.”

      •

      Home-schooled until the age of 14, when her mother, Euphemia Brock Slater, a Mayflower descendant, died from complications of rheumatic fever, Ms. Slater has been on the move ever since, accruing degrees and experiences in the manner of a Henry James heroine: boarding school at Interlochen, the fine arts academy in Michigan, where she met Ms. Fernholz; undergraduate work at the University of St. Andrews in Scotland and the University of Colorado, which gave her a music scholarship; graduate work at Dartmouth, Johns Hopkins and the Sorbonne; and various grand tours through Europe, India and the United States.

      “With my upbringing, I felt a moral obligation to contribute to humanity and the humanities, at this time when everybody is sounding a death knell for the humanities,” Ms. Slater said. She wore a ladylike green-and-white silk Prada dress and pink platform pumps. Brady wore the muck from the lake, where he had sneaked off for a swim, despite the snapping turtles.

      “I started sketching out how this vision would look: a small sanctuary for a small group of people to just have a respite from the chaos,” she said. “To experience this sort of lifestyle in a contemporary space because it promotes values that are so important, not just the arts but sustainability and mindfulness.”

      At this point, Brady dropped a well-gummed ball on a reporter’s knee. “He is an only child, so he gets a little extra,” Ms. Slater said, apologetically. Her personal assistant, Kyle Van Cook, appeared with a tray of macarons in Easter-egg colors, ice water and coffee. Three months ago, Mr. Van Cook answered the ad Ms. Slater posted on Craigslist: “Must love dogs and the arts,” it read.

      Ms. Slater bought this house in 2009, and has spent the last five years renovating it to a high gloss. “I’m a recovering perfectionist,” she said, explaining how she chose each stone in the multicolored slate roof, traveling to a quarry in Vermont to find just the right mix of yellow, purple, blue and black Welsh slate. For her front and back doors, she looked at French and Italian Renaissance motifs and sketched a design with hand-carved rosettes. Inspired by the interiors of the Hermitage in St. Petersburg, Russia, her dining room has been hand-painted in “a quasi-Fabergé look,” as she put it, with colors drawn from her Versace Russian Dream china pattern. In what used to be the tennis court of this once 40-acre estate — “I lament the 32, but I am grateful for the eight,” Ms. Slater said — she laid in a vegetable garden, an Amish chicken coop and a clutch of bee hives. She thought hard about the Transcendentalists: What would Emerson do?

      In March, her zoning lawyer sent a letter to the neighbors explaining that she had applied for a special permit to hold educational programs on her property. By May, stung by the angry protests of the neighborhood groups that had sprung up to oppose her, she had withdrawn the application, though another application, to register the Mayapple Center as a nonprofit, was still in the works. She commissioned a traffic study, which she said determined her activities would have no impact; she asked to speak to the neighborhood association, where she said she outlined her vision and offered to put a deed restriction on her property; and she put out fliers inviting the neighbors to come see her. “But they cut off all contact,” she said. “I am respectful of other people’s perspectives, and I know that even the smallest changes can bring fear. I did have all kinds of support, too, neighbors who said we want to cultivate this fragile vision with you.”

      Alan Pearce, a lawyer who has lived in North Stamford since the 1970s and is a director of the North Stamford Residential Preservation Association, which is responsible for the Stop Mayapple signage, said he and his neighbors are concerned about traffic, noise and property values, and fearful that Mayapple’s activities and scope may expand unchecked. “She’s very passionate and very sincere,” Mr. Pearce said of Ms. Slater. “I don’t have a problem with what she wants to do, but we don’t want it in a residential area. Things start out a certain way, and they seem to grow like Topsy.”

      •

      In the last decade, the landscape of private artists’ retreats has exploded, from three or four programs to more than 500, said Elaina Richardson, president of Yaddo, the 114-year-old artists’ residency in a Victorian mansion in Saratoga Springs, N.Y., and one of the oldest. The earliest American residency programs, Ms. Richardson said, came out of a high Victorian ideal that sounds a lot like Ms. Slater’s manifesto, a notion that artists needed respite from the demands of the marketplace and the big, dirty industrial city.

      “So it was a pastoral ideal and a belief in art that would uplift people and be supportive for the good of all mankind,” Ms. Richardson said.

      Poignantly, Yaddo was estate-planning for a couple whose children had predeceased them. “What was important is they put a lot of structures around it,” Ms. Richardson said.

      Now, she said, there are all kinds of models, from those on vast estates to urban renewal residencies. “Some are as if you are a guest in a private house, and are very intimate,” Ms. Richardson said. “Some charge fees. Another model is you leave a piece of work. But I think, in this proliferation, people are underestimating how overwhelming things like the selection process can be, how you vet candidates, the paperwork, the tax consequences. If you don’t put some structures around it, it’s likely to unravel. What begins as a lovely idea drowns in the reality.”

      Mr. Pearce said his group, which has 200 supporters, would be meeting soon with Stamford’s zoning enforcement officer to impel Ms. Slater to cease any activities not permitted under zoning laws. “Given the scope of what Michelle is proposing,” he added, “it has become an issue for all of North Stamford, not just her immediate neighborhood. Allowing Mayapple Center would be a terrible precedent.”

      Still, there are those who find the opposition, and the signs that have been planted to advertise it, to be another kind of neighborhood blight.

      “For all you knuckleheads w/ your Stop Mayapple signs thanks for destroying our local real estate market,” reads a post in all capital letters on the North Stamford Association’s Facebook page. (That group also opposes Ms. Slater’s plans.) “You should be ashamed of your childish behavior.”

      Jeffrey Price, a gallerist who lives nearby, said: “If you’re going to criticize Mayapple for anything, you might get on its case for being elitist. But the neighbors are making it seem like a combination of a halfway house and a Walmart.”

      Mr. Price and his wife, Esta, have lived in the neighborhood for 12 years (his gallery, Artist’s Market Inc., has been in Norwalk for 40 years). “We’ve watched this community grow,” he said. “To have an anti-arts initiative? Come on, really? That just breaks my heart and makes me angry.”

      The kerfuffle has become so heated that not all Ms. Slater’s supporters are comfortable being identified. “We have lived here a long time,” one wrote in an email, “and I don’t want to start a controversy with my neighbors that may not be readily resolved.”

      Is Ms. Slater in violation of local zoning laws? Kathryn Emmett, director of legal affairs and corporation counsel for the City of Stamford, said that hers is a single-family-home neighborhood, and that there is a possibility “that something that is a school or an educational institution could be permitted, but it would require going through a board approval process.”

      If Ms. Slater were to change tack slightly and follow a Yaddo model, inviting artists to stay and work as her guests, would that be allowed without a permit?

      “I don’t think the question is whether it’s a commercial enterprise,” Ms. Emmett said. “It’s about the usage: Is it a private family home or more of a business in a private residence? A lot of longstanding organizations may potentially be grandfathered in. They predate zoning.”

      And, as she added, noting Yaddo’s 400-plus acres, “the hundreds-of-acres model is nice if you can have it.”

      William J. Hennessey Jr., the zoning lawyer hired by Ms. Slater, said that she would refile for a special permit after “we do additional community outreach and education.”

      He added: “In my experience, people are resistant to change, and the best way to go about changing people’s minds is to be completely transparent. More often than not, it overcomes people’s objections and they have this ‘aha’ moment, at which point constructive dialogue begins.”

      Ms. Slater is not so sure. She said she would take stock in the fall. If this summer has taught her anything, it’s that Mayapple, as she opined, is a state of mind: “We could hold it anywhere.”

      What is a Mayapple plant, anyway?

      Ms. Slater shook her head and smiled. “It’s a poisoned fruit,” she said.

      Correction: August 7, 2014

      An article last Thursday about an effort to establish the Mayapple Center for the Arts and Humanities, an artists’ retreat, in Stamford, Conn., misstated part of the title of one of its workshops. It was “Confusing the Controller,” not “Controlling the Controller.”
