Slow Death On Campus

By GEORGE JUDSON,
Published: December 2, 1991

Should the University of Bridgeport soon end the long fall from its heyday in 1970 by closing its half-empty campus and going out of business, it would leave two legacies to the world of higher education.

Its increasingly painful struggle to stay alive during the last two decades will have shown, first of all, how very hard it is to kill an American university. Yet it will also have demonstrated just how to do that, step by step.

Death, if it comes, will have been helped along by the shrinking pool of college-age students and the financial pressures of the recession that challenge all colleges and universities. But its root cause will be a poisonous conflict between the administration and the faculty over academic ambition: what the university should be, and whom it should serve.

That conflict, which erupted in a faculty strike in 1990, scared away students and created a large group of professors who, locked out of their jobs, hoped for the university's death. It also set trustees on a desperate search for financial salvation that led this fall to the doorway of the Unification Church, further damaging the private university's wounded reputation. Demography Changed

"Bridgeport is suffering for the situation in its city and the state and in the region all compounded," said Stephen J. Trachtenberg, president of the University of Hartford from 1977 to 1988 who now heads George Washington University in Washington. "Demography changed on them. The economy went south. And then they fell to fighting among themselves rather than dealing with the enemy that they shared."

Many administrators at other colleges and universities, watching uneasily as Bridgeport, almost out of cash, lops off departments and colleges, take its crisis as a warning of what can happen when financial pressures divide a university.

In Connecticut, those pressures have recently caused Yale to cut some sports and close its main dining hall, Wesleyan to stop admitting students without regard to financial need, Post College to affiliate with a Japanese university and Hartford College for Women to merge with the University of Hartford.

At Columbia University in New York City, in a dispute echoing a major point of conflict at far less prestigious Bridgeport, 26 departmental chairmen threatened this week to quit if the College of Arts and Sciences, the center of undergraduate education, takes the brunt of cutbacks threatened by a projected deficit.

"Bridgeport is a sufficiently plausible scenario, it is not so sui generis that it can't happen again," said Sheldon Steinbach, general counsel of the American Council on Education in Washington, which lobbies for the nation's colleges and universities.

For many years, the University of Bridgeport was a success story. Founded as a junior college in 1927, incorporated as a university in 1947, it grew rapidly in the 1960's as members of the baby-boom generation went to college in unprecedented numbers.

Construction was constant on its 86-acre campus, across a city park from Long Island Sound. Departments and colleges were added and expanded; as late as 1977, the university acquired a law school.

But despite the university's growth, its status was insecure. As early as the late 1960's when enrollment peaked at 9,100, there was talk that it would someday be taken over by the state. As construction continued in the 70's, trustees accepted demographic projections that showed enrollment inevitably shrinking. The Critical 1970's

Bridgeport took few steps in the critical years of the early 1970's to focus its identity. Instead, the most significant development was the formation of a faculty union, a chapter of the American Association of University Professors, in 1974.

The union was born out of longstanding frustration with administrators and newly found pride in the university, say professors who remain loyal to it now that it has been broken and driven off campus.

Pay raises and promotions had been handled arbitrarily for years. Some professors tolerated these practices because they saw Bridgeport as a stop along the way to careers at major research universities. Then the academic job market dried up.

"A lot of us found that we were going to stay here," Ms. Reinhart said, "so then we had a real reason to get this place regulated." As a new president, Leland Miles, took office in 1974, the administration and faculty were forming conflicting visions of the university.

Dr. Miles moved to strengthen business and engineering programs, the main interest of many trustees. The demand for liberal arts studies was falling, he said, and the humanities "should enter into a service relationship with technology."

But liberal arts professors saw the arts and sciences as the foundation of any serious academic enterprise. Every move toward the professions, they said, undermined the university's real strength. Long Strike in 1978

In 1978, after a long strike, the union had won a new contract under which tenured professors could be laid off, but only if administrators declared a financial emergency or reduced or eliminated a department or college.

"They always refer to '78 as the year the administration lost control of the school," said Gordon Haas, a striking English professor.
