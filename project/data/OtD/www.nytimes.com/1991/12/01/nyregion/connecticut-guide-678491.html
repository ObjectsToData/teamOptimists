CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: December 1, 1991

HOLIDAY HOUSES

Christmas celebrations begin this week at historic sites around the state. The Butler-McCook Homestead, 396 Main Street in Hartford, will be open tomorrow through Dec. 15, from noon to 4 P.M., with a display of family artifacts, toys, cooking preparations, Christmas decorations and a tree. Admission is $2, children $1.

Candlelight strolls through the Webb, Deane and Stevens Houses in Wethersfield will take place Thursday through Saturday, 6 to 8:30 P.M., starting from 211 Main Street. Indoor and outdoor decorations and customs from the 18th to the 20th century highlight the tours, which end with the distribution of cookies made from antique recipes. Tickets at $6, $4 for children, may be reserved by calling 529-4552.

On Main Street in Suffield a display of antique toys is featured at the King House Museum and the Hatheway House, Saturday and next Sunday from 11 A.M. to 3 P.M. The period rooms will be decorated for the holidays, and refreshments will be served at the King House. A combined admission fee will be $3, $1.50 for children. JASON PROJECT

The Maritime Center in Norwalk is the only place in Connecticut, and one of 20 sites nationwide, where people may see and talk to scientists of the Jason Project as they conduct explorations thousands of miles away. Through robotics, computers and satellite communication, an exploration of the Galapagos Islands by Dr. Robert Ballard, who discovered the wrecks of the Titanic and the Bismarck, will be the subject. His team will be investigating marine and land animals, volcanoes and other geological aspects of the area.

The Jason Foundation for Education, in Waltham, Mass., devised the project and received funds from a consortium of private industry, scientific research organizations, museums and educational groups.

The interactive transmissions will be in progress from Monday through Dec. 14 at 10 and 11:30 A.M., and at 1, 2:30 and 4 P.M. Admission is $5.50. The Maritime Center is at 10 North Water Street. Call 852-0700 for more information. ART BY THE INCH

Artworks limited in size to 144 inches in any configuration are on view at Artspace in New Haven through Dec. 21. One artist submitted a painting that measures 1 by 144inches. More than 70 other artists are represented, in media ranging from pastels to photography, glass, bone and steel. The pieces are all for sale at prices said to be compatible with their size. The gallery is at 70 Audubon Street, open Tuesday through Saturday from 11 to 5. HOPKINS EXPERIMENT

For 30 years the Johns Hopkins Medical Institutions have benefited from a program called "The Humanities and the Performing Arts in Medical Education," dubbed the Hopkins Experiment. At Yale on Thursday the program will be discussed in a lecture by its creator, Dr. George B. Udvarhelyi, professor emeritus of neurosurgery and director of the office of cultural affairs at Johns Hopkins.

Dr. Udvarhelyi organized concerts and invited painters, psychologists, writers, poets and playwrights to talk about their work and relate it to medicine. "It is an organic part of the university," said Clara Gyorgyey, who coordinates a similar but smaller Humanities in Medicine program at Yale.

Because of the heavy schedule that medical students and doctors must follow, the sessions are frequently their only exposure to the arts outside the university or hospital. The lecture, open to the public without charge, begins at 5 P.M. in the Beaumont Room of the Yale School of Medicine, 333 Cedar Street, New Haven. ELECTRONIC MUSIC

New music for electronic instruments, underwritten by the Connecticut Commission on the Arts, the National Endowment for the Arts and other state, local and private agencies, may be heard free in three concerts. Works by David MacBride, Howard Rovics, George Logemann and Darrell Katz are on the programs; electric cellos, double basses, clarinets, guitars, keyboard synthesizers and computers will be used.

Concerts are scheduled for Friday at 7:30 in the Mark Twain Carriage House in Hartford, Saturday at 8 P.M. at the Danbury Music Center, and next Sunday at 2:30 in Shafer Hall at Eastern Connecticut State University in Willimantic.

Performers include Dawn Ellen Whaley, Jeffrey Krieger, Robert Black, Christina Rovics and Martha Summa. The composers will be present at each concert to discuss their work. Admission in Hartford is $5, students $3; admission in Danbury is by donation, and the Willimiantic concert is free. Call 257-8233 for more information. THEATRICAL DESIGN

Tony Walton's 30-year career as a designer for some 100 theatrical productions, films, operas, ballets and television shows may be seen in a retrospective at the Hurlbutt Gallery in Greenwich Library, Thursday through Jan. 16. Some 40 productions are represented in 17 individually lighted set models, 150 sketches for sets and costumes, 4 dioramas of the musicals with which he was associated, and 10 costumed figures from the movie of "The Wiz."

Some of the shows and films in the display are "Anything Goes," "A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum," "The Boyfriend," "Mary Poppins," "Sophisticated Ladies," "Six Degrees of Separation" and "The Will Rogers Follies."

At 8:30 on Friday evening the library will show "The Boyfriend," directed in 1971 by Ken Russell and starring Twiggy, Max Adrian and Tommy Tune. The British musical on which it was based was produced in 1954 as a spoof of 1920's musicals. Admission is free.

The library is at 101 West Putnam Avenue (Post Road). Gallery hours are Monday through Friday, 10 to 6. Call 622-7900 for more information.
