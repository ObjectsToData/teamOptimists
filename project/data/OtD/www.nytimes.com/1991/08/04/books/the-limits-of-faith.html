The Limits of Faith

By Mark Silk;
Published: August 4, 1991

THE UNSEEN SHORE Memories of a Christian Science Childhood. By Thomas Simmons. 173 pp. Boston: Beacon Press. $17.95.

All that many of us know about Christian Science is that it is a religion of people who do not believe in going to the doctor. Apart from this peculiarity, Christian Scientists seem like ordinary middle-class folks. In cities and towns across the nation, their columned temples suggest a stolid and conventional faith.

In fact, Christian Science is one of this country's more eccentric religious creations. Founded by a remarkable New Englander, Mary Baker Eddy, more than a century ago, it not only purports to effect cures the way Jesus did, but it embraces a kind of neo-Platonic metaphysics in which the material world is but an unreal emanation of corrupt "mortal mind." Believers strive to live in the purely spiritual world of absolute Divine Love. All imperfections, including physical pain, result from a failure to achieve this higher plane. Spiritual healing means it has been attained.

Central to the practice of Christian Science is the giving of "testimonies" that describe how the believer has been healed of physical, mental and moral defects. Such testimonies can be found at the end of Eddy's grand opus, "Science and Health With Key to the Scriptures," and figure prominently in Christian Science periodicals and religious services. Frequently a testimony begins by describing the sufferer's immersion in the material world and the failure of conventional medicine to cure his or her condition.

"The Unseen Shore: Memories of a Christian Science Childhood" is perhaps best seen as an inversion of this genre. The author, Thomas Simmons, a poet who teaches the humanities at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, movingly describes the physical and psychological ills he endured while growing up in a Christian Science home, and recounts how spiritual healing repeatedly failed to cure him. His journey to health required more than just accepting the legitimacy of medical treatment, he tells us; he had to acknowledge the reality -- the spiritual worth -- of the physical world, with all its imperfection, finiteness and changeability.

Mr. Simmons' book is not simply a memoir, composed after the personal struggle has been waged and won. He makes clear that the writing was itself a spiritual struggle that raised old ghosts and compelled him to think through a new conception of the divine. In the manner of Christian Science testimonies, "The Unseen Shore" is a public witness of faith -- though not necessarily in Christian Science; the author has left the church.

This is the book's strength and weakness. Mr. Simmons' voice comes through with a freshness and candor that leave no doubt of the authenticity of his struggle. He recounts his life in the unadorned, unguarded manner of an intimate conversation or confession. At the same time the book has a certain shapelessness, a recursive quality in which the author returns again and again to his personal struggle to reconcile himself to the physical world. And except for his mother, who is fully and compellingly drawn, the other characters in the book are props in a one-man show.

For all the distress that Christian Science caused him, Mr. Simmons understands its appeal. He himself tried to become a practitioner, or Christian Science minister, while he was in college and did not actually break away from the religion until 1983, when he was well into his 20's.

The great challenge of traditional Western religion is theodicy -- justifying the existence of evil and suffering in a world ruled by a good God. Christian Science declares that the evil and suffering simply do not exist, and that through faith we can transcend the pain they seem to inflict. That can be a wonderfully comforting notion, but in the end it was cold comfort to Mr. Simmons.

Although the denomination will not release membership figures, those who study it see evidence of a significant decline in recent years. Within the Protestant family, this hardly differentiates Christian Scientists from mainline Congregationalists, Presbyterians, Methodists and Episcopalians.

But there are signs of a deeper cultural rejection of what Christian Science stands for. Since 1974, state legislative action against Christian Science practice has been on the rise, and in the last few years a number of Christian Scientists have been prosecuted for failing to provide their children with medical care. This is in distinct contrast to earlier decades, when spiritual healing was publicly accepted as a legitimate form of treatment.

It has been argued that the success of medical science has driven alternative therapies from the field. But 10 years into the AIDS epidemic, this seems rather to beg the question. A better explanation may lie in a fundamental shift of attitudes toward the corporeal and away from the incorporeal.

The late-Victorian piety of Eddy's day denigrated the body, more or less doing away with the orthodox belief in bodily resurrection in favor of a completely spiritualized heaven. The earlier part of this century witnessed the growth not only of Christian Science but also of spiritualism and a host of secular "mind cures."

Today, by contrast, it is the body that obsesses us. What we put into it, how we exercise it -- these are our new religious disciplines. Concern with the physical state of the earth has itself become a kind of religion. For many middle-class Americans the idea of transcending the material world must now seem increasingly obscure. In this regard, "The Unseen Shore" represents an intriguing document of the religious sensibility of our time.
