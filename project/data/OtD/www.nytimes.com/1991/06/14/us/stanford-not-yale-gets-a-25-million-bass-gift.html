Stanford, Not Yale, Gets A $25-Million Bass Gift

By ANTHONY DePALMA
Published: June 14, 1991

For the fifth time in just over a year, a member of the Bass family of Fort Worth has made a huge contribution from the family's oil fortune to an institution of higher education. But this time it was Stanford, not Yale, that received the gift.

Robert M. Bass, a graduate of the Yale Class of '71 and the Stanford Graduate School of Business, Class of '74, announced yesterday that he and his wife, Anne, were giving $25 million to Stanford in recognition of the university's century of academic advances.

For Stanford, celebrating its centennial this year, the gift is also a symbolic affirmation of its standing in the academic community, after a year in which the California university's reputation was damaged by revelations that millions of dollars in Federal research money had been improperly spent on items like a 75-foot sailing yacht and furnishings for the university-owned house lived in by Stanford's president, Donald Kennedy. Signal of Stanford Support

The stream of bad news from the Palo Alto campus this year was believed to have hurt Stanford's standing and caused concern that alumni might withdraw their support. The Bass gift is likely to be considered a signal that such support remains strong.

For Yale, which has received $80 million in four separate gifts of $20 million each from members of the Bass family over the last year, yesterday's announcement was less a disappointment than a moment to share the limelight.

There has been word for months that Mr. Bass, the founder of the Robert M. Bass Group, an investment firm, would make a large contribution to Stanford because he is a trustee there. But Yale officials say this does not necessarily mean Mr. Bass, 43 years old, will decline to follow the lead of his father and three brothers, all Yale graduates, and also contribute a substantial gift to Yale. Praise for Achievements

The president of Yale, Benno C. Schmidt Jr., is traveling in the Far East and was not available for comment. A spokeswoman, Martha K. Matzke, said, "We obviously are deeply grateful for such remarkable support, as no doubt Stanford is."

Through a spokesman, Peter Rosenthal of Howard J. Rubenstein Associates of New York, Mr. Bass said he plans to continue to be a Yale supporter, but added that his first commitment is to Stanford.

In a written statement, Mr. Bass praised the intellectual achievements of Stanford and said his gift would be used to "help Stanford pioneer further advances in the sciences, engineering, medicine and the humanities."

The money is the third largest gift to Stanford's $1.1 billion centennial campaign, ranking behind a $70 million gift from David Packard and a $50 million donation from William Hewlett. The two Stanford alumni began the Hewlett-Packard Company.

Three weeks ago, Mr. Bass's father, Perry Richardson Bass, Class of '37, gave Yale $20 million to build a molecular biology center. In April Lee M. Bass, Class of '79, endowed 11 professorships in the study of Western Civilization. In October Sid R. Bass, Class of '65, gave his gift to support humanities teaching. In May 1990 Edward P. Bass, Class of '68, established the Yale Institute of Biospheric Studies.
