ANTIQUES; When Chiefs Dined, Opulence Reigned

By RITA REIF
Published: November 3, 1991

Bird-shaped masks open to reveal legendary creatures or human faces. A feasting bowl depicts a woman with bear-shaped breasts or a man crawling with a whale on his back. The image of a tribal chief sitting on the head of a grizzly bear and holding a wolf to his chest is carved on a 10-foot pole.

Dramatic interplay between people and animals, birds and beasts is captured in the artworks exhibited in "Chiefly Feasts: the Enduring Kwakiutl Potlatch" at the American Museum of Natural History, through Feb. 23. These works bring to life centuries-old legends of the Indians of the Pacific Northwest.

Most of these objects were used at 19th-century potlatches, ceremonial banquets held to validate a chief's status, celebrate a marriage or mark a death. Opulence reigned at these feasts, which might last for days and always ended with the presiding chief giving to his guests almost all his possessions. What the Kwakiutl chiefs did not part with were the kinds of ceremonial masks, feasting bowls, puppets, rattles, carved figures and poles assembled in this show, objects they cherished and used frequently at potlatch.

The exhibition is the first comprehensive survey by a United States museum of Kwakiutl (pronounced Kwa-kee-OOT-l) art. Most of the 120 artifacts on display were acquired by the museum between 1897 and 1902 by the anthropologist Franz Boas, then an assistant curator at the museum, and his Indian guide George Hunt. The items were bought by Hunt from the Kwakiutls or were found in caves.

"This is the first show we've done with the assistance of native people since George Hunt came to New York in 1901 and worked with Boas to design and create the Northwest Coast Indian Hall," said Dr. Aldona Jonaitis, the curator of the exhibition, a vice president of the museum and an expert on Northwest Coast art. At Boas's insistence, she said, Hunt provided detailed descriptions of virtually everything he found. He also recorded tales told to him by the Indians of the legendary meanings of these artifacts.

The catalogue for the show ($60 clothbound, $25 paperback), published by the museum and the University of Washington Press, uses excerpts of letters between Boas and Hunt to tell how the collection was acquired.

Because of Hunt's thoroughness, Dr. Jonaitis said, "the museum's collection of Kwakiutl art is among the largest and best documented anywhere." However, most of the 1,000 Kwakiutl objects Hunt acquired are unknown to museumgoers because most of them have been in storage.

The graphic impact of these robustly carved masks of bees, bears, eagles and ravens is intensified by the vivid paints the Kwakiutl used; favorite colors were red, green, blue and black. Some masks were rigged so dancers could open them to reveal images of other creatures. For example, inside a raven mask are the faces of a whale and an anthropomorphic being with a beaklike nose. A thunderbird mask opens to uncover a human face framed by images of birds and animals.

One of the more compelling masks depicts two images of the hero Born-to-Be-Head-of-the-World. A mask depicting a fierce-eyed man whistling is dark green and gold on the outside; when opened, the face is dazzlingly transformed -- the lips are relaxed, and the skin is a brilliant white.

Especially ferocious are masks of long-beaked birds with flaring nostrils, feathered heads and large glass eyes that are identified as attendants to Cannibal-of-the-North-End-of-the-World. They represent legendary birds that would split open their victims' heads with their beaks and eat the brains. Less brutal are a mask of an eagle nesting atop a painted face, another of a raven with an ermine in its mouth and a third of a wasp with bulging copper eyes, its head swaddled in cloth.

Feasting dishes were invariably outsized, up to 16 feet long, and were imaginatively carved from tree trunks. The largest such vessel on view is a nine-foot server in the form of a woman lying on her back with bowls for breasts and navel. On her head are patches of fur -- fetishlike touches that identify her as the Wild Woman of the Kwakiutl. Another bowl, shaped like a fat sea otter, shows the creature chewing on a sea urchin while a broken human figure clings to the otter's tail.

The exhibition was financed in part by grants from the National Endowment for the Humanities, the New York Council on the Arts and the Jon Ben Snow Memorial Trust. It will travel to Victoria, British Columbia; San Francisco; Washington D.C., and Seattle.
