CAMPUS LIFE: Michigan; Reconstructing English as Spoken in Days of Yore


Published: September 29, 1991

The S's were tough and the W's promise to be difficult, but if all goes as planned, editors of the Middle English Dictionary will be defnining words that begin with the letter Z before the turn of the century.

Their endeavor to define 70,000 Middle English words began at the University of Michigan in 1930. It is now nearing completion with the help of a recent grant of $745,443 from the National Endowment for the Humanities.

With the last entry, its editors say, the dictionary will become the most comprehensive record of the English language for the period 1100 to 1500 - from the time after the Norman conquest to the advent of the printing press.

For now, 12 associate editors are plowing through "U" and "V" words, like "ulceren" which means "to cause ulcers" and "Veyne", which sounds remarkably like its modern counterpart, "vein". Someday, 13 Volumes

The dictionary will eventually consist of 13 bound volumes, the editors say. But to please scholars unwilling to wait, the editors have been publishing their work in soft-cover installments since 1952.

The 17th of 18 installments of "S" words is almost ready for publication. When finished, it will be the longest segment of the dictionary - 28 pages filled with words like "setten" which means "set" and has just as many uses as the modern-day word, from "set a table" to "set out on a journey" to letting a mold "set".

"S" has the largest number of words of any letter in both Middle and modern English, said Robert Lewis, an English professor at the University of Michigan and the dictionary's edtior in chief. "It has something to do with the Germanic past, which cannot be recovered," he said.

Project organizers say the dictionary was brought to the University of Michigan in 1930 after attempts to start one had failed at Stanford University and then at Cornell University; in the midst of the Depression, neither of those institutions had been able to raise the money for research. The universities gave their research to Michigan, which had already undertaken an Early Modern English dictionary project. 'A Love of Words'

Today, the university is providing almost half the financing for the project; the national endowment provides the other half. Of the project's 12 editors, some hold adjunct faculty positions in the university's English department. Others have advanced degrees from Michigan or were recruited from scholarly journals, many with backgrounds in old English or linguistics.

One editor, Robert Mory, joined the project after being laid off form his job as a technical writer for Exxon in 1985. Mr. Mory, who has a Ph.D. in English historic linguistics, said he had devoted six years of his life to the undertaking, working full-time, because of "a love of words, a love of the sense of history you get when you read a document that spans six centuries, and the sense of creativity you get when you construct a definition that has a logical sequence and sturcture."

Both staff and scholarly materials are housed in quarters in a building on the street that is the hub of student shopping and social life in Ann Arbor, just upstairs from one of the neighborhood's popular bars. So it is that above the hubbub of Michigan students buying bagels and other groceries, the dictionary scholars are quietly trying to understand the subtleties of "forshuppild," which is a person who has changed for the worse, or "pyrdowyt," a mournful tune. Literary Sleuthing

The project has taken so long, 61 years, because the work is so painstaking. Some of the Middle English terms, found in medieval transcripts, have no counterparts in modern English; defining them thus requires a diligent kind of literary sleuthing.

"Dictionaries always seem to take a long time, but this one has taken especially long since we need actual data to construct a definition," said Professor Lewis. By data he means not only a direct quotation from a medieval transcript but an understanding of the context in which a word appears in order to determine what it means.

With each dictionary entry, the editors have included the quotation in which the word was found. "The main virtue of our collection is that in presenting the data to the reader, the reader can judge wheter we have made the right decisions in defining a word," Professor Lewis said.
