CAMPUS LIFE: Michigan State; Televised Course For 1992 Approved Despite Protests


Published: October 13, 1991

Michigan State University's Academic Council approved the institution of a mandatory course last week that would be taught, in part, in televised lectures, despite student protests.

The course, Arts and Humanities 201, would become part of the core curriculum in the 1992 academic year and would be required for all freshmen and sophomores. The course would handle approximately 6,000 students each year.

Some 6,740 students, however, signed a petition presented to the Academic Council protesting the plans for televised lectures and demanding that the course be dropped.

Steven D. Schwinn, chairman of the Associated Students of Michigan State, called the council's vote "the clearest-cut example of the administration ignoring the student voice." Mr. Schwinn, a humanities senior from Plymouth, Mich., said that every student group had expressed its disapproval of the course.

"This is another example of the university's neo-colonialism attitude," said Benjamin H. Emery, a sociology junior from Lansing, Mich., who help found Students for Quality University Education, which organized the petition drive. "The student voice spoke its opinion and the university said, 'That's nice, but we know what's best for you.' " Procedural Inquiry

Both Mr. Schwinn and Mr. Emery said they were investigating a possible procedural discrepancy in the course's approval process that could be used to reverse the Council's decision.

The 134-member Academic Council comprises 35 undergraduates, 66 faculty members and 33 department deans and administrators. It is responsible for approving all new courses at Michigan State.

Alan Fisher, a history professor and director of the Center for Integrative Studies in Arts and Humanities, said the class would meet in groups of 50 four times a week. Three class periods would be divided into two segments, with 30 minutes devoted to a taped video presentation and 20 minutes to a discussion led by a graduate student. The fourth class period -- spent in groups of 25 -- would be a writing workshop, also under the guidance of a graduate student.

Professor Fisher said the video portion of the class would not be a standard lecture, but a segment "produced like a PBS special," which would include film clips and feature nationally acclaimed scholars -- something that could not be provided in a standard core-curriculum class.

The course will focus on American culture from 1492 to 1992 and will examine both external and internal influences on art and literature, Professor Fisher said. Together with a second course, Arts and Humanities 201 will replace the three-course humanities requirement in 1992 when Michigan State switches to a semester system.

But Mr. Emery said that given the time needed to set up the video segment and take attendance, only 10 to 15 minutes of class time would remain for a discussion of the video, leaving less than 15 seconds per student for opinions to be voiced.

"Considering that students are paying for this course, it's a very expensive video rental," he said.

In an interview last week, Provost David K. Scott said the course would be an example of improving the learning process using electronic aids. Mr. Scott said he was pleased to see the students interested in curriculum issues, instead of the usual political ones that often concern them.

"This course," Mr. Scott said, "is a component of a dynamic university using new applications and new techniques to bring this university into the future."
