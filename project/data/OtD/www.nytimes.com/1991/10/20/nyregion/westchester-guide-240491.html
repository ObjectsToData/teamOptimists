WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: October 20, 1991

COMPOSTING TIPS

What better time to start composting than now, when fallen leaves are plentiful. If instructions or incentives are needed, the Cornell Cooperative Extension stands ready to provide them at two backyard compost-demonstration sites. One site has been open for a year at Muscoot Farm in Somers. Another, for southern Westchester, will open Saturday at the Lenoir Preserve on Dudley Street in Yonkers, with tours and demonstrations offered at no charge from 1 to 4 P.M.

It is estimated that composting can save the county $40 a year per family in waste-disposal costs, and homeowners can figure for themselves how much they will save on fertilizers and plastic bags.

While a variety of commercial systems at wide-ranging prices are available, a homemade compost bin is just as efficient and a lot cheaper. "Whether you have an apartment terrace, a tiny lot or an estate, it's worth it to compost," said Nancy Cook, senior horticulture consultant for the White Plains branch of the Cornell Cooperative Extension.

Piling garden waste in a corner will work, but concentrating it in a bin made of four posts and some wire fencing or just a roll of snow fence will do the job faster and neater. Decomposers in liquid or powder form, Ms. Cook said, have not been shown to be any more effective than a shovelful of soil, which contains the same microorganisms.

Lenoir will be the site of another program beginning at 2 P.M., when a campfire will be lighted, and Peggy Turco, a naturalist, will perform Lenape Indian songs, stories and dances suitable for children and adults. If it rains, the campfire will be eliminated, but the program will be held inside the gatehouse. For more information, call 968-5851 or 593-2600. MOZART'S ANGRY WOMEN

George Jellinek, musicologist and WQXR radio personality, will give two lectures on Mozart that he characterizes as nondoctrinaire at Marymount College in Tarrytown. Tomorrow at 7:30 P.M. he will talk about the composer's "angry women."

"More than other composers, the women in his operas were very angry," Mr. Jellinek said, "yet Mozart had a very loving relationship with his mother and his wife." The Queen of the Night in "The Magic Flute," Donna Elvira and Donna Anna in "Don Giovanni" and Elettra in "Idomeneo" are some of the furious women he will discuss.

On Oct. 28 at 7:30 P.M. he will trace the last year of Mozart's life from January to December 1791, a period of terminal illness and disastrous financial problems that did not deter him from composing a prodigious body of music including the Requiem. Admission to each program is $15, plus a one-time registration fee of $3.

Marymount has also scheduled a talk by Mortimer H. Frank, curator of the Toscanini collection at Wave Hill, on Thursday from 7:30 to 9:15 P.M. Mr. Frank will discuss Tchaikovsky's "Pathetique" Symphony and some of the symphonies of Brahms, illustrating the variety of performance to be found in recorded interpretions by Wilhelm Furtwangler, Willem Mengelberg, Arturo Toscanini, Bruno Walter and Herbert von Karajan. Admission will be $15.

All the lectures will be held in the library. Call 332-7438 for reservations. RADU SERBAN EXHIBITION

Radu Serban, a Romanian artist, has had a long journey from his birthplace in Bucharest in 1943 to Perugia, Italy, where he studied architecture, to the University of Constantine in Algeria, where he organized a school of architecture, and on to Paris with his wife and children and finally to America in the early 1980's.

Mr. Serban experienced his share of troubles as well as travels.

As a young artist he could not exhibit, as a nonunion artist, in Communist Romania. Later, when he returned home for a summer vacation, he was forbidden to leave the country for several years.

A collection of his works assembled under the title "America: Reflections, Oil Paintings, Pastels" will be on exhibit today and next Saturday and Sunday at the Hopper House Art Center in Nyack, and the artist will give a gallery talk today at 2 P.M.

Admission to Hopper House, at 82 North Broadway, is by suggested donation of $1. Call 358-0774 for more information. EVENTS AT PURCHASE

Dr. Robert J. Sternberg, professor of psychology and education at Yale, will talk about his research into human intelligence, intellectual development, the relation of language to thought, social competence, love and close relationships and applications of psychology to education Monday at 8 P.M. in the auditorium of the Natural Sciences Building at the State University of New York at Purchase.

The title of his talk is "An Investment Theory of Creativity." Admission is free, and a reception will follow the lecture. Call 251-6630 for more information.

SUNY will also be the site of a conference called "Impact of the Media on the Formation of Culture," Saturday from 9 A.M. to 4 P.M. Designed to encourage conversation across the disciplines, the event will examine the pervasiveness of the media in the arts, social sciences and humanities.

Todd Gitlin, author and sociologist from the University of California at Los Angeles, will give the opening address. Speakers participating in panel discussions and workshops include John Hanhardt, curator of film at the Whitney Museum of American Art; Lucy Lippard, author and critic; Elayne Rapping, professor of communications at Adelphi University; Andrew Ross, humanities professor at Princeton, and Seraphina Bathrock, professor of media studies at Hunter College. A performance piece by Warren Lehrer and Judith Sloan completes the agenda.

Registration costs $10 in advance, $12 on the day. Call 251-6750 to register or to get more information. ZYDECO IN PAWLING

The Red Hot Louisiana Band led by C. J. Chenier, son of Clifton Chenier, known as the King of Zydeco, is arguably the only second-generation professional zydeco band in existence. The elder Chenier died in 1987, a week after passing on his accordion to his son Clifton Joseph.

The driving rhythm and raucous, infectious sound of the Red Hots can be heard and stomped to tonight at 8:30 at the Towne Crier Cafe in Pawling, at 62 Route 22. Admission is $15. For directions, reservations or more information call 855-1300. WEARABLE ART FOR SALE

Wearable Art, the name for one-of-a-kind clothing and accessories created by designers outside the mainstream, will be featured in a show and sale of unusual fashions for a third consecutive year at the Y.M.-Y.W.H.A. of Mid-Westchester today from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M.

Silver, gold, pewter and porcelain jewelry, homespun fiber sweaters, multicolored vests, scarves, children's clothes and lots of hats are among the items created for shoppers seeking something out of the ordinary. Admission is free. The Y is at 999 Wilmot Road in New Rochelle. Call 472-3300 for more information. 'RUSSIANS ARE COMING!'

Moussorgsky, Tchaikovsky, Stravinsky, Prokofiev, Rachmaninoff and Shostakovich are some of the composers to be studied in a series of lectures called "The Russians Are Coming!" by Richard Kapp, conductor of the Philharmonia Virtuosi.

The eight-part course for adults focuses on Russia's brief but brilliant period of classical composition in the 19th and early 20th centuries. The fee is $90, and the series begins tomorrow at 8:15 P.M. in the Hoff-Barthelson Music School at 25 School Lane in Scarsdale. Call 723-1169.
