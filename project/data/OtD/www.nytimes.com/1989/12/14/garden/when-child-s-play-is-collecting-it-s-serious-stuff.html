When Child's Play Is Collecting, It's Serious Stuff

PATRICIA LEIGH BROWN
Published: December 14, 1989

''IT'S not easy to find good erasers anymore,'' said Sammy Globus, who ought to know. The 10-year-old connoisseur was sitting on his bedroom floor in New York surrounded by the Sammy Globus Collection of Pencil Erasers, an assemblage that may one day do for bits of rubber what Andy Warhol did for cookie jars.

Sammy says his passion - erasers in the shape of airplanes, golf putters, Popsicles, assorted body parts and dinosaurs - began before he was in school. For him, as for other diminutive pack rats who have been bitten by the collecting bug, finding a choice object - like a Michelin Man eraser, admittedly sort of dirty ''because it was in my pocket for all of fourth grade'' - is one of life's small but beguiling pleasures. Those seeking provenance need look no further than Sammy's dentist's office or the baby sitter's Hawaiian vacation.

Their treasures are squirreled away in shoe boxes, egg cartons and Dixie Cups, but no matter. In the eyes of children, pebbles become diamonds, syrup bottles cut-glass crystal, a favorite fuzzy sticker a ''Winged Victory'' in a private Louvre.

Where once children's hunting and gathering instincts were made manifest in stamps and marbles, today's children may just as often be in hot pursuit of Japanese micromachines.

In Jersey City, 10-year-old Jessie De Haven and her 8-year-old sister, Kate, have amassed precisely 104 My Little Ponies, with gaudy earrings, pastel tails and coiffed manes. Their collection, including Snuzzle and Peachy, seems driven by a primal love of long hair and horses. Last year, Hasbro Toys, the perpetrators, anointed an 8-year-old girl from Bakersfield, Calif., Morgan Jacobson, the My Little Pony Mommy of the Year. Barbara Bush can't hold a candle to her. Morgan was mother to 364.

Whether great collectors are born or made is a debate that has waged for centuries, and will continue. Marilyn Karp, a collector and professor of art at New York University who has studied children and collecting, believes that ''for some, the instinct is just there.'' Some children are driven by the urge to provide a safe haven for a favorite object, she said, and others, by a fascination with objects and their differences, particularly those things that are ''tangential to their lives.''

Her daughter Amy, 9, for instance, finds delight in dice and the ''Inspected by'' tags found in clothing (the latter organized numerically). ''They're little messages,'' Amy said of these discards of adult culture. ''I wonder what No. 59 is wearing and whether he or she looks different from No. 84.''

That sense of wonder is something all young collectors share. Malcolm Forbes, who is now a grown-up, recalls that his own penchant forJU Page C10 Page C1> autograph collecting began as a 9- or 10-year-old when, armed with scissors, he would visit the office of his father, then a newspaper columnist, and snip signatures from the elder Forbes's business letters.

''I pasted the autographs into my notebook along with classmates' signatures and other luminaries,' '' he recalled. Mr. Forbes's budget has gotten bigger over the years, which is fortunate. ''The more valuable your stuff is,'' he observed, ''the less likely your mother will be to heave it out.''

Collecting prepares one for adulthood, said Enrique Cintron, 13, a student at the School of Science and Humanities who lives in East Harlem. ''I like cars,'' he said of his toy car collection, which he has annotated with index cards containing pertinent information on speed and cost. ''This keeps me interested. I learn a lot about them in case some day I need to know.''

His classmate Robert Martinez, 13, is known as ''the Comic Freak.'' His love of the ''amazing'' and ''spectacular'' Spiderman is amazing, spectacular - and lucrative. Robert, who wants to be a cartoonist, also wheels and deals at comic-book conventions and recently sold a duplicate for $50.

Some collections get out of control. ''I had my girl hamsters in one cage and the boys in the other,'' Robert said. ''But my little sister put some boys in with the girls and now I have 14.''

Samuel Velasquez, 14, another classmate, collects baseball cards the way George Steinbrenner does managers. He is waiting for a 1957 Mickey Mantle. In the meantime, he keeps his eight grocery bags full of cards on top of his bed and puts them in the closet at night. Like many serious young collectors, Samuel is a card purist.

''I don't like the bubble gum,'' he said. ''It tastes nasty. I don't like that white powder. It dirties the card.''

Collecting keeps kids out of trouble, Samuel added. This is no small consideration in his East Harlem neighborhood. There, he said, it is not uncommon for his peers to spend their free time in rooftop egg fights, bottle throwing or worse. Samuel has more important business to attend to. ''After school,'' he said, ''I go to a friend's house and trade cards.''

For many young collectors, hero worship plays an important role. The walls of 13-year-old Iresol Cardona's bedroom in the Carroll Gardens section of Brooklyn are a paean to this young ballerina's love of dance. Iresol studies with the Joffrey and recently performed in the company's ''Nutcracker'' at City Center.
