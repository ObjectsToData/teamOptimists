'Field to Factory' Explores Migration at Hudson Museum

By ROBERTA HERSHENSON
Published: December 10, 1989

A PHOTOGRAPH of a tree is included in the Hudson River Museum of Westchester's current exhibition here: ''Field to Factory: Afro-American Migration 1915-1940.'' The picture seems quite ordinary until one reads the caption: ''Hanging Tree, New Madrid, Missouri.''

The exhibition of photographs, documents and artifacts - organized by the National Museum of American History and the Smithsonian Institution Traveling Exhibition Service in Washington - does not dwell on the lynchings and other brutalities suffered by blacks in the South. Neither are threatening letters from the Ku Klux Klan to migrants in an unnamed northern city, nor a housing injunction against black newcomers in Michigan, reproduced as indictments of the North, but as part of a broader picture.

The show explores the mass movement from one part of the country to another in terms of what an accompanying leaflet calls ''one of 20th-century America's least studied and most poorly understood historical events.'' One room showing life in the South - tenant farming, one-room school houses, segregated waiting rooms and drinking fountains - focuses on the reasons for going North. Change of City Life

A second room depicts the dramatic change of city life in the North, working conditions in factories and the formation of ghetto communities.

A final section asks ''Was It Worth It?'' and concludes that the ''great migration'' was ''a mixed blessing.'' Photographs of striking laundry workers and an unhappy letter from a migrant in Philadelphia are offset by pictures of black shopkeepers and restaurant owners and by well-dressed churchgoers.

When schoolchildren visit the exhibition, the Hudson River Museum's docents focus on the experiences common to migrants everywhere - the hope for a better life, the sadness of leaving friends and belongings behind, the uncertainty of the future.

''Try to picture yourself packing one suitcase - what are you going to put in?'' Elizabeth Schwartz, a docent, recently asked fifth graders from the Museum School of the Arts and Sciences at P.S. 25, next door to the museum. The children answered ''clothes'' and ''souvenirs'' until they realized there would be little room for souvenirs.

Two classmates themselves had moved to Yonkers from great distances: a girl from Rumania and a boy from El Salvador. The girl conceded that she had been a little ''scared'' to come.

The mixed group of black, white and Hispanic children then went on a ''treasure hunt'' devised by the museum's educators to involve students in the exhibition. As they scampered to find answers to such questions as how many months Southern children spent in school or how much a hired worker was paid to pick 100 pounds of cotton, few lingered by the photograph of the Hanging Tree, or the menacing Klan letters. Aspects of the Experience

There was no time for discussion, because the students had to return to school for lunch. Della Barr Brooks, another docent, said, however, that many children who had attended the show on earlier occasions had been especially struck by the Hanging Tree, as well as by the photographs of clothes worn by poor tenant farmers in the South.

The tree ''brings up some interesting issues,'' Ms. Brooks said, such as ''Howard Beach and the fact you can get killed for no reason but for being in the wrong place at the wrong time.'' Ms. Brooks said she thought it was ''a big mistake'' to call the exhibition a ''black show,'' because the experience of migration transcended any one group of people.

Nevertheless, the visiting P.S. 25 fifth-grade teacher, Sylvia A. Muckelvaney, said half the class had relatives who had migrated from the South. So did she and her husband, she added.

She said that after the museum visit she had asked the class to bring back anecdotes about the South from those relatives. She also told the students her own story: how her father, a college graduate, had moved to New York from the border state of West Virginia in 1940 and worked as a janitor for 10 years before finding a job in his field as a chemist. Her father eventually became a senior environmental health technician for Yonkers, a job he kept until his retirement several years ago.

Michael Jefferson, the Hudson River Museum's new director of public relations and marketing, also identified with the show. ''My grandmother used to tell me stories about being chased through the woods in Alabama,'' he said. ''She had 12 kids and they all came up North.''

The exhibition was curated by Spencer Crew of the Smithsonian Institution, who spoke last month at the Hudson River Museum in a free lecture sponsored by the Westchester African-American Historical Society. Mr. Crew's grandfather, Rufus Franklin Crew, was a migrant to Cleveland whose picture and the story of his children's educational and professional successes are included in the show. Lecture Scheduled Today

The exhibition, which was made possible at the museum by a grant from Pepsico in Purchase, will continue through Dec. 23. The museum is open Wednesdays, Fridays and Saturdays from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M., Thursdays from 10 A.M. to 9 P.M. and Sundays noon to 5 P.M.

There will be a 2 P.M. lecture today by Barbara Omolade on ''Black Families: Living, Loving and Surviving.'' The lecture is financed in part by the New York Council for the Humanities and is free to the public.
