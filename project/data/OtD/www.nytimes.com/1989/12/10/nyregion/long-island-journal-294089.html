LONG ISLAND JOURNAL

By DIANE KETCHAM
Published: December 10, 1989

No Caddies Need Apply

ON a wintry night, with the wind-chill factor at 4 degrees, Stan Cohen, Jerry Bolnick and Ernie LaFroscia were playing golf. ''We were supposed to go to the movies,'' said Mr. Cohen of Commack, who, having played golf only twice in his life, was now learning firsthand the terms rough, sand trap and out of bounds.

''They're playing Pebble Beach,'' said Pete Tonon, the owner of Pro Golf in Hauppauge, as he and a visitor watched the tenacious Mr. Cohen take seven shots to reach the tree-lined green. ''The other group down there is playing Spyglass Hill,'' Mr. Tonon said. ''That's in California too. And the ones on the end are playing a course in Portugal.''

In the summer Long Island offers an abundance of golf courses. In the winter, the selection is still there, but the golfers aren't. Pete Tonon thinks he has a way to keep clubs swinging year round.

A former Grumman A-6 systems engineer, Mr. Tonon is one of those former defense workers who needed to diversify. So he opened Pro Golf in Hauppauge. It offers indoor golf.

Golf is played indoors, at $30 an hour, by use of a simulator. Golfers can hit balls at a 10-by-12-foot screen that displays 35-millimeter slides of a golf course. The computer figures out how far the ball would have gone on the course. You even see the ball arching down the fairway. Mr. Tonon has added fake trees along the perimeter of his six simulators to give indoor golf an outdoor flavor.

There are advantages to playing golf indoors, golfers say. ''There's no wind,'' said Ted Derol of Holbrook. And you play courses you may have only dreamed about. ''Spyglass is extremely private, a very exclusive club near Monterey,'' said Bill Miller of East Patchogue. ''This is the nearest I'll come to playing it unless I meet a millionare.''

The same might be said for ''Stan in the sand'' Cohen, as the computer kept referring to Mr. Cohen. ''It's still your turn,'' Mr. LaFroscia said.

''At least I get to see a lot of pictures of the course,'' Mr. Cohen said.

''It's taken us an hour to play three holes,'' Mr. Bolnick said. ''Stan might be setting some type of record for indoor golf.'' A Medal for Billy Joel

Billy Joel was being treated liked Columbus about to set off for the new world. Draped around his neck was a huge bronze medal on a ribbon of gold. The medal had just been presented by the King Ferdinand of Suffolk County, Patrick Halpin, the County Executive. Standing nearby was Mr. Halpin's queen, his wife, Debra, appropriately dressed in royal purple and beaming from her new mother-to-be status. (The baby is due in June.) Since the singing Mr. Joel, an East Hampton resident, was about to begin a world tour to promote his new record album, his friend, Mr. Halpin, decided to send him off in style, with a Suffolk County Arts and Humanities medal proclaiming the singer Long Island's Citizen to the World.

''I never got a medal before,'' Mr. Joel said, as he stood looking like an Olympic champion about to have the national anthem played.

''I got a medallion once,'' he said, ''from Bordeaux in France. But I think they give that out to anyone who drinks their wine.''

Witnessing last weekend's private medal presentation and the foot-stomping hour-and-a-half rehearsal concert that followed took some doing. The location of the event, the former Suffolk County Police Academy building in Westhampton, wasn't released until the day before. And that night, the security made it seem like the Bush-Gorbachev summit. Those invited had to go through three police checkpoints to get into the building.

''We had no idea where we were going, and we're not sure where we are now,'' said one of the New York City Z-l00 radio contest winners who was bused in for the impromptu event.

Billy Joel and his band had been rehearsing in the police facility for the last month. ''It was the best-kept secret in Suffolk County,'' Mr. Halpin said. ''He paid Suffolk County $15,000 to use the building and he must have brought in $200,000 to the local economy by hiring local contractors to build the stage, cater the food, et cetera.''

To repay Mr. Halpin and the police for keeping the location private, and to get some type of audience response before the tour, Mr. Joel decided that several hundred could watch his last dress rehearsal. Radio contest winners and what seemed like the entire Suffolk County Legislature showed up.

The concert was one of the nicer perks of the job, said a legislator, Fred Thiele Jr., who was soon tapping his feet to the No. 1 song in the country, ''We Didn't Start the Fire.'' Valets at the Mall

Trying to find a mall parking space on a Saturday in December can turn many a holiday smile into a holiday sneer.

But Nancy and John Minerva of Massapequa were smiling away last Saturday as they pulled up to the curb at the Roosevelt Field Shopping Center and bounded out of their black Lincoln Continental. Taking a ticket from a smiling young man in red jacket and black bow tie, the smiling Minervas headed off to shop until they dropped.
