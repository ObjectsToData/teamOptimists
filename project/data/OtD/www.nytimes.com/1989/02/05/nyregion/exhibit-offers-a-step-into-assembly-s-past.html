Exhibit Offers a Step Into Assembly's Past

By Charlotte Libov
Published: February 5, 1989

The General Assembly affects the lives of everyone in Connecticut, but until now, there was no way to learn about its history. Now, the past is in easy view, as visitors who stop at the new Legislative Office Building in Hartford will discover.

Visitors need only walk through the lobby to learn about the bygone days when couples needed the legislature's permission to divorce, when lawmakers were likely to own slaves, or when such once-powerful but now virtually forgotten Connecticut figures as J. Henry Roraback, the Republican Party chairman of the 1920's, dominated the political scene.

The permanent exhibit, created by the Connecticut Humanities Council, is intended to challenge the historical knowledge of everyone from the 30,000 schoolchildren who visit each year to the lawmakers who pass it daily on the way to their offices, said Bruce Fraser, the executive director of the council. ''People should be informed,'' said Mr. Fraser.

''This is a central part of the government and I've had legislators say to me, ''Who is that guy?'' Mr. Fraser continued, referring to a large picture of Mr. Roraback. ''In his time, Roraback, ran this place, and now he's forgotten. He was as famous as John M. Bailey, who died in 1975, was the state Democratic chairman for three decades.

The $60,000 exhibit, which opened Jan. 17, was commissioned to celebrate the 350th anniversary of the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut. The document, which was adopted in 1639, is considered the first written constitution and a model for the United States Constitution.

The Orders were adapted from a sermon given by the Rev. Thomas Hooker, a founder of Hartford, and its themes echo through the exhibit. Even the exhibit's title, ''An Orderly and Decent Government,'' was taken from the sermon, Mr. Fraser said.

About 125 displays have been carefully arranged in chronological order on the eight-foot-high panels above the mahogany wainscotting. Designed in tones of champagne and rose ash to compliment the rosy marble building, the exhibit is a delight to State Senator John B. Larson, a Democrat of East Hartford, who championed the project.

A former high school history teacher, Mr. Larson became interested in the history of the legislature years ago, when he was given a copy of a State Register from 1892.

''This is the birthplace of constitutional democracy, but Connecticut history is just a footnote,'' Mr. Larson said. ''So I felt it was important to make sure our new Legislative Office Building contained much that is rich in Connecticut's history.''

Both collecting facts and assembling them into an interesting history is not an easy task, Mr. Fraser said. He chose an art consultant, Harrison Jenkins, to design the project and three historians, Matthew Roth, Bruce Clouette and Jeremy Brecher as research writers.

Photographs and exhibits were pulled from private collections and from the Connecticut State Library, the Connecticut Historical Society, the New Haven Historical Society, the Mattaluck Museum in Waterbury, the Wadsworth Atheneum in Hartford and the Yale Art Gallery, Mr. Fraser said.

The historians agreed that it was challenging to turn legislative history into a compelling exhibit.

My biggest difficulty is that the legislature's history is not a tangible thing,'' Mr. Roth said. ''It's ideology. When the legislature meets, the output is piles of paper.''

The designer, Mr. Jenkins had to make those piles of paper visually appealing. ''It's hard to translate a two-dimensional trip over 350 years into something sexy,'' he said. ''We tried to add quotes, do charts in different colors, and we hand-colored black and white images.''

The team, though, took delight in including interesting nuggets of information to be discovered gradually by those working in the building.

Photographs make up a large part of the exhibit, and some unusual ones include the members of the Organization of Women Legislators. Although often considered a modern group, the organization had 39 members in 1945. Another photograph shows a man living in a tree in Bushnell Park during the depression of 1873, and there is a striking picture of the State Capitol, with blackout curtains at the windows, taken five days after the attack on Pearl Harbor.

The exhibit begins with the settlement of Hartford and ends at the present. One fo the earliest displays is a drawing of the Puritans brutally subjugating the Pequois which sets the stage for the rest of the exhibit. The collection gives a realistic view of state history, Mr. Fraser said, ''not a sugar pill.''

The Colonial period concentrates on the rise and decline of the Puritan Commonwealth, a legislature enforcing Puritan values, and the change of the populace ''from a religious body to a society driven by profit,'' Mr. Fraser said.

It proceeds into the 19th century, when the legislature became a major force in the state economy, chartering banks and manufacturers, and building roads. By the mid-1800's Connecticut society became much more diverse, Mr. Fraser said, noting that, in 1842, the legislature rejected a petition that would have allowed blacks to vote.

A panel on the late 1800's ushers in the industrial era, but also highlights an interesting pair of sisters, the Smiths of Glastonbury, who refused to pay taxes, unless they could vote. They lost.

From 1885 to 1911, the legislature was concerned with labor, said Mr. Fraser, adding that 1887 was a year of great strikes. ''The legislature met for one long session and didn't meet again until 1888, which says something about how large a role they should play in that turbulent society, which was not much,'' Mr. Fraser said.

The panel showing the years 1912-29 is largely dominated by exhibits about Mr. Roraback, the Republican state chairman, who dominated the legislature.

The 1931-63 panel shows that ''all sorts of different forces were at work,'' Mr. Fraser said. ''There was suburbanization, movement from the cities, and the Democratic regained control of the legislature, largeley due to John Bailey, the democratic state chairman.''

The final panel, from 1964 to the present, attemmpts to portray the legislative changes that gave Connecticut the type of state government it has today.

''The story is so complicated,'' Mr. Fraser said. ''There had been the 'rotten borough' system of representation, where every town had the same number of representatives and 12 percent of the state's population could dominate the legislature. Finally, the battle for civil rights led to a new court-mandated constitution, and all of the that changed. So our system, as we know it, is only 25 years old.'' A traveling exhibit of legislative history will also be displayed at schools and libraries throughout the state. The permanent exhibit can be viewed when the Legislature Office Building is open, from 8:30 A.M. to 5:30 P.M. Monday through Friday. The building is on Capitol Avenue.
