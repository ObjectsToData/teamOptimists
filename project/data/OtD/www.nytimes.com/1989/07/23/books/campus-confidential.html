CAMPUS CONFIDENTIAL

By JOEL CONARROE; Joel Conarroe is president of the John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation and a member of the board of PEN.
Published: July 23, 1989

NICE WORK By David Lodge. 277 pp. New York: Viking. $18.95.

The much-anticipated publication of ''Nice Work'' offers admirers of David Lodge's fiction a chance to savor yet another installment in his delectable series of campus novels. Mr. Lodge's characters, both in the two previous academic satires and in this new work, are a 20th-century breed of peripatetic professors given to trading places, literally and figuratively. The first of these academic novels, in fact, is called ''Changing Places.''

Published in Britain in 1975, ''Changing Places'' features Philip Swallow, a mild-mannered don at a provincial school called Rummidge (modeled on Birmingham) who in the late 1960's temporarily swaps jobs (and wives) with Prof. Morris Zapp. Zapp is a cigar-chomping powerhouse whose usual academic roost is Euphoric State, located in a sunny part of America presided over by ''Governor Ronald Duck, a former movie actor.'' One of the literary influences on this stylish entertainment is Jane Austen, Morris Zapp's specialty and hence his meal ticket.

In ''Small World'' (1984), the exuberant, bawdy and wickedly satiric sequel, Professors Swallow and Zapp, now 10 years older, are back on their own campuses. But along with numerous other academics, some of them lovable and others quite unsavory, they spend a great deal of time discussing post-structuralism at literary conferences in Oslo, Ankara, Rome and points west. A thickly allusive novel, it mimics certain characteristics of the romance genre - coincidence, marvels, quests. Filled with delicious parodies of fashionable critical thinking, ''Small World'' has established itself as the only work of recent fiction, with the possible exceptions of ''Lolita'' and ''Pale Fire,'' that has been scrutinized by virtually every professor of literature from Bangor to Berkeley.

And now with ''Nice Work,'' his newest variation on the theme of trading places, the inventive Mr. Lodge has given us a campus chronicle that, though sporadically very funny, is less escapist and decidedly darker than its predecessors. The literary model this time - a far cry from genial Jane Austen or the sunny quest romance - is the Victorian industrial novel. We find ourselves in the grim world of Dickens's ''Hard Times'' and Mrs. Gaskell's ''North and South,'' a world in which the dust of bad factory air all but rises from the page as one reads. (Not all work is nice.) We are in a world, too, in which an anti-intellectual Government's attitudes toward education are not much different from those of Governor Duck, in this country, 15 years earlier.

And what of Professors Swallow and Zapp, our globe-hopping friends? They're here, but only in walk-on roles. Swallow, now grown a bit deaf (which renders him, if anything, even more endearing), is chairman of his department at Rummidge, doing what he can in the face of Thatcherite austerity. And the irresponsible Zapp, by now the best-paid humanities scholar in the States, drops by on his way to congresses in Dubrovnik, Geneva and Milan.

The newly minted characters in this elegantly plotted novel are Robyn Penrose, a young feminist literary theorist in pursuit of a permanent teaching position (nice work if you can get it), and Vic Wilcox, a middle-aged, culturally blank factory manager. In an effort to decrease the gulf between town and gown, a Rummidge faculty member has been assigned to ''shadow'' a businessman for a period of time, and so it is that Robyn leaves her academic nest once a week for the harsh world of earsplitting machinery and exploited immigrant labor. She soon learns, Jacques Derrida to the contrary, that not every text is the product of intertextuality and that there is something outside the text.

Meanwhile, the unlettered Vic falls in love not only with Robyn but with her ideas; by the end of the book he is quoting Tennyson, referring confidently to signifiers and even providing a quite respectable semiotic reading of a sexist cigarette advertisement. The way in which these two appealing characters are ineluctably drawn into each other's worlds is vastly entertaining.

It is also instructive. Mr. Lodge, who has published four books of literary criticism that are actually readable (many such tomes employ jargon to obfuscate what is already obscure), wears his learning lightly and shares it generously. One fringe benefit of his campus satires is enrollment in a kind of crash course on structuralism, metafiction and related subjects that interest him. Other common denominators in his novels include the improbable happy ending, the obligatory brief affair between a liberated young woman and a repressed older man and the unexpected windfall for a particularly deserving character.

In ''Nice Work,'' too, Mr. Lodge once again shows himself to be a student of the things of this world - of cars, food, clothing, architecture and, to be sure, Britain's invigorating weather. Readers who have not yet experienced his extraordinary powers of observation are in for a treat. As to how such readers might most agreeably proceed, it strikes me that while ''Nice Work'' can be read in isolation without any loss of effect, new Lodgers might well consider paperback editions of ''Changing Places'' (Penguin) and ''Small World'' (Warner) as amiable preambles to this most serious of the campus chronicles.
