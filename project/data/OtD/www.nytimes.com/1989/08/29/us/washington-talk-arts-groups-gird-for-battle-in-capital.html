WASHINGTON TALK; Arts Groups Gird for Battle in Capital

By BARBARA GAMAREKIAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: August 29, 1989

While most of Washington has vacated the capital this month, Anne Murphy is working the telephones in a downtown office, preparing for what is expected to be an arduous legislative session for the National Endowment of the Arts.

As head of the American Arts Alliance, an organization founded in 1977 to lobby for the arts, Miss Murphy is working not only to shore up support for the endowment's budget, but also to head off an amendment restricting Federal funds for the arts.

The amendment calls for a ban on public funds for art that is deemed obscene or that denigrates people's beliefs, a limitation Miss Murphy said was ''the largest controversial issue the arts community has had to face since the endowment was created 25 years ago.''

Senator Jesse Helms, Republican of North Carolina, proposed the measure because of endowment grants totaling $45,000 that supported the works of two controversial photographers, Andres Serrano and Robert Mapplethorpe. The Helms amendment is attached to a bill providing funds for the endowment in the current fiscal year; a House-Senate conference committee will consider the bill next month.

But there is concern among many people in the arts that the issue of withholding Federal support for certain types of art may also be raised when Congressional committees consider separate legislation in early September that would reauthorize the endowment as a Government agency. The endowment was established by Congress in 1965 as part of the National Foundation on the Arts and the Humanities, which was created to encourage and support progress in the humanities and the arts.

That legislation prohibits the endowment from interfering with the content of the art work it finances. The language in that bill, which Senator Helms's amendment would modify, reflects a fear of state-ordered and state-defined art.

Although arts groups contend that the Senator's proposal strikes at their First Amendment rights of free speech, Mr. Helms and his supporters say that withholding Government support for certain types of art is not the same as censorship. They maintain that the Government should not be required to finance art that much of the public would consider obscene and that such a stipulation would not, in any case, restrict what artists could create.

''That leads me to ask the other side of the question,'' Miss Murphy countered. ''Do you lose your First Amendment rights when you take Federal dollars? When we give grants to scientists at the National Science Foundation, do we try to control the content of their experiments? Do we ask libraries to only have certain books on their shelves when they get Federal dollars?'' A Widespread Lobbying Effort

Miss Murphy is working with arts institutions, grass-roots coalitions and groups like the American Bar Association, the American Library Association and the American Civil Liberties Union.

''There are thousands and thousands of citizens and hundreds and hundreds of art groups around the country taking part in this lobbying effort,'' she said. ''Our role is to spearhead it, give guidance, provide materials, come up with background information on the lay of the land and identify what needs to be done.''

As a Government agency, the endowment cannot lobby on its own behalf. That job must be done by surrogates like the alliance and by art institutions and individuals. Concern Over Reauthorization

One of those individuals is Livingston Biddle, a former chairman of the endowment, who said he had been quietly ''visiting'' influential lawmakers. ''I don't do anything official,'' he added, ''but I've been visiting regularly with old friends on the Hill who are connected with the arts.''

Dean Amhaus, of the Washington office of the American Council for the Arts, said, ''This isn't a time that anyone can relax.''

And John Hammer of the National Humanities Alliance said, ''There is a concern that the kind of issues raised by Helms may be raised during reauthorization.''

Miss Murphy and her staff are in touch with members of Congress and their staffs who are planning legislative strategy. Most members are back in their districts now, ''and that is where the lobbying is going on,'' she said. ''We get hundreds of phone calls a day from people asking what they can do to help, and I impress on all our member institutions that all lobbying is local.''

Miss Murphy is a familiar figure on Capitol Hill, where the alliance is known as the primary advocate for the arts in Washington. A native of Rhode Island, she arrived here in the early 1960's, taking a job with Representative John E. Fogarty, a Rhode Island Democrat. After Mr. Fogarty's death in 1967, she worked at the endowment and in public television before joining the alliance as its director 10 years ago. Hearings on Bush Nominee

The Helms amendment is just one hurdle in what Miss Murphy anticipates will be a long legislative year. Also scheduled next month are hearings on the nomination of John Frohnmayer, an Oregon lawyer selected by President Bush as chairman of the endowment, which has been without a head since the resignation of Frank Hodsoll in February.

''Once you have someone who is appointed by a President speaking for the endowment, that will carry more weight and be an important factor,'' she said.

Miss Murphy said that while the controversy over public funds for the arts is not a battle she welcomes, she is glad it has placed the role of the arts on the national agenda. ''The summer has certainly broadened the dialogue,'' she said, ''and it's been an opportunity to galvanize the arts community.''

She is preparing for the legislative long haul.

''I keep telling people this is the prologue in an opera; we have three acts left,'' she said. ''The prologue identifies the characters and the themes, but we would not be wise to think that whatever resolution takes place in the appropriation process is final. I think it is all part of a larger political agenda that will play itself out all the way through the authorization process.''
