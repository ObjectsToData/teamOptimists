SPECIAL SECTION: THE GENDER CARD; The S.A.T. Puzzle

By Laura Mansnerus
Published: August 6, 1989

In standardized testing, there is a basic understanding: Gender differences in scores are diminishing. And there is a catch: the Scholastic Aptitude Test.

It is a major exception, though not the only one. The S.A.T., on which girls score nearly 60 points below boys in the combined score of 1600, counts more than any other test most students ever take. Recent findings that the gender gap is closing on many tests has led the Educational Testing Service, which writes the S.A.T., as well as its many critics to question why the test works differently for men and women.

Girls' scores on the verbal portion, where they once had a small but consistent edge, slipped below boys' in 1972 and are now 10 points lower on average. The math gap has hovered between 41 and 52 points since the College Board, the test's sponsor, started announcing results 22 years ago.

Similar patterns have emerged with the American College Testing Program Examination, the other major college-entrance test, and with the Preliminary Scholastic Aptitude Test, although the P.S.A.T. gap has declined slightly over the last two decades.

In the same period, however, according to several meta-analyses, or analyses of other studies, gender differences in almost all measures of ability have become negligible. One such study, by Marcia C. Linn of the University of California at Berkeley and Janet S. Hyde of the University of Wisconsin, concluded that ''the gender difference in verbal ability is currently so small that it can effectively be considered to be zero.'' A similar study by Professor Hyde and Prof. Elizabeth Fennema at Wisconsin found the gap in mathematics to be nearly closed.

What makes the S.A.T. different is the subject of anger, puzzlement and many hypotheses. To those who have conducted the larger analyses, it appears that the more the test group resembles the general population - and the S.A.T. sample is fairly select - the smaller the gender gap. In math, it also appears that the more complicated the task and the older the test-takers, the worse girls do. ''The problem remains that in the area of math that remains most important, high cognitive-level problem-solving, the differences are not going away,'' Professor Fennema said.

On the S.A.T., this gap is widest, and most persistent, at the high end of the scale. Of those who score 800, the highest possible, in math, 96 percent are male, as are more than 80 percent of those who score above 750.

At the same time, said Ernest W. Kimmel, executive director for College Board Test Development at the Educational Testing Service, ''part of the puzzle is that the largest differences seem to be with the easier questions.'' Girls' Advantages in Mathematics

Girls do have some advantages in mathematics. They get higher grades than boys, which indicates that they are better at figuring out what the teacher wants. They generally do at least as well on achievement tests, such as the Regents exams, that measure specific material learned in the classroom. They are slightly more adept at computation and have improved in algebra - although these relatively simple skills, Professor Fennema said, are ''not terribly useful in our society.''

Apparently, girls also attack the tests more deliberately, which may be bad strategy on the S.A.T. Professor Linn cites an S.A.T. question that asked how many games a basketball team with a certain win-loss average would have to win to attain a certain season average. ''I've interviewed people who tell me they can just look at the problem and know what the answer is,'' she said. ''These people are all men. At talks, I can get people writing the whole equation on the blackboard. These are always women.''

John S. Katzman, president of the Princeton Review, an S.A.T. coaching program, said: ''It's kind of a macho thing. In general, guys have better test sense. They rely less on their knowledge of material and more on their knowledge of test-taking. It's likely that that will help you more in life than your knowledge of remainders. But don't try to pass it off as a test of innate ability.''

E.T.S. officials say that if boys take more risks and shortcuts, test results don't show it. Nancy W. Burton, the test service's program director, said that on the S.A.T. girls guessed as often as boys and, on average, answered only one fewer of the 60 math questions than boys did.

A different hypothesis lies in demographics. About 52 percent of S.A.T.-takers are girls, up from 35 to 40 percent in the 1950's, and because their sample is larger, they are slightly less well prepared academically and slightly lower on the socioeconomic scale than male test-takers.

Dr. Burton found that after scores were adjusted for the slight differences in background, girls did as well as boys on the verbal section and closed about half the gap in math.

The verbal score gap is less puzzling. Dr. Burton said she suspected that changes in teaching had erased girls' advantage, but she acknowledged that several decades ago the test makers added questions with a science or business context and changed the reading comprehension portion in the 1970's to include one more science passage.
