SPECIAL SECTION: THE GENDER CARD; All in the Game

By Joseph Berger
Published: August 6, 1989

Unlike most women, Ami Radunskya is partial to mathematics. But then hers was not a typical woman's upbringing.

When she was a child, her father, a mathematical economist, gave her a book about the great men of mathematics. For Christmas she received a slide rule. Now, 34 years old and the mother of a 7-year-old boy, she is a graduate student in mathematics at Stanford University concentrating on an arcane specialty called ergodic theory. But she is still something of a curiosity - a brilliant woman in a field where excellence is a man's prerogative.

Two decades after women began demanding greater equality in schools and in the workplace, there are still striking differences between men and women in their intellectual predilections and performance. The differences have narrowed in some areas over the past 10 to 15 years. Men seem to be catching up to women in reading and writing; women have chipped away at an evident handicap in math.

Nevertheless, the disparities persist - a constant irritant to Americans who are committed, at least in their stated ideals, to the view that men and women are created intellectually equal. 'At Every Stage, We Lose Women'

''At every stage of the pipeline, we lose women,'' said Phyllis Moen, director of the sociology program at the National Science Foundation. ''We lose women when they choose their courses in junior high school and high schools. We lose them when they choose majors at the undergraduate level and certainly at the graduate level.''

Three decades ago, intellectual differences between men and women were not only tolerated but appreciated; girls were lauded for communication skills, boys for their prowess at chess and math puzzles. Schools were charged with maximizing everyone's ''natural'' potential.

In the late 1960's, however, members of the women's movement increasingly called attention to the fact that jobs requiring facility with math paid better than those requiring verbal abilities. Different meant unequal, they argued, and they assailed schools and standardized tests for perpetuating those differences. Now, as the nation confronts serious shortages of engineers and scientists, Government officials and educators are trying to eliminate these gender-related differences so that both women and men will be available to fill those roles.

The issue remains complex and volatile. Data abound documenting gender distinctions, but the data can be misleading, since they often compare different populations. Starting in 1972, for instance, high school girls lost the edge they once enjoyed over boys in verbal scores on the Scholastic Aptitude Test, a benchmark for college admissions. But the girls of 1972 were a different lot from those of 1962; many more came from homes where parents did not have college educations and many more were from minority groups with traditional education deficiencies. The new test takers, researchers say, weakened the quality of the pool and allowed the boys to catch up. How to Explain the Disparities? Explanations for the disparities are even more elusive. They focus on physiological differences between men and women, the tenacious cultural conditioning that still routes boys and girls into separate roles, and bias in the tests themselves. In addition, the explanations themselves are often colored by the political viewpoints of the researchers. And at any rate, it is often pointed out that the performance of large groups of boys and girls says little about the potential of a single boy or girl. After all, there are women like Ami Radunskya who can reach the same mathematical peaks as men.

Some general conclusions can be drawn, however.

* In elementary, junior high and high school, girls read and write more proficiently than boys. In computation, girls and boys do about equally well until junior high school, when math problems get more complex and boys start to outshine girls. These differences have diminished slightly over the last two decades.

* On college admissions tests, high school boys have caught up with girls on verbal skills, but girls still lag significantly behind in math.

* At the highest testing levels, disparities in performance become acute, with bright boys far exceeding bright girls in every subject save English.

* In college, women get better grades over all, but are generally strongest in the arts and humanities, while men excel in math and science. On standardized examinations for graduate school, men outperform women in nearly every subject.

Increasingly, the explanations for gender distinctions focus on one phenomenon. Sometime during junior high school, at an age when children begin to cement their sexual identity, many girls give up on math and science. Authoritative Confirmation of the Gap

The most recent and authoritative confirmation that a so-called gender gap exists came in a 50-page report released in April by Gita Z. Wilder and Kristin Powell, two researchers at the Educational Testing Service in Princeton, N.J., the company that writes many of the nation's leading tests.
