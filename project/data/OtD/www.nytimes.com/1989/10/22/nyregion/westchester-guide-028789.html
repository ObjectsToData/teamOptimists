WESTCHESTER GUIDE


Published: October 22, 1989

FROM JOHANNESBURG

Drama, song, story, dance and mime are combined in ''You Strike the Woman You Strike the Rock,'' to be presented at 8 P.M. Saturday in the Performing Arts Center at the State University of New York at Purchase.

Conceived by the Vusisizwe Players and the Market Theater of Johannesburg, the production takes its name from a protest song of the 1950's that commemorates a march by 30,000 Pretorian women protesting apartheid laws of South Africa. The double jeopardy in being both female and black is the theme of the piece, which focuses on life under apartheid for three women from varying backgrounds.

The Market Theater, founded in 1976 in a converted Johannesburg produce market, is a multi-racial company where the works of Athol Fugard, Pieter-Dirk Uys and Adam Small received their first and only support within the country. Today it has grown into a complex of three theaters, an art gallery, bookshop, restaurant, bar and flea market.

Tickets at $16 and $20 may be reserved by calling 251-6200. RECLUSIVE ARTIST

The paintings of a reclusive artist who never exhibited or sold his work are being shown 65 years after his death at the Cornelia Cotton Gallery in Croton-on-Hudson through Dec. 2.

What makes the paintings of Frederick Sykes unusual is the attachment he formed for trees, houses, bodies of water and other elements in the landscape of the Hudson River Valley, which, in his solitude, he substituted for human relationships. Bridges, fences, roots, stumps and stones are painted with a palpable humanism usually reserved for portraits and figurative art.

Sykes was born in England in 1854 and came to this country as a child, living first in Brooklyn Heights, then settling near Poughkeepsie. His paintings were kept by his family after he died and they are now for sale. The gallery is at 111 Grand Street. Call 271-6802 for viewing hours. MINI-DRAMA, PLUS

Eric Chartier, a French stage and screen actor and broadcast personality, is the sole performer in ''Spectacle Bicentenaire,'' a mini-drama about the French Revolution to be presented in French by the Alliance Francaise of Westchester and the Department of Continuing Education at Mercy College. Curtain time is 2 P.M. today in the Hudson River Room of the college's main building on the Dobbs Ferry campus. Admission is $4.

A second program, in English, is a history of the French Liner Normandie, from her maiden voyage in 1935 to her final trans-Atlantic crossing only four years later, when she was gutted by fire in New York Harbor. Registration for this begins at 11 A.M. on Nov. 5 in the Bonnie Briar Country Club in Mamaroneck; the lecture and brunch follow.

The talk by Dale Harris will cover the Normandie's place in maritime and art history as the fastest, most efficient and most luxurious ocean liner of its time, with salons, reception rooms, cabins, suites and recreational areas decorated and furnished by the best of France's artists and craftsmen. Mr. Harris has taught literature at Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers and art history at Cooper Union, and has lectured about cultural history at the Metropolitan Museum and the Smithsonian Institution The fee for his talk is $25. Call 937-6676 for reservations or more information. CHAMBER SERIES

''Chamber Music in Great Mansions,'' a new series by the Bronx Arts Ensemble, opens today with the harpsichordist and raconteur Igor Kipnis in works by Bach, Mozart and George Rochberg, a contemporary American composer whose ''Nach Bach'' (meaning according to, or in accordance with Bach) was composed expressly for Mr. Kipnis.

Three concerts in the series will be held in private mansions overlooking the Hudson River in Riverdale, all at 2 P.M. on Sundays. Rafael Figueroa, the cellist, will perform Dec. 10 and the violinist Elmar Oliveira is scheduled for May 13.

Single tickets are $15, or $9 for students and the elderly. Series subscriptions cost $40; for students and the elderly, $24. Call (212) 540-1899 or (212) 601-7399 for reservations and locations of the houses. JEWISH LIFE

Two films about differing aspects of and attitudes toward Jewish life will be shown at the State University of New York at Purchase in the theater of the Humanities Building, free of charge.

''Tkies Kaf'' (''A Vilna Legend'') was produced as a silent film in Poland in 1924, then re-released in the United States with sound in 1933, narrated by the American actor Joseph Buloff. The original cast includes Esther Rokhl Kaminska, a leading Polish-Yiddish actress, in her only movie role. The film, based on Hasidic folklore, will be screened at 7:30 P.M. Monday. It is to be introduced by J. Hoberman, a movie critic for The Village Voice, who will lead a discussion afterward.

''Fishke der Krumer'' (''The Light Ahead''), one of the last American- made Yiddish talkies, takes a dim view of shtetl life and Jewish clericalism. It was released in 1939 with David Opatoshu in the title role. He was then a member of the Yiddish Workers' Theater in Manhattan. Prof. Mel Gordon, a theater historian at New York University, will introduce and discuss the film, starting at 7:30 P.M. on Nov. 6. For more information, call 251-6500. MEDICARE VIEWS

''What Medicare Changes Mean for Your Financial Future'' is the subject of a program to be conducted by two experts in the field at 1 P.M. Friday in the New Rochelle Public Library. Various Medicare provisions and proposals that have been in and out of favor with Congress will be evaluated in terms of their projected effect on a retiree's financial planning.

Ann Griffin and Joanne Hickey, who will conduct the program, are employed by Investor's Diversified Services, an American Express Company in White Plains. Ms. Griffin is a financial planner registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission, and Ms. Hickey, who is also registered with the commission, is a former litigation specialist with the Liberty Mutual Insurance Company. To register for the free program call the library's community-relations office, 632-7878. SEASONAL SKILLS

Boscobel's popular pre-Christmas, one-day workshops, held in the mansion's meeting room from 10 A.M. to 3 P.M., begin with dried-wreath making on Thursday, Friday or Saturday. The flowers and plant material are from Boscobel's own gardens. Bandboxes will be made Nov. 9 through 11, and Christmas decorations, including wreaths and garlands, mantels, fruit pyramids and other 19th-century-inspired holiday adornments will be the subjects on Nov. 29 and 30 and Dec. 1, 2, 6, 7 and 8.

The daily fee is $25, and it covers instruction and most materials. Advance registration is required, and participants should take a bag lunch. Call 265-3638 for reservations.

Boscobel, a restored decorative arts mansion-museum, is in Garrison-on-Hudson eight miles north of the Bear Mountain bridge on Route 9D by car. It can also be reached on Metro-North. $90ELEANOR CHARLES
