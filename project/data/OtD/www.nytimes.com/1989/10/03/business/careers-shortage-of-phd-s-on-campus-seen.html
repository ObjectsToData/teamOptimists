Careers; Shortage Of Ph.D.'s on Campus Seen

By Elizabeth M. Fowler
Published: October 3, 1989

EDWARD T. LEWIS, 55 years old, a college president, and a poet by avocation, says that after years of overabundance there is a shortage of Ph.D's. ''We are beginning to see it now in the humanities, along with the usual shortages in engineering, mathematics, computer science and, to a lesser degree, in other sciences,'' he said last week.

''In 1971 when I got my doctorate there were virtually no jobs for English professors,'' he said, adding that he had specialized in poetry. He taught English literature at the University of Puerto Rico, but when he wanted to return to the mainland he could find no openings.

To stay in academia he entered administration at Cornell University's Graduate School of Business in 1974, soon becoming an associate dean. Six years ago he moved to the presidency of St. Mary's College of Maryland, in St. Mary's City. Part of the state system, the school is a liberal arts co-educational institution.

Now, almost 20 years after his difficulty in finding a teaching position, he said: ''The most obvious problem in academia is the shortage of professors. It will be across the board in two or three years, and before the year 2000 almost half of the current professors will retire.''

He said the shortage had meant fierce competition for professors. ''Less qualified applicants have to be hired in many cases,'' he said, adding that schools were nonetheless paying higher salaries to get them.

As a result, colleges have to invest proportionately more in faculty support programs like housing and mortgages, earlier sabbaticals for younger professors, and course release time for faculty members doing special research or writing books, Dr. Lewis said.

''I think young people considering careers should think about getting a Ph.D.,'' he said. ''It will be a very attractive sellers' market for them.''

In his view, for the first time in many years, bright young people can pursue a favorite subject, even esoteric ones like philosophy and classics, and work toward a Ph.D confident that they can probably find jobs in their academic field.

One sign that indicates the changed environment came two years ago when New York University's Graduate School of Business Administration quietly ended a nine-year-old summer program called Careers in Business for Ph.D's. It had been partly supported by New York State, which was concerned that Ph.D.'s were not able to find teaching jobs in the 1970's.

Dr. Ernest Kurnow, professor emeritus who teaches statistics at N.Y.U. and headed the program for eight years, said last week that ''about 500 had gone through the program and 90 percent went into business.'' He believes that most have remained in business.

The program, and a few similar ones, created a new market for the Ph.D.'s, he said.

But by the time the program was ended, many colleges and universities had broadened undergraduate curriculum requirements in liberal arts courses, which opened up some teaching jobs in the humanities.

''We found that those who signed for our program in the final years were not youngsters but mostly older holders of Ph.D.'s dissatisfied with campus life or unable to get tenure,'' he said.

Janet R. Marks, who has a Ph.D. in anthropology, took the program in 1981 not so much for a corporate job but to ''get exposure to business courses,'' she said. At the time, she explained, she had completed ''all but the dissertation'' required for a Ph.D.

''In addition to giving us the core courses of the M.B.A. program,'' she said, ''the program gave us personal interest tests and consultations to help determine where we wanted to go. We interviewed at some good companies.'' She added that she was one of the few who decided to stay in college administration, using the program to help her in her career.

After a start as a librarian in the N.Y.U. graduate business school, she rose through the ranks. She is now an assistant dean for student affairs at the school and has just received a promotion to associate dean for M.B.A. education, effective in January.

Another more recent indication of the changed market for young Ph.D. holders was the study announced by Dr. William Bowen, head of the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, and Julie Ann Sosa, an Oxford University graduate student, called ''Prospects for Faculty in the Arts and Sciences.'' It also predicted the shortage of Ph.D.'s, especially noting ''a real shortfall'' in the humanities and social sciences in the late 1990's and the early part of the next century.
