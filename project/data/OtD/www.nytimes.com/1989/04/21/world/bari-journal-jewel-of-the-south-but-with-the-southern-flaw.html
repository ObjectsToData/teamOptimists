Bari Journal; Jewel of the South, but With the Southern Flaw

By CLYDE HABERMAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: April 21, 1989

This city on Italy's Achilles tendon is sometimes called the Milan of the south.

It has a keen commercial spirit and a willingness to look beyond its Adriatic shores, born of centuries of trading with the Arab world and other points east.

No one would argue that Bari shares Milan's gleam or finger-snap pace. But along its sun-sprayed, palm-lined streets are well-dressed strollers and fashionable stores whispering of an affluence not often associated with Italy's troubled south.

''We are people who are used to being better off than everyone else in this region,'' said Franco Passaro, president of the major local bank.

But he and others acknowledge that even the Milan of the south is still, by definition, the south, and in Italy that means unemployment and low wages, frustration and petty crime.

First-time visitors to Bari are warned repeatedly to beware of purse-snatchers, and a serenade of car alarms attests to the frequency of either vehicle break-ins or mechanical breakdowns. European Integration Ahead

That there are serious social and economic problems in the Mezzogiorno, as the Italian south is called, is about as much a revelation as the fact that the residents here eat pasta. But the approaching economic integration of Western Europe in 1992 has created new uncertainties, especially for a city like Bari.

Many civic leaders say they believe that because of their entrepreneurial traditions and their freedom from the organized-crime syndicates that control much of the Mezzogiorno, they have the means for a genuine economic takeoff. Others, however, worry about their ability even to taxi to the runway.

''The Mezzogiorno knows what it must do to insure its survival,'' said Gianfranco Dioguardi, president of Tecnopolis, a high-technology consortium here. ''But if change does not come, then survival will be very difficult. We will be forced then to live at the margins.''

As Italy prepares for the end of 1992, when the European Community will move toward a single market by eliminating trade barriers, many Italians regard the south as equivalent to a lead weight strapped to their running shoes in their race against the rest of Europe. Industries, financial services and high-tech resources are heavily concentrated in the north. Here, the needed capital and labor skills are in short supply.

The gap between north and south remains formidable despite billions of dollars that the Italian Government and the European Community have poured over the last 40 years into the Mezzogiorno, which means midday, a reference to the fierce midday sun that bakes the region.) Money Dribbled Away The assistance went far to eliminate backwardness and the gut-wrenching poverty that once defined regions like Sicily and Calabria. All too often, though, money dribbled away. Steel and chemical factories were built, for example. But when global demand for those types of heavy products dwindled in the 1970's, they were summarily abandoned. Now, Italians refer to the plants as ''cathedrals in the desert.''

Northerners blame basic southern inefficiency, ineffectual local governments and the Mafia for the waste. But southerners say the money was never enough, and they criticize the north for exploitation and Rome for poor central planning. Too much was spent on make-work jobs, they contend. That may have raised incomes, they say, but it fostered a welfare mentality and contributed little to long-term development.

By some yardsticks, the divisions are wider than ever.

Unemployment in the south is 21.6 percent, compared with 12.4 percent nationwide and only 6 percent in the prosperous northern province of Lombardy. The average per-capita income in Calabria is less than half that in Lombardy. Public services, rich sources of complaints across the Italian archipelago, are often worse in the south than elsewhere. A Degree Is No Ticket

It was once thought that education was the ticket to good times, but that is no longer so.

Attilio Alto, rector of the University of Bari, said many of his students obtain degrees in traditionally respected fields like philosophy and jurisprudence, only to discover, as F. Scott Fitzgerald wrote, that no one is more limited than the well-rounded man. It would not hurt to have a few more engineers and computer scientist, Professor Alto said.

Armed with their humanities diplomas, Mezzogiorno graduates find few desirable jobs, and they contribute to the region's 45 percent unemployment rate for those aged 14 to 29. In the past, many of those young people, especially the men, would have migrated north. Now, with job prospects dim for workers lacking technical skills, they stay home.

''The high unemployment presents worrisome social conditions,'' Mr. Passaro said. ''But I don't think the situation is explosive.''

Like many of this city's 360,000 residents, he looks to 1992 with measured confidence.

Unlike other parts of the Mezzogiorno, Bari does not depend on one industry for its well-being but rather on a balanced blend of agriculture and small companies. The per-capita gross product here was $6,118 in 1986, the last year with complete statistics; that compared with $5,254 in Reggio Calabria, a hard-pressed city on the toe of the Italian boot.

Mr. Dioguardio's Tecnopolis is touted as a potential mini-Silicon Valley. A new, still-incomplete complex for wholesalers called Baricentro is put forth as an example of the city's commercial aggressiveness.

If any corner of the south can prosper, it is Bari, people here say, although they recognize that they are still in the Mezzogiorno, far from the center of Europe.
