Colorado's Sahara Sands

By DYAN ZASLOWSKY; DYAN ZASLOWSKY lives in Evergreen, Colo., and writes frequently about the West.
Published: April 30, 1989

SHORTLY after dawn one day in October I arrived at Great Sand Dunes National Monument in south-central Colorado to watch the sun peel onto the sand. There was a glorious sunrise as I hiked toward the sweeping crescent-shaped ridges of sand that, on a knife edge, split the darkness from light.

It was cool at that early hour, about 7:30, but fall - like spring -is a good time to wander among these 700-foot-high dunes, the tallest in North America. They offer countless compositions to photographers, the best of which are evident early or late in the day when the play of form and light changes every moment. My own favorite aspect of the dunes is the way a long walk through them feels on my bare feet. Shoes ruin the palpable beauty of the dunes. Wearing them compresses the sand into grains that feel like glass. Without shoes the sand turns to velvet underfoot. Once I went to the Great Sand Dunes in the middle of the day when the sun was blazing and the sand was too hot. Even on an October afternoon, with the air at 72 degrees, the sand's temperature reached 90. In summer, 140 degrees is typical for the sand, and therefore midday in this season is too oppressive to be enjoyable, although the air temperature may be bearable.

On my autumn morning the sun had already carpeted the steep slopes above me, while the subfreezing temperature from the night before had sunk into the shadowed troughs and left them as cold as a well. Passing through these low points, I considered putting on the sneakers slung over my pack. Instead I dug my feet into the ground as far as I could to reach the remnant of warmth stored beneath the surface from the previous day's heat.

Great Sand Dunes National Monument was established in 1932 to preserve the dunes and their surroundings. The monument, which includes a portion of trailed forest slope in the Sangre de Cristo Mountains northeast of the dunes, is administered by the National Park Service, which staffs a visitor center throughout the year. Nearby campsites are available on a first come first served basis; only sites for groups may be reserved.

The Great Sand Dunes may well be the oddest natural spectacle in Colorado. Capt. Zebulon Montgomery Pike saw them in the winter of 1807 during an exploratory mission of the Louisiana Purchase's southwest boundary. In his journal he reported that the dunes' appearance ''was exactly that of the sea in a storm, except as to color, not the least sign of vegetation existing on them.'' A thousand miles from an ocean and several thousand miles from the Sahara, which, according to some travelers, they resemble in miniature, the dunes are exquisitely irrelevant in this place, a creased scrap of raw material left over from the creation of the world. One of the visual pleasures of visiting in fall is to see the forests of aspen flare brilliantly on the mountainsides and confuse the landscape's context even more.

THE dunes cover 55 square miles. On the eastern side they lap the toe of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains, and on the west the sand gently slopes into the San Luis Valley. The Great Sand Dunes stand in big company: the mountain range bolts out of the ground and several of its peaks top 14,000 feet.

The San Luis Valley, 7,000 feet below these snowy summits, is almost three times the size of Delaware. The valley is as flat as an outstretched palm, and broadcasts that emptiness peculiar to Western basins, a sense that there is a lot of sky weighing on a thin line of earth.

Mountains and valley consort with the wind to keep the dunes in place, give or take a 20-foot lateral creep east or west each year. Meanwhile, they are built higher grain by grain. Chiefly, it is a southwesterly wind that arranges the dunes. It originates on the far side of the San Luis Valley 60 miles away in the San Juan Mountains, which also reach 14,000 feet in places. Southwesterlies blow steadily across the arid valley, sweeping up the friable silt that had collected there when it was the bottom of an ancient lake. The wind bounces the dirt along the ground in a refining process called saltation. There are no obstacles in the wind's path until it runs head on into the Sangre de Cristos. Loaded with grit eroded from both the San Juan Mountains and the San Luis Valley, the wind is usually unable to rise over the Sangre de Cristos. If the burdened southwesterly does make it up to the range's lower passes, a strong northeasterly wind is there to press it back. Repeatedly, the cargo of fine particles falls back into the seam binding the San Luis to the Sangre de Cristos and the signature of the last prevailing wind is written in the direction the sand crests face.

The dunes' general blondness is attributed to the San Juans' sandstone across the valley. Close scrutiny reveals dark grains of sand as well as light ones. These are granitic particles of the Sangre de Cristos, which are eternally dissolving into the small rivers created by their snow-melt in early spring. Medano Creek, for instance, is born at the top of 13,290-foot Mount Herard in the mountain range. Every spring it washes Sangre de Cristo granite into the valley, where the wind commingles the fairer grains with the darker ones and blows them into the dunes.
