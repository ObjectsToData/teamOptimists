DON'T BE CRUEL OR REASONABLE

By JENNY TEICHMAN; Jenny Teichman, a fellow of New Hall, Cambridge, is the author of ''Philosophy and the Mind.''
Published: April 23, 1989

CONTINGENCY, IRONY, AND SOLIDARITY By Richard Rorty. 201 pp. New York: Cambridge University Press. Cloth, $34.50. Paper, $10.95.

British and American philosophy has recently become extraordinarily scholastic, obsessed with questions about how many philosophers can sit on a niggle. Modern French philosophy, on the other hand, is often highly obscure. To his credit, Richard Rorty, a professor of humanities at the University of Virginia, resists both scholasticism and obscurity, and has written a book lay people can understand. It is a mix of literary criticism and philosophy. I will discuss the philosophy.

Mr. Rorty holds that in the human race ''socialization . . . goes all the way down,'' which is to say it accounts for all our behavior. From this it follows, he thinks, that there is no such thing as an objective difference between cruelty and kindness, no such things as human nature and human solidarity, no such thing as objective reason and no such thing as truth. His conclusion is ''there is no way to bring self-creation together with justice at the level of theory'' - which means there is no way to reconcile public and private good.

It has to be said that schools of philosophy whose slogans take the form ''there isn't any such thing as such and such'' (for instance, nature, truth, soul or whatever) are nothing new. They have always been around and, what is more, they have always been popular with the general public. ''Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity'' will have many readers.

In my view people believe that socialization ''goes all the way down'' because they want to believe it; it's a political dogma. The evidence for it is weak, to say the least. Science indicates that the genetic code takes over for part of ''the way down.'' Reason and experience tell us that no socialization can alter the fact that ''when one is 'designing a gun or an airplane' two and two have to make four,'' as Mr. Rorty quotes George Orwell saying.

Mr. Rorty has answers to this kind of move: he says that reason itself is a ladder that ''can now be dispensed with - and should be dispensed with, in order to help bring the liberal utopia . . . into existence.'' But wasn't the dispensability of reason supposed to follow from the thesis about socialization? Can it also be used to support that thesis? Alas, pointing out logical circularities is using reason again, and as such involves a refusal to deal with Mr. Rorty's philosophy on its own terms.

The author likes liberalism, and wishes to defend it. Can he do this while denying the validity of reason? Has he painted himself into a corner? Maybe not. For he holds that liberalism can be defended by the use of the creative imagination.

The liberal ironist, an idealized figure for whom Mr. Rorty designs a utopia, feels that cruelty is the worst thing there is (this is the ''liberal'' bit) but also believes that there is no objective ''better'' and ''worse'' (this is the ''irony''). Mr. Rorty exhorts us all to develop creative imagination by watching television docudramas and reading books, particularly the kind of novels and ethnographic works that depict cruelty. He thinks this will diminish the chances of our becoming cruel.

The reason-lover might respond as follows: First, it looks as though Mr. Rorty is committed, after all, to there being at least one common natural human faculty, the imagination. For if not, how can he recommend a program for its development? Second, given his assumptions, it is hard to see how he can so confidently predict the result of imaginative investigations into cruelty; don't forget that these are to be carried out by those who do not believe in an objective ''better'' and ''worse.'' Might not the recommended television watching and book reading destroy their liberalism, leaving only their irony, for example? But again we are attempting to give logical, theoretical, reasonable rejoinders; again we fail to enter into discourse with Mr. Rorty on his own terms.

So now - on the principle that if we can't beat them let's join them - I shall follow Mr. Rorty's advice and use my creative imagination.

Consider the liberal ironist. My creative imagination tells me that he certainly does not expect ever to be the victim of cruelty in his own person, and that his irony about values rests on that supposition. My imagination also tells me that he is heavily padded with money and with unwarranted self-esteem, and that his personal safety rests partly on the fact that his fellow citizens, most of them, are not moral ironists.

We might compare him, too, in imagination, with people who have spoken or written about the cruelty that they themselves have suffered: let's say, with Alexander Solzhenitsyn or Primo Levi. How does he compare, according to your creative imagination? My creative imagination tells me that the liberal ironist, when compared with men like these, shows up as shallow, priggish and voyeuristic. Now let's ask: what does the creative imagination tell us about the probable sources of the liberal ironist's special dogmas - namely, that there is no such thing as human nature or human solidarity or objective morality or objective truth?

These dogmas are not grounded in reason, nor indeed in the imagination. Some philosophers will trace their first articulation to Nietzsche, a few to Plato's Thrasymachus. But forget the philosophers. The creative, ethnography-reading imagination tells us, surely, that these dogmas represent nothing more or less than the tribal mores of teen-age human males. As such they can be explained in terms of the evolutionary history of the human race, just as the temporary ''loner'' behavior of the adolescent male lion can be explained in terms of the evolutionary history of lions.

Why do philosophers elevate the temporary instinctual behavior patterns of the male human teen-ager into (allegedly) self-evident truths? The creative imagination readily suggests an answer, but it is not a very polite one.
