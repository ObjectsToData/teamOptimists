WASHINGTON TALK; The Budget

By David E. Rosenbaum
Published: April 4, 1989

This is one person's experience playing the Deficit Reduction Game, a computer game developed by a Federal budget official to illustrate the challenge of balancing the budget. The choices the player faces at each step are the same ones confronting White House and Congressional budget negotiators as they try to cut the deficit from more than $150 billion to zero in five years.

Step 1. Establish principles. Given dozens of alternatives for limiting spending or raising taxes, these guidelines seemed reasonable:

* No cuts in programs for the poor, who suffered more than most groups during the Reagan Administration and are only a small fraction of the budget anyway.

* No across-the-board freeze in domestic programs. Politicians, like umpires, are paid to make tough calls.

* No direct cuts in Social Security benefits. For one thing, they would never be passed by Congress.

* No increase in income tax rates, to avoid upsetting the delicate compromises of the 1986 tax law and, because of his campaign promises, President Bush.

* Divide deficit cuts as evenly as possible between revenue increases and spending reductions and divide spending cuts more or less evenly between military and domestic programs.

Step 2. Cut spending. The game gives five alternatives for military spending: two for large spending increases, two for substantial reductions and the fifth for a one-year spending freeze, essentially what President Bush has proposed. Take the freeze. Save $13 billion.

The options listed for Social Security involve reducing benefits, a no-no under the self-imposed guidelines. But under the revenue alternatives can be found a plan to tax 85 percent of the benefits. Save $4.5 billion.

Medicare seems a fertile ground. Without changes, spending will almost double by 1994. Alter the way payments are made to hospitals and doctors. Raise slightly the premiums paid by the elderly for supplementary insurance, arguing that this is fair because Social Security benefits are keeping up with inflation. Save $18 billion.

Reduce cost-of-living increases for retired Federal military and civilian workers. Save $6 billion. Change the agriculture price support rules. Save $3 billion. Charge user fees for various commercial and recreational enterprises. Pick up $2 billion.

Other cuts are relatively nickel and dime: eliminate money for Amtrak, reduce mass transit grants, cut subsidies for the arts and humanities, end some postal subsidies, among many others - painful choices all and politically controversial but one way to make sure half the burden of deficit reduction is to come from spending cuts. Save altogether $6 billion.

Step 3. Raise taxes. Wealthy people gained most from the tax cuts of the Reagan years. Set further limits on deductions for business meals and entertainment, raise $5 billion; tax capital gains when the holder dies, raise $6 billion; put a $20,000 ceiling on the mortgage interest a couple can deduct in any year, raise $3 billion.

Raise the gasoline tax of 9.5 cents a gallon by 25 cents. That puts the price at the pump about what it was a decade ago. Raise $20 billion. Increase taxes on cigarettes, beer and wine. Raise $7.5 billion. Step 4. Calculate. The computer finds a hidden bonanza. The deficit cuts thus far save $30 billion in interest payments. Another $30 billion is gained from expected economic growth. That puts the deficit in fiscal year 1994, the fifth year of the game, at only about $1 billion.

Step 5. Compromise principles. Freeze spending on most domestic programs for one year but allow spending for welfare, food stamps and other programs for the poor to rise by 5 percent a year above what will be needed to keep pace with inflation. Save $2 billion, leaving an actual budget surplus of $1 billion.

Step 6. Take stock. On paper, the player has now eliminated the deficit. No new spending has been allowed for waste at nuclear power plants, new prisons, the homeless or the war on drugs. But then, neither President Bush nor Congress has addressed those problems either.

A disk containing the Deficit Reduction Game can be obtained from Art Stigile, Office of Management and Budget, Washington, D.C. 20503. Buyers should enclose a check for $10 made out to the Treasury. LOTUS 1-2-3 2.0 or 2.01 is required to operate the game.
