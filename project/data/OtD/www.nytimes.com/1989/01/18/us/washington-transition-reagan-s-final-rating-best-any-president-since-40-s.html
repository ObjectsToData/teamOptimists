WASHINGTON IN TRANSITION; Reagan's Final Rating Is Best Of Any President Since 40's

By STEVEN V. ROBERTS, Special to the New York Times
Published: January 18, 1989

As President Reagan prepares to leave office this week, two-thirds of the American people approve his performance over the last eight years, the highest rating given any President at the end of his term since World War II.

The latest New York Times/CBS News Poll showed that Mr. Reagan has maintained this extraordinary popularity by succeeding in the two areas that are central to any President: peace and prosperity.

While 68 percent approved of his overall job performance over eight years, 71 percent supported his handling of foreign relations, particularly with the Soviet Union, and 62 percent backed his handling of the economy. The margin of sampling error was plus or minus three percentage points.

Ironically, even though Mr. Reagan campaigned for the Presidency in 1980 and 1984 by running against Washington, his popular tenure in the capital has markedly increased public trust in government. It even increased support for a larger Federal establishment providing more domestic services. While the President has often decried government as the cause of the nation's problems, not the solution to them, a clear plurality now rejects that argument and sees a need for more government, for the first time since Mr. Reagan took office. Criticism in Critical Areas

This demand for bigger government, and more services, reflects a widespread feeling that despite steady economic growth and promising international trends, all is not well for many citizens. While most Americans express vast affection for the first President in 28 years to complete two full terms, many of the 1,533 people interviewed by telephone from Jan. 12 through 15 criticized his record in a number of critical areas, from drugs to education to the environment.

Moreover, in regard to one of the most painful episodes of the Reagan years, more than half of those questioned still say Mr. Reagan is lying when he denies knowing that money from Iranian arms sales went to help the rebels in Nicaragua.

Nancy Reagan, in a recent televised interview on CBS News's ''60 Minutes,'' conceded that the scandal had caused a ''tough time'' in the White House. Referring to her husband, she said: ''That was the first time that his credibility was ever, ever questioned.''

The President offered his own explanation for the enduring popularity this poll demonstrated. Asked why he thought so many people approved of his performance, Mr. Reagan replied through a spokesman: ''Well, I genuinely like people, and I think, perhaps, people can tell.''

''But more importantly,'' Mr. Reagan added, ''I've always thought of myself as a citizen politician, speaking up for the ideas and values and common sense of every day Americans. That's what I've always tried to do, and maybe that's what it is they like.''

Historians tend to agree that at this point Mr. Reagan's approval rating reflects warm, even sentimental feelings about the man himself, more than a hard-eyed evaluation of his policies and their implications. Assessments of Historians

Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., who has written extensively about John F. Kennedy and Franklin D. Roosevelt, said of Mr. Reagan: ''He's like a nice, old uncle, who comes in, and all the kids are glad to see him. He sits around telling stories, and they're all fond of him, but they don't take him too seriously.''

Mr. Schlesinger, who is Schweitzer Professor of the Humanities at the City University of New York, conceded that Mr. Reagan's ''rapprochement with the Soviet Union has been enormously popular,'' and that the country was now enjoying ''a fairly buoyant economy.''

Stephen E. Ambrose, biographer of Dwight D. Eisenhower and Richard M. Nixon, attributes part of Mr. Reagan's popularity to his ''very good luck'' in succeeding a string of failed Presidents.

''If he just stayed out of war, and avoided a major depression, almost anybody would have that rating after what we've been through,'' argued Mr. Ambrose, a professor of history at the University of New Orleans.

In addition, he said, the American Presidency ''has become a symbol that is almost a monarchy,'' and like the Queen of England, Mr. Reagan's personal standing does not depend entirely on government policy. ''As the Presidency becomes more imperial, more symbolic, he gets rated on that basis,'' said Professor Ambrose.

But Frank Donatelli, Mr. Reagan's political adviser, said the poll measured more than the President's personal charm. ''Some people dismiss it as personality, but I would call it leadership,'' Mr. Donatelli said. ''There's nothing a President can do to please a majority of the people all the time. He has to take too many actions, there are too many shades of gray. What makes a great President is the ability to take certain actions the public might not agree with, and still maintain their trust.'' Previous Highs Are Tied

While it is common for Presidents to lose popularity in their time in office, Mr. Reagan's current approval rating of 68 percent ties his previous high, recorded in December 1985 and May 1986. And it is 27 points above his low point, reached in the recession of early 1983.
