FOREIGN AFFAIRS; Opportunity For Bush

By Flora Lewis
Published: January 25, 1989

The proliferation of all kinds of Soviet-American exchanges in the last couple of years is staggering. Where once they involved a small number of experts, usually carefully vetted by Moscow, now people are jaunting back and forth to take a joint look at almost every imaginable issue.

Once the gates of political-military confrontation began to open, a flood poured through that demonstrated an intensity of interest and eagerness for contact scarcely imagined. The Johns Hopkins School of Advanced International Studies has already issued a whole series of proposals for further projects, ranging from a bilateral endowment for the arts and humanities through cooperation in surgical oncology to how to convert nuclear missiles to peaceful use.

The talk goes well beyond the familiar propaganda sparring and cautious probing to practical matters far outside of politics. It shows how much was blocked by the taut barriers, and how easy it is to open up once the political leadership wills it.

This isn't the euphoric complacency, succumbing to artful seduction, that the cold warriors worried would afflict the West if Moscow ever got smart enough to smile. It includes a lot of hard-headed approaches to real problems that couldn't be tackled when it had to be assumed that one side's meat was the other side's poison.

That's why the cries of dismay at U.S. agreement to let Moscow be host at a human rights conference in 1991 are hard to understand. It's amazing that Moscow wants to be the site of another review of rights still being denied, but it's a good place for the West to keep pushing for standards set in the Helsinki accords to be honored.

Obviously, the same freedom of access and discussion required at previous conferences must be assured. The Russians should be aware they risk a fiasco if not.

Immediately after Vienna, their Rumanian allies arrogantly proclaimed they wouldn't observe com-mitments just signed, and their Czechoslovak allies started bashing peaceful demonstrators and rearresting Helsinki monitors. Maybe Moscow can't prevent these outrages, but it should be embarrassed and expect to hear a lot about it. It has a stake now.

That is why the whole campaign for human rights and stubborn support for those who are persecuted by their governments have been so crucial. They created a stake for regimes that refused to recognize any obligation but their own power. It isn't enough to dole out prisoners, as the former Soviet prisoner Natan Sharansky has written, but it is evidence that continued pressure can bring results, and the closer to home the better.

The prudence the West needs to show isn't in dealing with the East but in judging its own objectives and enlisting Soviet self-interest in meeting them. President Bush is right to say he will continue to look for new accords but won't be in a hurry to make them.

Ex-President Reagan presided over a remarkable transition of America's sense of itself and its relations with the East. First there was the revival of self-confidence, and then a somewhat helter-skelter response to Mikhail Gorbachev's decisions to seek agreements and wind down conflicts.

The time has come to pause and consider how best to move to the new stage. The long term is easy - peace, well-being, freedom are the obvious goals. The medium term is where coherent thought is needed.

In the rush of change provoked by Mr. Gorbachev's efforts to stabilize and improve Soviet foreign relations so his country can focus on internal renewal, we have come to count on the Soviets to help us break a good many logjams. We expect them to make sure the Cubans leave Angola, and the Vietnamese withdraw from Cambodia. They cannot contribute much to a Middle East settlement at this stage, but it makes a difference that they no longer seem determined to prevent one.

Getting out of Afghanistan is their problem, but there is a certain common interest in how a new Afghan regime emerges. It would be bad for everybody if it turns out to be Khomeini-type. Central America is essentially our problem, but the Soviets influence Cuba, which influences Nicaragua. The example of political settlements elsewhere can help.

We seem to be looking to the Russians to get us off the hook of Mr. Reagan's dreamy commitment to Star Wars as part of a strategic arms reduction treaty. Maybe a ban on nuclear tests would ease the mess we face with nuclear weapons plants.

Now we must go beyond wondering what Mr. Gorbachev has in mind to what we want to achieve. Mr. Bush has a splendid opportunity.
