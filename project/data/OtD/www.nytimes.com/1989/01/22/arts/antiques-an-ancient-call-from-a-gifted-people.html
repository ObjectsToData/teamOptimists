ANTIQUES; An Ancient Call From a Gifted People

By RITA REIF
Published: January 22, 1989

The world of the ancient Nazca civilization of South America comes alive in a killer whale rendered in a whiplash of clay and in boldly painted images on bottles and jars, depicting whiskered cougars, fierce-eyed condors and hummingbirds soaring.

Nazca polychrome pottery, made between 100 B.C. and A.D. 800 on the southern coast of what is now Peru, dominates the assemblage of 70 ceramic, metal, textile and shell items in a large showcase of the serenely lighted new Hall of South American Peoples at the American Museum of Natural History.

The vitrine of Nazca artifacts is one of several dozen in the 11,000-square-foot space used to exhibit 2,300 objects made over 12,000 years. The museum's South American archeological and ethnographic holdings - most of which have been off view since 1959 - represents one of the largest and finest treasuries of such material in the United States.

''We have an unsually strong Nazca collection,'' said Dr. Craig Morris, chairman of the museum's department of anthropology, who organized the show's Andean archeology section. Nazca craftsmen who excelled in ceramics perfected pre-fired painted decoration on pottery. The large repertory of images these aboriginal people developed vividly portray their everyday concerns about fertility and the predatory creatures of the sea, land and skies. The Nazca items were acquired between 1915 and 1945, mostly from existing collections. The vessels on view, selected from about 500 Nazca pots in the museum's collection, are in a near-perfect state of preservation, said Peter Kvietok, a museum research assistant who specializes in the study of ancient technologies and ancient music. ''On most, the major conservation treatment required was the removal of salt that remained from surrounding soil.'' The metal mouth masks, shell necklaces and tiny brilliantly colored woven birds were selected to show how some of the same themes were executed in a variety of materials. Whiskered feline images, suggesting probably the two largest predatory animals known to the Nazca - the cougar or puma from the highlands and the jaguar from the jungle - are a case in point. They are painted on the faces of trophy heads, vaguely human and partly animal, that embellish several pots. They are also cut out of gold sheeting to enhance a mouth mask that looks as festive as a gift wrapping topped by a clutch of ribbons.

Nazca pottery evolved from highly realistic and strongly expressive works of the early Nazca (100 B.C. to A.D. 300) through more sophisticated portrayals of the middle period (300 to A.D. 400) to the proliferation of motifs, refined shaping and more abstract detailing of the late period (400 to A.D. 800). Although Nazca iconography is still being studied, Mr. Kvietok said, much is already known of this pre-Incan civilization - some of it from the imagery depicted on these ceramics.

A clay pottery figure of a man with an elongated pointed head depicts a fashion among the Nazca that predated the Chinese practice of binding the feet of small girls. ''The Nazca applied pressure to the heads of infants, using boards or cord wrappings to reshape their heads,'' Mr. Kvietok said. ''I suspect the deformed look was an indication of social status among the elite.'' The figure wears a large shell necklace and a bikini. Other humans are shown holding rattles, or with a serpent coiled like a hat on the head - a symbol of fertility.

The sculptural vessels, which take the shapes of a killer whale, the head of an animal and the body of a fisherman who is swimming and catching fish with his bare hands, are among the most impressive. These early Nazca pots are vigorously shaped and graphically arresting, painted with bold strokes and vivid colors.

Other cylindrical, round and oval vessels are decorated with a beady-eyed condor, clawing crayfish, foxes, snakes and monkeys. ''The monkey wasn't found on the coast so it was probably brought by exchange to the area,'' Mr. Kvietok said. Plants - beans, maize and peppers - are another favorite theme on these tomb vessels, which were probably very like those pots used in everyday life by the Nazca, Mr. Kvietok said.

Nazca potters also made several of the musical instruments seen in a nearby showcase. An earthenware panpipe in a deep red, from the middle period, echoes the shape of a hummingbird's wing. The ancient musical instruments in this case, which also include a Nazca ceramic drum and two trumpets, were used to create the tape of musical sounds and Andean Indian music heard in the hall. ''Part of the tape plays the notes the instruments were capable of producing,'' Mr. Kvietok said. ''Then, for the rest, we played some very simple melodies of Andean music.''

Money for the redesign, reconstruction and installation of the Hall of South American Peoples was provided by the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, the City of New York and the Anschutz Foundation, established by Philip F. Anschutz, the Denver oilman who is chairman of the Anschutz Corporation.

Since the patrimony laws of Peru forbid removing Nazca and other pre-Columbian works without official permission, collectors are warned by to be extremely careful in acquiring them. The laws state that even though a property may have been purchased openly in the country of origin, all such objects are owned by the Government. They are regarded as stolen when removed without government authorization.

Nazca ceramics are occasionally seen in galleries and auctions. Sotheby's has sold such vessels for between $500 and $5,500 in recent years. A killer-whale vessel from a well-known collector was sold there for that top price in May 1987.
