ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: January 22, 1989

After 14 years, ''The Papers of William Livingston'' a major publishing project that was edited by Carl E. Prince, was completed last month when its fifth and final volume was issued by the Rutgers University Press.

Livingston was the first State Governor of New Jersey, from 1776 to 2790. Thomas H. Kean counts him among his forebears because Mr. Kean, who was elected Governor in 1981, is directly descended from Peter Van Brugh Livingston, William Livingston's older brother.

''The key to the importance of the papers,'' Dr. Prince said in an interview, ''is that for me and for the other editors and for the historians who have reviewed these volumes, the knowledge that they convey elevates Livingston for the first time to the front rank of America's founding fathers.''

Although the project has been critically acclaimed within the academic community, Dr. Prince admits that he has been asked more than once, ''Why was it so essential to publish these papers?''

He tells his questionsers, he said to open the books ''and experience a national treasure first hand.''

''Both in Governor Livingston's public and personal correspondence,'' Dr. Prince said, ''we find a vivid chronicle of the Revolutionary War; we see the story of the shaping of this country leaping from the lines of his pen.''

''In comparison,'' he said, ''so many of the usual accounts of our war for independence seem downright dull.''

The volumes document the history of New Jersey's mid-18th century evolution from a colony in chaos. Sects and splinter groups of anti-Royalists and Whigs fought to cut the colonies' ties with England while living next to Loyalists who supported British rule.

Never before has the drama of New Jersey's formative role in the revolutionary process been so closely scruntinized with direct access to the original sources. The papers are a boon to historians and readers who hunger to learn more about the drama.

Dr. Prince, a professor of history at New York University and currently a visiting scholar at the University of California at Berkeley, recalled that he first became involved with the project in 1974 when Richard P. McCormick, a Rutgers professor, suggested that he compile, edit and publish the Livingston papers as a contribution to the Bicentennial of the American Revolution. In 1963 Dr. Prince had received his Ph.D. in history under the tutelage of Dr. McCormick, one of New Jersey's foremost historians and a member of the New Jersey Historical Commission.

Grants from the New Jersey American Revolution Bicentennial Celebrations Commission, the National Historical Publicationsand Records Commission and the National Endowment for the Humanities helped to finance the project, and New York University provided money, graduate assistance, an office and library space.

The first two volumes, which appeared in 1979 and 1980, were published by the New Jersey Historical Commission; the final three were published by Rutgers, which is selling the complete set for $200.

''There's a facinating quality about Livingston, a kind of peripatetic inquisitiveness,'' Dr. Prince said in describing the ease with which the successful and articulate New York lawyer, the father of 13 children opted in 1772, at the age of 49, for a life in rural Elizabetow, the site of what is today the city of Elizabeth. His home there, Liberty Hall still stands.

In 1774 Livingston, who was a member of one of the wealthies colonial families, became embroiled in the local opposition to British rule. Two years later, the squire of Liberty Hall would become the general commanding the New Jersey militia. Chosen as Governor by the Legislature in 1776, he retained that post through 14 terms until his death in 1790 at the age of 77.

The portrait of the Livingstons that emerges from the personal correspondence in the volumes often brings to life, but does not enshrine, 18th-century life. The family was part of America's colonial ''royalty.'' The encounters of father and daughters, favorite and disinherited sons, and daughters-in-law are heightened by gossip, scandal and the presumption of class in a presumably classless society - all against the backdrop of the once idyllic Liberty Hall.

There, as the Revolutionary War intensified, assassins sought out Livingston - unsuccessfully. It all makes for a saga no less tumultous than Evelyn Waugh's ''Brideshead Revisited.''

''Some of the personal insight to be gleaned from these volumes,'' Drr. Prince said, ''come from the description of Livingston's pleasures and pains in raising his children, not unfamiliar in our day where so many times persons of eminence expect their offspring to follow a particular pplan of advancement.''

He contends, however, that ''there is no forgetting that this distinguished family was unique for its era in the way that Livingston's daughters were encouraged to be strong, independent women.

Sarah, the Governor's fourth daughter, married John Jay, a foundling father, Minister to Spain, Governor of New York and first Chief Justice of the United States. Another, Susannah, married John Cleves Symmes, who settled in Ohio and founded the city of Cleveland.

A third daughter, Mary, married - and divorced - a member of Congress, James Linn of New Jersey.
