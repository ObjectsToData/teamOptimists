When Violence and Terror Strike Outside the Schools

By FELICIA R. LEE
Published: November 14, 1989

''I just walk straight home and I don't look at nobody or talk to nobody,'' said Jackie Peterson, a 15-year-old sophomore at Julia Richman High School on the Upper East Side of Manhattan, describing her afternoon routine. ''People are waiting for people after school to jump on them.''

Violence inside New York City schools is still a problem, as seen by the fatal shooting yesterday of a student at a Bronx high school. But school officials and students say that what frightens most students these days goes on just outside the schools, often when classes are through for the day.

Students and principals report that bands of youths periodically roam from school to school, picking fights with students and robbing them.

''I'm sure we're all feeling the same way - we're under siege,'' said Joan Jarvis, the principal of Bayard Rustin High School for the Humanities in the Chelsea section of Manhattan. ''The whole system is held hostage by a roving band of crazies who want to cause trouble. It breaks my heart to make this an armed camp for my children, who are very good.''

Not all of the violence near schools can be laid to these fast-moving, shadowy ''posses.'' In one recent case, a 19-year-old named Gemel Mitchell was shot and killed by a fellow Richman student in a subway car. It was a continuation of a fight that started in the school cafeteria when someone threw a cookie at Mr. Mitchell. When Do Posses Strike? And yesterday, a 17-year-old student was shot and killed between classes at Alfred E. Smith High School on East 151st Street in the Bronx. The police said there was no evidence that gangs were involved in the killing.

It is chiefly the posses, though, that create an air of foreboding among the city's teen-agers. Because the gangs do not seem to have stable membership or a steady turf no one knows what to expect. Wearing gold can make a student a target of robbery; so can a stray remark or simply being in the wrong place at the wrong time.

''A lot of the time, you get two guys fighting over a woman,'' said Temajean Seda, a student at Bayard Rustin. ''Then, we heard there was a fight because someone said Chelsea guys are soft.''

''Some of these kids don't feel they fit in anywhere,'' Ms. Seda said. ''They feel important when they're in gangs, but this is a good school and we didn't used to have this kind of trouble. We're sick of them.''

Sgt. John Galea, the commander of the New York City Police Department's youth gang intelligence unit, said the schools are always most troubled at the beginning and the end of the school year. He said New York is fortunate, in fact, because it does not have extremely violent, tightly organized gangs like those in Los Angeles and Chicago. Informal Gangs

Rather, the sergeant said, the groups identified as gangs by New York students come together informally and disperse just as quickly. Youths intent on mischief travel from school to school, adding members along the way ''like a pickup game of basketball,'' he said.

The group members range in age from 12 to their early 20's, but most are high school age.

''We do certainly have a problem during our school day and that's because New York City school kids have to travel distances to get to school,'' Sergeant Galea said. ''We have a lot of groups out there and nobody knows who the players are.''

Sergeant Galea said it has been difficult to contain the loosely organized gangs because they are not as easily identified as the old-time gangs. They form spontaneously, strike quickly and at random, and keep moving. Stay Home, Stay Safe

On Halloween, the police said, Richman students made up the bulk of a gang that went on a rampage on Third Avenue from 78th to 86th Streets, randomly punching people. Thirteen youths were arrested after they invaded a restaurant, pushed over tables and struck two waiters.

''On Halloween, it was safer at my house,'' said David Meja, a 16-year-old Richman sophomore. He was among the 45 percent of public high school students who stayed home on Halloween, many because they said they expected trouble.

In addition to the Halloween incident, two Richman students, including Mr. Mitchell, were shot to death in unrelated incidents away from school in the last few weeks. They were among eight New York high school students shot to death in eight days.

Robert Marquez, a 17-year-old Richman freshman, said he thought the gangs made trouble ''because they enjoy it.'' He added: ''It's like they get power and attention. They can get more girls.'' 'You Can Feel the Tension'

''We're losing our self-esteem,'' said 17-year-old Erasma Bares, the president of Richman's Spanish club. ''The blacks, the minorities, the Puerto Ricans have been taken to be the violent people. When a bunch of us go into a restaurant they make us wait.''

Part of the problem is that few can match names, faces or motives with the violence. The students talk about gangs like the Decepticons, the Autobots or the Chelsea Boys, but Sergeant Galea said their infamy far exceeds their actual deeds. Most students concede they have never seen a particular gang in action.

''When you go out of the building, you can feel the tension,'' said Christine Abdul, a 17-year-old senior at Bayard Rustin.

'' You want to protect yourself,'' she said, ''and at the same time you don't want to hang around the school when it starts to get late.''
