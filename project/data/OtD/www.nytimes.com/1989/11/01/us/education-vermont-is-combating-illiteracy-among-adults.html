EDUCATION; Vermont Is Combating Illiteracy Among Adults

Special to The New York Times
Published: November 1, 1989

Ed McAuley had some advice for Vermont's Commissioner of Education: ''Put all your money, all your best teachers, into the first six grades. No one should leave the sixth grade without learning how to read.''

Mr. McAuley, of Barre, Vt., offered the advice at an unusual conference recently, and he spoke from experience: he graduated from high school without being able to read and write.

As he addressed Commissioner Richard Mills, Mr. McAuley joined about 250 other people who had first-hand experience facing the obstacles of illiteracy. They were gathered here on Oct. 17 in the first statewide convention of illiterate adults. Most of the participants were students in the state's Adult Basic Education program, which means they are on the way to overcoming their educational handicaps.

Conference organizers and participants often spoke of the courage it took to show up and acknowledge that they were adults who were still learning how to read. And, in return, they received support and encouragement from others like themselves. State Literacy Project

The idea for the conference, ''Celebration of Books and People,'' grew out of discussions among teachers of Adult Basic Education, the staff of the Vermont Reading Project and the Vermont Council on the Humanities.

The logistics were daunting, said Sally Anderson, director of the reading project. ''Many of these students don't have telephones or cars. Many have small children. We had to think about how to get the word out and then how to get them here.''

Buses and baby sitters were hired with money from the humanities council and a grant from the International Business Machines Corporation. And despite a chilling rain, the students came.

The education groups, in conjunction with public libraries, are working toward a goal of full literacy for Vermont's estimated 58,000 illiterate adults within 10 years, and toward that end, the groups have been sponsoring book discussions for newly literate adults for the last four years. A statewide conference ''just seemed like the best next step,'' said Victor Swenson, director of the humanities council.

Many of the participants came clutching copies of ''The Great Gillie Hopkins'' (Harper & Row), an award-winning children's book by Katherine Paterson. For some, this story of a smart, but angry foster child was the first book they had read, and they were thrilled that Mrs. Paterson, a resident of Barre, was one of the conference speakers.

In discussions, conference participants told how much they had identified with Gillie, moving frequently, changing schools often and always feeling like an outsider. 'Hardest Thing I Ever Did'

Mr. McAuley said that while he had had a strong family upbringing he missed out on an education, being ''pushed through'' school despite poor grades. He had worked as a machinist for 28 years, hiding his handicap from colleagues, his four children and nearly everyone else but his wife. But then the factory in which he worked closed, forcing Mr. McAuley to admit his need for help in finding another job.

''Asking for help was the hardest thing I ever did,'' Mr. McAuley said. He enrolled in Adult Basic Education in 1985, scheduling his first home tutoring visit ''when I knew no one else would be home.''

A year later he found his present job as an inspector with a company that makes modular housing, and he spent much of his time reading job-related materials. Last year he read a book for the first time in his life: ''Iacocca,'' the autobiography of Lee Iacocca. He said he had become interested in the Chrysler Corporation chairman from watching television.

Mr. McAuley's comments to Dr. Mills came in the workshop ''Advice to the Public Schools.''

The commissioner said, ''This isn't just a topic - I really need advice.'' He said he would particularly like to help the 1,200 Vermont students who drop out of school each year to stay in school.

The advice Dr. Mills received ranged from reinstituting corporal punishment - ''unacceptable,'' the commissioner said - to making kindergarten mandatory.

Carol Houston of Morrisville said: ''I didn't have kindergarten, and when I got to first grade, I was scared out of my pants. I felt like a failure from Day One.'' 'Horror' and Encouragement

Dr. Mills said he was dispirited by some of the ''horror stories'' he heard, but was encouraged when workshop participants spoke with one voice in dealing with a 16-year-old who had just dropped out of school. They urged returning.

In suggesting that the state's school system concentrate on elementary education, Mr. McAuley said, ''If a sophomore quits school knowing how to read and write, then you haven't really lost the student, because when he wakes up at age 20 or 25 and realizes he wants his education, he'll have the skills to go get it.

''But if a sophomore quits school because he can't read and write, then you really have lost him.''
