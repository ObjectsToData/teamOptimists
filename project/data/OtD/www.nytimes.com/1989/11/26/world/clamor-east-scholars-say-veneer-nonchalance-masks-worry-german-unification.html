CLAMOR IN THE EAST; Scholars Say Veneer of Nonchalance Masks Worry on German Unification

By ERIC PACE
Published: November 26, 1989

Experts at American universities say anxiety about the prospect of German reunification is widespread in Europe and the United States despite declarations by Western leaders that they are unconcerned and that the issue should be resolved by the Germans themselves.

Reached while visiting Munich, Fritz Stern, a Columbia University professor who is an authority on Germany, said ''there is concern in Europe'' despite the ''official statements from Western leaders saying they are not worried.''

Mr. Stern, the Seth Low Professor of History at Columbia, said he believed that ''an early reunification is extremely unlikely, for any number of reasons.''

''The only possibility, I would say, would be that if the magnificent and disciplined spontaneity of the East Germans gets out of hand, so to speak, and forces a decision,'' he said. ''If that occurs, then one doesn't know. Then everything's up for grabs.''

''At the moment, I think there is puzzlement and reluctance, even in Bonn'' about reunification, he said. ''I hope it is not ever going to happen without very careful negotiations with the four Allied powers'' - the United States, the Soviet Union, Britain and France. Neighbors Remember the Past

''What I'm concerned about right now is that when Germans start thinking about a future of greatness, their neighbors begin to think about the past,'' he went on. ''This has disturbing psychological implications.''

''On the other hand,'' he said, ''one ought not to forget that the circumstances in which a united Germany would find itself,'' say, at the beginning of the 21st century, ''would be very different from the conditions that existed in 1900.'' Then, he said, ''Germany faced a nonintegrated Europe, and its power was greater than that of any of its neighbors.''

Speaking from California, Gordon A. Craig, an expert on Germany and a former president of the American Historical Association, said he found ''a good deal'' of concern in the United States, as well as Europe, about reunification - concern that he said ''is offset by our admiration of the progress that democracy has made in West Germany, which is undeniable.'' Just an Enlarged West Germany? Professor Craig, who is retired from Stanford University, said, ''I don't think reunification can be avoided in the long run.'' He observed that some commentators, including Vice President Dan Quayle, ''have indicated that they believe it would be fine because a reunified Germany would be simply an enlarged German Federal Republic.''

''Other people, including myself, would consider that a singularly incautious statement,'' Professor Craig said. He noted that other analysts, including Henry A. Kissinger, have said ''they believed reunification was inevitable, but nevertheless indicated that they hoped it could be done, if it must be done, under certain restraints and not too fast, and without destroying the balance of power in Europe, which has been based, after all, on a divided Germany and has endured and given peace for over 40 years.''

Professor Craig, whose books include ''The Germans'' and ''The End of Prussia,'' suggested that the negative feelings were a growing undercurrent in the United States. He added, ''I was surprised to find in the San Francisco Chronicle a cartoon showing Adolf Hitler wearing a label saying 'Reunification.'''

The professor rejected that linkage, saying, ''We have never had any occasion to fear a sudden flare-up of the kind of nationalism'' represented by Nazism, ''or a return of an Adolf Hitler and that sort of thing.''' Fear of Shift in German Thinking

But he raised questions about the thinking and politics of a reunited Germany, saying ''There is no doubt in my mind that a nation of 80 million people, united under one government, will sooner or later begin to think differently than a divided Germany of 57 million on one side and 17 million on the other.''

''How differently they will think, we don't know,'' he added. But he said there was likely to be a greater emphasis on the view that ''we Germans are a nation in the middle of Europe with our own interests, and we are not a Western nation.'''

Professor Craig, an emeritus J. E. Wallace Sterling Professor of Humanities at Stanford, added, ''It would be interesting to speculate on what the composition'' of the parliament ''would be in a new united Germany, how many new parties there would be, and what the strength of the pro-Western democratic parties would be compared with the others.''

William E. Griffith, the Ford Professor of Political Science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and a specialist on contemporary European history, said he felt ''there is more anxiety'' about the prospect of reunification ''in private among non-German West European and American elites than has been publically stated.'' Changes Wrought by Defeat

But Professor Griffith, an official of the American Embassy in Bonn in 1985 and 1986, said, ''The governments of the West are - quite correctly, in my view - supporting the concept of German unity, assuming that the East and West Germans want it.''

''The polls show that West European and American mass public opinion generally seems to endorse this,'' he said, ''and I personally think the fears of these elites are incorrect.''
