Book Notes

By Edwin McDowell
Published: September 27, 1989

Protesting Protesters

''A Day in the Life of China,'' a book of photographs of China, which was excerpted this week in Time Magazine, began last year as a joint venture between Collins Publishers of San Francisco and two Chinese publishers. But it ended a few months ago in a parting of the ways. When Collins refused to remove photographs and text mentioning the student democratic movement, the Chinese disassociated themselves from the project and canceled the Chinese edition of the book.

''We understood their dilemma, but could not comply,'' Bruce W. Gray, president of Collins, said of the Chinese demands. ''It was impossible to ignore the student movement, though the pressure to do so got to be pretty heady.''

The book consists of 200 photographs taken by 90 photographers from five continents. They dispersed throughout China to shoot events that occurred on April 15, 1989. But that turned out to be the day that the reformist Communist Party leader Hu Yaobang died, setting off the student protests of the next seven weeks.

That evening, a Chinese photographer working on the book project heard about the students gathering at Beijing University and managed to photograph the first protest posters - including a poster that said, ''The wrong man died.'' Another photo, also included in the book, shows a crowd watching a monkey dressed as an officer of the People's Liberation Army. Best and Next-Best

Next Sunday, in what may be a publishing first, books by the same author will be No. 1 and No. 2 hard covers on The New York Times nonfiction best-seller list: ''All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kindergarten'' (No. 1) and ''It Was on Fire When I Lay Down on it'' (No. 2). Both are collections of inspirational essays written by Robert Fulghum and published by Villard Books.

''Kindergarten,'' published last October, has been on the list 48 weeks; after an initial printing of 60,000 copies it now has one million in print. ''Fire,'' published last week, already has 500,000 copies in print, after an initial printing of 450,000.

Peter Gethers, the editorial director of Villard, said there were risks in publishing books by the same author less than a year apart, but Villard gambled that customers wanted another Fulghum book. In fact, Mr. Gethers said, publication of ''Fire'' has spurred sales of ''Kindergarten.''

What also entered Villard's thinking is that the paperback edition of ''Kindergarten'' is scheduled from Ballantine Books in December. ''That will slow down the sales of the hard cover a bit,'' Mr. Gethers said, ''but our sales force is absolutely convinced sales will remain strong through Christmas.''

As to the possibility of delaying the paperback, since Villard and Ballantine are both part of Random House Inc., Mr. Gethers said that would not be practical. Not only did Ballantine pay a lot of money for the book ($2.2 million), but its promotional campaign is also tied to December publication. Joining a Consortium

It is now definite that Wesleyan University Press, ending more than 30 years as an independent press, will join the University Press of New England, a publishing consortium for nine other universities, including Brown and Dartmouth. Word of the move, which will be effective Jan. 1, evoked considerable criticism from authors and friends who did want to see the press surrender its independence, but university officers say their decision was prompted by a desire to cut costs.

The University Press of New England, founded in 1970, publishes about 50 books annually, primarily in the social sciences and humanities. Wesleyan will add about 15 new titles a year to that total, including those in its acclaimed poetry series, one of the oldest extant.
