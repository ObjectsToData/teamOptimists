Many Candidates Enter Race For Top National Arts Post

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: March 20, 1989

In the month since Frank Hodsoll resigned as chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts, a campaign for the job has developed that has become more intense than any since the endowment was created by Congress in 1965.

Those enlisted to champion one candidate or another to President Bush, who will make the appointment, include a former President of the United States, the Secretary of State, the Secretary of the Treasury, a veritable army of friends and associates of the Bush family, a Rockefeller or two, two Buckleys, several dozen influential corporate chief executive officers and nearly a quorum of the United States Senate.

Why such interest in a chiefly administrative $82,500-a-year job with only a small staff? ''The chairman expresses the commitment of our nation to the arts,'' explained Livingston Biddle, who held the post during the Carter Administration. ''The person who holds the job is at once regarded as Santa Claus by the whole arts community, whatever the season, or Scrooge, hoarding his resources. He is also regarded as the Oracle of Delphi when it comes to the arts.''

Previous chairmen included the theatrical producer Roger L. Stevens and the foundation executive Nancy Hanks. Although the job offers a platform for speaking out about cultural policy, all previous chairmen have limited their efforts to convincing legislators that the arts are worthy of the public money being spent on them. As a result, the endowment's budget has risen from $5 million in 1966 to $169 million this year. But chairmen, preoccupied with building a broad consensus, have shied away from pressing policy choices that might have proved divisive. Politically, those who have held the post have been members of the President's party.

For the last decade, the agency has been dispensing about $150 million a year in grants to arts organizations and individual artists, from the Metropolitan Opera in New York City to the woodcarving workshops of the Kickapoo Indians in Horton, Kan. The job fell vacant last month when Mr. Hodsoll resigned to take a top job in the Office of Management and Budget.

With the President's decision reported to be imminent, most of the declared candidates are flashing their old school ties, sending their influential supporters into battle and staking out positions on the arts that they hope will catch the eye of the President or the First Lady. Barbara Bush is believed to be taking a special interest in the appointment. The First Lady served on the board of the Houston Ballet in 1979 and 1980, and has made most of the decisions about which entertainment and cultural events the Bushes attend.

The most visible of the candidates are Schuyler G. Chapin of New York City, 66 years old, a former dean of the School of the Arts at Columbia University and onetime general manager of the Metropolitan Opera; Barnabas McHenry, 59, of Harrisburg, Pa., general counsel to the Reader's Digest and a trustee of numerous arts organizations from the Metropolitan Opera and the New York City Ballet to the Egg Theater in Albany; Milton Rhodes, 44, of Great Falls, Mont., president of the American Council for the Arts, a service organization that predates the National Endowment; Lois Burke Shepard, 51, of Hartford, director of the Institute of Museum Services, a Republican fund-raiser who acted as George Bush's spokesman on the arts during the Presidential campaign, and Samuel Lipman, 54, of Los Gatos, Calif., publisher, music critic and cultural commentator of the neo-conservative magazine New Criterion. High and Low Profiles

Visibility, of course, does not always insure success, and the job might be given to someone who has done no campaigning, like Marta Casals Istomin, the artistic director of the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts, who is married to the noted concert pianist Eugene Istomin. She would make a ''fine chairman,'' said Ralph P. Davidson, chairman of the Kennedy Center.

When it comes to old school ties, Mr. McHenry could not be better outfitted, being, as he put it, ''a very dear friend'' of Secretary of the Treasury Nicholas F. Brady and having roomed at Princeton for four years with Secretary of State James A. Baker 3d, who is one of the President's closest advisers. ''That was thinking ahead,'' said Roger L. Stevens, the first chairman of the National Endowment, who is one of Mr. McHenry's supporters. An Enthusiastic Endorsement

Mr. Chapin went to the Millbrook School with former Senator James Buckley of New York, a few years ahead of the columnist William F. Buckley, who has written enthusiastically about the Chapin candidacy to Chase Untermeyer, chief of personnel at the White House.

Mr. Rhodes has mounted a campaign worthy of a Cabinet-level job. Using members of his high-powered board of directors of the American Council for the Arts, Mr. Rhodes has enlisted as his champions former President Gerald R. Ford, 16 United States Senators, at least a dozen chief executive officers and numerous friends and acquaintances of the Bush family.
