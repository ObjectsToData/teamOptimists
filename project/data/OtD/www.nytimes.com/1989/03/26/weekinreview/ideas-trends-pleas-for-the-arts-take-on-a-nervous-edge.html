IDEAS & TRENDS; Pleas for the Arts Take On a Nervous Edge

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: March 26, 1989

IT came as much needed comic relief. Cora Cahan, executive director of the Feld Ballet in New York, was delivering an impassioned statement last week before the House subcommittee that sets the budget of the National Endowment for the Arts. ''We are in peril!'' she exclaimed. ''Oh, yes,'' came the muted reply from Representative Sidney R. Yates, the Chicago Democrat who is chairman of the powerful House Appropriations Subcommittee on the Interior and Related Agencies, ''this subcommittee also handles endangered species.''

It got a good laugh. But Ms. Cahan's message was sounded over and over by Colleen Dewhurst, president of Actors Equity Association; Kitty Carlisle Hart, chairwoman of the New York State Council on the Arts; the actor E. G. Marshall, and two dozen others who came to testify that arts support from the Federal Government did not keep up with inflation during the Reagan years. And that, they said, has now brought about a crisis. Such pleas are not unfamiliar at the annual hearing on the arts budget, but they were delivered with special urgency.

Across the country, the arts establishment fears that the budget deficit will prompt a ''more will than wallet'' Congress to cut domestic programs like the arts endowment in order to spend more on new initiatives like the campaign against drugs, aid to the homeless and health services for AIDS victims. These concerns also come at a time when the Bush Administration is seeking a new chairman for the endowment.

Fears were heightened this month by what arts professionals now call ''the Boston massacre, part II.'' The Massachusetts House of Representatives fought off an attempt to eliminate financing for the State Council on the Arts and Humanities, only to wind up cutting its budget in half. Barbara Anderson, executive director of the Citizens for Limited Taxation, ridiculed the state council as ''trendy, tony, pretentious, presumptuous, elitist.'' Since the Massachusetts cuts were prompted largely by the unexpected discovery of a $600 million deficit, and since other states face large deficits now, arts supporters are worried that, as one person at the Yates hearing put it, ''We all live in Massachusetts.'' Starting in New York

Government support of the arts - a tradition in European countries like West Germany, which gave $1 billion last year to its theaters alone - began in the United States in the early 1960's when New York and a few other states established agencies to distribute modest grants. The National Endowment for the Arts was created in 1965 by an act of Congress. Two years later, in response to the endowment's offer of $25,000 planning grants, virtually every state created an arts agency to foster cultural activities. By 1988, state arts agencies were employing 1,300 workers and 1,800 volunteers to distribute $270 million, a sum that handily outstrips the $169 million budget of the National Endowment.

Economic studies have done much to stimulate political support for arts agencies. In Massachusetts, for example, a report found that the arts draw about $1.2 billion into the state each year. But over the past four years, six state legislatures have seriously debated proposals to eliminate the agencies. Prompted in each case by a local economic crisis like the huge deficit in Massachusetts or the collapse of oil prices, which was devastating in Texas, Louisiana and Alaska, none of these efforts have succeeded in wiping out the agencies. But their budgets have been slashed.

Is the Federal arts budget about to be hit? Milton Rhodes, president of the American Council for the Arts, a nonprofit advocacy group, said that although the Federal arts budget has not kept pace with inflation over the last eight years, appropriations for the National Science Foundation and other Federal agencies have been significantly stepped up.

Leonard Garment, who was a special counsel to the President during the Nixon Administration and helped persuade Mr. Nixon to urge Congress to increase arts spending about 800 percent during his time in office, said that fresh thinking by arts advocates is essential.

''Circumstances have changed,'' he said. ''One has to find out what is right for this time and place.''

Representative Yates, a vigorous champion of the arts budget, is to decide who gets exactly how much next month. The full House Appropriations Committee is unlikely to challenge him when it takes up his budget in the spring, but there could be a floor fight when the appropriations bill reaches the full House this summer. That means arts advocates have about three months to prepare their battle plan.
