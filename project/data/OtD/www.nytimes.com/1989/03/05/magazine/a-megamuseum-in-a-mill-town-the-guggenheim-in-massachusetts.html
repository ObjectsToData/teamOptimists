A MEGAMUSEUM IN A MILL TOWN; The Guggenheim in MAssachusetts?

By Deborah Weisgall; Deborah Weisgall writes frequently on the arts.
Published: March 5, 1989

WHEN I FIRST SAW THESE mills, I thought they were too big.''

Thomas Krens smiles ironically at the idea that he would find anything too large. ''That lasted for about two weeks. Now I don't think they're big enough.'' Krens, 42 years old, the former director of the Williams College Museum of Art and now the director of the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York, is standing on the flat roof of an abandoned 19th-century factory in North Adams, Mass.

This building is part of a monumental complex of 28 structures. The view from the roof is of rosy brick facades with tiers of shallow-arched white wooden windows ringing black-topped courtyards; decorative iron bolts in the brick anchor the post-and-beam construction. Beyond the factory and the surrounding town, the steep northern Berkshire hills rise abruptly. Vibrating with clattering machinery, the Sprague Electric mills until 1984 dominated North Adams physically and economically; they epitomized 19th-century industrial power and optimism. Today, obsolete behemoths, they bear witness to the area's economic decline. But Thomas Krens wants to see them resurrected as the world's largest museum of contemporary art. And more.

The Massachusetts Museum of Contemporary Art, or MASS MoCA, as it is commonly known, tentatively scheduled to open in the fall of 1992, will be the centerpiece of a vast economic-redevelopment project. This will be the museum's entrance, he says; this building will be the hotel; that one will become offices; over there, retail stores and restaurants; he turns and indicates where the condominiums will go. His businessman's polished shoes and pin-striped trousers are topped by a funky scuffed leather jacket and a Miami Dolphins cap. He ignores the freezing rain and the dusting of snow on the hills and talks numbers: the factories contain 720,000 square feet of space and occupy 20 acres of land. The earliest were built in 1865, the newest 75 years later.

At 6 feet 5 inches tall, Krens towers over his visitors, Count Giuseppe Panza di Biumo, an Italian industrialist and art collector, and his wife, Giovanna.

In their 60's, compact and dapper, they stand together under their umbrella like elegantly tailored salt and pepper shakers. They murmur to each other in Italian, and nod affectionately at Krens - they believe in him. They, and their art, are part of his vision.

Krens takes his visitors inside, through one of the original aerial walkways linking the factories, a covered bridge with corrugated metal walls. He pries open a stuck fire door. ''This room is 340 feet long and 60 feet wide,'' he continues. ''By itself, that is more exhibition space than the Guggenheim, and there are three stories in this building.'' He smiles again, a quick Bugs Bunny grin, not to convey warmth, but to make a point: this project is his size.

The group crosses another walkway, sidesteps a pile of iron shavings, climbs three flights of stairs. Krens takes the steps two at a time and shoves open another fire door. Ranks of wooden columns divide a huge room into bays. ''We can replace the columns with steel beams,'' he says. This structure is perhaps the most eccentric in the complex. Built on the delta where two branches of the Hoosac River join, it is boat-shaped, narrow at the prow, broad at the stern.

Count Panza and his wife love this building. During the 1960's and 70's they bought American minimalist art: Donald Judd's austere rows of metal boxes, Dan Flavin's neon installations, which wash rooms with mystical color. For years, American and European museums have been courting the couple; none of the relationships was consummated, though in 1984 they sold 80 paintings to the Los Angeles Museum of Contemporary Art, for $11 million.

''We have been drawing plans for these rooms,'' says Panza. ''And changing them,'' his wife says, smiling. ''The collection will go to the Guggenheim Museum,'' Panza says. ''We have known many museum directors, and Tom is the only one who understands an entire program.'' The Guggenheim, Panza explains, ''is an established institution.'' But the Panzas expect that the collection will end up in North Adams.

WITHOUT THE GUGGENHEIM, THE NORTH Adams museum is nowhere. To succeed as an artistic institution it depends on its connection to the Guggenheim. ''I'm not sure which came first for Tom, MASS MoCA or the Guggenheim'' says Stephen D. Paine, a Boston collector and a member of the Williams College Museum of Art visiting committee. ''But they matured at the same time.'' By late 1985, Krens, then the director of the Williams College museum, had focused his interest and ambition on contemporary art. He knew that nearby North Adams, with its many abandoned factories, offered in abundance the space he needed to show it. He approached Mayor John Barrett 3d with an idea for a museum as an economic-redevelopment project; Barrett suggested Krens consider using the Sprague complex. Around the same time, Krens began negotiating with the Guggenheim to become its director.
