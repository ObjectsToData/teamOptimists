The Bicentennial: What to Do Abroad . . .

By DAPHNE ANGLES
Published: March 19, 1989

Paris

Paris is celebrating the bicentennial of the French Revolution with an array of exhibits, shows and parades.

The major events:

June 17. At the Eiffel Tower, built exactly 100 years ago for the centennial of the revolution, music, plays, sound and light shows and other performances will take place in, around and under the monument, afternoon and evening.

June 25. In the afternoon, a parade on the theme of the revolution and equality will wend along the left bank of the Seine and end on the Champs de Mars. The parade will be made up of 2,500 direct and indirect descendants of famous figures of the revolution, that the Paris town hall tracked down, some as far away as Peru. Twelve choirs and orchestras from 12 European countries will participate.

July 8. To celebrate the common fight of France and America for independence and democracy, several events will take place around Paris: a morning inauguration of the renovated Square des Etats-Unis, in the 16th arondissement just behind Musee du Costume, afternoon concerts in the gardens of the Palais de Chaillot by United States university bands and an evening concert from a houseboat anchored off the Quai de l'Hotel de Ville by the American Waterways Wind Orchestra.

A parade of 51 orchestras (mostly American university bands) from 46 states will go from the Hotel de Ville to the Palais de Chaillot (Trocadero) that afternoon.

July 13, 14 and 15. These dates will be the height of the celebrations. On the evening of the 13th, the Opera de la Bastille will be officially inaugurated by the President of the Republic, and a program will be shown to officials and guests. Popular celebrations will start when, at 10 P.M., the orchestras come out onto the Place de la Bastille, in the shadow of the newly restored Colonne de Juillet, and entertain the crowds for the traditional eve-of-Bastille-Day balls. The next morning, a national holiday, the traditional army parade will march from the Place de l'Etoile to the Place de la Concorde. That evening the trendy designer Jean-Paul Goude will stage an immense parade from Porte Maillot to the Place de la Concorde, featuring 9,000 performers, drum players, flag-bearers and costumed artists.

Aug. 26. For the 200th birthday of the declaration of the Rights of Man, the tight-rope walker Philippe Petit will cross the Seine on a rope strung from the Trocadero esplanade to the second floor of the Eiffel Tower. The event will be followed by a rock and jazz concert Other Paris Events

From early May to October, the Tuileries Gardens will become a sort of permanent fair of the revolution, for adults and children, with plays and music performances, open-air exhibits and playgrounds.

On a smaller scale, a film festival on the theme of the revolution will be presented under a large tent in front of the Paris town hall, Place de l'Hotel de Ville, from June 8 to June 24. The films include Wajda's ''Danton,'' Renoir's ''La Marseillaise'' and, most famous of all, Abel Gance's ''Napoleon,'' accompanied by the Orchestre Colonne. Each film will be shown at 8 P.M. each night, with an additional midnight showing on Friday and Saturday. ''Napoleon'' will be shown on June 21, 22, 23 and 24. Tickets - they are free - can be obtained each day in the various town halls of Paris (Mairies d'arrondissement), for shows the same night. Information: 42.76.67.00.

The Louvre will show, from Oct. 27 to Feb. 12, a large retrospective of the works of David, the first since 1948. Information: 42.60.39.26.

The Grand Palais will be host to Maurice Bejart's specially created ballet, ''1789,'' from May 2 to May 27 (except on the 24th). Mr. Bejart's ballet company, the Ballet de Lausanne, will dance to excerpts from Beethoven's First, Seventh, Eighth and Ninth symphonies. Tickets (telephone 48.78.75.00) are from $17 to $41. Mail reservations may be ordered from Entree Spectacles, BP 27509, 75424 Paris Cedex 09.

Complete information on the entire bicentennial program for Paris and details on each of its elements will be available in five Kiosques Bicentenaire, to open mid-April in Paris at Rond-Point des Champs-Elysees, Place de la Bastille, Eiffel Tower, Place de l'Hotel de Ville, Montparnasse. A list of the events in Paris will be on sale for $1.60. A list of events in France is available at the Mission du Bicentenaire, 7 Avenue Franco-Russe, 75007 Paris; telephone 45.67.17.89.

Documents, books and information on the French Revolution and the celebrations of 1989 can also be found at the Mediatheque de la Revolution, inside Beaubourg, from March to September this year (42.77.12.33).

Another center for information and research is Espace 89, on 31 Rue du Pont-Neuf, open all year (42.21.06.93). Finally, the French Government Tourist Office and Travel Assistance International have introduced a toll-free number (800-821-2828) for schedules of events, weather conditions, exchange rates and other information. In the Provinces

For historical, technical, visual and financial reasons, the most spectacular celebrations of the bicentennial will take place in Paris and Versailles. However, this should not deter visitors from going off into the province (a very French word meaning everything in France that is not Paris). On several key dates celebrations will be held in almost every village, town and city in France. Celebrations will be held all year too in the entire country, although dates and details are not necessarily worked out yet.
