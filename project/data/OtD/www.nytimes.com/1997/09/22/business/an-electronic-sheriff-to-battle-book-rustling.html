An Electronic Sheriff to Battle Book Rustling

By DOREEN CARVAJAL
Published: September 22, 1997

Faced with the vast, open range of the Internet and its inherent risks for rustling, the United States publishing trade association will today announce a global campaign to organize the equivalent of an electronic roundup, a newly developed digital system to brand electronic books and journals that could otherwise wander away unfettered.

The tagging system, which carries the rather awkward title of ''digital object identifier'' or its nickname, ''DOI,'' is just a first step toward taming the frontier so that the brands will eventually enable businesses to organize and track material, charge for purchases and secure copyrighted material with high-tech envelopes or ''wrappers.''

With such protections in place, publishers predict that other industries in music and film could likely participate, ultimately igniting a virtual business boom among companies that have been reluctant to invest in the Internet without adequate copyright safeguards.

''The Internet doesn't really respect boundaries,'' said Patricia Schroeder, the former congresswoman and president of the Association of American Publishers, which backed the system's development. ''What really happened is that publishers needed to find a way to protect themselves. This is like branding cattle.''

In the forefront of the movement to tag text is a collection of international publishers who produce time-sensitive scientific, legal and medical journals, which are more heavily circulated on the Internet than popular trade books. But all publishers face the same fear: that books can run away with the aid of a simple scanning device and a few clicks of a mouse.

''We are extremely interested in this because of the anxiety all of us feel in the digital world,'' said Charles R. Ellis, chairman of John Wiley & Sons, which in October is making all of its journals available on line. Wiley is one of almost a dozen publishers and trade associations that have begun to test the system, registering more than 250,000 tags.

The DOI brands, with their ability to lay the groundwork for a variety of Internet functions, make the Dewey Decimal system of the country's libraries seem like a creaky antique.

Numbers can be assigned to text and images, in whole or in part. An entire encyclopedia could carry one number, but so could a chart in a medical journal, and so could a single photograph. The numbers can range up to 128 characters; individual prefixes would be assigned for each company. And the tags function like Social Security numbers, following the material even if a company sells out to another corporation.

Most users will probably not even be aware of the brands, which will route their requests for material in a split second through a computer directory operated by the Corporation for National Research Initiatives, a Virginia-based nonprofit organization that developed the system.

The publishing trade association is already preparing to use the DOI system as part of more elaborate strategies for on-line purchases and the creation of encrypted keys or hidden marks to protect digital material from unauthorized copying. The technology is still evolving, but in the future those security measures could take many forms. For example, users may be able to read material on a computer screen, but will not be able to print it or download it to a floppy disk.

It is this uncertain future that is already raising concerns in the library community, which is wary of the fees that could charged for material circulated on the Internet.

''We believe it is extremely important that the technical ability to encrypt material and make it available only on a pay-per-view basis should not become a dominant public policy model,'' said Adam Eisgrau, the legislative counsel for the American Library Association, which claims 57,000 members.

The branding system will make its international debut at the Frankfurt Book Fair in mid-October when the American publishers will hold a forum to explain the system. The fees for participation have not been set. A.A.P. officials estimate that it would cost several hundred dollars for a publisher to obtain a company prefix, but then would cost only pennies more for each number.

Trade publishers like Simon & Schuster are already ready and willing to participate once open registration begins after the Frankfurt fair. Other industry associations are also pondering whether the system could be applied to their products.

If the DOI system gains international support, the publishing associations will likely create a nonprofit foundation to manage it from offices in Geneva and Washington, according to Mr. Ellis, the chairman of Wiley and a member of the International Publishers Association committee that is studying the issue.

The project already has supporters abroad, among them Robert Kiernan, chairman of Routledge, a London-based company with offices in New York that publishes journals in the humanities and social sciences.

''It's really laying down the path to the future,'' said Mr. Kiernan, who added, ''I think the acceptance is nearly universal. The levels of enthusiasm range from ho-hum to brilliant. What we have to do is present its availability to the marketplace. I don't think it will be a revolution, but it will be faster than evolution.''
