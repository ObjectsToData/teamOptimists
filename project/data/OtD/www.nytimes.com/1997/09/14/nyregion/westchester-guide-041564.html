WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: September 14, 1997

CELEBRATING BOOKS

IN a triple celebration of 50 years of the Great Books Foundation, 20 years of Great Books discussion groups in Westchester County and 5 years in which the Westchester Great Books Council has been in operation, a gala dinner, open to the public, is scheduled tomorrow at 6:30 P.M. at Maison Lafitte in Briarcliff Manor.

Dr. Joseph Hankin, president of Westchester Community College, will be the first recipient of the Westchester Great Books Council's Guardian of Civilization Award, to be presented each year to an individual who has helped further interest in literature and learning.

The keynote speaker will be Christopher Lehmann-Haupt, daily book reviewer for The New York Times, who will talk about the decline of books and their future.

The Great Books Foundation, founded in 1947 in Chicago, has inspired the formation of dozens of reading and book-discussion groups around the country, in which more than 20,000 people meet regularly to explore the texts of classics, modern fiction and short stories. In addition, more than a million children from all 50 states take part in Junior Great Books programs, which promote interest in literature and a spirit of intellectual inquiry.

Writers are encouraged to attend the dinner and bring their books, taking part in discussions with other readers and writers. Tickets are $40, $30 for students under 19. The number for reservations is 762-5106.

ON STATE GOVERNANCE

An informational session on a New York State Constitutional Convention is to be held by the League of Women Voters of Briarcliff, Ossining, Croton-on-Hudson and Cortlandt on Thursday at 7:30 P.M. in the Quality of Life Building of Sing Sing Prison in Ossining.

The meeting is aimed at helping voters deal with the outcome of such a convention and what it could mean if the state changes the way it operates. Voters will be asked to vote for or against holding the convention on Nov. 7.

Jeanne Richman, a Westchester league vice president, will be the principal speaker, supplying information about changes that can be expected if the ballot question is passed. For directions or more information, call 941-6984.

DRAWINGS ONLY

Drawing -- that most basic of visual art forms -- is the sole medium exhibited in the new show opening on Thursday at the Pelham Art Center. Fifteen artists have contributed drawings, some of which hew to traditional representational works and others that explore innovative uses of drawing materials and abstracted subject matter.

Janet Culbertson portrays the creatures of nature in recognizable images but her mixed media drawing of a Galapagos tortoise, for example, is startling for its scale, texture and connection with the mythic aspect of the animal.

An experimental technique by Meri Bourgard reworks interior spaces in relation to the people who inhabit them, resulting in a continuum of past, present and future, while Violet Baxter's scenes of Union Square and the Greenmarket in Manhattan are comfortingly familiar.

The gallery, at 155 Fifth Avenue in Pelham, is open Tuesdays through Fridays from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. and Saturdays from 10 A.M. to 4 P.M. Call 738-2525 for more information.

CRAFTS AT EMELIN

The Emelin Theater in Mamaroneck is observing its quarter-century anniversary this year and the age of maturity for its 21-year-old arts and crafts festival, going on today from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M.

More than 75 exhibitors will display jewelry, silver, clocks, lamps, folk art, leather goods, dolls and other handiworks suitable for getting a head start on holiday shopping. Entertainment, pony rides and an array of international foods are also available all day, and the only charge in addition to a $3 admission price is an extra $2 for children's puppet shows at 11:30 A.M. and 2 P.M.

This annual event helps support the year-round programs that have made the Emelin a one-of-a-kind institution in Westchester, providing plays, dance, cabaret, music of all kinds and lectures. Among notables who have appeared there in recent months are Isaac Stern, the violinist, Diane Shuur, pianist, Robert F. Kennedy Jr., lecturer, and the Paperbag Players, favorites of audiences of all ages.

The Emelin is on Library Lane off Route 1. The rain date is Sept. 28. Call 381-3375 for more information.

PET FAIR

An Open House and Pet Fair where no pets are allowed is taking place today from 1 to 3 P.M. at the South Putnam Animal Hospital on Baldwin Place Road in Mahopac. Visitors will be given tours of the newly completed hospital and an opportunity to talk to doctors and staff.

Questions about pet training, behavior, health and grooming services will be discussed, and information about rabies and other diseases and disorders will be available. Demonstrations will be given on handling many kinds of creatures from farm animals, reptiles and ferrets to conventional house pets.

Free pony rides will be available for children, and there will be refreshments as well as free souvenirs for pets left at home.

Call 628-1834 for more information.

REVIEWING ZIONISM

''The Future of Zionism: A Centenary View'' is the title of a talk to be given by Shlomo Avineri on Thursday at 8 P.M. in the Humanities Theater of Purchase College. This is the 100th year since the the first Zionist Congress was convened in Basel, Switzerland, in 1897, organized by Theodor Herzl.

A professor of political science at the Hebrew University in Jerusalem, author of books and articles on political philosophy that have been translated into many languages, Mr. Avineri is considered an expert on the theories of Hegel and Marx and on modern Zionism as one of the revolutionary ideas of this century.

The college is at 735 Anderson Hill Road, and admission will be $5. Call 251-6500 or 251-6516 for more information.

RUSSIAN JAZZ AND ART

Arkady Figlin, a Russian jazz pianist, will perform next Saturday at 8:15 P.M. during an Evening of Jazz and Art at the Jewish Community Center, 371 South Broadway in Tarrytown.

Mr. Figlin has been in the United States since 1991, appearing in supper clubs in New York, Memphis, New Orleans, and Washington. He has won top honors at jazz festivals in France and Belgium and appeared as piano soloist in a performance of Gershwin's ''Rhapsody in Blue'' with the Moscow Philharmonic.

The art works were provided by seven Russian artists who have exhibited in galleries and museums in Europe and the United States, including shows at Lincoln Center and the Metropolitan Museum of Art. The works, in a variety of media, are priced from $250 to $1,200.

Tickets are available at $10, $15 and $25 by calling 366-7898. ELEANOR CHARLES
