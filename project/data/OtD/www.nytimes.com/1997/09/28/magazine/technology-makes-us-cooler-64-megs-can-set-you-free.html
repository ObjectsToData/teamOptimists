Technology Makes Us Cooler; 64 Megs Can Set You Free

By Gerald Marzorati
Published: September 28, 1997

More than 25 years have passed since a guy in my freshman English class asked me if I wanted to help blow up a computer center. He approached me after class one fall afternoon and gave me a soul shake for something I'd said about a Vonnegut novel we'd all been discussing -- something about alienation, or maybe it was dehumanization. As we cut across the campus together, a couple of late-to-the-action student-radical types in our jeans and work boots and ratty Shetlands, he got into a long rap about Marshall McLuhan's growing technological pessimism, how McLuhan was saying that ''the electric situation of instant information'' was erasing people's individuality, leaving them empty and scared. Television was McLuhan's chief culprit, but my classmate was looking ahead and seeing nothing but the computer.

He waited until we reached his dorm to mention the bombing plot, then invited me up to his room, where he sketched it out, sort of. A high-school friend of his had an older brother who knew somebody connected to the Weather Underground. The computer center was nearby at the University of Pennsylvania. There was work being done there on the Bomb, or mind control, or something. There was going to be a meeting in Philadelphia soon, though where and when, exactly ... anyway, was I up for it?

''Wow,'' I recall saying with just the right nuance, getting across (a) that I was hip to the whole technological menace thing and (b) ''No way.'' He then did what everybody I knew did in those days to get beyond an awkward social moment. He put on a record. And picturing him now, absently tucking his hair behind his ears as he hunched over his stereo, I see that I had a glimpse of a coming revolution -- just not the one that he was plotting at the time.

His turntable was state of the art, manufactured by a boutique Cambridge, Mass., outfit called AR, and it rested alongside a high-end, 105-watts-per-channel McIntosh amplifier. The speakers on either side of this setup were the size of night stands, and it turned out that my host had made them himself, determining the right combination and placement of woofers and tweeters only after an extensive consultation of the hi-fi journals. The would-be anti-tech terrorist was an audio freak!

An extreme case, maybe, but the whole 60's counterculture -- the student radicals, the hippies, the human-potential expanders and all their fellow travelers, younger and older -- had its wires crossed about technology. Despite all the dark talk about the new electronic machines dispatching humankind to a drab and fettered future, 60's nonconformists tended to get most loud and hedonistic and free when tethered to a wall socket.

This was a generation that broke with the older lefties and bohos by cheering in '65 when Dylan went electric. This was a generation that couldn't have forged its sense of itself and then announced it to everybody else without stereos and dashboard-mounted eight-tracks, P.A. systems and Woodstock-strength portable generators, wah-wah pedals and FM bands and whole-world-is-watching evening-news broadcasts. This was a generation for whom the latest technology played as large a role as the latest esthetic development or political idea in determining what was new, different, radical -- what was cool.

But the counterculture remained in deep denial about this. Technology wasn't very organic and it cost a fair amount of money and let's not get into that, O.K.? What made the computer such a terrific target was that, unlike stereos and other consumer electronics, it wasn't anything you considered using or imagined you might one day buy -- at least if you happened to be nonconforming, say, on a leafy Main Line campus.

In fact, at the very moment I was being let in on the plan to destroy Penn's computer center (never happened, of course), a company in California called Intel was about to produce the first microprocessor. Soon, in Northern California, a far-reaching revolution would come at the end of hundreds of soldering guns, a lot of them wielded by counterculture types who saw that the point was to liberate the mainframe computer, not blow it up. This revolution eventually enlisted a lot of those 60's youths who took their stereos very seriously. What seems so obvious now should have back then: the future belonged to those who thought that really cool equipment could set you free.

The argument against the computer in the 60's was essentially poetic. The concern was for the soul -- for how your habits of heart and mind might be frazzled by the new machines, and especially for how personal liberty might be sinisterly undermined by the hulking mainframes that were more and more becoming an aspect of everyday life. ''Do Not Fold, Spindle or Mutilate'' was the message on the keypunch cards college students scrambled to secure to get into classes they wanted, and the thing to say as we raced around during registration was, Yeah, what about us?

And everybody saw -- and saw the future in -- ''Alphaville,'' the 1965 Jean-Luc Godard movie that quickly became a campus filmfest and art-house staple. Godard insisted that movies could be a form of cultural criticism, and ''Alphaville'' is a romantic plaint about the coming high-tech dystopia, cleverly disguised as a sendup of Hollywood spy thrillers and cheesy sci-fi. In futuristic Alphaville, people are efficient but affectless, a result somehow of computer programming (that term!) that has robbed them of a language with which to express their emotions. The State runs the computers and oversees, too, an elaborate call-girl ring, what with there being no passion or anything. The film ends movingly -- or maybe, as with so much of the 60's, you had to be there -- with its hero, a tough-guy outsider named Lemme Caution (Eddie Constantine), speeding away from Alphaville (Paris in the middle of the night) and trying to teach one of its soul-numbed citizens (Anna Karina) the word ''love.''
