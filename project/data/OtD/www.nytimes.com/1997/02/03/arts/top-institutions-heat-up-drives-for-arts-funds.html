Top Institutions Heat Up Drives For Arts Funds

By JUDITH MILLER
Published: February 3, 1997

Though it has not been officially announced, the New York Public Library is embarking on a $430 million capital campaign to refurbish its structures, expand its programs and create a new $12 million humanities center, the largest fund-raising drive in the library's 102-year history.

The library is not alone. Spurred by a soaring stock market, huge increases in the number of wealthy patrons, tax incentives and the need to keep pace with new technology, several of New York City's leading cultural institutions are also planning to raise, or have already begun raising, tens and hundreds of millions of dollars. Campaigns for capital funds and endowments by institututions including the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Museum of Modern Art and Carnegie Hall are without precedent in scale or ambition, say arts experts, fund-raisers and donors.

At the same time, the campaigns' impact on smaller arts groups in New York City is being fiercely debated within the arts world. Spokesmen for some groups said they feared that the campaigns, coming at a time of reduced public support for the arts, might further diminish money available for smaller organizations.

''There is a just a finite amount of money out there,'' said Norma P. Munn, who heads the New York City Arts Coalition, which represents small as well as larger institutions.

But a majority of experts said the campaigns would invigorate the city's aging cultural infrastructure and produce a resurgence of the arts in New York unparalleled in recent history, a boom that would benefit smaller and large institutions alike.

''We've entered a period of institutional excitement comparable only to that which occurred after the Civil War until World War I when several of the city's great civic and cultural institutions were built,'' said Frederick A. O. Schwarz Jr., the city's former Corporation Counsel. ''We are on the verge of another huge leap in the city's cultural life, a new renaissance.''

Michael I. Sovern, president emeritus of Columbia University and president of the Shubert Foundation, agreed. Larger institutions, he said, tend to compete for funds against one another, not against small ones. ''The effect of these campaigns,'' he said, ''is to increase the size of the philanthropic pie.''

The competition itself, said Glenn D. Lowry, the director of the Museum of Modern Art, spurs ever greater giving. ''It seems counter-intuitive,'' said Mr. Lowry, whose museum is planning to announce a multimillion-dollar capital and endowment campaign later this year. ''But that is the psychology of philanthropy, which is different from the psychology of commerce.''

While universities and hospitals are accustomed to waging virtually permanent capital campaigns, said Elizabeth McCormack, a senior board member of two major foundations and an adviser to the Rockefeller family, she could not remember a time when so many of the city's cultural institutions had initiated major capital drives. ''The danger is that they will all go to the same people for funds,'' she said. But so far, she noted, astonishing sums were being given, from wealth controlled not just by a few families but in many hands.

Charitable giving has steadily risen along with the number of the city's new millionaires, who have tripled in number in the past decade, according to Treasury Department figures. The Metropolitan Museum of Art, for example, which has already raised $240 million of its three-year-old $300 million campaign, has received gifts of $1 million or more from 60 donors.

Giving to the New York Public Library, which is supported by both public and private funds, reflects the breadth and depth of support for an institution that includes 87 libraries spread over 130 miles, a collection of 54 million items and 14 million reader-visits a year, including an average of 40,000 city children who come to the branch libraries a day. In addition to the 29 donors who have given the library $1 million or more and the trustees, who have pledged $100 million, some 42,000 ''Friends'' gave between $1 and $1,250 last year, of whom 20,000 donated between $40 and $125; some 15,000 supporters gave $100 or less. Contributions in all categories, but especially from the ''Friends,'' have risen steadily.

Paul LeClerc, the president of the library, said his institution had no choice but to begin its ambitious ''Second Century Campaign.'' ''If we fail to expand and refurbish,'' he said, ''we risk betraying Ben Franklin's commitment at the founding of the American library movement to bring the book, and now the database, to all people, free of charge.''

The city's cultural needs are immense. The library alone plans to raise $145 million for capital expenditures at the main library on 42d Street and its branches, $100 million for technological improvements and $185 million to increase its endowment and operating expenses. Computerizing and digitizing essential research documents, that is, scanning images or texts and converting them into digital formats that can be called up and sent electronically, is hugely expensive, Mr. LeClerc said.
