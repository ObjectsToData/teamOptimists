A Man of Opinion And of History

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: May 17, 1997

From across the political spectrum they came yesterday to pay tribute to a scholar who still wears his liberalism like a bright badge of honor.

Progressives, reformers, neoconservatives, Clinton Democrats and others who have long since given up calling themselves liberals were paying tribute to Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., professor emeritus, Pulitzer Prize-winning historian and Presidential adviser, in the first of a series of events to honor the scholar in the months leading to his 80th birthday.

With his wife, Alexandra, beside him and an audience of about 150 in attendance, Professor Schlesinger sat in a front seat, beamed and shook hands through a day of academic speeches and commentary at the Harold M. Proshansky Auditorium of the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, on 42d Street between Fifth and Sixth Avenues.

William E. Leuchtenburg, the University of North Carolina historian and former president of the Association of American Historians, praised Professor Schlesinger as an example of Emerson's belief that ''there can be no scholar without the heroic mind.''

These days, his heroic mind is concerned with time.

''When you're on the brink of 80, time becomes the most precious of commodities,'' Professor Schlesinger said recently.

So, retired in name only since 1995, he is busily pursuing a life of work, parties and honors.

The work includes the completion of ''The Age of Roosevelt,'' his magnum opus on the life of Franklin Delano Roosevelt; an updated edition of his 19th book, ''The Disuniting of America,'' which was a 1991 assault on radical multiculturalism, and the conclusion of his memoirs, now at the 300-page mark and growing at a rate of 2,500 words on the good days. ''It's great fun, very enjoyable,'' he says.

The parties and honors include events like yesterday's celebration of a life and work whose characteristic scholarship has attracted liberals and conservatives alike. Besides yesterday's academic conference honoring Professor Schlesinger at the university where he was the Albert Schweitzer Professor of Humanities for nearly 30 years before his retirement, other tributes are planned in the months leading to his birthday on Oct. 15.

Grandest among the parties scheduled throughout the summer and into the fall is the one being arranged by William J. vanden Heuvel, former United States Ambassador to the United Nations. It will be held on the professor's birthday at the Century Club, and no one will be surprised if President and Mrs. Clinton drop by.

At 79, Professor Schlesinger radiates energy, speaking in complete, often eloquent sentences. His impertinent dotted bow tie retains its jiblike trim, and his sharp-edged wit is drawn, cocked and ready to drill a hole in any blaspheming conservative. His daily regimen is snappy: a walk to and from his office from his apartment on East 51st Street, broken by a 10 1/2-hour day of work on his memoirs.

Professor Schlesinger's fellow liberals adore him these says not merely because he has reached a venerable age but partly out of guilt; he, unlike many, still preaches the Old Testament of the New Deal. ''I'm an unrepentant and unreconstructed liberal and New Dealer,'' he said in an interview, peering over a cluttered desk. ''That means I favor the use of government to improve opportunities and enlarge freedoms for ordinary people.''

At yesterday's academic conference, ''Liberalism at the Crossroads,'' Mr. vanden Heuvel described Professor Schlesinger as ''the most faithful exponent of Rooseveltian liberalism, and perhaps the only liberal in the room.''

Also taking part in the conference was Gertrude Himmelfarb, a former colleague at the C.U.N.Y. Graduate School, who is a conservative. She explained that while she intended to let others sing the praises of Professor Schlesinger's ''devotion to the welfare state,'' she agreed to take part in the gathering to salute his early anti-Communism and his present skepticism about multiculturalism.

In ''The Disuniting of America,'' Professor Schlesinger argues against the extremes of multiculturalism, which he defines as the sense that America is ''composed of groups more or less ineradicable in their ethnic characters.''

Summing up yesterday's conference, Professor Schlesinger drew laughter and applause when he expressed his long-run optimism by saying, ''We can count on sex and love to defeat those who would disunite America.''

Asked in the interview whether he was uncomfortable about opposing multiculturalism, he said no. ''That's like 40 or 50 years ago when I was a vigorous anti-Communist and was told I shouldn't be an anti-Communist because that put me in company with Joe McCarthy,'' the professor said. ''I said a good liberal had to be against both Joes -- Stalin and McCarthy. In the same way, while I'm against the excesses of multiculturalism, I'm also certainly against the monoculturalism of the right.''

Nor does he believe that his strong political convictions present any conflict with his professional judgments as a historian.
