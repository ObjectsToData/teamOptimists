House Panel, 31-7, Advances the Balanced-Budget Deal

By JERRY GRAY
Published: May 17, 1997

Using the tax-cutting and spending deal struck by President Clinton and Republican leaders as a blueprint, the House Budget Committee took the first legislative step today toward enactment of a balanced-budget bill.

''We've been struggling with this for a long time,'' Representative John R. Kasich, the Ohio Democrat who heads the Budget Committee, said as it voted 31 to 7 to approve a resolution that will guide future action on tax cuts and spending.

The Senate Budget Committee is to vote on its resolution on Monday, and Republican leaders said they expected both the House and the Senate would give final approval by the end of next week.

After two weeks of haggling, Republican leaders and the White House announced on Thursday night that they had agreed to a written deal on a five-year budget plan. The final deal, whose broad outline follows the agreement that the political leaders announced earlier this month, includes $115 billion in savings to the Medicare program and an $85 billion package of tax cuts. Among them are a $500-a-child tax credit, broad-based and permanent reductions in the capital gains tax, ''significant'' reductions in the inheritance tax and an expansion of Individual Retirement Accounts.

Thursday night's deal locked in place what earlier had been only spoken agreements. In the end, the Republicans won guarantees that they would have the controlling say in how the tax cuts would be distributed, the specifics of which will be left to the Ways and Means Committee. And the White House won assurances that it would get roughly $35 billion in tax credits for post-secondary education, as well as protection against cuts in education and social programs it considers most important.

''This agreement includes the most significant increase in education funding in 30 years,'' Mr. Clinton said. ''Even more important, it provides the largest single increase in higher education since the G.I. bill in 1945, more than 50 years ago.''

But even as some Republicans and Democrats were toasting one another today in a spirit of bipartisanship, Speaker Newt Gingrich's office was boasting in a private memorandum that Republicans had prevailed over the Administration and Democrats in many important areas.

The memorandum said Republican negotiators had been able to thwart White House efforts to increase welfare spending for legal immigrants who entered the country after August, when the new welfare law took effect. And the memo said Republicans had won language in the final deal that provisions reducing capital gains and estate taxes could include indexing for inflation or losses on sales of residences.

''The agreement substantially scales back the President's insatiable appetite for more Government spending programs,'' the memorandum continued, listing 40 programs that it said the Administration had failed to protect.

Those programs include several of Mr. Clinton's favorites, including Americorps, the volunteer service organization, and Goals 2000, the President's education improvement program, as well as some perennial Republican targets like the Legal Services Corporation, the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities.

The agreement calls for $1.69 trillion in spending for fiscal year 1998, which begins on Oct. 1, with a deficit of $90.4 billion. Although that will probably represent an increase from this year's anticipated deficit of $75 billion, the negotiators said deficits would decline in each of the succeeding years, ending with a surplus of $1.3 billion by 2002.

The agreement was signed off by President Clinton, Speaker Gingrich, Senator Trent Lott of Mississippi, the majority leader, and Senator Tom Daschle of South Dakota, the minority leader.

Some Democrats in the House have raised objections in the past over cuts in capital gains taxes, criticizing the idea as a tax break for the wealthy.

Drawing up a budget resolution is the first step in the legislative process toward a budget. The resolution, which does not require the President's signature, sets spending limits and certain other conditions for the committees that will actually write the legislation that will become law.

But before that happens, the full House and Senate must approve their respective resolutions, reconcile any differences and then vote again on identical bills.

Mr. Lott said on Thursday night that he expected that entire process to be completed by the time Congress breaks for the Memorial Day recess starting on May 23.

Most of the budget and its details will still be shaped by the Republican-controlled tax-writing and spending committees. But the deal between the Clinton Administration and the Republican leaders is meant to insure that each side's top priorities will be protected.

Republicans made the tax cuts a priority item. But the only specifics they laid out in the budget deal was that the per-child tax credit should be $500. It will be left largely to the tax-writing House Ways and Means Committee, led by Representative Bill Archer of Texas, to decide the size of the reductions in capital gains taxes and inheritance taxes and who will receive the benefits.

The White House and Congressional Democrats argued for and won a much more expansive list of priority items, the largest of which was the $35 billion the President wanted for post-secondary education programs, including a tuition tax credit.

In addition, the Administration was able to protect about a dozen domestic spending programs, some of which have been frequent targets of Republicans. All of the protected programs are to receive the spending that Mr. Clinton proposed in his 1998 budget. Among those are Head Start, Job Corps, bilingual and immigrant education and Pell grants for college students, which are to increase by $300, to a maximum award of $3,000.
