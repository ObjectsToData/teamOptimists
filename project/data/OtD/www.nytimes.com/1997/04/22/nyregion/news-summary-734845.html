NEWS SUMMARY


Published: April 22, 1997

INTERNATIONAL A3-8

Clinton Approves Ban On Burmese Investment

President Clinton has approved a ban on new American investment in Myanmar, formerly known as Burma, because of human rights abuses there, a senior Administration official said. A1

Chirac Calls Elections in France

President Jacques Chirac of France, left, dissolved the national legislature and called an election for May 25, a year early. Mr. Chirac appealed for support for the agenda that he and Prime Minister Alain Juppe have set for a common European currency, contending that Europe can remain a power only if its currency remains strong. Mr. Chirac's electoral gamble caught the left-leaning opposition parties off guard. A4

Continuity the Byword in India

Prime Minister I. K. Gujral took office at the head of India's fourth Government in 11 months, vowing to continue with free-market reforms that have begun to revitalize India's economy and to pursue improved relations with Pakistan. Mr. Gujral, who heads a Cabinet virtually identical to that of his predecessor, H. D. Deve Gowda, stressed continuity. But many Indian commentators predicted that Mr. Gujral would have a brief tenure. A4

Zairians Attack Refugees

Avenging what they said was the killing of at least six of their compatriots by Rwandan refugees, Zairian villagers near Kisangani attacked refugees and aid workers. The villagers went on to attack a train loaded with food and then looted an aid program's warehouse. A5

Questions Over Irish Donations

The specter of political influence-peddling arose in Ireland when Ben Dunne, a businessman, told a Government tribunal that he had given nearly $2 million to former Prime Minister Charles J. Haughey. Mr. Haughey denied that he had received any money from Mr. Dunne, the part-owner of Dunnes, a department store. A6

New Uniforms for Hong Kong

A vanguard of 40 officers and soldiers of the Chinese Army arrived in Hong Kong in advance of the switch to Chinese rule. Uniformed but unarmed, the troops gingerly navigated their convoy through the city's formidable traffic. The soldiers crossed into Hong Kong moments after a Royal Navy ship fired a 21-gun salute in Victoria Harbor to honor Queen Elizabeth's birthday; the British troops were later ordered to salute their eventual replacements. A8

Iraq Defying Flight Ban

Iraq said it would defy a United Nations prohibition on international flights and would send helicopters to pick up pilgrims returning from Saudi Arabia. The United States urged Iraq to reconsider, but said it would not shoot down civilian helicopters. The U.N. did not rebuke Iraq for an earlier violation of the no-flight zone, when an Iraqi Airways jet flew pilgrims to Saudi Arabia. A4

NATIONAL A10-16

Court Bars Some Suits Over Child Support

The Supreme Court ruled that mothers seeking support from their children's absent fathers were not entitled to sue states for failing to do an effective job of collecting child support. But in an opinion by Justice Sandra Day O'Connor, the Court kept open the possibility of lawsuits against states for ignoring specific Federal requirements, like time limits for passing on to families the money collected from absent fathers, as opposed to complying with Federal law in general. A15

Use of Electric Chair Allowed

A judge paved the way for Florida to resume its method of execution, ruling that the electric chair does not amount to cruel and unusual punishment. Lawyers for a man who has been sentenced to death had cited a fiery execution last month in challenging electrocution as unconstitutional. A15

Barry Shifts on Death Penalty

Marion S. Barry, the Mayor of Washington, long an opponent of the death penalty, said he now favored executing killers of police officers. Mr. Barry said he would submit a bill to the City Council calling for the execution of defendants found guilty of the first-degree murder of on-duty law-enforcement officers. A16

Flooding Red River Crests

The Red River crested at about 54 feet in Grand Forks, N.D., nearly twice the flood stage, but it will be at least three weeks before residents can return. And when they do, many will face crushing losses; as much as 90 percent of the city has been inundated. Meanwhile, President Clinton announced plans to visit the area. A1

Jury Selection Progresses

The questioning of potential jurors in the Oklahoma City bombing trial ended. Prosecutors and lawyers for the defendant, Timothy J. McVeigh, will now begin winnowing down the 99 panelists until jurors and alternates are seated. A11

Aircraft Search Suspended

The military suspended recovery operations of aircraft wreckage in the mountains of Colorado, confident that a missing warplane had been found and that the pilot had not survived. The wreckage has been spotted at an altitude of more than 12,000 feet, up a narrow trough, but snow has been forecast, and it could be days before the Air Force can thoroughly explore the site. A10

Drill Sergeant's Defense Begins

Lawyers for an Army drill sergeant charged with rape and sexual harassment set out to demonstrate that his accusers are habitual liars who openly yearned to have intimate relations with him. In a case that has come to symbolize the Army's deep problems in integrating the sexes, defense lawyers for Staff Sgt. Delmar Simpson began their defense by calling witnesses who depicted him as a fine and caring leader. A12
