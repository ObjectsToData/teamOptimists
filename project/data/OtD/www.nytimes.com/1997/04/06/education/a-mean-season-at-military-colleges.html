A Mean Season at Military Colleges

By ERIC SCHMITT
Published: April 6, 1997

For Matt Luzzatto, enduring four years of regimented life at the United States Military Academy has its rewards: an Army officer's commission, a coveted West Point diploma and access to one of the country's most influential alumni networks. ''It's not a fun place to go through,'' said Mr. Luzzatto, a 22-year-old senior from Milton, Vt. ''But it's a great place to come from.''

April Henson was impressed with the pre-med program at North Georgia College and State University in Dahlonega. But finding an available slot meant enrolling through the university's military cadet program, a prospect she initially balked at. Now, says the 19-year-old sophomore from Stone Mountain, Ga., she likes the cadet program's mix of discipline and civilian life. ''I have a lot of options,'' said Ms. Henson, dressed in fatigues and shiny black combat boots. ''If I join the Army after this, it'll pay for medical school.''

To David Zirkle and other native Virginians, the 158-year-old Virginia Military Institute in Lexington is known reverently as the Holy City. Growing up in Richmond, Mr. Zirkle said V.M.I. was the only college he wanted to attend. ''It's all almost intangible,'' said Mr. Zirkle, nowa 22-year-old senior at V.M.I. ''I wanted the discipline and the sense of honor.''

Talk to today's military college students, and you hear a panoply of reasons for their becoming full-time cadets. Many crave a structure they find lacking in civilian schools (including the roughly 400 campuses that offer Reserve Officer Training Corps programs). Others want a tough physical challenge, or even a sense of mission. ''I know it sounds corny, but I've always wanted to serve my country -- either in the military or as a civilian,'' said Johnathan Collier, a 23-year-old West Point senior from Steubenville, Ohio.

But despite their appeal, military colleges and universities today are struggling to defend their relevance; in the process, they are coping with more change than in decades.

Coeducation is roiling some of these spit-and-polish institutions. At the same time, the military's peace-keeping missions around the world are requiring the academies to teach a new set of skills. And a string of scandals at the Army, Navy and Air Force academies -- including incidents of cheating, sexual harassment and even rape charges -- is prompting questions about what taxpayers get for their money at the elite, Pentagon-supported institutions.

Behind the parade of crisply pressed uniforms and honor codes, the nation's military colleges are scrambling to confront their crises -- with varying degrees of willingness and success.

Only nine institutions in the country are considered senior military colleges.

Three of them -- West Point, the Naval Academy in Annapolis, Md., and the Air Force Academy in Colorado Springs, Colo. -- are financed by the Pentagon, which spends between $210,000 and $282,000 to produce one graduate. (The United States Coast Guard Academy and the United States Merchant Marine Academy produce graduates who enforce maritime law and work as seamen aboard American vessels, but both are under the Department of Transportation, not the Pentagon, despite their disciplined approach.)

The Citadel in Charleston, S.C., and V.M.I. are state-supported. Two others -- Texas A&M in College Station and Virginia Tech in Blacksburg -- are large, civilian, state colleges with strong military programs. And the remaining two are private schools: North Georgia College and State University is a small state civilian college with a separate corps of cadets. And Norwich University in Northfield, Vt., is the nation's only private senior military college.

What makes these colleges ''military'' (there is no formal designation, just agreement in the military establishment) is that at all nine, student cadets or midshipmen follow a strict regime. They live in barracks, dress in military-style uniforms and follow military-style lives so regulated that in some cases students must master a prescribed way to fold T-shirts and line up toothbrushes in bathroom cabinets.

Such lives appeal only to a select few. Right now, 19,000 students -- a tiny percentage of the almost 9 million undergraduates at four-year colleges -- attend the nine senior military colleges. But the popularity of these colleges, resurgent during President Reagan's era, has been declining. Applications increased a bit this year at some campuses, but only because of concerted recruiting and advertising drives.

Indeed, in a sign of the times, admissions officers from four of these colleges -- North Georgia, the Citadel, Texas A&M and Norwich -- held a two-day meeting in January at the Norwich campus to discuss ways of combating the public misperceptions of their institutions.

''Parents tend to think the young men and women who attend military colleges are a bunch of Rambos, and that's far from the truth,'' said Frank E. Griffis Jr., Norwich's dean of admissions. ''We're also dealing with a whole generation of parents from the 60's who were anti-war and antimilitary and are now rearing their children that way.''
