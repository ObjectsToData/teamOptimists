THINK TANK; Tales Twice, Indeed Thrice: Rereading Personal Classics


Published: December 6, 1997

''It is tedious to tell again tales already plainly told,'' Odysseus remarks in ''The Odyssey.'' But many book lovers would disagree, so The New York Times asked writers and readers about the last book they read twice. Here are their responses, compiled by Mary B. W. Tabor.

STEVEN PINKER, -- professor of psychology and director of the Center for Cognitive Neuroscience at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and author of ''How the Mind Works'' (W. W. Norton, 1997).

''Homicide,'' by Martin Daly and Margo Wilson (A. de Gruyter, 1988): ''It is a sleeper of a book but a truly brilliant and often sardonic scientific analysis of why people kill each other. It is one of those books where every paragraph has a gem of thinking and prose in it. I read books twice if three conditions are met. One, it has to be by a truly original thinker who is likely to repay a second reading by having insights crammed into paragraphs that one could easily skip over the first time. Second, it has to be a book by a perfectionist, someone who loves writing and who takes great care with every sentence, especially someone who has a subtle, dry wit. And third, it should be connected in some tenuous way to my own work, so that I don't feel guilty about rereading it and I can call it research.''

ALFRED KAZIN, -- critic and professor emeritus of English at the City University Graduate Center.

''The Count of Monte Cristo,'' by Alexandre Dumas: ''I've been looking for years for a book that was a favorite of my boyhood, Dumas's 'The Count of Monte Cristo.' The other day, after years of looking for it, I found the paperback edition. It was so much edited from the version I'd read as a boy, but it was the only edition I could find. So I read it again with great joy, the same joy I had when I was a kid.''

PATRICIA J. WILLIAMS, -- professor of law at Columbia University School of Law and columnist for The Nation.

''Summer,'' by Edith Wharton: ''I don't know why I read it twice, but I guess it's because it is so beautifully and succinctly written and because it captures a social yearning and class consciousness that is part of American Lit that I don't think gets appreciated much.''

LEON BOTSTEIN, -- president of Bard College and music director of the American Symphony.

''Anna Karenina,'' by Leo Tolstoy: ''I read it twice because it had made an impression on me at a much earlier stage of my life and I wanted to see how I would react to it after 20 years of not having read it. In fact, I was so impressed with the book, so taken by its power, by its insight that I was amazed that I had gotten through it when I was 17 or 18. A great book is a book that needs to be read more than once, the same way we look at a picture more than once or the way a musician performs the same work over and over. It's never the same and you're not the same when you come back to it.''

CAMILLE PAGLIA, -- professor of humanities at the University of the Arts in Philadelphia.

''Auntie Mame: An Irreverent Escapade,'' by Patrick Dennis: ''I consider it my Bible. I read it periodically. Other people have dismissed it. But I consider it one of the great books of my life and one of the great books of all time. I have no respect whatsoever for what is called the serious novel of the postwar era. This book, on the other hand, has a kind of magic insight into sex and gender and society in the period since World War II.''

JOHN UPDIKE, -- novelist.

''Redburn,'' by Herman Melville: ''I read it thinking I'd never read it before, and halfway through I discovered there were notes in the back that I must have made in my 20's. So here was a case of rereading a book I thought I'd never read. It didn't seem especially familiar. I quite liked it, but I had apparently read it as many as four decades ago. I also reread 'Moby-Dick' recently, but in that case I did remember that I'd read it before and so that was a purposeful act of deepening my appreciation. The fact is it doesn't take too many years before we pretty much forget almost everything about a book and we could probably reread many of them for profit and pleasure.''

OPRAH WINFREY, -- producer and talk show host, whose ''Oprah's Book Club'' segments have helped boost book sales.

''Beloved,'' by Toni Morrison (Knopf, 1987): ''I carried it around like it was the Bible while I was shooting the film and prior to that I had read it twice. Her writing is such that you can't take it all in the first time. You have to spoon-feed it to yourself. I can read Toni Morrison's work over and over again and find something new every time.''
