CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 23, 1997

ON PESTICIDES

The legal use of and cumulative exposure to pesticides applied to food crops, forests, lawns and gardens, golf courses, highways, swimming pools, wallpaper, shower curtains, rugs, paints and even some shampoos, constitute ''an uncontrolled human experiment,'' according to John Wargo, associate professor of environmental policy and risk analysis at the Yale School of Forestry and Environmental Studies.

Professor Wargo will give a free talk entitled ''The Future of Children's Environmental Health'' on Wednesday at 4 P.M. in the Whitney Humanities Center, 53 Wall Street, on the Yale campus.

The number for more information is 203-432-1345.

ARTISTS IN NEW CANAAN

The paintings, photography, prints and constructions of five artists may be seen today through April 20 at the Silvermine Guild Arts Center in New Canaan. A reception is scheduled today from 2 to 4 P.M.

Visitors may actually play with Arthur Burke's creations -- pulling cords and handles, sliding doors that open and close compartments. Elizabeth Coleman's boxes, by contrast, are abstract and monolithic in shape and color; Alistair Dunn's mystical photographs of the English coastal beaches and isolated structures -- he studied at the Royal Institute of British Architects -- are ominously arresting.

Small mixed-media works by Barbara Wilk and abstract monoprints by Susan Sharp round out the exhibition, open from Tuesday through Saturday, 11 to 5; Sunday from 1 to 5. The gallery is at 1037 Silvermine Road and the number for more information is 203-966-5617. A donation of $2 is requested.

ANTIQUES SHOW

An antiques show featuring some of those unusual items that one hopes to find but rarely does, will be held at the East Ridge Junior High School in Ridgefield today from 10 to 4:30.

From Blue House in Wilton, there will be a mid-19th century English model of a stagecoach; an 18th-century Queen Anne drop-leaf dining table; an 18th-century English tea caddy in rosewood, and an 1850's Englishwoman's traveling case, also in rosewood, complete with a hidden compartment.

On display from the Bittersweet Shop in Gaylordsville are several Windsor chairs, a 1790 Queen Anne tray-top stand in cherry, trade signs, a nested set of eight spice boxes from 1858, and a sailing ship weathervane.

Scandinavian antiques are the specialty of the Country Gallery in Rupert, Vt., including a kitchen cupboard with etched-glass doors, a drop-leaf desk, chimney cupboard, sconces and needlepoint pillows.

Admission for early buyers at 8:30 A.M. will be $10, after 10 A.M. admission will be $3.50. The school is on Route 102, reached from Exit 15 of I-95 or Exit 40 off the Merritt Parkway to Route 7 North, onto Route 33 North, to 102. The number for more information is 203-222-2259.

BIRD LIFE

The Connecticut Ornithological Association will hold its 13th annual meeting next Saturday at Middlesex Community-Technical College in Middletown. The focus will be on bird life along the Connecticut shore and the effect of Long Island Sound's environmental health on coastal and shore birds.

Registration, at $12, will be held from 8 to 9 A.M., followed by eight speakers, discussions among attendees and experts, an award presentation and a brief business meeting. A buffet will be available for $8.50.

The numbers for more information are 203-284-6129 (days) or 860-429-4038 (evenings).

YIDDISH CULTURE

A three-day celebration of Yiddish culture and language will begin today at the University of Hartford, 200 Bloomfield Avenue, West Hartford. Sholom Aleichem's ''Song of Songs'' will be read in Yiddish by Curt Leviant, professor of Hebrew and Yiddish literature at Rutgers, at 1:15. Admission is free.

Sponsored by the Maurice Greenberg Center for Judaic Studies at the University of Hartford, the Charter Oak Cultural Center and Greater Hartford Jewish Community Center, the festival includes films and music as well as talks.

The art of translation will be explored by three experts at 3:15 P.M. in the Konover Center of the university, free of charge. At 7:30 P.M. Yiddish folk songs are to be sung by Cantor Joseph Ness and the Beth El Temple adults' and children's choirs in the Charter Oak Cultural Center, 21 Charter Oak Avenue, Hartford. Tickets at $12, discounted for people 65 and older, may be obtained at the door or by calling 860-249-1207.

An introduction to Jewish cinema will be held tomorrow at 1:30 in the Gilman Theater of the Greater Hartford Jewish Community Center, 335 Bloomfield Avenue, West Hartford. The speaker will be Michael C. Steinlauf, senior research fellow at the Yivo Institute for Jewish Research in Manhattan. There will be a screening of the 1937 Yiddish film ''The Jolly Paupers.''

Zalman Mlotek, composer-pianist and faculty member of the Yeshiva University Belz School of Music, will talk about ''The Many Voices of Yiddish Song'' on Tuesday at 8 P.M. Admission will be free at the Wilde Auditorium of the Jack Gray Conference Center on the university campus. More information is available from Charter Oak, 860-249-1207; the university's Judaic Studies Department, 860-768-4964, or the Jewish Community Center, 860-236-4571, ext. 351.

STUDIO TOUR

An offbeat field trip to the Fairfield Hills property of a master stone mason and sculptor is planned for Saturday by the Westport Nature Center. Meeting at 9:30 A.M. in the center's lobby at 10 Woodside Lane, the group will travel to Tim Currier's Sticks and Stones Farm, where he will lead a tour of his studio, his acres of stone-studded hillsides and the display gardens for his carved stoneworks.

The fee is $6, or $4 for people 65 and older. The number is 203-227-7253 to reserve a place or obtain more information.

DEVIL'S DEN IMAGES

More than 90 photographs taken by 24 professional and amateur Connecticut photographers are on display through April 12 at the Weston Public Library, 56 Norfield Road.

The subject is the Nature Conservancy's Devil's Den Preserve on the Weston-Redding border, and one picture shows the cloven hoofprint that gave the preserve its name. Discovered by Colonists along what it now the Godfrey Trail, it was described as ''so large it could only have been made by the Devil.''

The remaining photographs reveal the natural beauty of the Den's 1,746 acres and the adjacent 62-acre Katharine Ordway Preserve, havens for plants, animals, hikers and nature lovers. Viewing hours are Monday through Friday from 9 to 5, Saturday from 10 to 4, Sunday from noon to 4, and Wednesday evening until 8. The number is 203-226-2836 for more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
