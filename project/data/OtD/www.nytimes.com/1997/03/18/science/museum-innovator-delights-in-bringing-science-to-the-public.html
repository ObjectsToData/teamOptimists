Museum Innovator Delights in Bringing Science to the Public

By MALCOLM W. BROWNE
Published: March 18, 1997

AS an audience of wide-eyed children watched, a young woman wearing safety glasses and a laboratory coat decanted fuming liquid nitrogen from a thermos flask and demonstrated some of the startling things that happen to rubber balls, balloons and other objects chilled to 320 degrees below zero Fahrenheit.

Liquids like mercury freeze as hard as lead, balloons shrivel to small prunes and rubber balls shatter when dropped. Watching from the sidelines, her boss at the New York Hall of Science, Dr. Alan J. Friedman, nodded approvingly and recalled some of his own experiences with extreme cold.

''For my doctoral thesis at Florida State, I investigated magnetic transitions that occur at the temperature of liquid helium,'' Dr. Friedman said. ''It was fun. I loved the adventure of discovery.''

''Later, as I began specializing in the communication of science to nonscientists, I worried that by giving up research in physics, and the opportunity to be first to know new truths about nature, I would lose something important. But I gained something just as important: the satisfaction of helping people to know some of the pleasures of science. I'm speaking not just of children, but of their teachers and everyone else in whom the spark of curiosity is kindled.''

Behind his graying beard, Dr. Friedman exudes boyish delight as he explains to museum visitors how things work and imagines new ways to depict scientific subtleties to nonscientists. He has succeeded so well that last month the American Association for the Advancement of Science, America's premier society of professional scientists, presented him with its Award for Public Understanding of Science and Technology.

Dr. Friedman calls his brand of teaching ''informal science education,'' and what it lacks in hard mathematics and the rigors of testing, it makes up in appeal, he believes. Its main object at the New York Hall of Science in Flushing Meadows, Queens, is to lure young minds from inner-city populations.

Most of the children visiting the museum on an average day are black or of Hispanic descent, as are about 80 percent of the museum's young assistants and red-coated ''explainers'' -- high school or college students recruited for a museum program called the Science Career Ladder. As they learn to explain to visitors the science underlying museum exhibits, the explainers, more than half of whom are girls, must master some of the science themselves. For their services, they are paid small stipends and are given a variety of educational perquisites, which Dr. Friedman believes have paid off.

A recent survey of 91 alumni of the program, the museum reported, showed that 34 percent had gone on to become professional teachers, 15 percent were in science-related careers, and 9 percent were full-time museum employees. Only 2 percent were unemployed.

The museum has been a learning experience for Dr. Friedman himself.

''It's impossible to know what goes on in a child's head, so you just have to keep experimenting with the exhibits, just as you would in any other research project,'' he said. ''As a matter of fact, there is a great need for more formal academic study of what works and what doesn't in informal science education.''

In 1984, at the age of 42, Dr. Friedman became director of the moribund New York Hall of Science, which has since enjoyed phenomenal success under his stewardship, particularly in innovative display.

For instance, it had long troubled Dr. Friedman that the atom was generally depicted in museums, on television and in the popular press as a miniature planetary system, in which the atomic nucleus occupied the place of the Sun, with little electron balls circling in neat orbits like those of planets. But with the advent of quantum physics in the 1920's and 1930's, scientists learned that real atoms are not like that at all. An atom's electron shells are actually diffuse probability clouds that abruptly change shape and size, absorbing or emitting light as they are excited to higher energies or fall to lower ones.

Representing such an object in a realistic way was a staggering challenge, but in 1988 Dr. Friedman's technical team succeeded. Starting with a darkened room for the exhibit, the team rigged up a gadget that jiggles a small projection screen up and down. The motion is linked to a computer-driven laser system. With the screen moving rapidly and a dot of laser light speeding over it faster than the eye can perceive, the illusion of a giant, three-dimensional quantum atom of hydrogen leaps out at a viewer. A visitor, moreover, can add ''energy'' and watch the atom sprout lobes and change shape, or emit a photon of light when it loses energy.

(The museum's quantum atom performed impeccably for nearly a decade but is undergoing some repairs and maintenance.)

Another museum innovation, the creation of an inventor, Budd Wentz, was a new type of microscope equipped with a special light system and a large frosted-glass viewing screen. The microscope is powerful enough to view the activity of paramecia and other microorganisms, but because of its novel design, a half-dozen children at a time can watch the action. The microscope became so popular with museum visitors that Mr. Wentz has since manufactured them for some 60 institutions around the world.
