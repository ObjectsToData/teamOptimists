From Oasis to Thinning Battlefield

By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: July 15, 1997

WHAT'S HAPPENED TO THE HUMANITIES?

Edited by Alvin Kernan

267 pages. Princeton University Press. $29.95.

After being a central part of Western education for more than two millenniums, the humanities are in trouble. Or so this fascinating, if highly uneven anthology suggests.

''What's Happened to the Humanities?'' -- which includes essays commissioned by such prominent scholars as Denis Donoghue, Gertrude Himmelfarb, Lynn Hunt and Frank Kermode -- has been edited by Alvin Kernan, professor emeritus of humanities at Princeton. Like Mr. Kernan's provocative 1990 book, ''The Death of Literature,'' it offers a thoughtful and often disturbing assessment of recent developments in academia.

According to studies cited in this volume, higher education has expanded rapidly since World War II, but interest in -- not to mention, support for -- the humanities (that is, the study of literature, art history, music, religion, philosophy and history) has seriously slumped. In 1966, the humanities accounted for 20.7 percent of all bachelor's degrees awarded, and 13.8 percent of all doctoral degrees; in 1993, those percentages had dropped to 12.7 percent and 9.1 percent respectively. With cutbacks from traditional sources of support (like the National Endowment for the Humanities, the National Humanities Center and major foundations), financing responsibility for the humanities, Mr. Kernan writes, ''is being increasingly shifted'' to colleges and universities themselves and they frequently ''cannot, or will not, make up the losses from other sources.''

Such statistics, says one writer in this volume, suggest that students are turning away from the so-called softer fields of the liberal arts to focus on professional and vocational areas that offer brighter career prospects. Another contributor sees evidence of a broader and even more discouraging trend: that ''the humanities disciplines are coming to be perceived as increasingly less relevant and less necessary to modern life than other forms of knowledge.''

At the same time, the humanities have become a noisy battlefield in the culture wars, a battlefield on which debates over deconstruction, multiculturalism and gender studies continue to rage. In an essay titled ''Democratization and Decline,'' Lynn Hunt, a professor of history at the University of Pennsylvania and a co-author of ''Telling the Truth about History,'' argues that feminism and multiculturalism are ''intellectual trends that seem to follow inexorably from the changing demography of higher education,'' namely a rise in the number of women and minority students in the student body and faculty.

''The increasing emphasis on the social construction of knowledge and identity,'' she writes, ''probably stems from these same changes, for previously excluded groups are especially sensitive to the ways in which social structures and social meanings have shaped their lives.'' In another essay, David Bromwich, a professor of English at Yale University and the author of ''Politics by Other Means,'' disputes Ms. Hunt's emphasis on demographics, arguing that the politicization of scholarship began in the 1960's as reaction to the Vietnam War galvanized the belief that ''all choices are political choices, that every intellectual interest serves some social end.'' In subsequent decades, scholarship itself came to be seen as a vehicle for social reform. It became fashionable for radical historians, Mr. Bromwich writes, not only to question ''consensus history'' but also to ''pardon the defeated conspicuously and withhold all pardon from the victors.''

As a high-minded and scrupulous devotion to facts increasingly gave way to overt advocacy, traditional methodology began to fall out of favor with some historians, says Gertrude Himmelfarb in a forceful essay that recalls the strongest chapters of her 1994 book ''On Looking Into the Abyss.'' ''The liberation from the tyranny of the discipline,'' she writes, ''takes many forms,'' from ''a liberation from footnotes'' and a rejection of archival research to an abandonment of narrative history, which is said to rely upon the ''tyrannical'' concepts of causality and chronology. Empowered by deconstruction's emphasis on the indeterminacy of texts and Michel Foucault's theory of hegemonic power, Ms. Himmelfarb argues, the new historians enshrined subjectivity over objectivity and in the process made relativism more and more an end in itself.

In another tough, insightful essay, the critic Denis Donoghue assesses the impact that these same developments have had on literature and our habits of reading. By examining several examples of literary criticism (all concerned with passages from ''Macbeth''), he adroitly shows how traditional readings, based on the careful study of language and structure, have increasingly been supplanted by polemical arguments focused on the social (e.g., gender and class) aspects of a text. By applying the criteria of the social sciences to literature, he suggests, the new critics are reducing highly complex, highly ambiguous works of art to simple self-serving propositions.

In the strongest essays in ''What's Happened'' -- most notably the pieces by Mr. Donoghue, Ms. Himmelfarb and Mr. Bromwich -- the authors shrewdly analyze the dangers posed by new wave, post-modern scholarship. Though the reader may not agree with all their assessments, their arguments are powerfully and judiciously marshaled and laid out in succinct, lucid prose.

Other essays in this volume are decidedly weaker. Christopher Ricks's essay on metaphor, which tries to make the case that there are limits to theory and theorizing, is a solipsistic and willfully playful piece that sheds little light on its subject. And Carla Hesse's essay on the impact of digital technology on libraries is larded with the sort of obfuscatory academic jargon that makes so much recent critical writing unpleasant and unrewarding to read. Most of the remaining chapters in the volume, like John H. D'Arms's look at trends in financing and Francis Oakley's look at changes in the humanities classroom, are less persuasive essays than informative pieces of reporting.

All in all, a highly eclectic volume, but a volume that should be read, or at least read selectively, by the reader interested in the intellectual, social and pedagogical changes sustained by the humanities in the closing years of this century.
