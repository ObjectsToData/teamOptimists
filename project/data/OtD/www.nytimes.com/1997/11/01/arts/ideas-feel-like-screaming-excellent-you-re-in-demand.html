IDEAS; Feel Like Screaming? Excellent! You're in Demand

By SARAH BOXER
Published: November 1, 1997

Hysteria is back, sweet with the scent of Victorian madness. Perhaps it's because of the millennium, or the influence of television and the Internet. Perhaps it's the fault of feminists and literary critics, or the cold mechanistic ways of doctors. Maybe hysteria never really left us. Maybe it's never been here. Whatever the case, hysteria, a psychological ailment with no known physical cause, multifarious symptoms and a checkered past, is more fashionable than ever.

''The new hysteria studies,'' writes Mark S. Micale, in ''Approaching Hysteria: Disease and Its Interpretations,'' (Princeton, 1995), ''have been transformed over the past decade from a ''scholarly backwater'' into ''one of the most active and dynamic research sites within the humanities.''

When doctors abandoned the term hysteria in the 1970's, literary critics and social scientists eagerly grabbed it for themselves. But they are still arguing about just what they have rescued from obscurity -- a malady or a metaphor?

Some of ''the new hysterians'' (a term cleverly coined by Elaine Showalter, a humanities professor at Princeton University) are historians looking at the details of a bygone illness. Others are scholars who see hysteria as a kind of metaphor, an expression of malaise, anxiety or despair.

At least one of the new hysterians, Ms. Showalter, has declared in her book ''Hysteries'' (Columbia, 1997) that an epidemic of hysteria is sweeping over America. People who think they have been visited by aliens, people who think they took part in satanic rituals, people who think they have multiple personalities and people suffering chronic fatigue syndrome and gulf war syndrome are all, in her book, hysterics. Her thesis, needless to say, is not universally embraced.

The debate over what hysteria is isn't new. Mr. Micale, a historian at Yale University, notes that in Hippocratic times hysteria was dizziness, heavy breathing and hyperemotionality caused by the hystera, or uterus, wandering through the body. In the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, the symptoms of hysteria were stigmata diaboli, or numb patches of skin thought to be marks of the devil.

Modern hysteria was born in the 19th century. The French neurologist Jean-Martin Charcot believed the dramatic fits of hysterics at the Salpetriere Hospital were caused by a physical or emotional trauma. And Freud's hysterics always had physical symptoms -- facial tics, ulcers, paralyses -- that functioned as clues about the repressed desire or anxiety (usually about sex) that caused them. Finally, the last great wave occurred during World War I and World War II, when men suffered from ''shell shock'' and ''battle fatigue.''

In the 1970's hysteria was killed off as a legitimate illness. The American Psychiatric Association wiped the old term from the pages of ''The Diagnostic Manual of Mental Disorders'' and, according to Mr. Micale, replaced it with '' 'factitious illness behavior,' 'dissociative disorder -- conversion type,' 'histrionic personality type,' 'psychogenic pain disorder' and 'undifferentiated somatoform disorder.' ''

Cast off by psychiatrists and doctors, hysteria was first resuscitated by feminists who saw it as a sickness caused by the male social order. Helene Cixous, now a professor of literature at the University of Paris, wrote, ''We might say that the Absolute Woman, in culture, the woman who really represents femininity most effectively, who is closest to femininity as prey to masculinity, is actually the hysteric.'' Hysteria was the purple heart of embattled femininity, worn by Bertha Pappenheim (Freud's Anna O.), Virginia Woolf and Alice James.

Many other feminists, however, were troubled by the attempt to turn the hysteric into a feminist icon. As Ms. Showalter argued in ''The Female Malady,'' hysteria was a punitive diagnosis, reserved for women who did not act the way men thought they should and for men who were branded as ''feminine.''

The idea that hysteria might be culturally based jump-started another group of scholars. George Rousseau, an English professor, traced the hysteric's passage through literature. Roy Porter, a medical historian, used hysteria to look at changing representations of the mind and body. Sander Gilman, a humanities professor at the University of Chicago, used the label of hysteria to follow the course of social stigmatization, in women and Jews. Jan Ellen Goldstein, a historian at the University of Chicago, showed how Charcot's hysterics served his anticlerical aims. Debora Silverman, an art historian at U.C.L.A., analyzed why Charcot's hysterics looked and acted the way they did.

The latest twist in the field, however, is Ms. Showalter's declaration that we're in the midst of an epidemic of hysteria. She has applied the diagnosis to men as well as women, to people with physical symptoms as well as to those with strange beliefs, even to herself. What all hysterics have in common, Ms. Showalter writes, is an inability to express personal despair. So they ''blame external sources -- a virus, sexual molestation, chemical warfare, satanic conspiracy, alien infiltration -- for psychic problems.'' And they contract their strange persecutory beliefs from what she calls ''hysteries,'' fashionable narratives spread by talk shows, the news media, the Internet and self-help books.
