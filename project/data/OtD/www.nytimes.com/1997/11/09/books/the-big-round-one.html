The Big Round One

By Robert Eisner
Published: November 9, 1997

THE END OF TIME

Faith and Fear in the Shadow of the Millennium.

By Damian Thompson.

374 pp. Hanover, N.H.:

University Press of New England. $26.

QUESTIONING THE MILLENNIUM

A Rationalist's Guide to a Precisely Arbitrary Countdown.

By Stephen Jay Gould.

Illustrated. 190 pp. New York:

Harmony Books. $17.95

DamiAn Thompson has chosen an apt title for his fascinating, unsettling yet sympathetic study of millenarianism, ''The End of Time.'' Time is the most nostalgic of elements: time past, which determines who we are and how we got here; time present, which in its more poignant and pleasurable moments is quickly acquiring the patina of memory; and time still to come, that backdrop for the projection of both our fears and our hopes for the physical and spiritual Arcadia we ache to return to.

For 4,000 years or so the world has seldom been free from one or another sect that in order to face the burden of time has needed to believe in its imminent end -- the apocalypse or millennium. Strictly speaking, the millennium refers to the glorious thousand-year reign of Christ's Second Coming, a golden age that will precede a final battle where good triumphs and the world comes to an end -- all as set forth in the Apocalypse (or Revelation) of St. John. Loosely speaking, since eschatology comprises salvation, both terms may refer to any approaching golden age or fiery end of the world.

Thompson argues persuasively that although apocalypticism in its purest manifestations ''is the refuge of an embattled minority, it is not always sharply distinguished from what we regard as the mainstream of Western thought.'' Indeed, the residents of Rancho Santa Fe, Calif., interviewed by the news media about their Heaven's Gate neighbors who had just committed suicide, remarked again and again how they had seemed just like regular folks. Presumably the neighbors had not yet seen the cult's promotional video.

Given that the Creator must be a mathematician, there is an impulse, Thompson says, ''to align God's calendar with man's.'' Thompson, a former religious affairs correspondent for The Daily Telegraph in London, uses the first part of his book to trace that impulse back to the ancient Mesopotamians, through the Persian religion of Zoroaster, the Book of Daniel in the Bible and through Greek, Roman and early Christian thought up to the modern era. He draws entirely on secondary material for this historical panorama, and at times it shows in the vagueness of his facts and analysis. Eyewitness accounts of the Papal Jubilee of 1300 and autobiographies of a 17th-century apocalyptic movement are tantalizingly referred to but never quoted. That said, this section is as handy a summation as you'll find of four millenniums of millenarianism.

Christian preoccupation with the end started right at the beginning. Ambiguous passages in the Gospels and Epistles and the kaleidoscopic visions of Revelation encouraged early Christians, persecuted by the Romans, to believe in an imminent reward at Christ's Second Coming. Neither the first nor last radical movement to turn conservative, the church, once legalized by the Roman Emperor Constantine, soon acquired wealth, prestige and a stake in the future. Talk of the millennium was allowed to lapse. The abstract idea of uniform time hardly existed outside of a monastery. The illiterate masses were seldom aware of the exact year and got their Bible stories in church, where in neither sermons nor stained-glass illustrations was the Apocalypse a popular subject.

A certain drop-the-bomb fervor can be detected about the year 1000, but the evidence supports nothing like the mass hysteria commonly alleged. Most of the Christian sects Thompson cites start their end-time bomb ticking from the date of God's creation of the world, and there are as many ways of calculating that as there have been calculators. Bang! will resound, some said, exactly 6,000 years from the creation of the world (the Babylonian system of numbers keeps rearing its monstrous sexagesimal heads). The common denominators in these predictions have been the coincidence of Armageddon with the normal life expectancy of the cultic accountant and, most important, that they have all been wrong so far. The leader of the End-of-the-World picnic party in the theater skit presented by the old Beyond the Fringe troupe says, ''We must get a winner one day.''

Said leader -- I think it was Peter Cook playing the part -- relied not only on ''ancient pyramidic scrolls'' but on ''me Ingersoll watch.'' Clocks became more accurate and affordable about the time the printing press, and the Reformation placed Scripture in the hands of everyman and replaced tradition (a k a authority) with personal interpretation. The pocket calculator and the automobile odometer, clicking all the nines over to zeros, only encourage the Beast.

Which brings us to the bulk of this dense book, the end of our own century. Thompson barely mentions the Jonestown carnage of 1978, in which more than 900 people died, including an American Congressman. But he devotes a chapter each to the Aum Shinrikyo cult of Shoko Asahara in Japan and the Waco debacle, and for this American edition he has added an epilogue on the Heaven's Gate send-off. Curiously, the Branch Davidians' leader in Waco, David Koresh, and Marshall Applewhite of Heaven's Gate were both musicians. I can't help wondering what tunes they might be jamming with on the far side of the Pearly Gates -- ''Cabin in the Sky'' perhaps?
