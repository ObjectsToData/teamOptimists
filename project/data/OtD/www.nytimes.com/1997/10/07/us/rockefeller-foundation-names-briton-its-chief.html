Rockefeller Foundation Names Briton Its Chief

By JUDITH MILLER
Published: October 7, 1997

The Rockefeller Foundation has chosen Gordon Conway, a British agricultural ecologist and the vice chancellor of the University of Sussex in England, to be the 12th president of the foundation, one of the nation's oldest and largest.

Dr. Conway, 59, a dark horse for the prestigious post, will be the first person who is not a United States citizen to head the foundation, which has $2.8 billion in assets and pledged $107 million in gifts last year, almost 70 percent of it abroad.

The foundation, endowed by John D. Rockefeller in 1913, awards grants to promote agriculture and development, the arts and humanities, human rights and family planning. Dr. Conway will assume the presidency on April 1.

In selecting Dr. Conway, the Rockefeller board has underscored its traditional global orientation and historical mission of fostering science-based development abroad at a time when some large American foundations are focusing on challenges and needs closer to home.

Peter C. Goldmark Jr., the current president, who announced last year that he wanted to step down, said that since so many of the world's problems were increasingly transnational, the choice of a non-American to head Rockefeller was consistent with the foundation's outlook.

''Drugs, weapons and people looking for jobs cross borders almost as if they don't exist,'' said Mr. Goldmark, who had asked not to be involved in the selection of his successor. ''While the foundation must be strong in the domestic and international arenas, its distinctive role is as a premier international foundation.''

Rockefeller is one of the few American foundations that emphasizes foreign affairs. According to Giving USA and the Foundation Center, which monitor giving, grants in international affairs constitute only 1.3 percent of total American giving and 4 percent of American foundation giving. But Ann E. Kaplan, the editor of Giving USA, said that Rockefeller's selection of a non-American president, the slight rise since the early 1990's in corporate overseas giving, and Ted Turner's recent $1 billion gift to support United Nations programs and relief suggested that ''an emphasis on global issues may be a new philanthropic trend.''

Alice Stone Ilchman, chairwoman of Rockefeller's board and president of Sarah Lawrence College, said that the search committee she had headed insisted on international experience as one of four criteria in choosing from among more than 250 candidates. Fifteen candidates were interviewed by the board, and a half-dozen were still contenders when the trustees met last week in New York to make their final choice.

The selection of Dr. Conway surprised some American foundation leaders, many of whom receive Rockefeller support and spoke on condition of anonymity. Though highly respected in his field and well known among professionals at the Ford Foundation, where from 1989 to 1992 he was its representative for India, Nepal and Sri Lanka, Dr. Conway is not well known among American foundation chiefs.

''That's because he doesn't come from the American philanthropic community,'' said Susan Berresford, president of the Ford Foundation, which spent about 40 percent of its $332 million last year abroad, making it a far larger giver in that sector than Rockefeller. ''But Gordon is a terrific person, as well as a sound, experienced grant maker'' who will bring the foundation ''a fresh and different perspective.''

But some foundation officials expressed surprise that the board had passed over two strong internal minority candidates -- Angela Glover Blackwell, a senior vice president and a black American lawyer with expertise in civil rights and urban affairs, and Lincoln Chen, a vice president and an Asian-American scientist with a tenured chair in international health at the Harvard University School of Public Health.

Confirming that both Ms. Blackwell and Mr. Chen were finalists, Ms. Ilchman said the board hoped that both would remain at Rockefeller to continue their ''cutting edge work.''

Mr. Chen, who was known as the stronger inside candidate because of his foreign expertise, was in China and could not be reached for comment. Ms. Blackwell did not return calls to her office.

Dr. Conway, in an interview, said it was increasingly difficult to distinguish between American and international challenges.

''We're moving into a world in which many of the problems we face are common,'' he said.

Dr. Conway pioneered a type of pest management in Borneo in the 1960's, and in the 1980's he became one of the first to define the concept of sustainable agriculture, which is critical to the development of poor countries. In 1992, he became Sussex University's vice chancellor, the equivalent of president at an American university. He has a doctorate in systems ecology from the University of California at Davis and is the author of more than 100 articles and books. His latest book, ''The Doubly Green Revolution: Food for All in the 21st Century,'' will be published next month by Penguin.
