Music Notes; Muti Cancellation Irks Philadelphians

By ALLAN KOZINN
Published: March 8, 1993

A skirmish seems to be brewing between Riccardo Muti and the Philadelphia Orchestra. Mr. Muti, who became the orchestra's Laureate Conductor after relinquishing the music director's post to spend more time in Europe (he is music director of La Scala, in Milan), had agreed to spend four weeks with the Philadelphia in the 1993-94 season. It will be the first full one under the direction of his successor, Wolfgang Sawallisch, who conducted the orchestra last week in Avery Fisher Hall.

But just as the Philadelphia was about to announce its plans for the forthcoming season -- and after the brochures had been printed -- Mr. Muti withdrew from his commitment. He could not be reached at his home in Ravenna. His manager, Judie Janowski, at Columbia Artists Management Inc., said that the conductor had not made any official statement about the reasons for his withdrawal, but she suggested that Mr. Muti would be "easing up on his schedule overall." 'Very Disappointed'

Mr. Sawallisch, in a statement released through the orchestra, put it somewhat differently. "I spoke with Maestro Muti, and he is very disappointed that he will be unable to perform in Philadelphia next season as we had hoped. His commitments with La Scala and the Vienna Philharmonic have overextended his schedule."

Indeed, one aspect of Mr. Muti's cancellation that the orchestra finds particularly irksome is that Mr. Muti will actually be in the vicinity during one of the periods he has canceled. He had been scheduled to lead the orchestra on Feb. 25 and 26 in Philadelphia and on March 1 at Carnegie Hall. He was also scheduled to conduct the Vienna Philharmonic at Carnegie Hall on March 2, 3 and 4. Ms. Janowski said that the Vienna dates had not been canceled.

Joseph Kluger, the Philadelphia Orchestra's president, was circumspect.

"Conductors' schedules are complicated," Mr. Kluger said, "particularly conductors who run opera houses. They want to be all things to all people, and they can't be. But there's a saying in the sports world, about it not being over until the fat lady sings, and we don't consider this finished. Maestro Sawallisch is trying to get a fuller explanation about what caused him to do this." Muti Expected in April

If Mr. Muti and his former orchestra cannot settle their differences by telephone and fax within the next few weeks, they can deal with them in person, assuming that Mr. Muti turns up for concerts scheduled in Philadelphia between April 20 and May 3, and at Carnegie Hall on April 27.

Ms. Janowski insisted that Mr. Muti's love for Philadelphia had not cooled, and that there was no reason to expect him to cancel his April and May performances. Peaceful Coexistence

On the heels of the unusual ascent on the British pop music charts of Henryk Gorecki's Symphony No. 3, Q magazine, a British rock journal, devoted a section of its March issue to the surprisingly long history of classical-pop coexistence. In addition to an interview with Elvis Costello and the Brodsky String Quartet about their collaborative album, "The Juliet Letters," the magazine offers a two-page history of crossover efforts, cataloguing rock musicians' flirtations with classical instruments and forms and classical musicians' dabblings in rock.

But the issue's handiest feature is a single-page, small-print listing called "You Know That Bit in the Old Spice Ad: They Came, They Saw, They Nicked Bits of Classical Music." Starting with Jackie Wilson's 1960 hit, "Alone at Last" (which borrowed a theme from Tchaikovsky's First Piano Concerto) and ending with Take That's 1992 remake of "Could It Be Magic" (a 1976 Donna Summer hit based on a Chopin prelude), the list includes 55 examples of rock's direct lifts from the classics. Judging from the list, Tchaikovsky, Beethoven, Bach, Grieg and Pachelbel seem particularly susceptible to melodic pilfering.

Q also has a small classical-music section, an unusual feature in a rock magazine. Besides brief articles and briefer reviews, it has started a "Great Composers" series, designed to look like old trading cards. The current honoree is Puccini, whose "character" listing reads:

"Cheery, sybaritic, womanizing millionaire racer of fast cars and speedboats, given to massacring wildfowl with expensive shotguns. Quasi-fascist blend of sentiment and sadism. (All his operas contain ditty about 'a little house' for two; all his heroines are tortured, in 'Turandot,' literally.)" AllEverything About Beethoven

Schroeder, the Beethoven-obsessed pianist who dwells in the Peanuts comic strip, may have to start saving up for a computer and a modem. This summer, Beethoven scholars and other fans will be able to dial into a database in which they can research every conceivable facet of the composer's life and music. The database, which is the first computerized dial-in bibliography devoted to any composer, will be available free of charge (apart from the cost of a manual) and can be accessed from anywhere in the United States.

The Beethoven Bibliography Project, as the database is officially called, is being compiled at the Ira F. Brilliant Center for Beethoven Studies at San Jose State University in California, and will include citations for thousands of books and articles about Beethoven, and eventually, perhaps, even full texts. The principal sources for this information will be the Beethoven Center's own extensive library, as well as the collection at the Beethovenhaus in Bonn.
