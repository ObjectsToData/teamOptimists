CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 28, 1993

CHINESE SCHOLARS UNITE

Scholars from the People's Republic of China and from Taiwan will discuss their differences and their future in a day and evening conference at Yale on Saturday. The event is open to the public for a fee of $20 and free to students.

The morning session will cover Chinese culture, past and present; literature and media development in Taiwan and the mainland, and the future of Chinese ethical thought, science and technology and institutional reform.

From 1:45 to 5 P.M. the topics will include the future of trade between the mainland and Taiwan; investment possibilities and economic cooperation; political relations and democratization and legal conflicts and the implications for the United States.

Jonathan Spence, professor of history at Yale, will deliver a keynote address at 7:30 P.M., after which there will be a table discussion until 10 P.M.

Among the speakers scheduled throughout the day are Howard Lamar, Acting President of Yale; Chen Yih-young , president of the National Open University of Taiwan; Tu Wei-ming, professor of Chinese culture at Harvard; Duan Ruichun, visiting professor from Beijing University; Chang Xiuze, professor of economics at Nankai University in Tianjin; Hsu Tzong-li, professor of law at the National Taiwan University in Taipei, and other faculty members specializing in Asian studies from American, Taiwanese and Chinese mainland universities.

Some presentations will be in English, others will be in Chinese. Lunch and dinner are available in nearby restaurants, or on campus with special tickets. Registration will be at 8:30 A.M. in Sheffield-Sterling-Strathcona Hall at Prospect and Grove Streets. For more information call 230-9685. MAKING MATZOH

Most people are familiar with the Old Testament story about how the Jews, escaping from Egypt, baked a kind of bread called matzoh by setting unleavened dough out on rocks in the broiling sun until it was cooked.

At Passover, the Jewish holiday commemorating the Exodus each spring, food markets carry a variety of packaged matzoh, and now, thanks to Beth Israel Synagogue, 40 King Street in Norwalk, anyone can learn how to bake it at home.

Today at 9:30 and again at 10:30 A.M. matzoh-baking workshops will be held in which participants will make the dough, roll it out, bake it and take it home. The event will end with a film on the production of matzoh. All ages, from children to the elderly, are welcome. Admission is $3 to cover the cost of materials. Call 866-0534 for more information. GRANDPARENT STORIES

Grandparents will be invited to make half-hour videotaped readings of stories or chapters of books to send to their grandchildren in a program that starts Thursday at the Farmington Library. "So many families live far from the grandparents; we found this fills a need," said Lucille O'Connor, who directs the program.

While some grandparents do make such tapings at home, "We find that older people are not fond of fiddling with electronic equipment," Ms. O'Connell said. Besides, the library has a large selection of new books covering the grandparent-grandchild relationship.

The tapes will be played back for the reader and an occasional re-taping may be made if the first one is not satisfactory. Grandparents should bring a blank tape or they may purchase one at the library.

Appointments must be made by calling 673-6791. SCHWEITZER LECTURER

Jane Goodall, the naturalist who gained worldwide recognition for her work with primates in Tanzania, will deliver the first International Albert Schweitzer Lecture at Yale on Friday at 4 P.M. in Battell Chapel. Her subject will be "Chimpanzees: Living Bridge Between Man and Beast" and admission is free.

The lecture series was established by the International Schweitzer Institute for the Humanities and the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale. Three lectures will be presented each year featuring international figures speaking on topics of concern to Dr. Schweitzer during his lifetime: social justice, medicine and health, human and animal rights, the environment, nuclear disarmament and world peace.

Ms. Goodall will speak a second time on Saturday at 4 P.M. in Hamden, where the Schweitzer Institute's new center is to open at 515 Sherman Avenue on the campus of Quinnipiac College. Her talk, titled "Some Lessons From Our Nearest Relatives," will be presented in Alumni Hall and admission is free. PILOBOLUS WORKSHOPS

The Pilobolus Dance Theater will hold two two-day workshops at the Mattatuck Museum in Waterbury. Classes from 10 A.M. to 4 P.M. Saturday and next Sunday will be held, one for those who have had little or no experience in dance movement, and another one for those who are somewhat experienced.

Teaching will focus on improvisational movement for the inexperienced, and on choreography and performance for the more advanced students. Each workshop is limited to 30 people; the instructors will be the artistic directors of Pilobolus.

Drawing from their own interests and experiences, participants will collaborate on creating new dances that they will perform informally on Sunday at 4 P.M. for a public audience.

To register for a fee of $40 for either of the two-day workshops, or to obtain more information, call 868-0538. There will be no charge for those attending the 4 P.M. performance.

Pilobolus will perform next Sunday at 8 P.M. at Central Naugatuck Valley Higher Education Center. Tickets are $15, or $10 for students and the elderly. Call 575-8039 for reservations. SOUNDS CUCKOO

"Imitazione del Cuccu" for violin and continuo by Walther, "The Cuckoo and the Nightingale" by Handel and "Le Rappel des Oiseaux" by Couperin hint at the genre of music to be performed on Saturday at 8 P.M. in the First Congregational Church of Darien.

It consists of concerti and other works that imitate the sounds of cats, dogs, cuckoos, nightingales, hens and goldfinches, among other wild and domesticated creatures.

Musicians of the Old Post Road, who created the program, perform on authentic period instruments. They include Michael Bahmann, harpsichordist; Suzanne Stumpf, flutist; Dana Maiben and Julie McKenzie, violinists; Anne Black, violist, and Daniel Ryan, cellist.

The church is at 14 Brookside Road and admission will be $10, or $5 for students and the elderly. MALL FOR MALL

England's restored 18th- and 19th-century shops and shopping arcades are the subject of a talk to be given today at 2 P.M. by Lorella Brocklesby, a professor of architecture and cultural historian from London who is currently an adjunct professor of humanities at New York University. The program will be at the Yale Center for British Art, 1080 Chapel Street in New Haven and admission is $5.

Burlington Arcade and its top-hatted beadles, Covent Garden, The Colonnades in Bath, and elegantly restored Georgian and Victorian arcades in Stanford and York are among the venues to be discussed and compared with the ubiquitous American mall.
