The Paris of Phantoms

By Marina Warner;
Published: April 11, 1993

ATGET'S SEVEN ALBUMS By Molly Nesbit. Illustrated. 428 pp. New Haven: Yale University Press. $55.

IN 1920, Eugene Atget wrote to the new director of France's Commission for Historical Monuments; he had inventoried the range and variety of his photographs, and concluded, "I might say that I possess all of Vieux Paris ." "Vieux Paris" was the province of campaigning antiquarians; it meant cobbles and craftsmen, narrow side streets and prostitutes, Renaissance wrought iron, medieval alleyways, turreted inner courtyards. Atget had been reproducing all this over decades of wandering in the city; he called his photographic work "documents" under the rubric "Paris pittoresque." He was documenting the porte-cocheres with their lion-headed knockers and spandrels of floating sylphs, the deliverymen's handcarts and horse wagons, the vitrines with serried ranks of corsets and stalls drooping with fish or packed with cloth caps, the silvered mirrors of the basins in gardens like the Luxembourg, and mustachioed store dummies in the latest fedoras.

Yet ever since Berenice Abbott bought his archive, after Atget's death in 1927 at the age of 70, no one has been easy with this slant on the merely picturesque, the merely documentary. Atget's images are so much more peculiar than that, with their eerie banishment of people, their patches of incandescence, their skewed compositions, their emphasis on inconsequent matters, their "mistakes" (like the photographer's shadow clouding the subject). Walter Benjamin famously recalled that "not for nothing were pictures of Atget compared to those of the scene of a crime" -- after the perpetrator has just left. But that's not quite right, either; the phantoms whose presence Atget intimates may be unsettling, but they aren't criminal.

Molly Nesbit, in her fine study "Atget's Seven Albums," explores a brilliant array of evidence to discover what Atget's presences mean and how his enigmas achieve their effect. Painstakingly trawling through archives, applying the latest and sharpest tools of theoretical analysis, with bravura intelligence she surveys the reverberative beauty of the images on many fronts and comes up with a radical reassessment. Atget is no longer the seamless, universal, artist-in-spite-of-himself primitive who was hailed by Benjamin, Clement Greenberg and John Szarkowski. He emerges here as a complex entrepreneur, a committed political independent, a determined and even witty contestant of the changing urban scene and, not least, one of the rare artists to achieve one of modernism's goals: pictures of reality's dense mysteries that do not stand for something else. No allegories. A cart is a cart is a cart. A room is a room is a room.

The "Seven Albums" in question are paper volumes of 60 photographs each, pasted in and captioned by Atget and sold by him to various libraries in Paris, like the Bibliotheque Historique. In all, Ms. Nesbit calculates that Atget sold 16,748 prints to the nation in the course of his career. He did not exhibit; he conducted his business as if he were a research agency, or an antique-dealer-cum-bookseller. By deciphering his business address book, Ms. Nesbit shows how systematically Atget targeted his private clientele, marketing his photographs to many who were in the business of conserving Paris or reinventing it nostalgically: set decorators, history painters, architectural ironmongers, illustrators in the popular magazines. It often comes as a shock to see the uses to which purchasers of Atget's images put them. One cartoonist set some naughty innuendo in a bedroom from Atget's archive, and a military artist draped resplendently uniformed dragoons over a windmill's ladder and brace, previously recorded with such affectless dignity by Atget's camera. But the business side of his activity throws light on some of the formal peculiarities of his framing: the Moulin Rouge turns into a backdrop to the stretch of the street in front, wide and deep enough for a stage musical.

THE albums were as often as not catalogued by librarians under "moeurs" -- customs -- a category that seemed to embrace satisfactorily the odd lot of subjects Atget chose to record: not only old streets and ornaments, scrollwork, sign paintings and shopkeepers' goods on display, but also rag-and-bone yards and the eerie stilled emptinesses of the old ramparts and ditches of Paris's fortifications, scheduled for redevelopment but saved and prepared for use in World War I.

Ms. Nesbit, a fellow of the Getty Center for the History of Art and the Humanities in California, turns her considerable powers of analysis to each album in turn and presses, in each case, new material to bear on the interpretations. As a maker of "documents," it turns out Atget was not altogether reliable; he took liberties with the labels on the photographs of domestic interiors, attributing his own apartment, for instance, to an "artiste dramatique." It was true he had started life as an actor, performing in night schools in an adult education program, but Ms. Nesbit implies that as a photographer he continued to play parts of concealed advocacy. Arranging his album of interiors to begin with his own crammed, functional space and to end with the frivolous, luxurious spaciousness of the home of the actress Cecile Sorel, he was revealing the decorative style achieved by the working poor in spite of their circumstances, and thus covertly marking the differences in class in the city.
