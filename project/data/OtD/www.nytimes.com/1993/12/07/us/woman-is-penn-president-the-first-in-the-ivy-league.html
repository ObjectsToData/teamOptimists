Woman Is Penn President; The First in the Ivy League

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: December 7, 1993

Judith Rodin, the provost of Yale University, has been chosen president of the University of Pennsylvania, becoming the first woman to head an Ivy League institution.

Dr. Rodin, 49, a behavioral psychologist who is a Philadelphia native and a Penn alumna, will assume the post next June and face a number of problems, including a relatively low endowment, the need for academic consolidation and issues of racial strife and free speech.

As provost of Yale, Dr. Rodin was a candidate for the Yale presidency after the resignation of Benno C. Schmidt Jr. She led the university during the difficult year after Mr. Schmidt resigned, instituting a system to reduce the budget deficit and calming the faculty members and students, who were concerned about cutbacks and layoffs. 'I'll Be Watched'

Speaking by telephone from Philadelphia, Dr. Rodin said "the Ivy League's having a woman at the highest level is symbolic."

"I understand I'll be watched," she said.

While Yale and Penn both have had women as acting presidents, the appointment of Dr. Rodin is the first in the Ivy League, which comprises Brown, Columbia, Cornell, Dartmouth, Harvard, Penn, Princeton and Yale. Among the women at the highest level in major academic institutions are W. Ann Reynolds, chancellor of the City University of New York; Nannerl H. Keohane, who was installed as president of Duke University this fall, and Katherine Lyall, the president of the University of Wisconsin.

Dr. Rodin said her vision was "to make Pennsylvania a leading institution and a great university with a truly international perspective."

She will succeed Claire Fagin, the nursing department chairwoman who has been Penn's acting president since the resignation of Sheldon Hackney last June. Mr. Hackney is now chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities. 'Exciting and Dynamic'

Last fall, Dr. Fagin asked not to be considered as a candidate for the job; instead, she announced that she would lead the university during the search for a president and then return to teaching.

Alvin Shoemaker, chairman of the search committee and chairman of the board of trustees, said his committee interviewed more than 60 people and found Dr. Rodin "the most exciting and dynamic" of all the candidates. He said the search committee was particularly pleased at Dr. Rodin's connections to Philadelphia, where her mother still lives, and to Penn, where she received her bachelor's degree in 1966. She received her Ph.D. from Columbia University in 1970.

"We haven't had one of our graduates serve as president in about 50 years," Mr. Shoemaker said.

As to her ability to deal with a relatively low endowment, $1.35 billion, Mr. Shoemaker said that recent efforts to raise money had paid off and that "her main job will be to keep the momentum going." (By comparison, Harvard University's endowment is $5.8 billion, the largest in the Ivy League.)

Another challenge that Dr. Rodin faces is academic consolidation. Last October, to cope with relatively low income from its endowment, the university's School of Arts and Sciences eliminated religious studies, civilization and regional economic science departments, shifted the astronomy department into physics and brought in outside administrators to overhaul the Slavic languages department. Many faculty members were distressed by the action.

Mr. Shoemaker said he thought Dr. Rodin's experience at Yale would help her deal with these and other problems. Trouble developed at Yale last year after Mr. Schmidt announced a plan to close a budget gap by cutting nearly 11 percent of the faculty members of arts and sciences and by cutting programs and departments. The faculty members and students rebelled, leading to Mr. Schmidt's departure.

Richard Levin, the new Yale president, said yesterday, "Thanks to her skillful leadership, the faculty was quite satisfied with the outcome of restructuring."

Dr. Rodin said: "The actual change at Yale was carried out by me. We engaged the faculty. With the necessary discussion and participation, they can be made to feel good about consolidation even if the outcome is difficult and results in a closed department." Problem of Racial Tension

At Penn, Dr. Rodin will also face problems arising from campus racial tension and the issue of free speech.

In one incident last year, a white student called five blacks students "water buffaloes" after they refused to break up a party in front of his dormitory. The blacks students complained to a university panel that he used the word "black" in his remark; he denied this.

In a second incident, a group of students organized by the Black Student League destroyed about 14,000 copies of The Daily Pennsylvanian, a student-run newspaper. The black students said they were protesting what they believed to be a racist slant of the newspaper.

In the first case, the complaint was eventually withdrawn, and in the second, university officials dismissed disciplinary proceedings against the black students.

Nevertheless, Mr. Hackney, who was then president, was widely criticized for the university's code regulating offensive speech and for his handling of the two incidents.

Last month, Dr. Fagin rescinded the speech code as unworkable and appointed a commission, composed of faculty, students and trustees, to recommend how to handle the matter to the new president.

Dr. Rodin joined the psychology department of Yale in 1972, became chairwoman of the department in 1989 and was appointed in 1991 by Mr. Schmidt as the dean of the graduate school. Less than a year later, after the provost quit in the midst of the faculty revolt, Mr. Schmidt promoted her to provost.

As a behavioral psychologist, Dr. Rodin did research on eating disorders, establishing a link between a mother's behavior and the daughter's. She also has studied obesity and jealousy.
