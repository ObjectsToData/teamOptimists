Campus Journal; Happy Endings in Class By Studying Cinderella


Published: August 11, 1993

Rossini composed an opera about her, Julie Andrews played her in a Rodgers and Hammerstein musical, and Jerry Lewis played a male version in a movie. She's been analyzed by Freudians and Jungians.

Cinderella might have blushed at all the attention.

But 15 elementary and high school teachers think so highly of Cinderella that they gathered at the University of Rochester recently for a six-week seminar filled with the essays, films, musicals, picture books and operas her story has inspired through the centuries.

The teachers, who met in the university library around a long table laden with books, caramel popcorn and a ceramic slipper centerpiece, spoke of using Cinderella and other fairy tales in their English classes, even organizing courses around the themes that recur in fairy tales.

"So why are we studying Cinderella?" asked Prof. Russell Peck, a medieval scholar at the university who is directing "Cinderella and the Beauty and the Beast," a seminar sponsored by the National Endowment of the Humanities. He answered his own question with a bit of autobiography -- a story with roots in the debate on American campuses over how to study art, literature and history.

A few years ago, he said, he and many colleagues found themselves under attack. Feminist and minority scholars, among others, complained that traditional scholarship overlooked their concerns, that its perspective was forbiddingly white and male.

"My ideology was attacked on all sides," Professor Peck told the teachers. "I was informed that my ideas were socially constructed. I was accused of reinscribing patriarchal ideologies, that I was a racist. This was, of course, all true. And I deconstructed all of the things I believed in."

As Professor Peck pondered how he could change his methods of scholarship and teaching, he turned to something he knew well, fairy tales.

As director of the university's 1990-91 freshman English program, he built much of the required course around Cinderella and the themes he said the story reflected, like the possibility of a poor person making good and the need for a woman to marry to rise in society.

He saw in fairy tales a way to address some of the issues roiling the nation's campuses. He reasoned that since so many societies have produced so many versions of Cinderella -- at least 2,000 by the count of some folklorists -- discussion about the different treatments of her could be valuable.

Many of the teachers saw analyzing Cinderella in the context of contemporary women's issues as a way to introduce students to those issues and the arguments they have provoked. Many young girls, they said, might be surprised that Cinderella could find satisfaction simply in marriage without a career.

Old tales can also be spun in new ways, as Marguerita Rudolph did in "The Good Stepmother," in which Cinderella insists on choosing her father's new wife in the hope of preventing abuse.

Monica Edinger, who teaches the fourth grade at the private Dalton School in Manhattan and who attended the seminar, said she used fairy tales not only to teach literature but also to illustrate how old tales are recycled in popular culture.

She instructs her students to plumb television shows for fairy tale themes. The students then compose their own Cinderella stories, often with sitcom characters. "One girl wrote that the fairy godmother gave Brenda her bikini," Ms. Edinger said, referring to the Shannen Doherty character on "Beverly Hills 90210."

"I'm not going to say sitcoms are valuable, but this is the reality; that's what people watch," Ms. Edinger said. "My main interest is to get the kids to think critically. This exercise gets to the root of why they escape into television."

Beverly Schwartzman, an elementary school teacher in San Antonio, said using African, American Indian and Mexican folk tales in her fifth-grade class had raised her pupils' interest in their schoolwork -- an achievement in itself, she said, considering the hard lives her mainly Mexican-American students face in an impoverished barrio.

"I'm not one to use textbooks," Mrs. Schwartzman said. "I'm a pretty good storyteller. What I see in fairy tales is what I call universal themes. When you have children who do not enjoy reading or cannot read well, how do you capture and keep their interest? When you tell those universal themes, it doesn't matter if the story comes from Africa or Asia."
