House Votes a Cut In Money For Arts

By WILLIAM GRIMES
Published: July 17, 1993

In a setback for the National Endowment for the Arts, a solid majority in the House of Representatives approved a motion on Thursday to cut the agency's proposed financing for next year by 5 percent, or $8.7 million. The motion, introduced by Representative Cliff Stearns, Republican of Florida, during deliberations on appropriations for the Federal arts agency, the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Institute for Museum Services, passed by a vote of 240 to 184, as moderate and conservative Democrats joined the regular, mostly Republican opponents of the agency.

If the measure passes the Senate and House-Senate conferees, it would cut the arts agency's budget for fiscal year 1994, proposed to be $174.9 million, to $166.2 million. 'Great Concern'

Officials at the arts agency, which is without a chairman, declined to comment on the vote, but in a news release expressed "great concern and disappointment."

"We are saddened over what appears to be harsh action by the Congress in singling out the arts among Federal cultural programs," the release said.

In floor debate, supporters of the Stearns motion pointed to the need for all Government agencies to accept a share of the budget cuts needed to reduce the Federal deficit.

Efforts to reach Mr. Stearns for comment were unsuccessful.

Supporters of the arts endowment said that the agency had already accepted its fair share of sacrifice. Since the early 1980's, the agency's budget has remained virtually flat. Last year, Mr. Stearns succeeded in getting $3 million cut from the budget.

"You have budget-cutting fever on the Hill," said Judith Golub, the executive director of the American Arts Alliance, which lobbies on behalf of nonprofit arts institutions. "But the N.E.A. has already taken its hit."

On Wednesday, Congress defeated an amendment introduced by Representative Philip Crane, Republican of Illinois, calling for the arts endowment to be eliminated outright. That vote was 322 to 105; last year, a similar amendment was voted down by 329 to 85. Debate Mentions Whitney

Much of the debate on the Stearns measure focused on the Whitney Museum of American Art, which the Christian Action Network, a conservative lobbying group, attacked in a letter it sent to freshman members of Congress on June 30.

The letter, which called for Congress to cut the arts agency's funds entirely, focused on two current exhibitions at the museum, "Abject Art: Repulsion and Desire in American Art" and "The Subject of Rape," whose works include depictions of excrement and genitalia. The letter asserted, incorrectly, that these were the only exhibitions at the museum.

The National Arts Endowment did not directly give money in support of either exhibition. Rather, it gave a $20,000 grant to the Whitney's Independent Study Program, whose fellows organized the shows. The grant was approved in September 1992 by the agency's acting chairwoman, Anne-Imelda Radice.

In a speech on the floor of the House, Representative Amo Houghton, Republican of upstate New York, called attacks on the Whitney a red herring. He suggested that the Stearns measure was part of a strategy of nibbling the agency down to nothing. "I get the feeling that the real thrust here is to eliminate all Federal arts funding altogether," he said. "The words aren't there, but the arithmetic is." Dollars and Cents

Members of several arts organizations said that in the end arguments about dollars and cents might have carried more weight than the kind of moral attacks that the endowment had been the object of in recent years. The budget ax is swinging, they said, and the agency, as an easy target, got sliced.

"They didn't like the Crane proposal because it's a draconian step, but the Stearns amendment gave people an out," Mr. Houghton said. "They can go back to their constituencies and say, 'We really are for cost reduction.' "

Ms. Golub said: "It's our job to tell people that the arts are part of the solution, not part of the problem. This vote gives us the incentive to get the story out about what the nonprofit arts institutions do in congressmen's districts. This gives us our marching orders."
