The Rehabbing of America

By Michael Massing;
Published: January 24, 1993

THE MAKING OF A DRUG-FREE AMERICA Programs That Work. By Mathea Falco. 254 pp. New York: Random House/Times Books. $22. RECKONING Drugs, the Cities, and the American Future. By Elliott Currie. 405 pp. New York: Hill & Wang. $25.

OVER the last 12 years, America's drug problem has erupted from a low-grade fever into a raging contagion. In 1981, when Ronald Reagan took office, the nation had about three million hard-core drug users; today, the figure is around six million. The number of deaths by drug overdose has soared, and drug-related homicides have pushed murder rates to record levels in cities across the country. All of this has occurred despite an eightfold increase in Federal antidrug spending. Clearly something has gone wrong.

Now the Democrats get their chance. During their years out of power, alternative drug strategies have piled up like Chinese menus under the door. Mathea Falco and Elliott Currie provide two new ones. Both authors criticize the Republicans for relying too heavily on law enforcement. Both reject drug legalization as too extreme an alternative. Both propose a new middle way for dealing with drugs. Otherwise, their approaches have little in common -- an indication of how sharply today's liberals can differ.

"The Making of a Drug-Free America: Programs That Work" comes with a seal of approval from the new Administration. Vice President Al Gore has publicly endorsed the book, and Ms. Falco was an adviser to Bill Clinton during the Presidential campaign. A visiting fellow at New York Hospital-Cornell Medical Center, Ms. Falco says she decided to write the book as an antidote to the widespread pessimism she has encountered about drugs. At a meeting of Midwestern business and civic leaders, she says, a newspaper publisher asked, "How can we do anything if we don't know what works? We'll just have to write off this generation until someone comes up with some answers." Determined to find them, Ms. Falco spent five years visiting schools, clinics, treatment centers and community groups all over the country. She found many programs that did seem to work, and her book provides a sort of consumer's guide for policy makers.

In Westchester County in New York, for instance, Ms. Falco came upon the county's Student Assistance Program, "one of the most promising approaches to preventing teen-age substance abuse." In the program, health professionals working full time offer students at some schools regular confidential counseling. Ms. Falco attended a session at one high school in a plush suburb. Here, beside a lake, flower beds and a parking lot filled with expensive cars, a group of ninth-grade girls discussed the "Monday morning oh-no's" -- the foolish things they did while high or drunk at weekend parties.

Jill B., as Ms. Falco calls her, a social worker trained in adolescent development, offered advice about how to resist peer pressure to drink or smoke dope at parties. "We would die without Mrs. B.," one of the girls tells Ms. Falco. "She's the only person we can talk to honestly." A 1989 study of Westchester high schools found that the rates of drinking and drug use were one-third lower among students at schools offering the program.

In an inner-city Detroit neighborhood, Ms. Falco toured streets with members of a church-led citizens' group called Reach (Reach Everyone Administer Care and Help). Going beyond the usual street patrols, Reach engaged residents in a number of volunteer programs, such as feeding the elderly, operating a day-care center and, especially, renovating houses. Using local sweat equity, Reach bought and repaired two dozen houses, some of them previously used by drug dealers. By thus galvanizing the community, Ms. Falco suggests, Reach has managed to banish open drug dealing from the area and reduce property crimes by 25 percent.

Ms. Falco is especially bullish on treatment. She writes glowingly of visits to a therapeutic community in Tucson, Ariz., an acupuncture clinic in Miami, an Alcoholics Anonymous meeting in Boston, and Matrix, a program for wealthy cocaine users in Beverly Hills, Calif. "Drug treatment remains our best hope for breaking the costly cycle of crime and addiction," she writes, "but we have not yet begun to meet the nationwide need for new programs." Noting that treatment is available for fewer than 15 percent of the nation's addicts, Ms. Falco would channel funds from prison construction and border patrols into rehabilitation centers and methadone maintenance clinics.

On this point, Ms. Falco is quite eloquent. The United States, she declares, needs a "new vision" of drug policy, one focused on "reducing demand, changing attitudes and engaging citizen participation." The question is, does her vision go far enough?

Ms. Falco, who directed the State Department's antinarcotics bureau under Jimmy Carter, foresees a very limited role for the Government. "The most promising strategies," she writes, "are coming not from Washington, but from communities working to find new solutions to their drug problems." Federal spending to combat drugs, now $12 billion a year, is quite enough, she believes.
