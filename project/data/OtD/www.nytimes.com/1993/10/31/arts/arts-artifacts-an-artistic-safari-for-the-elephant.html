ARTS/ARTIFACTS; An Artistic Safari for the Elephant

By RITA REIF
Published: October 31, 1993

The african elephant, the largest land animal, means different things to people these days, but many contemporary attitudes were anticipated in the art of sub-Saharan people. The beast's awesome size, aggressiveness, friendliness, exploitation and endangerment all come alive in "Elephant: The Animal and Its Ivory in African Art," an exhibition at the Metropolitan Museum of Art through March 27.

A potent symbol of power and royalty to Africans, the elephant has been depicted throughout the continent since antiquity on both humble and magnificent objects. The images in the show, which focus on the animal's massive size, fanlike ears, huge tusks and wrinkled trunk, take the form of sculpture, masks, headdresses, stools and the prow of a boat. Many of the works incorporate elephant ivory, bone, hair or hide.

Masks, the most creative of these pieces, range from realistic portraits with bulging eyes and swooping trunks to abstract carvings that clearly inspired Cubist artists. The most haunting is a patinaed bone mask of a human face, its eyes and mouth eerily encircled with white pigment.

The 75 objects at the Metropolitan represent a third of those in the show when it opened a year ago at the Fowler Museum of Cultural History at the University of California at Los Angeles. The show was financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities and organized by the Fowler, which produced the catalogue ($69 in cloth, $39 in paperback). The items were lent by African, American and European museums and collectors.

While leopards and antelopes have also had symbolic meaning attributed to them by Africans, "it was the elephant that captured the imagination of the West," says Kate Ezra, associate curator in the Metropolitan's department of the arts of Africa, Oceania and the Americas. In addition to their size and domination over other animals, the elephant fascinates because of similarities to human beings: its structured society is rooted in the concept of family, and it has a long life span of up to 70 years.

"And the elephant moves its trunk like a human hand," Ms. Ezra says. "It grasps, feeds itself, fights off its enemies and nurtures its young with its trunk."

This resemblance to humans is reflected in the fingerlike tips on the trunk of a metal elephant found in Nigeria, the show's most impressive sculpture. Devised sometime after the 15th century by an unknown artist, the study is among the largest castings in African art. Bulky and with overly long legs, this is a creature with charm; it has thick eyelashes and bristly hair on its ears, back and tail. "Even the toes are indicated," Ms. Ezra says.

The earliest elephants displayed are those etched on an Egyptian ivory comb, the size of a large postage stamp, from 3400 B.C.; it depicts three elephants stomping on cobras. A 10th-century bronze pendant in the shape of an elephant's head is one of the earliest such sculptures made in tropical Africa.

Far more poignant are the carvings that spiral up the sides of several tusks, a specialty of 19th-century Kongo artists in the region now known as Zaire. The richly detailed scenes document the daily life of natives and colonists, showing Africans fishing, hunting and trading or working in chains as porters; interspersed with these images are scenes showing Europeans toting rifles as they feast, hunt or beat their servants.

Other native sentiments are revealed in the carvings on ivory saltcellars, which were status symbols for Europeans. A sculpture of an African riding an elephant half his size crowns the lid of a vessel from the 15th or 16th century; it was carved in the area now known as Sierra Leone. Since elephants were not ridden in Africa, this object is all about prestige.

"The size of the carved figure accentuates the man's status," says Ms. Ezra. "He has literally twice as much power as the most powerful animal."

If one elephant was powerful, two were doubly so, at least to the Bamileke people who lived in what is now Cameroon. The twin elephants forming the base of a red-white-and-blue stool are bold geometric studies with disklike ears, curved tusks and triangular legs. Covered with brilliant red tubular beads, the stool was probably made for a 19th-century Bamileke chief.

Another 19th-century beaded elephant from Cameroon adorns a headdress worn by a Bamileke dancer. It is a puzzler.

"Almost everyone mistakes it for a mouse," Ms. Ezra says. For more than a century, she explains, Cameroon artists used a generic animal form of a humped-back figure with bent skinny legs to depict leopards, antelopes and even elephants -- sometimes with startling results. While this piece sports big round ears and a forked stick encrusted with white beads for tusks, the artist omitted the trunk and covered the body with a reddish-brown cloth and scarlet beads.
