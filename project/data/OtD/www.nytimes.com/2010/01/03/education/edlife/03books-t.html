Embracing the Marketplace

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
By PATRICIA COHEN
Published: December 29, 2009

Champions of the market can turn up in the oddest places. At the same time that bankers and businessmen are acknowledging the downsides of unregulated capitalism, college and university reformers are urging the academy to more closely embrace the marketplace.

Saving Alma Mater: A Rescue Plan for America’s Public Universities
by James C. Garland
The University of Chicago Press,
hardcover

Amid the raft of new books on the failings of higher education, some challenge the longtime separation between ivy-covered idealists and real-world demands. Scholarly disdain for getting and spending, they argue, has caused serious trouble both in the classroom and in the budget office.

In his slim book “The Marketplace of Ideas,” Louis Menand, an English professor at Harvard and a Pulitzer Prize-winning author, offers to answer a few questions about the humanities, like why professors all seem to have similar politics and why it is so difficult to implement a core curriculum.

His compact history of how the modern university developed, however, does much more: It offers a fresh perspective on familiar arguments and dogmas, including the century-old belief that a liberal arts education should be separate and distinct from baldly utilitarian concerns.

This article of faith, Mr. Menand explains, derives in part from the way reformers like Charles William Eliot, Harvard’s president, structured the modern American university system in the late 19th century. College became “the gateway to the professions.” Those who wanted to become doctors, lawyers and university professors first had to make their way through an undergraduate degree before being permitted to pursue an M.D., J.D. or Ph.D.

Allowing graduate schools to monopolize professional education compelled colleges to offer students something other than vocational training: knowledge for its own sake.

That remains a treasured goal. In September, Drew Gilpin Faust, Harvard’s current president, wrote in this newspaper: “We should remember that colleges and universities are about a great deal more than measurable utility. Unlike perhaps any other institutions in the world, they embrace the long view and nurture the kind of critical perspectives that look far beyond the present.”

Yet to Mr. Menand’s 21st-century eye, the split is nonsense. “The divorce between liberalism and professionalism as educational missions rests on a superstition: that the practical is the enemy of the true,” he writes.

The division not only marginalizes the humanities, he argues, but also contributes to the fractured nature of a college education and a nagging sense of irrelevance. Today, fewer than 10 percent of undergraduates major in one of the humanities (compared with a quarter who major in business), partly because they fear it won’t help them get a job. Unless, of course, they want to be a professor.

But even here, the system has broken down. Higher education sharply contracted once the baby-boom generation returned their caps and gowns. That has left too many Ph.D.’s chasing too few tenured slots. The recent financial crisis has made things worse by causing older professors to delay retirement and by pressuring institutions to use more part-time faculty.

Only the most devoted students are willing to engage in this keen competition, which has the effect of narrowing the range of people who decide to pursue an academic job. This is why, Mr. Menand suggests, professors tend to think alike.

Academics, he writes, “are conditioned to think that their workplace does not operate like a market, even as they compete with one another for status and advantage.”

There is no easy fix, Mr. Menand concludes. “Until professors are produced in a different way, the structure of academic knowledge production and dissemination is unlikely to change significantly.”

JAMES C. GARLAND, the former president of Miami University in Ohio, would also like the university to embrace the market, although for a different reason. Mr. Garland is concerned with putting public university systems on a solid financial footing. Although they educate 80 percent of the nation’s college students, public institutions have seen their quality sapped by shrinking government aid, changing demographics and growing income inequality. In “Saving Alma Mater,” Mr. Garland argues that government should end subsidies altogether and allow supply and demand to rule.
