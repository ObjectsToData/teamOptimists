Students Receive Fewer A's, and Princeton Calls It Progress

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By KAREN W. ARENSON
Published: September 20, 2005

Princeton University significantly reduced the number of A's it gave out last year, according to a report released yesterday by the university, but it still wants to do more.

Roughly 41 percent of the grades given in undergraduate courses last year were A-pluses, A's or A-minuses, down from 46 percent the previous year and 48 percent the year before that. In April 2004, the Princeton faculty set a goal of 35 percent. The school year that ended in May was the first year under the policy.

"We are all very proud of our faculty for committing themselves to this very bold initiative," said T. Kyle Vanderlick, the chairwoman of the chemical engineering department and a member of the committee that reported on the changes in grading. "And we were delighted with the progress we have made."

The committee, which included deans, department chairmen and other faculty members, said if there was as much change in grades in the current academic year as there had been in the last academic year, Princeton would reach its goal at the end of this school year.

Nancy Malkiel, dean of the college and one of the architects of the plan to cut grade inflation, said that reducing the number of A's was important to give students a more accurate picture of how they were doing and to inspire them to work harder.

"If we're giving them the same grades for their very best work as for their good work," she said yesterday, "we're not giving them well-calibrated guidance about the difference between very good and best, and we're not challenging them to do their very best work."

Princeton was not alone in its generous grading, she said. A Princeton survey of the eight Ivy League colleges plus Stanford, M.I.T. and the University of Chicago, made before Princeton adopted its tougher grading policy, found that A's accounted for 44 percent to 55 percent of their undergraduate grades.

She said she did not know how many other schools, if any, had adopted similar policies, but that other schools had invited her to speak about what Princeton was doing.

Ms. Malkiel said that when she made a report last spring to the deans of Ivy League colleges and several other prestigious schools, "some of them said, 'We've got to go back to work on this.' "

There had been some protest from students and faculty members when the grading policy was adopted. One-third of the faculty voted against it and some students expressed concerns that they would not be able to compete with other schools for fellowships and for admission to graduate schools and professional programs like medicine and law.

And there are still some reservations about the policy. Leslie-Bernard Joseph, president of the undergraduate student government, said yesterday that there were definitely concerns about the grading policy, and that students were studying it and planned to recommend changes.

"People have had their work graded down because of the guidelines, even though they're only supposed to be guidelines," Mr. Joseph said. "We have a lot of anecdotal evidence about this."

Before Princeton adopted the new grading policy, some of its departments were tougher than others in their grading. And some made more progress last year than others.

According to the report, the humanities departments reduced their A's last year to 46 percent from 56 percent. The social sciences cut A's to 38 percent from 43 percent. Engineering reduced A's to 43 percent from 48 percent. The natural sciences, which were already at 36 percent A's, held steady.

Ms. Malkiel declined to identify the departments that had made little progress, but said their number was "fewer than the fingers of one hand."
