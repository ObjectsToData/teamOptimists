Exhibition Review | 'Treasured Maps'

Mapping Everything From Eden to the New York Subway

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Single-Page
  • Save Article
By EDWARD ROTHSTEIN
Published: September 15, 2005

The first map in a 1857 atlas created by Edward Quin in England is dated "B.C. 2348," and it is a map that would not be made today: most of the page is taken up with the blackness of a thick cloud. At its center, the darkness is parted in a roiling circle of light. In the distance can be glimpsed a map fragment, which includes two rivers, a mountain and a lake. It is labeled Eden.

It is God's view of the new world, part of "An Atlas of Universal History." Subsequent maps include 1491 B.C. ("The Exodus of the Israelites") and A.D. 1 ("The Roman Empire in the Augustan Age"). In each the world revealed in the parted clouds expands, incorporating what has come before.

At the very end, points out Alice Hudson, the chief of the map division at the New York Public Library, and the curator of a provocative new exhibition at the Humanities and Social Sciences Library, the darkness will have receded, leaving the map of the contemporary world in illuminated clarity.

It is as if God's vision had turned into man's. And something like that happens in this show, "Treasured Maps," which, along with Quin's maps, samples the library's extraordinary collection of 400,000 maps and 20,000 atlases. The exhibition, which will be on view through April 9, anticipates the scheduled December reopening of the renamed and renovated Lionel Pincus and Princess Firyal Map Division. The displays are roughly historical, beginning with 17th- and 18th-century maps, in which images of a divine presence are commonplace, and ends with New York subway maps in which cosmic order lies in the hands of train dispatchers.

The sampling is dizzying, and, in fact, the exhibition suffers a bit from its miscellany. Its more than 80 objects include a 1705 sea chart in which the scientist Edmond Halley recorded compass readings across the globe and a 1957 insurance map of Lower Manhattan showing ownership of the land on which the World Trade Center was later built - a map used after 9/11 to trace the site's archaeology.

Yet the maps' variety, their changes in style and in purpose, also reveal the shared origins of items now considered commonplace. Creating such maps once demanded great costs. One 1784 map shows the courses of James Cook's first two global journeys, the vast oceans crossed by fragile-colored lines marking his voyages. (On the third he was killed.)

Risks were accepted because of the knowledge that maps promised. Maps not only told one where one stood, but defined the importance of that place. In recent years maps have often been criticized because they do not, as was once believed, present God's truth, but a human truth reflecting the perspectives of their makers. This makes them still more revelatory: they record not just how much is known about the world, but what is considered important to know.

Thus a 1672 Dutch map of the Mediterranean by Pieter Goos is unconcerned with the blank expanses of land beyond the coast, but is meticulous about naming the ports lining this most ancient of sea-faring regions. A 1664 map of the Danube by Joan Blaeu makes the 1,750 miles of that river into an epic terrain spread over two folio pages, an image reflecting its importance in trade and battles between East and West.

One 17th-century star map pictures the constellations as fully realized creatures - a crab, a swan - in the heavens. In a way, this is what all maps do: organizing the world, shaping vast seas and land masses into conceptual images of hemispheres and continents punctuated with decorative cartouches and fleurs-de-lis. Those shapes reflect habits of mind and material interests.

But those interests are not always what we might assume. Recent attempts to discern the meanings of maps have sometimes overlaid a contemporary political grid, demonstrating that explorers' maps, say, reflected imperialist ambitions. Yet an 18th-century map of India and an 1813 map of Africa testify to not mere control, but to attempts by Western travelers to take into account the great variety of these lands and their kingdoms.
