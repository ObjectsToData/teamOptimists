More Buyers Are Thinking Small

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Single-Page
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By PATRICK O'GILFOIL HEALY
Published: November 13, 2005

IS the safest bet in New York real estate also the smallest one?

Years ago, studios and one-bedrooms were the dingy wares at the bottom of Manhattan's property bin. Brokers disdained the tiny commissions their cheap prices brought. Common wisdom dictated that small apartments were poor investments.

But recent market reports show that studios and one-bedroom apartments are increasing in price, while the values of larger apartments are not keeping pace.

Studios and one-bedrooms made up 55 percent of all sales from July to September - the most recent quarter - compared with a market share of 50 percent last spring. Their average values climbed 11 percent, while prices on larger apartments fell an average of 16 percent.

Market data show that the average studio in Manhattan now costs $428,831, and the average one-bedroom is $687,744. In some neighborhoods, studios are more expensive, per square foot, than the average for all apartments. The most recent median price data was not available for different sizes of apartments.

"I've seen a huge increase in the studios, and it's because of the first-time buyer," said Vickey Barron, an agent with Prudential Douglas Elliman. "A few years ago people would have waited. They would have passed on the studio and pounced on the one-bedroom. Now the one-beds are priced out of their range. They're coming out of the woodwork."

The shift has surprised some brokers, who remember when small apartments languished on the market.

"When I first started in the business 10 years ago, most brokers wouldn't even handle one-bedrooms," said Denice Rich, a broker with the Corcoran Group, because there was too much supply, and movement was slow. "I was the only one who would take the sale."

Of course, this sector hasn't become magically inoculated against slow spells and price drops. But real estate brokers and analysts say small apartments are now more resilient. For one, construction in Manhattan is geared toward sprawling luxury and not modest affordability, limiting the number of new one-bedrooms and studios entering the market.

Because they are cheaper to buy and maintain, small apartments also make natural second homes or pieds-à-terre for commuters or suburbanites tiptoeing into the city, according to Jonathan J. Miller, president of the appraisal firm Miller Samuel. And they are often easier to rent, and draw high rents relative to an owner's mortgage burden, making them more profitable investments for buyers looking for rental income.

The buyers buoying this wedge of the market are largely students and young professionals planting a foot in Manhattan, rather than families of four squeezing into a too-small home, brokers say. On the low end of the scale, they are singles looking for the cheapest studio available.

On the higher end, they are buyers who could have afforded a two-bedroom in 2000 but now must settle for a large one-bedroom.

Typical of the young buyers is Laura Cave, 24, who bought a 450-square-foot studio at 20th Street and Seventh Avenue after moving from California to study graduate-level humanities at New York University. As with many younger buyers in the studio market, Ms. Cave's parents financed the purchase, and she pays rent to them. The apartment is in all their names.

Because Ms. Cave plans to stay in New York after she graduates, her family decided that buying a small place would be a smart investment. They wanted an apartment near Gramercy, Murray Hill or Chelsea that could be renovated to increase its value.

Inventory analyses show that about 140 studios a month are arriving on the market this year, about the same rate as last year. Many of those were beyond Ms. Cave's grasp, or below her standards. The cheaper ones - around $300,000 to $350,000 - were tiny and shabby, with slanted floors and bad lighting. Other buildings had income requirements or policies against parental buyers.

"We didn't really have a lot of options because our price range was really low," Ms. Cave said. "We were like, anything that wasn't a hovel, we'd look at. It was definitely a shock coming from L.A."

Eventually, they found the studio on 20th Street, a clean and bright space with a sunken living area, separate kitchen and enough room for a full-sized bed and bulging sofa.
