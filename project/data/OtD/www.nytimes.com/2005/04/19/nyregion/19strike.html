Yale and Columbia Graduate Students Push for Union

 By MAREK FUCHS

Published: April 19, 2005

n the shadow of an oversize inflated rat, graduate assistants at Columbia University marched in an oval yesterday to begin a five-day strike to support their efforts to form a union and win better pay and benefits.

At Yale University in New Haven, graduate assistants were doing the same, refusing to teach undergraduate classes, write problem sets or conduct statistical research.

The strikers face a difficult challenge, however. In July 2004, the National Labor Relations Board ruled that teaching assistants are students, not workers, and cannot force universities to recognize their union through the regular organizing process. The only way students can win union recognition is to persuade the universities to grant it voluntarily.

University administrators agree with the ruling, said Alissa Kaplan Michaels, a Columbia spokeswoman, describing the relationship with graduate students there as one of education and collaboration. "Teaching is an integral part of preparing for a teaching career," she said.

At Yale, a spokesman, Tom Conroy, echoed that view.

Yale "doesn't believe a graduate student union would improve the educational environment," he said. "At the end of the week, the university will believe what it believed at the beginning of the week."

Rachel Sulkes, 31, the spokeswoman for the strikers at Yale, said that the striking graduate students at the university did not seek the participation of union members on campus.

On the picket line at Columbia, where doctoral candidates pounded on drums and empty tubs, there was talk of long hours for pay that amounted to about $20,000 a year, with limited health care coverage.

"The health plan right now is don't get sick," said Stephen Twilley, 31, a picket captain who is pursuing a doctorate at Columbia in Italian and comparative literature. He marched with a sign saying, "In Sciopero," explaining that the Italian phrase referred more to the ideology behind the opposition, and less to the physical act of striking.

"It's much more of a state of mind, which is how I feel," he said.

Mr. Twilley pointed to successful efforts to unionize graduate assistants - who, he said, had an increasing burden of teaching and research - at places like New York University, though the efforts took place before the labor board's ruling.

Ms. Michaels said that as of late yesterday afternoon, Columbia's campus was running smoothly, and since graduate assistants taught classes at different times, there was no immediate gauge of how disruptive the strike might be.

Greg Klochkoff, a Columbia freshman, said that his humanities class, which is taught by a graduate assistant, met in Riverside Park so students would not have to cross a picket line. Mr. Conroy, the Yale spokesman, said that professors or nonstriking graduate students were taking up the slack, adding that no classes had to be canceled yesterday and that no campus activities were disrupted by the strike. But one Yale graduate student, Melissa Stuckey, who is studying history, said the section of a course in American studies that she teaches was canceled.

Helping to organize the strike yesterday was David Carpio, 29, who was studying for a doctorate in biology at Columbia before leaving to work for the United Automobile Workers to fight for better pay and working conditions for graduate assistants. He worked on the strike with Phil Wheeler, a regional director of the U.A.W., which the graduate students want to officially join.

"It's a great liberal institution," Mr. Wheeler said of Columbia, "but that's in what they teach. They practice something else."

Maida Rosenstein, president of Local 2110 of the U.A.W., which represents clerical workers at Columbia, said that the university supports the Republican-appointed N.L.R.B. when its rulings save them money.

Students at Yale want to become fully recognized members of Unite Here, a textile, hotel and restaurant workers' union.

Alan Brinkley, the provost of Columbia, and Chris Colombo, the dean of student affairs, wrote letters to students alerting them to the strike and calling for calm and for minimum disruption.

Avi Salzman contributed reporting for this article.
