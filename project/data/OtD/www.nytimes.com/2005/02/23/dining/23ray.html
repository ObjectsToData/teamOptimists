A Gift to Black Cuisine, From Ray Charles

 By MIMI READ

EW ORLEANS

RAY CHARLES was a red beans and rice man. When he came here in May 2003 to pick up an honorary degree from Dillard University, his dense schedule didn't stop him from calling Dooky Chase's, the Creole restaurant he sang about in "Early in the Morning Blues," where he'd eaten his favorite dish ever since he lived in New Orleans in the 1950's.

"He did send for his dinner - red beans and rice and fried chicken," said Leah Chase, the restaurant's well-known white-haired chef.

Now there is a more enduring connection between the degree and the dish. Six months later Charles donated $1 million to Dillard, a historically black private institution with 2,100 students, to create a chair in black culinary studies and other aspects of black culture. And less than a year after his death last June, Dillard has begun advertising the position in The Chronicle of Higher Education. The university says the job will be, in effect, the first professorship in African-American culinary history in the country.

"There are barely programs in food studies, let alone any kind of endowed specialized chair," said Amy Bentley, a culinary historian at New York University, one of the few places, including Boston University, offering master's degrees in food studies.

In the past few years there has been a rustle of academic activity focusing on food. The University of Michigan at Ann Arbor, for example, is creating one of the country's largest culinary research centers. It will house more than 20,000 books and objects, including menus, maps, photographs and magazines.

"We have particularly good African-American material here," said Jan Longone, curator of American culinary history at the university's Clements Library.

Still, most food scholars are working from the periphery, ensconced in English, history, anthropology and geography departments. Ms. Bentley said the Dillard position signals the coming of age of food studies and "the real contributions that Africans and Afro-Americans have made to American culture and particularly Southern culture."

No one has applied yet, said Danille Taylor, dean of humanities at Dillard, who added last week, "The position is open." She had previously said the university may take more than a year to fill the post.

But the name on the tip of several insiders' tongues is Jessica Harris, the African-American cookbook writer, culinary historian and journalist now teaching in the English department at Queens College. A former travel editor of Essence magazine who has peeked into pots on every habitable continent, Ms. Harris is an expert in West African foodways and how they have influenced cooking worldwide. She declined to comment on whether she would seek the Dillard job.

But she did deliver Dillard's first Ray Charles memorial lecture last October, underscoring the African influence on local cuisine and positing an image of New Orleans as the "pivot point" between the American South and the Caribbean.

Michael Lomax, now the director of the United Negro College Fund, was Dillard's president at the time of Charles's gift. He had been watching as Charles made donations to other black colleges, hoping that such munificence would flow to Dillard. In 2002 Mr. Lomax met Joe Adams, Charles's business manager, at a dinner at Morehouse College, and told him that Dillard wished to recognize Charles.

After receiving the degree Charles sent the university a check for $1 million.

"He talked about traditions of food preparation in the black community that were really a kind of art, that his family had been a part of," Mr. Lomax said. "He wanted to honor his mother, his grandmother and those who had a collective memory of Africa and coming to the New World and creating a cuisine. From his point of view, their knowledge needed to be understood, preserved and transmitted to another generation."

Mr. Lomax said he convinced Charles to broaden the focus of the chair to black material culture, the interdisciplinary field that studies foodways, buildings, music and other cultural artifacts in order to shed light on history, politics, race, gender and class.

John T. Edge, a food writer and the director of the Southern Foodways Alliance in Oxford, Miss., said Charles's gift is "a beautiful thing" in the face of fast foods and lost food customs. "There's a lot of people wringing their hands and gnashing their teeth over these things," he said. "I'm one of the hand-wringing, teeth-gnashing crowd. But here in one fell swoop Ray Charles does something."
