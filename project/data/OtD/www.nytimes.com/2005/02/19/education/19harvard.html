Rift Deepens as Professors at Harvard See Remarks

 By SARA RIMER

Published: February 19, 2005

Correction Appended

CAMBRIDGE, Mass., Feb. 18 - A mood of uncertainty settled over Harvard University on Friday in the aftermath of President Lawrence H. Summers's release of the transcript of his contentious remarks last month about the shortage of women in the sciences and engineering.

Many people were parsing and debating the often dense and rambling 7,000-word transcript, a long-awaited document that quickly became a must-read on campus.

A group of professors and students was collecting names on letters supporting Dr. Summers. Another group of professors, who had criticized Dr. Summers's leadership style at a tense faculty meeting on Tuesday, was continuing to discuss the matter.

Scholars normally immersed in research and teaching were preoccupied with questions about the leadership of the university, to which many of them have devoted their adult lives.

"My sense of the mood among key faculty is that they're deeply, deeply distressed about the way this entire thing is tearing the university apart, the Faculty of Arts and Sciences in particular," said Theda Skocpol, a government professor who spoke out against Dr. Summers at the meeting. "And I share that distress."

Daniel Laibson and Claudia Goldin, both economics professors, were among those leading the effort to collect the signatures of full professors throughout the university on a letter endorsing Dr. Summers's leadership.

"Everyone has a viewpoint," Professor Laibson said. "At this moment there's a great deal of intensity. All of us care passionately about this university"

Professor Laibson estimated that 160 professors had signed the letter so far, with many of them representing the sciences, he said. Dr. Summers has made the sciences a major focus of his administration.

An emergency meeting of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, which is considered the heart of the university, has been scheduled for Tuesday to continue the discussion of Dr. Summers's leadership.

Despite talk of a no-confidence vote, professors said such a vote would be highly unlikely at the meeting because so many had signed up to speak. As a practical matter, they said, the earliest date for such a vote would be March 15, at the regular meeting of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences.

Despite the concerns voiced at last Tuesday's meeting about what some faculty members described as Dr. Summers's intimidating style, there has been no organized demand for his resignation. On Thursday, James Houghton, a senior member of the Harvard Corporation, which appointed the president and is the only body that can remove him, affirmed the corporation's support for Dr. Summers, saying he had "brought energetic leadership to an unusually complex and demanding job."

The letter of support circulating among the faculty emphasizes Dr. Summers's strengths and the achievements of his three-and-a-half-year administration. "He devotes himself to finding and implementing policies that enrich the minds of Harvard's students and extend the frontiers of human understanding," it reads in part.

The letter also acknowledges that Dr. Summers "has made mistakes," and adds, "We agree that his personal interactions sometimes feel confrontational."

The debate over Dr. Summers's leadership has now extended to the professional schools. At the medical school, Dr. Nicholas A. Christakis, a physician who is a professor of health care policy, said he spent a day studying Dr. Summers's remarks and mulling over the letter of support before signing it.

"I'm sure hundreds of hours of expensive faculty time have been spent in the last two days in deciding what to do about this matter," Dr. Christakis said.

He added that he believed that Dr. Summers had been vilified and said he did not think Dr. Summers's remarks about women were "unreasonable."

Eric Chivian, an assistant clinical professor of psychiatry at the medical school who shared in the 1985 Nobel Peace Prize, said he did not sign the letter. Instead, Dr. Chivian said, he reluctantly wrote his own letter to the Harvard Corporation.

The letter criticizes what Dr. Chivian calls Dr. Summers's pattern of insensitive, divisive comments and describes reports from numerous faculty members of "Dr. Summers' reputation for abusing his position of power and for vindictiveness towards those who disagree with him."

Noting that he is an alumnus of both the college and the medical school, Dr. Chivian wrote that the recent remarks about women "should finally convince the corporation that President Summers has brought, and will continue to bring, embarrassment and dishonor to the university."

He also wrote that many alumni, himself included, "will no longer contribute money to the university as long as he is its president."

Dr. Chivian said in an interview that "it should be Harvard's extraordinary accomplishments in medicine, science, the humanities and in other disciplines that are daily front page news, not its crisis of governance, brought on by Lawrence Summer's failure of leadership."

"For the good of the university," he added, "it is time for him to resign."

For now, the campus is anticipating Tuesday's meeting. So many professors have expressed an interest in attending that the meeting has been moved to a larger room.

Correction Monday, Feb. 21, 2005
An article on Saturday about the divisions at Harvard over remarks by its president, Lawrence H. Summers, misstated the given name of an economics professor who is seeking signatures on a letter supporting Dr. Summers. He is David Laibson, not Daniel.
