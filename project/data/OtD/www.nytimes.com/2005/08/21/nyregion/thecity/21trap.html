The Best People-Watching the Sky Has to Offer

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By LILY KOPPEL
Published: August 21, 2005

ATOP a 40-foot ladder, the next New Yorker is poised to take flight. A daring young woman in black stretch pants and white socks, her ponytail swinging and her back arched, takes the trapeze bar and dives into the air.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Frances Roberts for The New York Times

On the sidelines at the trapeze school made famous by Sarah Jessica Parker, everyone's a critic.

A makeshift audience of about 30, a typical turnout for a pleasant weekend day, watches from the cement ledge that runs along the river below Canal Street between Piers 34 and 26, their backs to the bobbing kayaks and the hot pink sunset.

Passers-by are mesmerized, as if the figure were outlined in a spangled costume with fishnets and satin slippers. It doesn't matter that there is no formal "ta-da" moment. A little girl jumps and skips, imitating the woman's movements.

The New York Trapeze School in Hudson River Park, hung with trapezes, pulley systems and safety nets, takes up an area no bigger than a dog run. Its slim precincts, where Sarah Jessica Parker overcame her fear of flying on "Sex and the City," offer panoramic views of the Statue of Liberty, Ellis Island, the World Financial Center. But people don't come to look at the cityscape.

"It's like free entertainment," explained Erik Yesson, there on a recent Sunday with his wife and two young sons, who shouted out directions at the fliers like small ringmasters.

As students soar and swing, the crowd claps and cheers, shifting around to get a better view. And they aren't shy about critiquing novice acrobats. "Get that pelvis up!" yelled Lisa Isenstead, a former modern dancer who teaches humanities at the Manhattan School of Music. "Sometimes I bring my camera," she added. "How can I capture how I feel when I look at them? That feeling of hanging, spinning, dancing."

Truck drivers zooming along the West Side Highway throw out the occasional catcall toward the swooping figures, most of whom are women. But people are generally encouraging, said Manny Martinez, a trapeze instructor. "They start to worry about those that can't do it," he said. "When they finally do, they get a standing ovation."

As another trapeze artist swung across the sky, Dawn Daloisio watched intently through her oversized, Felliniesque sunglasses. She had turned the evening into an outing with friends and expressed a bit of awe at the intrepid fliers. "It's almost surreal," she sighed.

In a city rigged with insecurities, the school is a circus for the mind, a fanciful place to escape from the city's crowds and loneliness. "You are flying in New York City," said Jennifer Englert, who wants to be writer and recently wrote a poem about the trapeze. "It's your show."
