Report Says Teacher, Out Sick, Led Orchestra

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By SUSAN SAULNY
Published: August 18, 2005

In the rarefied field of orchestral conducting, she is noted for her command of Scandinavian masterworks. But in a report issued yesterday by the special commissioner of investigation for New York City public schools, the maestro is noted only as a music teacher who should be fired for repeatedly calling in sick, then heading to the concert stage.

An investigation into the teacher and conductor, Dorrit Matson, began in January, after administrators at the High School for the Humanities in Lower Manhattan realized - in large part because of a live radio broadcast and Internet postings - that Ms. Matson was conducting after calling in sick, often for days at a time.

She is one of several teachers to be accused in the last few months of using sick days to work a second job or to pursue hobbies. In April, a social studies teacher in Queens, Matthew Kaye, resigned after it became clear he had used sick days to perform as a professional wrestler. Teachers get 10 sick days a year; 3 of those can be used for personal business.

"This abuse of sick leave is completely unacceptable, and we will seek her termination" said Michael Best, the head lawyer for the Department of Education, speaking of Ms. Matson.

A native of Copenhagen, Ms. Matson began working in the city schools in 1992, four years after she founded the New York Scandia Symphony, of which she is the conductor and music director. She has been on unpaid leave since February, at her request, according to a Department of Education spokeswoman, Margie Feinberg.

Ms. Matson's classroom performance came under increased scrutiny in November when she called in sick for one week, the report said. During the week, the principal of the High School for the Humanities, John Angelet, was listening to public radio and heard Ms. Matson introduced during a concert broadcast from Trinity Church.

Mr. Angelet called her. "After a period of silence," the report said, "Matson asked the principal to deduct the days from her pay and then inquired whether Angelet 'knew what it was like to conduct a concert ill.' " Officials also checked the symphony's Web site to confirm concert dates and to determine whether Ms. Matson had conducted on days when she had called in sick.

Ms. Matson sought an unpaid leave in February because she was suffering from chronic fatigue syndrome brought on by a strained relationship with school administrators, according to a private doctor who treated her and who was quoted in the report. But she could still conduct if she wanted to, the doctor told investigators, "because she would be away from the environment which caused her the stress that resulted in the onset of the condition."

Ms. Matson did not return a message left yesterday on her home answering machine.
