orticultural Lectures
New York School of Interior Design, 170 East 70th Street; (718) 549-3200, extension 216.
The 2005 Wave Hill horticultural lecture series will start on Jan. 26 with Edwina von Gal, a New York landscape designer known for her minimalist designs. Ms. von Gal is designing the botanical park for Panama: Bridge of Life, Museum of Biodiversity in Panama, with the architect Frank Gehry.

The series will continue with Pearl Fryar, a self-taught topiary gardener who has created a whimsical sculpture garden in his three-acre yard in Bishopville, S.C., on Feb. 23. The final talk will be by Eliot Coleman, a farmer and educator who has developed techniques for four-season organic growing, on March 23.

Programs begin at 6:30 p.m. Reservations are recommended. The fee for the series is $50; single tickets, if available, $20.

Antiques and Art
Seventh Regiment Armory, 643 Park Avenue (66th Street); (212) 691-7297.
An antiques and fine art show by 100 dealers of items like Art Deco furniture, Scandinavian art glass and antique Chinese pottery, will open with a preview tonight from 6 to 9. Tickets, which include readmission through Sunday, $35.

The show is open tomorrow and Saturday, from 1 to 9 p.m., and Sunday, from 1 to 6 p.m. Admission: $12.

Gardens, History and Art
Christie's, 20 Rockefeller Plaza (49th Street); (718) 817-8747.
The New York Botanical Garden and the Bard Graduate Center for Studies in the Decorative Arts, Design and Culture will offer a four-lecture series about historic landscape design. The first program, on Tuesday, will feature Erik de Jong, a professor of garden history at Bard. He will speak about how gardens historically developed as symbols of renewal and social equality.

On Feb. 15, Elizabeth Barlow Rogers, the director of garden history and landscape studies at Bard, will talk about how Italy's historic estate gardens influenced American design. Flowers in the 18th-century designs of William Hamilton will be the topic on March 8, in a talk by Mark Laird, a senior lecturer in the history of landscape architecture at the Harvard Design School.

The final program, on March 29, will be about the work and writings of Andrew Jackson Downing in the Hudson Valley in the 1800's. The speaker will be David Schuyler, a professor of the humanities and American studies at Franklin & Marshall College in Lancaster, Pa., and a biographer of Downing.

The lectures are from 6:30 to 7:30 p.m. Reservations are required. The fee for the series is $90. Tickets for single lectures, if available, $25.

Inspired by a Garden
Wave Hill, Independence Avenue and West 249th Street, Riverdale, the Bronx; (718) 549-3200.

"Nature Might Have Been Responsible," an exhibition of textiles and digitally produced collages by Amy Helfand, an artist, will show in Wave Hill House Gallery through Feb. 28. The works combine elements of plants and architecture with an image drawn from a map of Wave Hill's grounds. Open Tuesday through Sunday, 10 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. Admission to Wave Hill is free from December through February.
