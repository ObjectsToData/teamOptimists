André Carrilho

he Author Tour! It killed Dickens. It wore out Mark Twain. It filled a stadium for T. S. Eliot. Philip Roth has not done it in recent memory. Thomas Pynchon never does it. Jane Austen and Emily Dickinson were never asked to do it. James Joyce, propagandist of silence, exile and cunning, would have declined to do it.

Thirty-eight years after the publication of my first novel, I (hereinafter to be referred to reticently, humbly, as Author) did it. What made it happen? A change of publishers; a munificent proposal to go on the road, all expenses paid; a lightning revelation -- enough of silence and exile! What, after all, have silence and exile ever done for Author but get her scorned as midlist, damned as a writer's writer, omitted between ''Oates'' and ''Paley'' on Barnes & Noble's shelves? As for cunning -- ah, let Author grasp this at last! Does Author, with all her white hairs, mean to kowtow forever before the footstool of Art? Or languish eternally as No. 543,972 on Amazon?

Reader, it was cunning -- or call it the illusory guile of hope -- that led Author to light out for the territories, where thousands of would-be book buyers slumber, waiting only to be tapped awake by the charismatic touch of Author Herself.

But halt. Three paragraphs in, and Author, supposedly an old hand by now, has not remembered to give the name of the novel that stormed the gates of city after city. Instead, Author will consult her notebook and recount stuttering recollections of interviews, photographers, trains, planes, limos, taxis, escorts, greeters, platforms, bookstores and diligent behind-the-scenes planners and facilitators, all requisite for the arousal of the gargantuan Public. Viz:

Early August 2004. Photo shoots. One month before publication day. Metaphysically speaking, tour has already begun, though Author, like George Eliot welcoming Henry James to her Sunday afternoon salon, will receive at home. Preparing for photographers' visits, Author dusts furniture, places copies of new novel in conspicuous positions, tries on various outfits with slimming effects in mind. Because days are hot, Author decides on homely toe-disclosing sandals. Outdoors, Author is shot hanging over railings, posed against trees. Inside, lamps and chairs trade places, obedient Author is shot standing, sitting, turning to fall of light to display eye-bags, grooves, jowls, worn drama of aging writer. Artist-photographer comes lugging huge archaic mahogany box on tripod, ducks head under black velvet hood: Lewis Carroll ogling Alice Liddell.

When pictures appear, only unlucky sandals draw comments. ''My grandmother has those exact same shoes,'' Author is repeatedly informed.

Mid-August 2004. Press interviews, mainly by telephone. Author is pleased: she can be invisible in ragged pajamas while literarily pontificating. ''What do you think,'' interviewer asks, ''of Philip Roth's new novel?'' Author excitedly reports she has read it, it swept her away! Author and interviewer engage in extended discussion of genius of Philip Roth. Later it occurs to Author she has mostly forgotten to speak of her own new novel -- did she even remember to say its name? Call from pundit-interviewer: ''What do you think,'' pundit asks, ''of the war on terror?'' Author listens politely while pundit exhaustively analyzes world situation, Hollywood, psychosexuality of aggressive behavior, lowering of moral standards. Call from fervent young journalist from Texas. Texas! Texas has heard of Author's new book! Author is impressed, begins to feel important.

Early September 2004. Amazing, stupendous, colossal promotion opportunity! Morning television show, viewed by millions, selects Author's novel for monthly book interview, invites Author for three-minute appearance. Not, however, under Author's own steam: format is for best-selling writer to choose Cinderella-writer for exposure to vast new readership. If Author can pull this off, she may turn out to be best seller herself! But can Author pull it off? What if cat gets her tongue?

Sept. 14, 2004. Author meets media coach. Apprehensive, Author arrives at media coach's Midtown Manhattan studio for practice session. Ignorant Author does not know what media coach is or does. But media coach is brilliant! Perceptive! Reassuring! Impersonates every possible species of television interviewer: breezy; bright; bored; brazen; stupid; intense; indifferent. Getting the hang of it, Author throws out pithy, best-sellerish responses, masters technique. Thanks to media coach, Author is now ready to conquer audience of millions.
