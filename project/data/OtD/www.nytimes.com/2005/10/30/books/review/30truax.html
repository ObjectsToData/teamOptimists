'Truth and Consequences': Suffering Fools

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Single-Page
  • Save Article
By ALICE TRUAX
Published: October 30, 2005

At the end of Alison Lurie's first novel, "Love and Friendship" (1962), two mothers are observing a rowdy game of blindman's buff in which the children are continually shrieking and colliding with one another, falling on the grass singly or in pairs. Then one of mothers realizes something is not right: all of the children are blindfolded. "They like it better that way," the other replies.

TRUTH AND CONSEQUENCES
By Alison Lurie.
232 pp. Viking. $24.95.

Forum: Book News and Reviews

Over the course of Lurie's distinguished career as a comic novelist, this metaphor has become a familiar one. As her characters blunder in and out of sexual and domestic arrangements, willfully blind to the lower-case havoc they are causing, we stand beside the author, amused and knowing, trying to suppress those unsettling flashes - an elbow here, a sneaker there - in which we recognize pieces of ourselves.

Occasionally a blindfold slips, however, and a protagonist will register that a major change has taken place in one of his or her intimates. Indeed, disconcerting transmogrifications are one of the author's specialties: a little girl mistakes her licentious father for a vampire; a pretty faculty wife is confronted in the mirror by an aging hag; a teenager dressing for Halloween is suddenly revealed to her father as the hideous witch she's become. "Truth and Consequences," Lurie's new novel, opens with just such a moment of estrangement, when Jane Mackenzie looks up from her vegetable garden and fails to recognize the man stepping out of a taxi at the end of her driveway:

"There was something about him that made her feel uneasy and a little frightened. He reminded her of other unwelcome figures: a property tax inspector who had appeared at the door soon after they moved into the house; an F.B.I. official who was investigating one of Alan's former students; and the scruffy-looking guy who one summer two years ago used to stand just down the road where the ramp to the highway began, waving at passing cars and asking for a lift downtown. If you agreed, before he got out he would lean over the seat and in a half-whiny, half-threatening way ask for the 'loan' of a couple of dollars."

But no, it is simply her husband, who has returned early from his office at Corinth University because - as usual - he is in excruciating pain.

Alan Mackenzie's transformation from a smart, sexy and academically successful architectural historian to a crippled whiner with a craving for junk food was not, in fact, instantaneous. Fifteen months ago, during a casual game of volleyball, he injured his back, and the exhausting round of unsuccessful therapies - exercise, drugs, acupuncture, surgery - has gradually eroded his gratitude and good humor. Once energetic both in and out of bed, he is now practically immobilized and heavily dependent on his wife. An expert on architectural ruins, he himself has become a ruin: the two follies he had built on his own property only serve to underscore his former hubris. Now, awash in a sea of medications and side effects and angered by his wife's solicitude and continued good health, Alan secretly believes his life is over. The university has awarded him a yearlong fellowship at the Matthew Unger Center for the Humanities, over which Jane presides, but he is too filled with self-loathing to care. Then he meets the Unger Center's current celebrity fellow, Delia Delaney.

Delia, who resembles a fugitive from a Botticelli painting, is a well-known author of fairy tales inspired by her rural Southern childhood. She floats through the Unger Center on a cloud of ruffles and lavender gauze, commandeering furniture and leaving the scent of orange peel in her wake. She writes in longhand, because "writing is a sensual act," and her work means everything to her. Unfortunately, she is frequently assaulted by migraines, whereupon she must lie down in the dark on her borrowed sofa, waiting for the pain to pass. She immediately recognizes Alan as a kindred spirit. Of course their spouses don't suffer, she explains to him, combing her lovely fingers through her red-gold mane of hair: they aren't creative people. She demands to see his follies, and then his sketches of possible future projects. Alan also learns that because of a vaguely alluded-to gothic trauma involving a flashlight, Delia doesn't like sexual intercourse (which he can no longer perform), but that she's avidly interested in everything that goes along with it.
