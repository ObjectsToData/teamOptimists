Under Siege on Schools, Mayor Pledges New Programs

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By DAVID M. HERSZENHORN
Published: October 22, 2005

In a campaign announcement timed to coincide with the admissions exam for New York City's specialized high schools - a nail-biting experience for nearly 30,000 children and their parents - Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg pledged yesterday to create a constellation of new high school programs, including seven new schools for top students.

Mr. Bloomberg said that the seven schools would be created by 2009 and that some would use the same entrance exam as the existing specialized schools, which include Stuyvesant High and the Bronx High School of Science. Those not using the test would use other criteria based on grades and test scores, he said.

One of the schools, for grades 6 through 12, would be affiliated with Columbia University and would be on its proposed Manhattanville campus, north of 125th Street. In a noontime speech at Columbia, the mayor said that if he won re-election he would also create 15 new "transfer" schools for students failing in traditional schools, five evening schools for students with daytime obligations, 20 job counseling sites and 10 new programs to prepare students for equivalency degrees.

That portion of the mayor's speech was clearly intended to respond to allegations by his opponent in the mayoral race, Fernando Ferrer, that his administration has not done enough to improve the city's high school graduation rate. And the Ferrer campaign issued a sniping comment yesterday accusing the mayor of coming late to the issue.

"Earlier this week, Mike Bloomberg discovered there was a 'crisis of affordability' in New York," said Christy Setzer, a spokeswoman for the Ferrer campaign. "Today he's discovered that there's a dropout crisis. We're glad he's been paying attention to Freddy's speeches. We just wish he would have learned a little more about the city he leads four years ago."

Mr. Ferrer's campaign got a boost yesterday from Senator Hillary Rodham Clinton, who appeared at a fund-raiser for him and criticized Republicans - though she did not single out Mr. Bloomberg by name.

The appearance with Mrs. Clinton ended a day that began with a fizzle for Mr. Ferrer when he bowed out of delivering a speech about the importance of small businesses at a conference organized by a group of black churches. The morning session was so delayed, campaign aides said, that Mr. Ferrer had to leave, although the campaign later released the speech he had planned to give.

By contrast, Mr. Bloomberg's announcement of new, selective schools - a prospect particularly appealing to middle-class voters - was timed perfectly to the start of the frenzied high school admissions process.

"Tomorrow the first round of city students will take the specialized high school admissions test, and I wish all of them luck," Mr. Bloomberg said. "But every year these schools are forced to turn away thousands of students who perform well on these tests. In fact last year, more than 26,500 students took the test, but only 4,100 were accepted."

He noted that the current acceptance rate, about 15 percent, made the city's specialized schools as competitive as Ivy League colleges. But while there is huge demand among city parents for better high schools, some analysts say it would be better to improve general high schools so they meet all students' needs.

Dona Matthews, the director of the Hunter College Center for Gifted Studies and Education, praised the mayor's plan but said that selective schools were more "triage" for top students than a long-term solution.

"Unless we are addressing some kind of special learning needs, I find the whole enterprise very questionable," she said. "If all we are doing is creaming the kids off of the top and giving them a better education with each other, that's hard to argue for."

The mayor did not take questions after his speech, and few details were made available later. Mr. Bloomberg did not say, for instance, how much the initiatives would cost, but his campaign later estimated that they would eventually cost $100 million a year, paid for mostly out of the existing schools budget.

The mayor said two of the seven gifted high schools were already being planned, the Columbia Math and Science school, and a humanities school in Brooklyn.

The Columbia school is scheduled to open in September 2007 and will take a total of 650 students. Half of them are expected to come from Manhattan north of 96th Street.

Mr. Bloomberg said he hoped to put the schools in poor, heavily minority neighborhoods that historically had not had high-performing schools. Aides said that the schools would ultimately serve 4,000 students and that about half of those seats would be filled using the exam.

Former Schools Chancellor Harold O. Levy, now the executive vice president of Kaplan Inc., the educational services company, praised the mayor's announcement, saying, "The opportunity to send your child to a specialized high school is one of the great gifts of the City of New York."

In 2002, Mr. Levy opened three new specialized schools: a math and engineering school at City College, a school for American Studies at Lehman College in the Bronx and a science school on the campus of York College in Jamaica, Queens. This year, Mr. Bloomberg announced that Staten Island Technical High School would become the seventh school to use the specialized exam.

Brian Jetter, the principal of the Queens High School for Sciences at York, said the school originally planned to have 500 students. Now in it's fourth year, it has 392 students because it never got as much classroom space as originally anticipated. But he said the school had grown in popularity and was no longer just a second choice to Stuyvesant.

Ruth Lacey, the principal of the Beacon School on the West Side of Manhattan, one of the city's most popular high schools, said more than 6,000 people attended open house events at the school this week.

"We were packed," she said. "People are desperate for good schools." Beacon had nearly 6,000 applicants last year for 250 seats.

After his education announcement, Mr. Bloomberg held a news conference in front of Mount Olivet Baptist Church in Harlem, where he received the endorsement of more than a dozen pastors, and business and community leaders.

Before yesterday's Ferrer fund-raiser, Mrs. Clinton said it was not her style to attack political rivals, and then added: "I happen to think that Republicans, whoever they are, are not good for New York, they're not good for America, and we've begun to see very clearly how damaging Republican policies have been over the last four and a half years under this Republican leadership in Washington. We need to elect Democrats in every position we can."

Reporting for this article was contributed by Karen W. Arenson, Diane Cardwell, Patrick D. Healy and Winnie Hu.
