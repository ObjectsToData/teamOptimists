Wide Open Spaces, and a Place to Park Your Horse

Phil Mansfield for The New York Times
EQUESTRIAN TOWN Horse farms are a common sight in Colts Neck, one of the wealthiest communities in Monmouth County, N.J. There are also public bridle paths throughout the township.

By JERRY CHESLOW

Published: September 12, 2004

HERYL KRAUSE-PARELLO says she and her husband, David Parello, moved to Colts Neck, N.J., from Staten Island last May because the Monmouth County community is "animal friendly." The couple, who have two dachshunds but no children, bought a four-bedroom ranch house on Old Farm Road, which backs up to one of the many public bridle paths that meander through the 31.7-square-mile community.

"Our neighbor to the left has three horses, the one to the right has one horse and the one across the road has three," said Ms. Krause-Parello, a nursing instructor. "Although we don't have horses yet, we will probably get one soon."

One of the wealthiest communities in Monmouth County — the 2000 census reported that 24 percent of households had annual incomes of more than $200,000 — Colts Neck is a mixture of rolling farms, rambling country roads, sprawling public parks and giant 6,000-plus-square-foot homes on lots of more than two acres. According to the community's master plan, 327 farm parcels occupy 6,000 acres, approximately 30 percent of the township, and woodlands take up another 7,300 acres. The most visible types of farming are horse breeding and training.

Lillian G. Burry, the mayor of Colts Neck, who is also a broker at Colts Neck Realty, is proud of the town's well-financed open space trust fund, which taxes residents 2.5 cents per $100 of their homes' assessed valuation and uses the money to buy development rights to farmland to keep it from being turned into subdivisions. So far, the fund has bought the rights to nearly 700 acres. Development is also impeded by 10-acre zoning rules, the lack of public water and sewer systems and the environmental sensitivity of the land itself — 90 percent is in the watershed of the Swimming River Reservoir, a major water source for much of Monmouth County.

"Several developers have challenged our zoning, and we prevailed in most cases because septic systems and wells don't allow for high-density growth," Mayor Burry said. There are no apartment buildings, and the only condominiums are at the Grande, a 276-unit mixed single-family and condo development that was built after the township settled a lawsuit in 1995 filed by the builder, the Westminster Realty Corporation.

Housing prices start about $400,000 for small older colonials or Capes on 36-by-100-foot lots in the older village sections along New Street, Heyers Mill Road and Bucks Mill Road. They rise to an estimated $40 million for a 25,000-square-foot Tudor-style mansion with two indoor pools and an outdoor one fringed by imported sand and equipped with a 100-foot-high waterslide set back about a quarter mile off a private road at the end of Colts Gait Lane.

In early September, 125 houses were listed for sale on the Multiple Listing Service. Among the more moderately priced homes are those in the 30-year-old Clover Hills section on the township's northwestern edge, where ranches and colonials on an acre or more sell for $650,000 to $1.8 million, depending on the lot size, location and improvements, according to George V. Illmensee, a broker at Illmensee Realty.

Despite the 10-acre zoning, most newer homes are built on two to three acres under a clustering arrangement called lot size averaging. The additional seven to eight acres per home are clustered into a larger tract that is restricted as open space in perpetuity. It can be used for greenways, farming or golf courses, of which there are five in the township. They range from the very exclusive — about 100 members — 225-acre, 7,138-yard Due Process Stable golf course, built by Robert E. Brennan, the penny-stock broker who was convicted of securities fraud in 2001, to the county-owned 180-acre Hominy Hills Golf Course, where weekday greens fees are $36.

The large property and home sizes have attracted many celebrities, among them Bruce Springsteen — who owns a 378-acre horse farm — Queen Latifah and Heather Locklear.
