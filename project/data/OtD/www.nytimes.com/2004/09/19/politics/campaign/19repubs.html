A Senate Race in Oklahoma Lifts the Right

 By SHERYL GAY STOLBERG
Published: September 19, 2004

ULSA, Okla., Sept. 15 - With its 60-foot-tall, 30-ton bronze of praying hands and an even taller prayer tower capped by a billowing eternal flame, Oral Roberts University is a strong symbol for Christian conservatives.

But the university's rules forbid mixing religion with politics. So when Dr. Tom Coburn, the Republican Senate nominee in Oklahoma, addressed students recently, the professor who welcomed him refrained from making an endorsement.

"But I don't think we would violate anything," the professor, John W. Swails, told the students with a bit of a nod and a wink, "by saying to you, 'We can pray for Dr. Coburn in this race.' ''

Conservatives around the country are pinning their hopes on Dr. Coburn, a blunt-talking former congressman and family doctor whose specialty, delivering babies, has helped make him a powerful foe of abortion. With his vehement opposition to gay rights and embryonic stem cell research and his willingness to challenge the Republican establishment - including President Bush - on what he sees as government spending run amok, Dr. Coburn is fast becoming a popular icon of the right.

His candidacy caught some Republicans by surprise; Dr. Coburn emerged as the Republican nominee after defeating a more moderate candidate who had the backing of the party establishment. Nationally, his primary victory was a bright spot for fiscal and religious conservatives, who have, with mixed results, tried to reassert themselves in Senate races this year.

While moderates and conservatives have always tussled for control of the Republican Party, this year the fight has been particularly intense. Unlike in the 2002 election, when Republicans prided themselves on coalescing around candidates early in the election season, the 2004 campaigns have amounted to a rebellion of the right, with hotly contested Republican primaries in Alaska, Colorado, Florida, Pennsylvania and South Carolina.

Nowhere has the outcome been as clear-cut for conservatives as here in Oklahoma, where Dr. Coburn's pointed pronouncements make for lively political discourse, not to mention a busy local press corps.

"I shoot straight up," he said recently on a campaign swing through the eastern part of the state.

It was an understatement if there ever was one.

As co-chairman of Mr. Bush's advisory council on H.I.V./AIDS, Dr. Coburn has railed against government advocacy of condoms, as opposed to abstinence, to prevent AIDS. He once complained that "the gay community has infiltrated the very centers of power" and said gays dictated policy at the federal Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

He has cast his Senate campaign, particularly his crusade against spending, in moral terms, saying it is "immoral to spend the next generation's standard of living." He has called the race a "battle of good versus evil" and said he favored "the death penalty for abortionists."

While criticizing state legislators in Oklahoma City, he used what he called "an inappropriate word" that even he conceded had gotten him in trouble.

In Oklahoma, party moderates privately express fear that such remarks will cost Republicans their race against the Democrat, Representative Brad Carson, who is running even with Dr. Coburn in the polls. In recent days, the race has turned nasty, with Dr. Coburn facing accusations from a former patient that he sterilized her without her consent. He denies it, accusing Democrats of a smear campaign.

Nationally, though, conservatives have high hopes for Dr. Coburn. Some see the former congressman as the conservative answer to another maverick politician, John McCain, Republican of Arizona.

"I think Coburn has an excellent chance, if he gets elected, of becoming a real national figure, the way McCain is," said David Keating, executive director of the Club for Growth, an antitax group that ran heavy advertising in the primary to help Dr. Coburn beat Kirk Humphreys, the former Oklahoma City mayor. "McCain had the Straight Talk Express. Coburn is the same way."

Mr. Humphreys had the backing of every major Republican figure in Oklahoma, including Senator Don Nickles, the Republican whose retirement has created the open seat. Though he is now backing Dr. Coburn, Mr. Humphreys said that he still thinks "I shape up better against Carson than Coburn does." But he said he knew he was in trouble the minute Dr. Coburn jumped into the race.

"He's kind of a cult hero in the conservative portion of our party, not just in Oklahoma," Mr. Humphreys said. "You can't get right of the guy."

The race was indicative of a national trend in which ideological outsiders have challenged establishment insiders.
