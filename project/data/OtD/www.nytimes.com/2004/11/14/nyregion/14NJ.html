Blume's Day

 By JENNIFER GOLDBLATT

UDY BLUME is one of those stealth cultural icons. She has been writing, mainly for children, for more than 30 years. And while the Roths, Rushdies, Updikes and their like grabbed the bulk of bookish headlines, all Ms. Blume did was win the hearts and minds of millions of teenage and pre-teenage readers with books - often steeped in New Jersey - like "Blubber," "Deenie," "Freckle Juice" and "Tales of a Fourth Grade Nothing."

But some literary chickens are coming home to roost for Ms. Blume. She is in line for a major award, and she is working with her son, Larry, a filmmaker, to co-produce a movie for Disney based on her novel "Deenie."

And looking back at a childhood spent in the Elmora section of Elizabeth, Ms. Blume sees many signs that point toward a literary career: all her neighborhood streets were named for writers like Byron and Browning, her house on Shelley Avenue was stuffed with books, and she constantly conjured stories inside her head.

Being a writer was far beyond what Ms. Blume imagined for herself back then. She was just a shy, skinny girl at Victor Mravlag Elementary School, the daughter of a dentist and a homemaker; she dreamed of being a ballerina, a cowgirl or a teacher.

"I was always making up stories," Ms. Blume said in a recent telephone interview from Manhattan. "But I never wrote them down. I loved reading, but it never occurred to me to be a writer."

Even so, Ms. Blume was obviously paying close attention. The apparently serene suburbs where she spent much of her childhood and adult life, where friendships were established along neighborhood lines and Manhattan's excitement was just a train ride away, have served as the setting in many of her many of her best-selling books, novels in which her characters grapple with challenges that range from puberty to divorce to incorrigible little brothers.

Ms. Blume's more than 20 books, which have been widely adored and scrutinized for their candid treatment of coming-of-age themes, have sold more than 75 million copies: 14 of them are on Publishers Weekly's list of all-time best-selling children's books.

And this Wednesday night, at the Marriott Marquis Hotel in Times Square, the National Book Foundation will give Ms. Blume its Medal for Distinguished Contribution to American Letters, making her the first writer of young adult books to receive the award, and putting her in the fellowship of Saul Bellow, Eudora Welty and Stephen King, among others.

For millions of young readers, Ms. Blume is more than an author; she is a confidante. Her ability to capture the complexity of growing up and her willingness to tackle subjects like menstruation and masturbation have earned her legions of loyal fans. She gets thousands of letters from them, asking that she write more about their problems, unravel more of life's mysteries.

"Please send me the facts of life, in number order," wrote Fern, a 9-year-old reader in a letter to Ms. Blume included in "Letters to Judy: What Kids Wish They Could Tell You" (1986).

Now, in an age where there is more openness about subjects that were considered taboo when she first started writing, Ms. Blume says that she doesn't get as many requests to sort out the birds and the bees. But children's need for wise, nonjudgmental friends to confide in hasn't gone away.

When it comes to talking about sex, "even the most willing and enlightened parents can still have a very hard time," Ms. Blume said. "I think there are people who still don't feel comfortable with their own sexuality, and the idea that their children will become sexual beings is more than they can deal with."

And, as the years pass, the reputation of Ms. Blume, who is now 66, only grows. When Ms. Blume visited Bennett Books in Wyckoff for a signing of "Summer Sisters" (a novel for adults) in 1998, women who had read her books as teenagers crowded the aisles, and clamored to speak with her.

"Almost everyone said something like, 'You saved my life as a teen' or 'I thought I was the only one with this problem,' or 'I had nobody else to talk to and I read your book 100 times,' " recalled Elizabeth Bennett, co-owner of the store. "People practically had tears in their eyes."
