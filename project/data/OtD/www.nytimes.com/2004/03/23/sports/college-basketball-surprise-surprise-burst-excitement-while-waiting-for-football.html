COLLEGE BASKETBALL: SURPRISE, SURPRISE; A Burst of Excitement While Waiting for Football

By RAY GLIER
Published: March 23, 2004

The students have left for spring break, but the remnants of their celebration Sunday night hang in the trees on University Boulevard. The toilet paper blends in with the budding dogwoods and blows through the grass in Bicentennial Minipark on the University of Alabama at Birmingham campus.

There does not seem to be a rush to clean up. Victories like this do not come along very often.

''I've washed windows on 12 stores this morning and everybody is talking about it, 'How about U.A.B.?' '' said Bill Whitfield, a retired firefighter who owns a window-cleaning business.

Whitfield wiped the suds off a pane of glass on a toy store and could not help but marvel over U.A.B.'s upset Sunday of Kentucky, the top-seeded team in the N.C.A.A. tournament.

''We're back on the map,'' said Whitfield, whose daughter graduated from U.A.B. ''It kind of fizzled down after Gene Bartow left, but it's coming back. Basketball will still be something to do before football starts again in Alabama, but when you win, it's fun.''

The fun seems to have spread all over town. The headline on the front page of The Birmingham News read ''UAB Shocks Kentucky,'' and radio talk shows trumpeted the compelling victory over the Wildcats in which the senior guard Mo Finley hit the winning shot with 12.2 seconds left.

The U.A.B. sports information office has received dozens of requests for interviews from news media across the country.

Television cameras were set up along the court inside Bartow Arena late Monday afternoon for interviews with players and Coach Mike Anderson.

Suddenly, the big news in town is not the spring depth chart for the University of Alabama or Auburn football teams.

Maybe the ninth-seeded Blazers' run to the Round of 16 will help U.A.B. fix its identity problem once and for all.

It is not ''Alabama-Birmingham'' with a hyphen, but Alabama at Birmingham, or, as some people refer to it: the University that Ate Birmingham.

The campus spreads over 75 blocks on the south side of the city. The east campus houses the medical school and biomedical research facilities. The west campus encompasses the traditional academic schools in arts and humanities and the sciences.

U.A.B. has 16,357 students, and a university spokesperson said the economic impact on the city was $2 billion a year.

Birmingham, the city, has no identity problem. Blacks were defiant here against racism, and there are monuments, museums and parks dedicated to the fight against segregation laws.

The city was also known as the ''Pittsburgh of the South'' because of the steel mills that sprouted up around the turn of the 20th century. When the mills started to close in the 1970's, U.A.B. was there to fill a void. The first presidents of the university were successful in the 1970's and 1980's in turning it into a thriving biomedical research school.

The university is the largest employer in Birmingham, which has a metropolitan population of 850,000.

The campus evolved to replace row houses where blacks lived, but university outreach programs like UAB HousingPlus helped develop newer, affordable housing.

William Brown, a 56-year-old retiree who is African-American, said: ''This university treated people right, it does things for black people here. I think they are community-oriented. And black people love their basketball team.''

One reason there is love for the team is Anderson, the 44-year-old African-American coach. He is from Birmingham and was an all-city guard at Jackson Olin High School in 1978. Anderson played at Tulsa for Nolan Richardson, his mentor. He coached at Tulsa and Arkansas for 22 years with Richardson before taking over at U.A.B. in 2002.

Anderson is starting to build a following in just his second year.

Few fans showed up early in the season for Anderson's Thursday night call-in shows at Full Moon, a barbecue restaurant near campus.

''Now the place is packed every Thursday night,'' said Scott Scharding, a physical education major from Birmingham. ''People love Coach. He's nice to all the students and stops and talks to us.''

Bartow Arena seats 8,500, but U.A.B. has had trouble selling tickets. A Feb. 18 victory over Cincinnati was standing-room only at 9,312, but the Blazers, who tied with four other teams for the Conference USA regular-season title, averaged just 4,909 fans at home games this season.

''They don't get much of a following, even though they are the only game in town,'' said Tim Hobbs, who works during the day for a truck leasing company and attends accounting classes at night at U.A.B.. ''The students support it O.K., but the professionals in the city haven't followed it. It's not the S.E.C., it's Conference USA, so that's probably the reason.''

Perhaps with a few more victories like the one over Kentucky, the fans will have more reason to pay attention to the other game in town.
