PRESERVATION; The Time Travelers In the Neighborhood

By JOSH BENSON
Published: July 11, 2004

THINK of historical sites and you may picture creaky structures and creakier curators reading from a dusty script. But caretakers of Westchester's heritage see themselves differently -- as proselytizers, educators and scholars in a dynamic field.

Many of them are called interpreters. ''When we say 'interpreting,' it means a distillation process,'' explained Margaret Vetare, 40, director of interpretation at Historic Hudson Valley, the not-for-profit organization that maintains and operates six historic sites in and around Westchester. ''We take a lot of academic data and try to distill it down to main ideas, and decide how those ideas played out. Somebody has to decide what stories to tell -- that's what we do.''

Historic Hudson Valley, which has an annual budget of about $7 million, oversees Kykuit, Montgomery Place, Philipsburg Manor, Sunnyside, Union Church in Pocantico Hills and Van Cortlandt Manor. Its goal is to make those places both resources for scholars and repeat attractions for anyone else with an interest in history. ''A lot of people don't know how radically this place has changed since maybe they came here in fourth grade 25 years ago,'' she said. ''Getting people to see this as an ongoing resource is a challenge.''

Recent activities include a $300,000 project, underwritten by the National Endowment for the Humanities, at Philipsburg Manor in Sleepy Hollow. It established a working garden of vegetables and herbs thought to have been grown in 1750 on the grounds. The project was part of an effort to illustrate elements of the lives of enslaved Africans who lived there in the 18th century.

To Ms. Vetare, who researched the garden by digging through the diary of Peter Kalm, an 18th-century Swedish botanist who had passed through the area, that sort of work allows the place to tell people things they don't know. ''I'm amazed at how many well-educated people I talk to whose jaws just drop to learn that slavery even existed in New York,'' he said. ''They just didn't learn about that anywhere else.''

Historic Hudson Valley's team of scholars includes a curator with an expertise in decorative arts, an architectural preservationist and a period horticulturalist.

There's also a food historian, Lavada Nahon, 45, a self-made expert who, through decades of amateur research and, more recently, cooking on an open hearth, has become an authority on the evolution of food production and preparation in 18th- and 19th-century America. A descendant of slaves, Ms. Nahon, who grew up in Dallas, has worked for the last two years as an interpreter at Van Cortlandt Manor. She works in the often uncomfortable period costume of frock, short gown, petticoat, fichu, apron, stockings and kerchief.

''But a lot of historians with a lot of book knowledge get things wrong because they're not trying to produce these meals in period-appropriate kitchens,'' she said.

Ms. Nahon, who has studied food history and owns an extensive collection of period cookbooks, says that she and her colleagues are learning to produce the elaborate meals that appeared on the tables at the time of Washington Irving.

Why should anyone living today care what Irving ate? ''People don't realize the possibilities of taste that existed back then,'' she said. ''Cooking was a way of life that doesn't exist for many of us in this rushed environment.''

Indeed, for Ms. Nahon and her colleagues, researching and reproducing history is anything but an academic abstraction. Ms. Vetare, for example, says that their work is essential to the demographically fluid areas in and around Westchester, if only to preserve a sense of place for current residents.

''People are constantly on the move,'' Ms. Vetare said. ''People are moving into this community, and people are leaving. How do they find out what makes this place special? Or if you've always lived here, but suddenly you've got Wal-Mart and T.G.I. Friday's making the Hudson Valley look an awful lot like the middle of Ohio or southern California, what role do historic sites play in maintaining the uniqueness of a region? I think we're a central part of that.''
