If You're Thinking of Living In / Franklin Township, N.J.; A Town Acting to Preserve Its Farmland

By JERRY CHESLOW
Published: November 15, 1998

EARLY this month residents of Franklin Township voted 6,092 to 2,834 to raise their property taxes by 3 cents per $100 of assessed valuation to preserve open space. Mayor Joan I. Bottcher said the new funds would be used to buy some local farmland before it is snatched up by developers.

The 47-square-mile community, which is still three-fourths rural, is coming under increasing development pressure, according to Mayor Bottcher.

''We will be raising about $1 million a year,'' said Mayor Bottcher. ''We will be able to use that money to get matching funds from state and county programs. It's hardly enough, but it is a start.''

The township is also trying to persuade farmers to sell development rights to the state under the Farmlands Preservation Program, which still allows them to farm but bans construction on the site.

Among those who have placed their properties in the Farmlands Preservation Program are Michael Peacos, an 84-year-old farmer and former Mayor, and his wife, Anna, a retired teacher. Mr. Peacos, who moved to the town in 1931, said he had seen it go from horse-and-buggy days to modern congested rush hours as commuters head toward or from the township's ramps leading to Interstate 287.

''My farm is one of four in the program and I am trying to persuade other farmers to do the same,'' said Mr. Peacos.

To underscore the gravity of the development pressure, Town Manager Frederick E. Jahn noted that 1,600 new homes were either under construction or in various stages of approval.

When completed within about five years, he said, they will increase the single-family housing stock by more than 10 percent. The most serious effect will be on the school system, which has weathered a 36 percent population explosion over the last decade alone. Single-family homes listed for resale in the Multiple Listing Service range in price from $98,000 for a two-bedroom ranch on one acre in the northern section of town to $850,000 for a three-bedroom, two-bath colonial on 84 acres fronting on Georgetown Road, a major artery.

The largest detached-house complex is the 996-unit Levitt Homes, built in the northern section of town in the mid-60's. A four-bedroom colonial there is now listed at $184,000.

There are also many custom homes on up to five acres, mainly in the southern section, off Butler Road. Several houses on Butler Road would probably sell for more than $1 million, said David P. Barrood of the Barrood Real Estate Agency in Franklin. He added, however, that none had ever been on the market.

The township has more than 8,000 condominiums in five complexes, all in the southern and northern sections. Mr. Barrood said prices ranged from about $70,000 for a one-bedroom to about $180,000 for three-bedroom town-house units in either of the areas.

Franklin also has one high-rise rental apartment building -- the 21-story Harrison Towers with 315 one- and two-bedroom apartments. One-bedrooms rent for $896 a month and two-bedrooms go for $1,205.

There are two garden apartment rental complexes in town, the 100 unit Parkside and the 400-unit Somerset Estates near the border with New Brunswick, where two-bedroom units rent for $795 a month and three-bedrooms for $925.

UNTIL five years ago, Somerset Estates was a high crime area that raised violent crime statistics in Franklin Township, according to Police Chief Daniel Livak. Since then, his department has instituted a community policing focused on Somerset Estates. The officers, who patrol on foot, bicycle or by car, have formed a bond with residents, said Chief Livak.

The result, he said, was a township-wide reduction in violent crime from 52.6 incidents per 1,000 residents five years ago to 37.5 incidents last year. There have also been a number of drug sweeps in Somerset Estates. In the latest, on Oct. 22, 20 suspected low-level dealers were arrested.

''Open-air drug markets have been targeted and the problem has been virtually eliminated,'' Chief Livak said.

Among the newer residents of Franklin Township are Ann E. and Scott E. McCall, who moved from nearby Milltown two years ago when they purchased a 20-year-old, three-bedroom split-level on a half-acre.

Mr. McCall, a general contractor, grew up in Franklin and wanted to live near his parents. Mrs. McCall, a free-lance medical transcriptionist, said she liked the rural nature of the township, but had been apprehensive because she had been told the Franklin Public Schools had a poor reputation. This, she said, had proved to be inaccurate.

''We have 7-year-old twins,'' she said, ''and I was surprised at how advanced the schools really are. My children started using computers in kindergarten and, by the end of the year, they could use a mouse, write stories and make their own computer coloring books.''

Superintendent of Schools Frank V. Pepe, Jr. said his staff worked hard to administer the diverse 5,600-student school system, where 667 students come from homes where English is not the primary spoken language. In all, 57 languages other than English are spoken in students' households and 389 students are enrolled in English as a second language courses throughout the system.
