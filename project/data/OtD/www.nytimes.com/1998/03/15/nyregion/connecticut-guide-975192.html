CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 15, 1998

Your Old House

What to do with your historic, or just plain old, house will be covered in detail during four Saturday workshops in Wallingford sponsored by the Connecticut Trust for Historic Preservation.

John Leeke, a contributing editor to This Old House magazine and a preservation consultant, will conduct the sessions, scheduled on March 28, and April 4, 18 and 25 at the Wallingford Town Hall, 45 Main Street.

Mornings will be devoted to lectures by teams of architects, contractors and tradespeople who will demonstrate how to deal with missing plaster and wood moldings, split window sills, lead paint and so on. Assessing deterioration, setting priorities, and deciding whether to do the work yourself will be addressed, along with scheduling and financing projects, and setting up a maintenance schedule.

Sometimes it is just not feasible to embark on a major restoration. That will be covered too -- in terms of preserving what is possible in modest increments.

Each afternoon will be spent at a different house, from a 1790 farm house in Wallingford to an 1892 Victorian in West Haven. At each site participants will learn how to determine the date of a house, trace its history, explore work being done currently, recognize and date work done by past owners, and adapt an old building to modern health and safety codes.

Each morning session, 9 A.M. to noon, costs $15, or $50 for all four sessions. Afternoon field trips, 1:30 to 4:30 P.M., cost $40 each or $140 for four. Reservations by credit card or more information may be obtained by calling (203) 562-6312.

Wethersfield Concert

A reception honoring Rhena Schweitzer Miller, daughter of Albert Schweitzer, the physician, musician, theologian and philosopher, will be held following a concert today at 7 P.M. at the First Church of Christ, 250 Main Street in Wethersfield.

The church has been chosen by the Albert Schweitzer Institute for the Humanities in Wallingford as the site for an organ festival similar to one initiated by the institute in the Netherlands as an annual memorial to Dr. Schweitzer. The American festival runs from Sept. 11 through 13 at the church.

Today's program includes works by Bach, Tchaikovsky, and Ralph Vaughan Williams, and the Requiem by Maurice Durufle, performed by a church choir and orchestra under the direction of David Spicer. Howard Sprout, baritone, and Brian Meneely, tenor, will be the soloists.

Tickets will be available at the door, at $10 for adults, $8 for students and people over 64, and $20 for a family. Free child care is available for children under 8. The number for more information is (860) 563-7759.

Retracing Livingstone

Quentin Keynes, great-grandson of Charles Darwin, nephew of John Maynard Keynes and descendant of a generally free-thinking set of forebears, will show his film, ''Up the Zambesi,'' and present a lecture on Thursday at 7:30 P.M. in the Community Room of the Easton Library, 691 Morehouse Road.

With the century-old diaries and correspondence of Dr. David Livingstone to guide him, Mr. Keynes retraced the doctor's journey along the river through central and east Africa. At one point he met a 106-year-old tribal chief who had known Livingstone -- the first white man ever seen in those parts.

In addition to documenting what remains of Livingstone's route, the film shows modern dams, suspension bridges and other 20th-century conveniences that have cut pathways into rural Africa. The number is (203) 261-0134 for more information.

Emerging Artists

Four emerging Connecticut artists will be present at the opening of their first exhibition in an established gallery today at 4 P.M. The Aldrich Museum of Contemporary Art and the Ridgefield Guild of Artists have collaborated on displaying the paintings of Steven DiGiovanni and Kathy Packer, the sculpture of Carey McDougall and the photography of Tertuliano. The show will run through April 5 at the guild on Halpin Lane in Ridgefield.

Mr. DiGiovanni's genre paintings are set in the rooms of working-class homes, often fraught with domestic tension; Ms. Packer's coloring and symmetry is reminiscent of early Italian Renaissance painting, but her subjects are today's adolescents.

Ms. McDougall sculpts women's evening gowns from the perspective of a feminist, and Tertuliano draws on his background as a boxer to inform his shots of small gyms where fighters spend their time training and worrying about the next bout.

Viewing hours are on Thursday through Sunday from 1 to 5. The number for more information is (203) 438-8863.

On Women's Rights

A display of letters, documents, books, pamphlets, artifacts and photographs tracing the history of women's rights in America from the 18th through the mid-20th century may be seen at the Harriet Beecher Stowe Center in Hartford through March and April.

Selected from the center's library and museum collections, they include a first-hand account of the trial of Susan B. Anthony, who was arrested for voting in a Presidential election; addresses on the status of women by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott, and a translation of the Bible by Julia Smith, a suffragist.

The center, at 77 Forest Street, was the home of Stowe, author of ''Uncle Tom's Cabin.'' It is part of Nook Farm, an enclave that includes the restored home of Mark Twain. Hours on Tuesday through Saturday are 9:30 to 4, and Sunday from noon to 4. Admission of $6.50 for adults, $6 for adults over 64, $2.75 for children 6-16, and free for children under 6, includes a tour of the house. The number is (860) 525-9317 for more information.

Diverse Art

After 30 years as an interior designer, Mary Anne Case has diversified, with a vengeance. An exhibition of her cartoons, illustrations, drawings and portraits will be on view at the Ridgfield Public Library through the end of March.

At one end of her spectrum are ''Subway'' and ''Alone Together,'' two of five cartoon strips that she creates on a computer, and at the other end are serious paintings and drawings that she has done in various parts of Italy. In between are illustrations for James T. Farrell's novel ''New Year's Eve, 1929'' and for a book of poetry by Menke Katz, a Lithuanian cabalist. Some of her pre-interior-design artwork is also included in this retrospective.

The library address is 472 Main Street. Hours on Monday, Wednesday and Friday are 10 to 6, Tuesday and Thursday 10 to 9, Saturday 9 to 5, and Sunday 1 to 5. The number is (203) 438-2282 for more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
