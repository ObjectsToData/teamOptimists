In Old England, A Silicon Fen

By YOUSSEF M. IBRAHIM
Published: January 4, 1998

THE rooms at Mount Pleasant House are filling up with new faces, furniture and ideas. Month after month, the five-story building, whose smoky green glass blends into the leafy texture of this ancient university town, is kept busy as yet another high-technology venture installs itself to produce new software or a growing company leaves for bigger quarters.

Moving in is the ponytailed Adrian Wrigley, working in jeans and a T-shirt -- and with nearly $2 million in venture capital -- to perfect software that allows faster, cheaper and livelier three-dimensional animation for the entertainment industry.

Moving out of the building, a kind of incubator for high-tech companies, are Mike Muller and the other employees of Advanced RISC Machines, which outgrew its space and now is piling into a 17th-century converted barn on the edge of town.

There, the company's ever-growing staff -- which now numbers 257, most of them researchers -- is designing microprocessors that are ''small, simple, low-power and fast,'' as Mr. Muller puts it, to go into everything from computers to mobile phones to televisions. The success of the business, a joint venture of Apple Computer and Britain's Acorn Group, is evident in the score of empty champagne bottles lined up along a corridor, each marking a big party held to introduce a new chip.

Almost every week, meanwhile, staff members arrive for the Microsoft Corporation's new research laboratory in Mount Pleasant House, the $80 million beachhead of William H. Gates's campaign to create a global network of brain factories for his software industry titan.

Welcome to the Silicon Fen, as insiders call the vast flat and marshy lands in and around Cambridge. In the last five years, the area has become home to more than 1,000 high-technology companies that employ at least 30,000 people and produce more than $3 billion a year in revenues. Unemployment here is a low 3.5 percent, compared with 5.7 percent for all of Britain and 9 to 17 percent in Europe.

This is no Silicon Valley. The pace here is neither frantic nor flashy, and people frown on loud manifestations of wealth. While Cambridge, the home of one of the world's most prestigious universities, has produced some 50 Nobel laureates, it is a relatively small city of 100,000 encircled by rural landscape. Executives still ride to work on bicycles, as they did when they were students. The buildings are low, the skies wide open and the pace civilized.

But the energy and enthusiasm are palpable.

''I have founded and helped in the launch of 25 companies in the past 12 years,'' boasts Herman Hauser, 48, whose progeny include Acorn, Britain's pioneering maker of personal computers. A multimillionaire entrepreneur, he is part-owner of Mount Pleasant House and head of the Amadeus venture capital fund, which adopts scientists with brilliant ideas who know nothing about how to turn them into businesses.

''It's real. It's happening,'' adds the effusive Mr. Muller, at 38 the director and vice president for business development of Advanced RISC Machines. ''It's a big buzz. Not quite Silicon Valley, but you have to be aware of the scale without underestimating the impact.''

IT isn't difficult to see why the technology industry has planted itself on the Fen. Unlike its intellectual rival, Oxford University, which is best known for its scholarship in the humanities, Cambridge has both a long history of collaboration with industry and a decidedly scientific bent. It was at Trinity College here that Charles Babbage, inventor of the mechanical calculator, studied mathematics in the early 1800's.

''As a base for high-tech engineering, Cambridge with its university is ideal and wonderful,'' said Mr. Hauser, who first came here in 1973, earned a Ph.D. in physics from the university and has stayed ever since. The ferment has also drawn Xerox, Olivetti, Stanford Research Institute, Oracle and Hitachi to establish laboratories or offices here.

But the appeal is not solely intellectual. ''For one thing, many people don't want to live in Seattle, California or America,'' said Prof. Roger M. Needham, a Cambridge computer scientist who recently took a leave from the university to become managing director of Microsoft's new lab. ''Let's face it,'' he added, flashing a somewhat mischievous smile. ''The quality of life is much better here.''

The balding professor, who has taught at Cambridge for nearly a quarter-century, energetically paces his sparsely furnished new office in Mount Pleasant House as he explains how good a time it is to be starting a technology venture in England.

There is capital available on the Continent now, something that was not often the case a decade or so ago. And with most members of the European Union -- if not Britain itself, initially -- moving toward adoption of the euro, Europe will increasingly act as a single market, by some measures much larger than America.

''There are 400 million people on this continent who can work here with no permit,'' Professor Needham said, as if surprising himself with the revelation.
