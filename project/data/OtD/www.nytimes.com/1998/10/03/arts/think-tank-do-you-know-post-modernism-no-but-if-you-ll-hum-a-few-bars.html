Think Tank; Do You Know Post-Modernism? No, but if You'll Hum a Few Bars ...


Published: October 3, 1998

This article was excerpted from an essay by Kevin J. H. Dettmar, an associate professor of English at Clemson University in South Carolina, in the Sept. 25 issue of The Chronicle of Higher Education.

Every other year, I teach an undergraduate humanities course, ''Post-Modern Explorers,'' that is devoted to scrutinizing commonly accepted ideas of creativity, genius and Western culture. Post-modernism is itself a notoriously difficult idea to pin down, but a simple definition would emphasize its skepticism toward any and all claims to truth.

While abstract theoretical thinking about the condition of post-modernity might be necessary and even effective in an upper-level or graduate philosophy seminar, it falls flat in a course that is taken by most students to satisfy a general-education requirement. Recently, though, I've stumbled on a pedagogy that does make post-modernism both palpable and palatable for a number of my students: We rock.

Some cultural history may explain why rock 'n' roll fits the bill. As Bill Richey, an associate professor of English at the University of South Carolina, and I have written in the introduction to ''Reading Rock & Roll: Authenticity, Appropriation, Aesthetics'' (due out next January from Columbia University Press), if rock 'n' roll didn't invent American post-modernism, it knows the gal who did. Rock 'n' roll came into being at about the same time that post-modernism began to exert its influence on American and European culture. On July 5, 1954, Elvis Presley recorded ''That's All Right'' at Sam Phillips's Memphis Recording Service; Samuel Beckett's ''Waiting for Godot,'' a harbinger of post-modernism if there ever was one, had opened in Paris just 18 months earlier and would soon come to America.

One core feature of post-modernism that almost everyone can agree on is its reliance on the twin strategies of irony and appropriation. Rock songs are ideally suited to illustrating those abstract and difficult concepts. Rock 'n' roll, with its mongrel pedigree and its intimate association with mass media and high-tech modes of production, has been a highly self-conscious and often ironic art form from the get-go -- and by no means the bastion of sincerity that many rock critics have made it out to be. Equally, romantic notions of ''originality'' are mainly foreign to rock: musicians know that most riffs are stolen (even if it may be convenient to disguise the fact) and that, as Bono sings in ''The Fly,'' ''Every artist is a cannibal, every poet is a thief.''

Talking about rock music helps students see why the Western cultural notion of singular creative genius may be too simple. Rock came of age on the radio, every song always just a button-push or a commercial break away from an influence, imitator or rival. Hence it has never been allowed to luxuriate in delusions of inviolable borders and artistic autonomy. Rock songs have always echoed other rock songs; with the advent of digital sampling, with which musicians incorporate segments of other artists' recordings, certain genres of rock now regularly, even obsessively quote other songs. (By one count, the opening 10 seconds of Public Enemy's ''Fight the Power,'' a tune made famous in the Spike Lee film ''Do the Right Thing''-- contains 17 samples.)

The ethics of sampling is still controversial in some circles. But when you stop to consider that Kathy Acker copied nearly 2,000 words from Harold Robbins's ''Pirate'' into her novel ''The Adult Life of Toulouse-Lautrec by Henri Toulouse-Lautrec'' or that the photographer Sherrie Levine has rephotographed and displayed Minor White's nude photographs of his son in exhibits of her own work, the hip-hop and trip-hop practice of sampling comes to seem not an aberration but mainstream post-modern practice.

Music -- especially recorded music, the music of mechanical reproduction -- is especially ripe for artistic appropriation, making that a concept I can demonstrate easily to my students. If I were to ''appropriate'' a square inch, randomly selected, from Leonardo da Vinci's ''Mona Lisa,'' no one would recognize it; but if you play four bars from Nat (King) Cole's song ''Mona Lisa,'' I can tell what it is.

My students have the same ability, which I exploit in a simple first-day exercise: I play the first bar of different well-known pop songs (''Stairway to Heaven,'' ''Respect,'' ''You Oughta Know,'' ''Losing My Religion'') to see if students can identify them. Everyone (except hard-core country fans) scores at least 80 percent -- their answers in some cases based on just two or three notes. Because music unfolds in time and not in space -- because it's serial, not parallel -- a little sonic quotation goes a long way. Long before the digital sampler, after all, poor George Harrison was nailed for unconsciously plagiarizing a riff from the Chiffons' ''He's So Fine,'' a musical phrase of just three quarter-notes.

It's impossible, on the other hand, to imagine a director being sued for stealing three frames, a poet for stealing three words or a choreographer for stealing three steps. In rock 'n' roll, it would seem, Walter Benjamin's theory of mechanical reproduction meets Fredric Jameson's theory of late capitalism: One can't help quoting, but no one quotes free.
