University Presses; Heavy Symbolism

By Mark C. Taylor
Published: October 25, 1998

THE LAST DINOSAUR BOOK

The Life and Times of a Cultural Icon.

By W. J. T. Mitchell.

Illustrated. 321 pp. Chicago:

The University of Chicago Press. $35.

Images of dinosaurs are everywhere; we are in the midst of what can only be described as ''dinomania.'' But why? In ''The Last Dinosaur Book,'' W. J. T. Mitchell maintains that dinosaurs are symptoms of what the literary theorist Fredric Jameson labels ''the political unconscious.'' Contrary to expectation, the figure of the dinosaur indirectly discloses the changing economic forces that have shaped modern society and culture.

Mitchell, a professor of English and art at the University of Chicago, analyzes what he describes as the ''cultural icon'' of the dinosaur. An icon, he explains, is an ''imagetext'' or ''combination of verbal and visual signs'' that is symptomatic of psychological, social and economic processes. With Darwin, Freud and, most important, Marx as his guides, Mitchell unearths the historical origin and development as well as the psychological and social functions of icons. The analysis of the dinosaur yields rich insights because it is ''the totem animal of modernity.''

Drawing on Claude Levi-Strauss's influential account of totemism, Mitchell insists that the dinosaur is ''a thing to think with, not of.'' When the totem is an icon, we think with imagetexts that collapse the distinction between the conceptual and the visual. The imaginative dimension of all reflection obscures the supposedly clear lines separating natural sciences, social sciences, arts and humanities. Mitchell sides with those who reduce nature to culture: ''Nature is culture; science is art. We don't ever 'see nature' in the raw, but always cooked in categories and clothed in the garments of language and representation.'' What makes the dinosaur a particularly rich resource for thought is its hybrid character: the dinosaur ''is a figure in constant symbolic motion, shuttling between science and fantasy, nature and culture, the image of the other and a mirror of the self.'' The ambiguity of the dinosaur provokes ambivalent responses.

To support his interpretation of these hybrid creatures, Mitchell creates a hybrid work. The book consists of 40 brief chapters, a coda and two superfluous appendixes. It is amply illustrated with images from popular culture, and its inventive design gives it the feel of a comic book. The chapters are numbered sequentially but can be read in any order. Mitchell attempts to persuade as much by amassing material as constructing arguments. However, as examples and illustrations proliferate, this dinosaur threatens to collapse under its own weight. When he finally admits that this ''shape-shifting, transitional figure . . . can seem to mean almost anything one minute and almost nothing the next,'' the point of the analysis appears to vanish.

But shift this apparently arbitrary assemblage of words and images ever so slightly and another figure takes shape. Like all good stories, Mitchell's narrative has a clear beginning, middle and end. Mitchell's deepest sympathies lie with Marx or, more precisely, the neo-Marxist brand of criticism popularized by cultural studies. The image of the dinosaur, he says, ''stands for the fate of the human species within the world system of modern capitalism.'' Far from monolithic, modern capitalism evolves through three distinct stages, identified by Fredric Jameson as market capitalism (1848-1890's), monopoly capitalism (1890's-1940's) and postindustrial or multinational capitalism (1940's-present). Without noting his source, Mitchell appropriates this narrative. There are three chapters in the story of dinosaurs: early modern (1840-1900), modern (1900-60) and post-modern (1960-present).

Though ancient creatures, dinosaurs are a modern invention. In the 1840's the English paleontologist Richard Owen coined the term ''dinosaur.'' For him dinosaurs were antediluvian monsters resembling ''terrible lizards,'' an image popularized by iron-and-concrete fabrications displayed in the Crystal Palace in 1854. In the second image, a dinosaur appears as a giant, erect reptile. Mitchell sees in this familiar icon a ''cultural allegory'' of monopoly capitalism: ''Giant corporate entities headed by gigantic individuals'' engage in life-and-death power struggles. But in a world governed by laws of social and economic Darwinism, colossal size is not always an advantage. In contrast to ''swamp-bound monsters of sluggish disposition,'' the birdlike raptors of the film ''Jurassic Park'' represent ''the new stage of post-modern capitalism, of 'downsizing,' flexibility, rapid strike forces, teamwork, adaptability.'' Acknowledging the importance of the information revolution for the third stage of capitalism, Mitchell labels one chapter ''The Age of Biocybernetic Reproduction.'' Computer technology and genetic engineering create a prospect of old monsters in new guises. The difference between Tyrannosaurus rex and the agile raptor is that between G.M. and Microsoft.

Thus ''The Last Dinosaur Book'' is a disaster story about imminent extinction of the human race. In the dinosaur we see our own dark future. The destructive monster out of control is capitalism, whose voracious appetite is satisfied only by proliferating instantly obsolete products. At the end of the millennium, this wasteful process threatens to become an omnivorous beast that consumes itself. Bound to a neo-Marxist perspective, Mitchell says biocybernetics ''is not a revolutionary break with the past like mechanistic modernism was'' but is an extension of the most pernicious aspects of consumer capitalism.

This one-sided critique fails to do justice to the complexity, diversity and novelty of the digital revolution. Without underestimating the destructive potential of new technologies, it is possible to imagine their constructive deployment. The obsolete narrative structure of this book is nearing the dinosaur's state -- extinction. That does not imply that Mitchell has not made a contribution, for, as he writes, dinosaurs are not only good to think of but are also good to think with. By thinking with ''The Last Dinosaur Book,'' we are able to see creative possibilities in our historical moment that Mitchell overlooks.
