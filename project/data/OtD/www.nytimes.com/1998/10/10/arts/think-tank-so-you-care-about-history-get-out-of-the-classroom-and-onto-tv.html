Think Tank; So, You Care About History? Get Out of the Classroom and Onto TV


Published: October 10, 1998

Simon Schama, a humanities professor at Columbia University, is to deliver the New York Council for the Humanities Scholar of the Year address on Wednesday at the Donnell Library Center in Manhattan (reservations: 212-233-1131, ext. 43). Following are excerpts from the lecture, ''Visualizing History'':

Writers like A. J. P. Taylor and J. H. Plumb, who combined scholarly research with journalism, taught us not to flinch at the poularization of history. Thirty, forty years on, though, bright, brave street-smart writing is not going to be enough to see us through the contemporary crisis of collective historical amnesia. We need to go beyond the book -- to the humming bazaar of contemporary culture: to the modern museum; to the rapidly accumulating infinite world of the cyberarchive, of interactive electronic history; to the movies; to the imminent world of digital television; and we need to do so not holding our noses or looking down them, but steadily right into the lens of the camera.

What I'm asking for, in other words, is for historians to stop being satisfied (or dissatisfied) with their walk-on roles as ''consultants,'' which, in all too many cases for television and film production companies in a hurry, often means little more than low-rent fact checkers. Rather, they ought to seek opportunities to become full partners or producers in these enterprises.

Two years ago the BBC came to me and asked me to write and present a 16-part television series on British history. Which is why these days I'm spending a lot of time standing on hills in remote parts of Britain staring at the sky and praying for the occasional ray of sunshine. One of the reasons I decided in the end to tackle this daunting enterprise was the simple fact, on the face of it surprising given British television's output of historical documentaries, that there had never been a single, continuous television narrative of British history before. On the first shoot I found out why. It's called the British weather.

The opportunity to produce a major public history using images and texts, sights and sounds, with any luck and great editing brought together in a single master narrative, seems to me to be tremendously worth attempting, and especially at a moment when all the nations of Europe, and beyond, are doing some hard self cross-examination about allegiance: what constitutes and what dissolves the bonds of national community? And despite the official and -- it seems to me -- callow fashion for ''rebranding'' Britain as ''cool Britannia,'' the premise of this television project is precisely that such a separation precludes any understanding of what it means to belong to a national community.

The ''cool Britannia'' marketing ethos in fact perpetuates precisely the kind of false dichotomies between a ''heritage'' Britain smelling of antique furniture polish and old tea cozies and the sharp cutting-edge Britain of technology and fashion; between a Britain of old texts and a Britain of new images; between Masterpiece Theater and ''Trainspotting'' -- that I'm most passionately committed to do away with. So we're using every kind of televisual technique at our command, from computer-generated imagery to time-lapse photography, from pilotless, radio-controlled helicopter cameras to microphotography developed out of medical apparatuses. But in the last resort all these toys would be just that without a central core of historical narration.

From the beginning the BBC has conceived of the project as a way to trigger a debate in the public at large on the relationship between past and future, and between the different nations of the not always so-united kingdom. So along with the television series, there will be local radio programs centered on specific sites with particular historical memories, an interactive Web site, of course, and materials including a CD-ROM and videos custom designed for use in schools and public libraries.

Now why, if the British can manage this, shouldn't American public broadcasting rise to the occasion as well? There have been, of course, tremendously important contributions to particular themes of American history -- the Revolution and Civil War, the history of the West. It seems the time is ripe for an overall look at the course of American history on a scale somewhat similar to our British history project. And just think, the lucky producer would only have to cover 500 instead of 2,000-odd years!

There is, of course a tremendous element of hubris-laden foolhardiness about the temerity of this whole exercise. Yet it still seems to me a marvelous opportunity to bring together the two realms of history, the scholarly and the popular, which have been separated for much too long. No doubt the pieces may not fit as neatly as we should like. No doubt there will be as many people who will be infuriated as entertained and edified by our enterprise. But that's only to be expected. For if as Macaulay said, the ideal history should be ''received by the imagination as well as by reason,'' another of my favorite historians knew full well that it was also ''an argument without end.''
