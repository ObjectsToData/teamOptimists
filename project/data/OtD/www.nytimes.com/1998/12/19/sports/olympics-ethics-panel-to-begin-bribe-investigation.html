OLYMPICS; Ethics Panel to Begin Bribe Investigation

By JERE LONGMAN
Published: December 19, 1998

The organizing committee for the 2002 Winter Olympics today empowered its ethics panel to begin investigating whether bribes were offered to secure the Games.

Seeking an independent investigation, the organizing committee said it would allow the five-member ethics panel to bring in outside attorneys and accounting firms as it determines whether improper payments, gifts and services were made in an effort to sway votes of members of the International Olympic Committee.

The ethics panel is to make its report by Feb. 11. The organizing committee said the report would be made public but did not say whether the report would contain a detailed explanation of payments made and the names of people who were paid.

The I.O.C. is also conducting an investigation after one of its senior members, Marc Hodler of Switzerland, charged last weekend that Salt Lake City and other Olympic cities participated in vote-buying schemes. Salt Lake City officials have acknowledged spending $400,000 on scholarships and athletic training for 13 people, including six relatives of I.O.C. members, but have denied that there was bribery.

''We acknowledge the seriousness of the matter, and we regret any and all embarrassment this has caused the city and state of Utah,'' Robert Garff, chairman of the organizing committee's board of trustees, said at a news conference. He said that organizers would do everything possible to ''insure the Games go forward with the highest level of integrity.''

He added, ''I can say these kind of incidents should not have happened.''

Organizers said it was premature to say whether any Salt Lake City Olympic official would be fired. One city councilwoman, Deeda Seed, has called for the resignation of Frank Joklik, the chairman of the Olympic organizing committee, and of Dave Johnson, senior vice president of the organizing committee.

Seed has also characterized the ethics panel investigation as a ''toothless tiger'' because it lacks subpoena power, sufficient independence and the ability to enforce its own recommendations.

Organizers sought to counter such charges today by saying that the ethics panel would be allowed to operate on an unlimited budget and to employ the necessary outside firms to conduct the bribery investigation. Organizers said they planned to follow the ethics panel's recommendations.

The ethics panel is headed by Gordon R. Hall, a former Chief Justice of the Utah Supreme Court; Patricia Hanna, dean of humanities at the University of Utah; David Jordan, a former United States attorney; Merrill Norman, an accountant, and Barbara Lockhart, a former Olympic athlete.

Utah Gov. Michael Leavitt, at his monthly news conference on Thursday, called for a quick and open investigation, saying, ''The world needs to hear about anything we wouldn't be proud of from us and it needs to hear it from us first.''

No New Medals for Babashoff

HUNTINGTON BEACH, Calif. (AP) -- Shirley Babashoff will not be getting more Olympic gold medals.

The swimmer, who won six silvers and two golds at the 1972 and '76 Olympics, accused the East German women of taking male growth hormones in 1976. Her cry fell on deaf ears, and she was considered a poor sport.

Last week, the International Olympic Committee rejected a petition by United States Swimming that would have withdrawn or reassigned the medals won by East Germans. The I.O.C. Juridical Committee said such action would create a host of legal problems, or open ''a can of steroids,'' as Babashoff likes to say. Babashoff, 41, now a Huntington Beach mail carrier, was not surprised by the decision. She told The Orange County Register that the I.O.C. does not care about the issue.
