PUBLIC LIVES

By James Barron
Published: August 20, 1998

Purplish Tie Turns Heads

Now, about the President's tie, the purplish Ermenegildo Zegna that PRESIDENT CLINTON wore in the Rose Garden at the White House on the day that MONICA S. LEWINSKY testified before the grand jury, prompting prosecutors to wonder whether he was sending her a secret signal.

The tie makers at Zegna were surprised to see it around the First Neck. The tie appears to have been made for Zegna's fall and winter collection in 1996. And KENNETH W. STARR, the independent counsel, has yet to call the manufacturer.

''We are very pleased, of course, that Mr. Clinton has a number of our ties that he chooses to wear,'' a spokesman for Zegna, THOMAS MASTRONARDI, said. ''It is our policy to refrain from discussing customers' shopping habits or gift-giving habits.''

The tie, which Ms. Lewinsky told prosecutors she had given Mr. Clinton on his 50th birthday two years ago yesterday, was ''no more or less popular'' than other silk print ties that year, Mr. Mastronardi said.

''It's gone,'' he said. ''We will not be reproducing it.''

That will disappoint some sharp-eyed shoppers. A spokeswoman for Saks Fifth Avenue said that five customers had asked yesterday if the tie was available. A spokeswoman for Bloomingdale's said that several shoppers had also asked for ''the Monica Lewinsky tie.''

Was Zegna a house brand at the White House? ROBERT S. BENNETT, the famously rumpled lawyer who represented Mr. Clinton in the Paula Corbin Jones lawsuit, modeled a blue Zegna suit at a ''political salon'' given by Zegna, Neiman Marcus and George magazine last Sept. 25.

''This is a first for me,'' Mr. Bennett was quoted as saying in Daily News Record, a men's fashion magazine. Daily News Record said that he called the two-button suit the best he had ever worn.

In a Word, No, No, No

The comedian CHRIS ROCK, the host of a late-night variety show on HBO, discovered yesterday that there was at least one word that cannot be said on early morning network television. Mr. Rock repeated a vulgarism three times on the ''Today'' show. Viewers on the East Coast heard it -- there is no stopping live television -- but NBC dropped the segment from the tape-recorded ''Today'' shown in the West.

''We are big fans of Chris's and admire his work,'' said ERICA PROTO, a spokeswoman for ''Today.'' ''He's been a guest before on the show, but his language today was inappropriate for morning television, especially for the many children who are off from school and watching at that hour.''

Later Mr. Rock had more to say.

''I find it odd that the people at the 'Today' show felt that talk of stained dresses and violent crimes was more suitable for morning television than me joking about whupping Ken Starr,'' he said in a statement.

But Ms. Proto said NBC objected not to what he said, but only to the word he had used. And used. And used. And used.

Humanities Star

''When we were looking for somebody to be a poster boy for the humanities, SIMON SCHAMA came naturally to mind,'' said JAY KAPLAN, the head of the New York Council on the Humanities. And so the group named Mr. Schama, a professor at Columbia University, its scholar of the year. The title carries a $10,000 honorarium, and he will deliver a lecture in October, during New York State Humanities Month.

''He's not the superspecialist that some scholars have become,'' Mr. Kaplan said, ''but can speak knowledgeably and impressively on subjects from Shakespeare to Rembrandt and the Druids to Robespierre.'' JAMES BARRON
