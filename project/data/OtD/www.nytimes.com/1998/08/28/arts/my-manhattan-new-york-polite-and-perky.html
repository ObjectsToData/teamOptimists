MY MANHATTAN; New York, Polite And Perky?

By PETER APPLEBOME
Published: August 28, 1998

It hit me last fall, the first day I began working again in New York, where I was born and grew up but haven't lived for 25 years.

I went out to lunch at a diner in Times Square. Only it wasn't a Times Square diner. It was a Times Square-theme diner, specifically, a retro Times Square-World War II-Boogie Woogie Bugle Boy from Company B diner with $4.50-a-glass merlot, $9.25 cheeseburgers and waiters in military garb singing ''Happy Birthday'' to stricken tourists.

Maybe I should have realized that these days the kind of Times Square-theme restaurant you would expect to find on Greenville Avenue in Dallas or at Lenox Square in Atlanta, places where no morsel of food comes without a concept, would be right at home in Times Square itself. Instead, it felt a little spooky, as if I had somehow wandered into an entirely new iteration of New York, at once identical to and utterly different from the one I thought I knew, like the Bizarro World of Superman comic books, where up means down, ''Me sad,'' means 'I'm happy,'' the sun is blue and the ocean yellow.

I realize it has been a long time since I left New York, to spend most of my adult life in the South. I was born on the Upper West Side and grew up in the suburbs. Back then Walt Frazier, Phil Jackson and Bill Bradley played for the Knicks; Thurman Munson, Bobby Murcer and Ron Swoboda played for the Yankees; only an insane person used the word ''coffee'' and ''grande'' in the same sentence, and nobody walked around barking buy-and-sell orders into cellular phones. No one had invented E-mail, the Trump Tower or Madonna.

But almost a year after coming back, I still feel a lot like Rip Van Winkle, who awakened and had to translate constantly between past and present. For me, the translating is between the New York of John Lindsay and Rudy Giuliani, the New York of Richard Nixon and Bill Clinton (well, some things remain the same). I'm not sure what's changed more, me or New York, but I'm still working on reconciling past and present.

Since this is, I think, 1998, it's hard not to start with ''Seinfeld.'' As the world knows, in one of the more celebrated episodes, Elaine wanders into the Bizarro Seinfeld world where the Jerry character is kind and considerate, the Kramer character always knocks politely and everyone is genuinely thrilled when the Fed-Ex guy, a warm and fuzzy version of Newman, walks in. That's pretty much my view of Bizarro New York.

Here I am in a city where the Dow, even with its current swoon, gyrates around at levels that back then would have seemed like science fiction; the crime rate is plummeting, and the Mayor lectures cabdrivers on their manners, wars against smut like a vengeful Southern Baptist preacher and tries to give tickets to jaywalkers the way they do in Dallas. Mozart is playing at the Port Authority Bus Terminal. Times Square is full of billboards for idiotically cheery little Teletubbies and the Lion King. It's big news whenever the Yankees lose.

''You think the market's going to be back to normal today?'' asked the Russian immigrant who cut my hair one morning last winter, in a sunny reversal of the genetic New York pessimism I remember. The Dow had gone down 146 points the day before, which he saw as a stunning deviation from the laws of nature yet discussed with the authoritative air that barbers (back when they existed) used to reserve for the Mets and Rangers. (Even I should have figured out it was a sell signal.)

The place doesn't seem utterly unfamiliar. There is the routine urban version of biblical plagues like gas lines exploding on Fifth Avenue, the scaffold collapse at the Conde Nast building, and chunks of Yankee Stadium ready to fall on your head. Despite the Mayor's pleading, the cabdrivers seem even more homicidal than they were when I spent a year as New York's most inept cabby, and the Mayor himself seems a poster boy for the sort of stunning arrogance that people outside of New York still think of as New York's defining characteristic.

Indeed, there was a level of Bizarro New York within Bizarro New York in the idea of Mr. Guiliani as the avatar of a perky new New York of family values and Letitia Baldrige manners. And from the hysterical daily street haiku of the tabloids or the amazing we-are-the-world, New York centrism of The New York Observer to the endless proliferation of street cuisine and ethnic restaurants, in some ways it seems just as distinctly, indelibly, obliviously Noo Yawk as I remember it.

I have to admit I have mixed feelings about the push/pull between the in-your-face primal Noo Yawk and the sanitized Bizarro New York Lite -- and not necessarily quite the same mixed feelings I might have expected to have when I left.

I still have enough ancestral New York in me that the notion of New York as Charlotte, N.C., with more cabs, bigger billboards and better noodle joints strikes me as pretty dispiriting, like the faux Times Square diner in Times Square. What next? Michael Bolton doing his version of ''New York, New York'' after Yankee games? Regis and Kathie Lee as major New York cultural figures? (Wait a second, Regis and Kathie Lee are major New York cultural figures.)

But it also seems kind of sweet that New York is doing its ungainly best at becoming more like the rest of the country. Let's face it, the best-selling recording artist of the 1990's is Garth Brooks. Marketers go to Tulsa, Okla., when they want to figure out what real Americans are like. The President, Vice President, Speaker of the House and the majority leaders of the House and Senate are all from the South. The chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities was co-editor of the Encyclopedia of Southern Culture, and the chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts used to run the Country Music Foundation. There's a message here.

Part of my confusion simply reflects the protean nature of New York. While I was gone, I not only missed the start of the late 90's Boom New York II, I also missed the ''Ford to City: Drop-Dead''-bankruptcy-era New York, the Annie-Hall-I-Love-New-York-comeback New York, the disco-era New York, the pre-1987 Gordon Gecko Boom New York and various other twists and turns of New York ephemera. The faux diner has just closed, and the end of the Dow's joy ride, which, depending on which market windbag you believe, may already be upon us, will, no doubt, usher in the next turn of the wheel. But for now, rather than a new New York, the current one has a lot in common with the halcyon one my parents grew up with that I never thought I'd see.

The trouble may be that I've come to see New York through the eyes of both Ratso Rizzo and Joe Buck in a country that's more the latter than the former. And given the imperfect choice between the guiding dictum of Southern life (be sweet; mind your manners) and the guiding dictum of New York life (you can never be too rich or too thin), I'm not sure I vote for New York.

Still, I'm willing to give the place a chance and try to fit in. My kids are learning the native patois from the vendors at Yankee Stadium (''Beah Heah!''). I've found an authentic country music hangout. There's a reasonably good barbecue place nearby. I'm relearning the considerable virtues of mass transit that actually works, summer days without triple-digit temperatures and sports pages that don't treat potbellied Southern football coaches as minor deities.

It may not quite be the Son of Sam-era New York I remember, but this may work yet.
