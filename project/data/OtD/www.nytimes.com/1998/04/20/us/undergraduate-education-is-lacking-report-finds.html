Undergraduate Education Is Lacking, Report Finds

By KAREN W. ARENSON
Published: April 20, 1998

The acclaimed research universities of the United States are shortchanging their undergraduate students, particularly freshmen, according to an unusually candid report from the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, one of the nation's leading education-policy organizations.

It said universities consigned undergraduates to classes taught by graduate assistants and failed to provide students with ''a coherent body of knowledge'' by the time they graduated.

The report, by a commission whose members included officials of research universities, says there is a longstanding division between research and teaching that should be ended and that universities should involve undergraduates in research -- whether in medical laboratories or humanities projects -- beginning in the freshman year. The report is being released today.

''What we need to do is to create a culture of inquirers, rather than a culture of receivers,'' said Shirley Strum Kenny, the president of the State University of New York at Stony Brook and chairwoman of the 11-member commission that wrote the report, ''Reinventing Undergraduate Education: A Blueprint for America's Research Universities.''

The report describes the situation for undergraduates in stark terms: ''Too often the freshman curriculum is a bore and freshman instruction inadequate. Senior professors, when they teach undergraduates, tend to teach majors in advanced courses.'' It added: ''As a result, freshmen -- the students who need the very best teaching -- may actually receive the worst.''

Robert Diamond, director of the Institute for Change in Higher Education at Syracuse University, who saw an early draft, applauded its recommendation for linking education and research more closely.

''It makes a lot of sense pedagogically and from the standpoint of serving the community,'' he said. ''Learning will be improved. Students will have a better sense of how faculty work and think. And it relates the classroom to the real world.''

The accusation that research universities fail their undergraduates by putting research ahead of teaching was a constant theme of Ernest L. Boyer, the former president of the Carnegie Foundation, who established the commission shortly before his death in 1995.

The commission argues that the nation's 125 research universities have as important a role in education as in research, since they grant nearly a third of the baccalaureate degrees though they represent only 3 percent of colleges and universities. ''To an overwhelming degree, they have furnished the cultural, intellectual, economic and political leadership of the nation,'' the report said.

The universities covered in the report emphasize graduate education and research and receive significant Federal research money. They range from Harvard University and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology to the University of Alabama at Birmingham and Kent State University.

Many of the report's proposals are familiar -- rewarding faculty members more for good teaching, using technology more creatively and fostering more interdisciplinary work -- but the commission tried to weave them into a plan to transform undergraduate education, and it offered examples of model programs.

The report also makes a variety of other recommendations, including placing freshmen in small groups where they live together and take courses together. At Duke University, for example, freshmen sign up for interdisciplinary programs in which they take two related seminars and a related writing course. Enrollment is limited to 30 students who are also grouped together in a dormitory.

Another proposal calls for getting students involved in research with senior faculty members. At the M.I.T., more than half of the undergraduates earn academic credit or hourly wages for work on research projects that they propose or that their professors are conducting.

Still another recommendation is that students be required to conduct original research that could become their transition into graduate school. Princeton undergraduates are required to conduct independent research or creative work in their junior year, which becomes the basis for their senior thesis.

The report says oral and written communication should be emphasized in all classes. The University of Iowa recruits graduate instructors for required courses in communications and research from all departments.

The commission also called for the universities to structure courses around research and problem solving. In one Harvard course in introductory physics, students record their answers to conceptual questions on computers in class. Then they meet in small groups to discuss their answers with one another, before the instructor discusses the responses.

Dr. Kenny said that although some of the suggested changes could be costly, the National Science Foundation and other groups that finance research are beginning to require that undergraduate students be involved in projects they finance.

For all the problems at research universities, the report said that they have the potential to offer ''a virtually matchless education,'' with their world-class faculties, high graduation rates, strong support for the arts, interdisciplinary programs and commitment to diversity.

The report will be available on the Internet at http://www.sunysb.edu/ boyerreport.

Besides Dr. Kenny, these are the members of the commission:

Wayne C. Booth, professor emeritus of English and rhetoric at the University of Chicago.

Milton Glaser, a designer and graphic artist.

Charles E. Glassick, senior associate at the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching.

Stanley O. Ikenberry, president of the American Council on Education.

Kathleen Hall Jamieson, dean of the Annenberg School of Communication at the University of Pennsylvania.

Robert M. O'Neil, director of the Thomas Jefferson Center for the Protection of Free Expression and professor of law at the University of Virginia.

Carolynn Reid-Wallace, senior vice president for education and programming at the Corporation for Public Broadcasting;

Chang-Lin Tien, chancellor emeritus and N.E.C. Distinguished Professor of Engineering at the University of California at Berkeley;
