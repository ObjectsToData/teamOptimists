THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: February 1, 1998

Indian Heritage

A major conference on Native American studies is to be held in New Haven at Yale University, open to the public free of charge. It is titled ''Translating Native American Cultures: Representation, Esthetics and Translation'' and runs from Thursday through next Sunday.

The Whitney Humanities Center at 53 Wall Street will be the main venue for performances, lectures, and panel discussions by people from many cultures including Pueblo, Osage, Muscogee, Sioux, Yupi'k, Apache and Mohawk. Traditional ceremonies will open and close each day.

''Princess Pocahontas and the Blue Spots,'' a play by Monique Mojica, will be performed on Thursday at 8 P.M. and Friday at 8:30. ''Medicine River,'' a Canadian film by Thomas King, will have its United States premiere on Friday at 2:30. The keynote address will be delivered on Friday at 5 P.M. by Elizabeth Cook-Lynn, author, member of the Sicangu Dakota nation and professor emeritus at Eastern Washington State University.

Saturday's events include an academic panel on language and translation at 9:30 A.M., and a second panel on literature at 2:15. A talk will be given at 1:30 by N. Scott Momaday of the Kiowa nation. His novel, ''House Made of Dawn,'' is the only Native American work to receive a Pulitzer Prize for literature. At 8 P.M., readings by various speakers and panelists will take place at the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library, 121 Wall Street. A reception and buffet supper at the library will be open to the public by reservation.

Representatives from Connecticut's Pequot and Mohegan Nations will speak on Sunday at 9:30 A.M., followed by the concluding session on religious traditions at 10 A.M. Special exhibitions dedicated to Native American cultures will be on view at the Beinecke Library and the Peabody Museum of Natural History. For reservations, information and more details, the number is (203) 432-2958.

Singing Valentines

Has the approach of Valentine's Day revived the perennial decision of bonbons, flowers or a token from Tiffany's? How about a Singing Valentine Quartet from the Coastal Chordsmen?

This group, a member of the worldwide Barbershop Singing Society, will dispatch a quartet to the loved one at home, office, restaurant, hospital, wherever. Dressed in black tuxedos the four will deliver a few love songs, a card with the sender's message and a long-stemmed rose for $35 in the Greater Bridgeport area.

Early reservations are recommended; the last couple of years have been sellouts. The numbers are (203) 874-6759 or (203) 254-9389 for reservations by credit card.

Self-Portraits

The remarkable and often unsettling self-portraits of Chuck Close and some his friends and relatives are on view at the Akus Gallery of Eastern Connecticut State University in Willimantic through February 27.

Back in the 60's the Yale-educated artist delved into photography as a legitimate source of artistic expression. This show, called ''Translations,'' refers to his transcribing of photographic images into paintings, films, prints, and other media.

By photographing a close-up of his own face and applying a grid over it, he produced an enormous painting of his head, showing every pore, hair and blemish. Some of his portraits are between five and nine feet tall, demonstrating his preoccupation with process and composition rather than the character and soul of the person.

Because of a spinal disorder that left him mostly paralyzed, the 58-year-old artist works with a brush strapped to his wrist, helped also by a lift that moves the canvas to desired positions.

The gallery is open on Sunday from noon to 4, Monday from 11 to 4:30, Thursday from 11 to 2 and 4 to 7, and Friday from 11 to 4:30. The number is (860) 465-4659 for more information.

Young Singers

A select group of 15 young singers from the 150-member Fairfield County Children's Choir will perform on Saturday from 3 to 5 P.M. at Staples High School in Westport. They will be joined by the singer-songwriter Don Conley, a recording artist and specialist in music for children.

A capella Renaissance songs and ethnic tunes will be mixed with jazz numbers and such familiar selections as ''The Boogie, Woogie Bugle Boy,'' ''Puff, the Magic Dragon'' and ''Skip to My Lou.'' Children in the audience will be invited for some impromptu dancing with the singers, and some singing-along.

The full Fairfield County Children's Choir is composed of children in grades 4 through 10, divided into three units: the Chorale, Concert Choir and Chamber Singers.

A silent and penny auction will be held during the concert, which will benefit the Children's Community Day Care Center in Westport. Tickets are $7 at the door. The number for more information is (203) 222-8668.

About a German Abbess

Hildegard von Bingen, a 12th-century German abbess, will be the subject of a slide-talk in German by Annette von Esser on Thursday at 7 P.M. in the meeting room of the Greenwich Arts Council, 299 Greenwich Avenue. The program is sponsored by Freunde Deutscher Sprache, the German Club of Greenwich.

Ms. von Esser is an authority on the subject of Abbess von Bingen, also known as St. Hildegard, although she was never canonized. She died in 1179 at the age of 81 and was well known in her own time for her visions and prophecies.

Her name and history faded into obscurity over the centuries, but lately she has been accruing recognition from scholars as an author, theological thinker, playwright and composer. Hildegard's musical plays were performed in the convent she founded near Bingen, and she corresponded frequently with emperors and theologians who appear to have accepted and admired her work.

Ms. von Esser, a native of Cologne, studied and taught theology in Germany and is currently a Ph.D. candidate in psychiatry and religion at the Union Theological Seminary in Manhattan. Admission to the talk is $7, and more information may be obtained by calling (203) 661-2133.

On Wildflower Meadows

Growing a wildflower meadow in preference to a mowed lawn is gaining acceptance, even popularity, but can be very frustrating. ''Wildflower Meadows: Let's Get Real!'' is the title of a program at the Bartlett Arboretum in Stamford to be held next Saturday from 10 A.M. to 2:30 P.M.

Wildflower species can be as picky as the most delicate hybrid perennial when it comes to soil and climate. Larry Weaner, an award-winning landscape designer since 1972 who focuses on naturalistic flora, will conduct the session. The number is (203) 322-6971 for reservations at $50; bring lunch.
