Allen Mandelbaum, Translator of ‘Divine Comedy,’ Dies at 85

By WILLIAM GRIMES
Published: November 5, 2011

Allen Mandelbaum, whose fluid, sensitive English version of Dante’s “Divine Comedy” stamped his reputation as one of the world’s premier translators of Italian and classical poetry, died on Oct. 27 in Winston-Salem, N.C. He was 85.

His son, Jonathan, said he died after a long illness.

Mr. Mandelbaum was well known for his translations of the modern Italian poets Giuseppe Ungaretti and Salvatore Quasimodo, and for his “Aeneid,” which won the National Book Award in 1973. His verse translation of “The Divine Comedy” was published in three volumes by the University of California Press in the early 1980s and was later brought out by Bantam in an inexpensive paperback edition that is still used widely in college courses.

Mr. Mandelbaum’s translation was immediately embraced by scholars and teachers as a highly readable, vigorous rendition of Dante’s epic, all the more impressive for being a faithful line-by-line rendering of the original rather than a loosely poetic reinterpretation.

“Here sighs and lamentations and loud cries/were echoing across the starless air,/so that, as soon as I set out, I wept,” Mr. Mandelbaum translated the entry of Dante’s narrator into the precincts of hell in Canto III of “Inferno.” It continued:

Strange utterances, horrible pronouncements,

accents of anger, words of suffering,

and voices shrill and faint, and beating hands —

all went to make a tumult that will whirl

forever through that turbid, timeless air,

like sand that eddies when a whirlwind swirls.

The translator Robert Fagles greeted Mr. Mandelbaum’s work as “exactly what we have waited for these years, a Dante with clarity, eloquence, terror and profoundly moving depths.”

Nancy Vickers, the president of the Dante Society of America, said in a telephone interview on Friday: “It is extremely readable, very responsible to Dante’s text and accessible, in the sense of being affordable. It is the perfect Dante for humanities courses and the first exposure that students will have to the text.”

Allen Mandelbaum was born on May 14, 1926, in Albany. The family moved from city to city during his childhood, but by the time he was 13 had settled in Manhattan.

After graduating from Yeshiva University he earned a master’s degree from Columbia in 1946 and a doctorate in 1951, both in English and comparative literature. He was named to the Society of Fellows at Harvard and spent most of the next 15 years in Italy, where he began translating Italian poetry. On returning to the United States, he taught English and comparative literature at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, where he was the executive officer, or chairman, of the doctoral program in English. In 1989 he was named W.R. Kenan professor of humanities at Wake Forest University.

He wrote several volumes of his own poetry, including “Journeyman” (1967), “Leaves of Absence” (1976) , “Chelmaxioms: The Maxims, Axioms and Maxioms of Chelm” (1977), “A Lied of Letterpress for Moser and McGrath” (1980) and “The Savantasse of Montparnasse” (1988).

His translation of Dante was followed by “Lectura Dantis,” a projected three volumes of scholarly canto-by-canto commentary on Dante’s text, with Mr. Mandelbaum as a general editor. The volume on “Inferno” was published in 1988, and the volume on “Purgatorio” was published in 2008.

After completing “The Divine Comedy,” Mr. Mandelbaum translated Homer’s “Odyssey” in 1990 and Ovid’s “Metamorphoses” in 1993.

In addition to his son, Jonathan, he is survived by his wife, Marjorie, and two grandchildren.

In 2000 Mr. Mandelbaum became the first translator to receive the Gold Medal of Honor from the city of Florence, Italy, which conferred the award for his “Divine Comedy” on the 735th anniversary of Dante’s birth.
