For War of 1812 Bicentennial, Indifference From Albany

Nathaniel Brooks for The New York Times

The Sackets Harbor Battlefield State Historic Site. New York will forgo a statewide bicentennial. More Photos »

By THOMAS KAPLAN
Published: November 24, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

SACKETS HARBOR, N.Y. — Clayton F. Nans, a 57-year-old retired Marine colonel, halted his Chevrolet pickup truck near Lake Ontario recently and pointed to a grassy embankment. It was the very spot, he said, where British troops first made landfall in this upstate village, before American soldiers fought off their invasion.

That engagement was two centuries ago, in the Second Battle of Sackets Harbor, a key New York moment in the War of 1812, whose bicentennial is fast approaching. And Mr. Nans, who periodically dons the blue woolen coat of an early 19th-century Marine as a War of 1812 re-enactor, is upset that New York State is not doing anything to commemorate it.

“I know people who can go their whole life and will be just as happy not to know about any War of 1812,” said Mr. Nans, who, as the uniform attests, is quite happy to know about the war: a life-size cutout of him and two of his children in period costume is on display in the local 1812 visitors’ center.

“This community thinks it’s important,” he said.

The war, which was fought between the United States and Great Britain over maritime rights, led to the burning of Washington, the drafting of “The Star-Spangled Banner” and the popularization of an American hero, Andrew Jackson, who later became president. During the war, the United States attacked Canada, which was British, and the Great Lakes region was a war theater, with battles in New York State from Buffalo to Plattsburgh. Sackets Harbor, about 60 miles north of Syracuse, was the American military’s Lake Ontario headquarters, home to several thousand soldiers, a 32-pound cannon and a fleet of shallow-draft warships.

Around North America, local and national governments are preparing to mark the anniversary. The Canadian government has earmarked $28 million to support as many as 100 battle re-enactments and commemorative events, as well as a new monument in the Ottawa area and the restoration of historic sites, and even an iPhone app that teaches about the war. American states from Michigan to Virginia have been planning for the milestone for years, setting up fireworks displays and war-themed license plates and educational programs.

But for three years in a row, governors of New York have vetoed legislation to set up a statewide bicentennial commission to plan events for the anniversary of the war, which has long been overshadowed by the more celebrated Revolutionary and Civil Wars. Gov. David A. Paterson, who vetoed the measure in 2009 and 2010, and Gov. Andrew M. Cuomo, who vetoed it in September, said New York’s tight finances meant the state could not afford to mount its own effort.

In a veto message, Mr. Paterson emphasized that he fully supported remembering the conflict and mused about the importance of honoring soldiers for their sacrifice. Mr. Cuomo was terser: he wrote that “although cultural and historical tourism is an important industry,” his administration believed the commission would cost the state $1.4 million, an unacceptable sum.

“It’s frankly kind of embarrassing,” said Keith Herkalo, the city clerk in Plattsburgh and the president of the Battle of Plattsburgh Association. “Wouldn’t we, collectively, as a state, want to tout our heritage? And the answer is, haplessly, no.”

Mr. Herkalo has not given up. He is now hoping that Plattsburgh can get grant money from another source — the State of Maryland. Maryland is seeking to spend $25 million in what Gov. Martin O’Malley has called a “once-in-a-lifetime opportunity” to teach about his state’s history.

In New York, long-ago wars have not inspired much recent excitement. While the 250th anniversary of the French and Indian War warranted the creation of its own commission when George E. Pataki was governor, a re-enactment last year of the war’s final battle had to be scaled back because of a drawn-out budget battle in Albany.

War of 1812 enthusiasts had expressed the hope that a coordinated, statewide campaign of bicentennial re-enactments and other events would draw thousands of people to battlefields and visitor centers in the farthest reaches of upstate New York, often economically struggling areas. Instead, Constance B. Barone, who manages the Sackets Harbor historic site for the State Office of Parks, Recreation and Historic Preservation, is planning more modest events on an ad hoc basis, including some in partnership with Canadian officials. War of 1812 sites around New York, as well as the State Archives and other historical organizations, are also planning events, and the New York Council for the Humanities is offering $3,000 grants to sponsor war-themed programming.

“Everyone’s kind of doing their own thing,” Ms. Barone said.

Here in Sackets Harbor, a village of 1,450 which overlooks Black River Bay and was settled in 1801 as a shipping outpost, the old battlefield is the heart of the community, like a New England town green, with a granite monument and a grove of 100 maple trees planted in 10 columns of 10 for the war’s centennial. A berm not much larger than a baseball diamond is all that remains of Fort Kentucky, one of the earthen strongholds built here during the war.

A small state-run visitors’ center is in a stately butterscotch brick building that was built in the 1840s as a lieutenant’s house. Today, it has exhibits showing this village’s role in the war, a small gift shop selling wooden toy muskets and a basement filled with re-enactment supplies.

The battlefield, which now hosts re-enactments, community picnics and the occasional paranormal investigation, is a state historic site, though not one of the most popular ones. It was scheduled for closing in a round of budget cuts during the Paterson administration, and although it was ultimately spared, tourist traffic is light enough that it is still handing out brochures produced during the administration of Gov. Eliot Spitzer.

The area has a strong connection to, and reverence for, the military. An Army base, Fort Drum, is about 20 miles away; a sign draped over a window of a residence on the main thoroughfare here welcomed an Army major back from a tour abroad; a scarecrow outside an ice cream shop is dressed in fatigues.

But the brick and stone buildings of a long-shuttered military base, Madison Barracks, sit in various states of decay, with some redeveloped into apartments. In an overgrown field behind an abandoned gas station, a monument erected in 1930 by the United States Daughters of 1812 stands all but forgotten.

Among the community of re-enactors trying to keep the memory of the War of 1812 alive locally is Theodore Schofield, 46, who works in social services for Jefferson County. Mr. Schofield had been a Civil War re-enactor, but he tired of driving to Virginia and Maryland for events, and he also has a family connection to the War of 1812 — two relatives were stationed here as militiamen around the time of the war.

This summer, Mr. Schofield played three roles in War of 1812 living-history demonstrations: in a New York militiaman’s uniform; in civilian garb; and in a Navy uniform, consisting of a short-waisted coat, a scarlet vest, a round hat that had been varnished to make it waterproof and a boarding cutlass.

“And that’s just some of my stuff,” Mr. Schofield said. “For camp stuff, I have lots and lots more — I have tents, canteens, I have two muskets, a boarding pistol.”

In upstate New York, at least, he is not unique in his enthusiasm for the subject, or in his desire to share it. At the visitors’ center, Mr. Nans, who is also a board member of the Sackets Harbor Battlefield Alliance, reached into a gray metal locker and pulled out a cylindrical uniform cap, called a shako, bearing the emblem for an American artilleryman and a white plume. He was apologetic, noting that the white plume would have denoted an infantryman.

“Actually, there would be a red plume,” Mr. Nans said.
