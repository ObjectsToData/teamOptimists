Academic Freedom, With Strings Attached?

By D.D. GUTTENPLAN
Published: April 3, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

LONDON — Has the Big Society, Prime Minister David Cameron’s term for the outpouring of public spirit and community engagement that he hoped would replace Britain’s shrinking public sector, turned into Big Brother?

That was the question that had the country’s academics buzzing last week following claims that the government had pressured — or in some versions, ordered — researchers in the arts and humanities to study the Big Society or risk having their funding eliminated.

All sides agree that the Department for Business, Innovation and Skills, in guidelines issued in December, stated that the government has “some key national strategic priorities” and that “it is appropriate for Ministers to ask Research Councils,” which oversee publicly financed research in Britain, to “consider how best they can contribute to these priorities.”

The document also said that the Arts and Humanities Research Council, which distributes around £100 million a year, or $160 million, “will direct a significant part of its funding into six strategic research areas,” the first of which was given as “communities and big society.”

“A.H.R.C. will systematically address issues relating to social cohesion, community engagement and cultural renewal contributing to the ‘Big Society’ initiative,” the guidelines said.

Meanwhile, the British Academy, a group of scholars in the humanities and social sciences who have long given out smaller grants to individual researchers, agreed to end that program, concentrating instead on fellowships “that contribute to national priorities.”

But that is where agreement ends. Although the dispute has been simmering for months, matters reached a turning point here last month when two historians accused the government of abandoning the principle that academic research should be immune from political interference.

Peter Mandler, professor of modern cultural history at the University of Cambridge and a vice president of the Royal Historical Society, said in a blog post that while peer review — the process whereby experts in any given field decide which projects are worthy of funding — “remains supreme in deciding which specific proposals” to finance, “the priorities now can and will come from government.”

At the same time Iain Pears, an Oxford art historian and best-selling novelist, wrote an article in the London Review of Books that claimed that “under cover of reasserting” the independence of academic research, the government “is pursuing a stealthy policy of undermining it.”

“It is all very Soviet,” Mr. Pears said in an interview. “The problem is that the British system is so dependent on government funding that once the government decides to use its supply of the money to gain control it’s very difficult to resist.”

The charges were denied by the government. “Prioritization of an individual research council’s spending within its allocation is not a decision for ministers,” said Lucy Bell, a spokeswoman for the Department for Business, Innovation and Skills. “The government supports this principle as vital for the protection of academic independence and excellence.”

“We explicitly refute that allegation,” Jake Gilmore, a spokesman for the A.H.R.C., said in an interview. The council’s decision to encourage research on “connected communities” goes back to 2008, he said — before the current government came to power.

“This focus predates the ‘Big Society’ vision by at least two years. Whether Gordon Brown, Nick Clegg or David Cameron were prime minister we would still be funding this because we think it’s incredibly important,” Mr. Gilmore said. “Sure, we used the language of the current government. You’re always going to use the language of the people you’re talking to.”

Robin Jackson, the British Academy’s chief executive, said in an e-mail that “there was no external pressure” to follow the government’s agenda. But Stefan Collini, a professor of intellectual history at Cambridge and a fellow of the academy, said the government has “more and more tried to tie strings to the money going to researchers.” The British Academy, he added “is almost wholly dependent on government funding. We were led to understand it was agree on these terms or perhaps funding would disappear.”
