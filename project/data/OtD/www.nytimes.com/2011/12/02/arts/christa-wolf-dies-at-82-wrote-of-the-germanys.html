Christa Wolf Dies at 82; Wrote of the Germanys

By DAVID BINDER and BRUCE WEBER
Published: December 1, 2011

Christa Wolf, a leading writer from the former East Germany whose novels, stories and essays explored the weight of history on ordinary individuals, especially and controversially including her own struggles with the legacy of Nazism and life in a Communist society, died Thursday in Berlin. She was 82.

Her death was announced by the publishing house Suhrkamp, which did not disclose a cause.

Ms. Wolf, who grew up in Germany under the Nazis, led a philosophically angst-ridden life that played out in her work. In novels like “Divided Heaven,” “The Quest for Christa T.,” “Cassandra” and “A Model Childhood,” she wrote about characters, largely women, whose daily lives were deeply colored by the political systems that governed them.

Strongly autobiographical and gravely moral, her books were widely read in both East and West Germany, and over the years she became respected as a kind of public conscience of a long-divided people. Still, she was criticized as being insufficiently outraged by the repressiveness of the East German regime, and her reputation was tainted by her opposition to the dissolution of the German Democratic Republic, as East Germany was officially known, even as the Berlin Wall was coming down.

Another blow to her standing came in 1993, with the revelation that she briefly served as an informant for the East German secret police in the early 1960s.

She was difficult to categorize — a loyal dissident, a critic of the regime, but a believer in socialist ideals. She argued that “an intense and radical confrontation” with the Nazi past was required of Germans of her generation.

She believed that Communism provided the means for the nation to cleanse its people of the residue of fascism, and she continued to live in East Germany even as the repressive elements of life there made her more and more disappointed in the socialist system.

“We in our part of Germany confronted German fascism uncompromisingly and thoroughly,” she said in 1989. “Today many of us see that we were in danger of just substituting one doctrine of salvation for another.”

In her work, Ms. Wolf presented everyday human dramas set against threatening backgrounds of war or repression or, in the case of the novel “Accident/A Day’s News,” environmental apocalypse. The book tells the story of a woman awaiting word about her brother’s brain surgery on the same day as the nuclear reactor breakdown at Chernobyl.

She examined issues of individual identity, artistic expression and personal responsibility under totalitarian rule. She addressed male domination and the social roles of women, national guilt, cultural conformity and the meaning of memory.

Her prose, described by the novelist Mary Gordon as “extremely dense; her structure is an intricately connected web of associations,” was nonetheless capable of aching emotion.

“Christa T.,” for example, is about an ordinary young wife and mother who felt revulsion at the shallow materialism of West Berlin, but later grew stultified and alienated in the arid collectivism of the German Democratic Republic.

In an early passage of the novel, the narrator, a college friend, begins a search for meaning in her friend’s life after Christa has died of leukemia:

“She’s disappearing. No ears now to hear complaints with; no eyes to see tears with; no mouth with which to answer reproaches. The complaints, tears and reproaches are still with us, and they are useless. Finally we’re shown the door and we try to find consolation in the oblivion people call memory.”

The book was criticized by the Communist Party as “a pessimistic attempt to replace Marx with Freud,” and published only in a limited edition in East Germany before gaining wider release in West Germany. This was a fall from grace for Ms. Wolf, whose previous book, “Divided Heaven,” about a family broken apart by the division of the Germanys, won a national prize.

Her work in the 1970s and ’80s earned her a reputation as a subtle but confirmed dissident, using her fame and the freedom that came with it to work within the system. Besides “Accident/A Day’s News,” her books in those decades include “A Model Childhood” (also known as “Patterns of Childhood”), an account of being indoctrinated into Nazism as a child; “Cassandra,” a history of the Trojan War as seen through the eyes of the doomed prophetess; and “No Place on Earth,” a meditation on artistic freedom set in the early 19th century.

Correction: December 9, 2011

An obituary on Dec. 2 about the author Christa Wolf referred incorrectly to her birthplace. The town where she was born, Landsberg, now known as Gorzow and part of Poland, was in the German province of Brandenburg, not Silesia.
