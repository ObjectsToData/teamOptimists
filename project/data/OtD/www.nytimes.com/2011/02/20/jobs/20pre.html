Independent Scholars: A Nomadic Lot

By KATHERINE WENTWORTH RINNE
Published: February 19, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

IN 1992, I quit my teaching job at the University of Arkansas, put everything into storage, cashed in my life savings — a stupendous $10,000 — and moved to Rome to begin the first phase of research for a project that has become my life’s work. My money ran out nine months later, but by then the project had begun to take on a life of its own, and I had become an independent scholar.

Patricia Jessee

Katherine Rinne, an independent scholar, in Rome, where a marker from 1495 noted a Tiber River flood.

The project is an online cartographic history of the urban development of Rome, focusing on how the city has been shaped by its water infrastructure for nearly 3,000 years. I thought that if I could help architecture, landscape and planning students to understand how water helped to define Rome, I might provide a model for looking at other cities.

Why did I take this career turn? I felt an urgency about my project, and knew that as a full-time faculty member I wouldn’t have time to focus on it until I was eligible for tenure in the distant future.

So now, having made the leap, I have become a member of an itinerant tribe in pursuit of ideas, knowledge and a place to write and do research. We are motivated and often well educated, and most of us work far longer than 40-hour weeks. Many of us could take high-five- or low- six-figure professional positions, even in today’s economy, but we have chosen to forgo financial security for the freedom to pursue work we love.

As independent scholars, we often don’t know where our next check will come from. We have waited tables, or answered phones for $7 an hour, or taken other temporary jobs just to have the freedom to do our own work. While others dream of new cars and exotic vacations, we dream of rent-controlled apartments and Guggenheim Fellowships.

This profession has a venerable history. In past centuries throughout Asia, the Middle East and Europe, talented scholars, musicians and artists actively sought out princely or imperial patrons, often living in their palaces and receiving a stipend for musical compositions, histories, poems, paintings or treatises.

Times have changed. Not many princes are looking for resident philosophers, poets or scholars these days. Funds now come from governments or foundations that may be seriously short on money. Scientists and mathematicians receive the largest amounts, while funds for arts and humanities work become scarcer each year.

Instead of living as palace guests, we take housemates, occupy garrets or sublets and do a lot of house-sitting, especially during the leanest times. We rely a great deal on our friends. We are often peripatetic. Over the last two decades, I have lived — in chronological order — in Rome; Los Angeles; Boston; Seattle; Rome again; Charlottesville, Va.; Boston again; Ames, Iowa; Rome again; Boston again; Washington; Rome again; Berkeley, Calif.; back to Rome, and again to Berkeley, where I have been for three years. It is my fervent hope that I will never have to move again.

Six of those years were funded, with residence in other cities a condition of acceptance. For five summers when I lacked funding, I returned to work at the generous invitation of my former employer, an architectural and urban design firm in Los Angeles. At other times, I have taught university classes. I have even been able to teach in Rome, where I can directly integrate my research into my architectural design studio and seminar teaching.

My situation is not that different from that of other independent scholars and writers. While a peripatetic life provides tantalizing material for the raconteur, it presents many challenges, including financial stability. Moving almost annually is exhausting and expensive, even though most of my belongings were in storage until my last move to Berkeley.

Health insurance is often only a dream, and the wildly fluctuating income can be nerve-racking and depressing. In unfunded years, I have earned as little as $8,000 to $10,000 in pretax dollars. In funded years, I have made $13,000 to $80,000. Needless to say, financial planning is hard, and building equity nearly impossible.

Nonetheless, I have developed a strategy that allows a modicum of stability. I live lightly. I still wear the good clothes that I bought when I had a “real” job many years ago. I have been fortunate to house-sit for, or live with, many of my friends, sometimes for months on end. In exchange, I baby-sit, care for pets or clean up after an earthquake. My gardening services are in particular demand.

At my nadir, I sold all my stocks and bonds, yet I have added to my I.R.A. each year, even if it’s only $500, not $5,000.

Enormous and far from complete, my project has taken over my professional life. But, finally, after years of research and writing, my first book, “The Waters of Rome: Aqueducts, Fountains, and the Birth of the Baroque City,” has been published by Yale University Press.

Had I known at the beginning what I’d have to endure to reach this point, I doubt that I would have had the courage to plunge ahead. Fortunately, I was utterly oblivious of the consequences.
