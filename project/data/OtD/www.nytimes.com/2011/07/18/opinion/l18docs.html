Test Medical Students on People Skills?

Published: July 17, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

To the Editor:

Enlarge This Image
Emily Flake

Re “New for Aspiring Doctors, the People Skills Test” (“Doctors Inc.” series, front page, July 11):

The multiple mini interview has pitfalls. Specifically, some applicants are better positioned to perform well because of past experiences and personality type rather than because of inherent personal qualities.

For example, applicants with backgrounds that have offered opportunities to perfect communication skills will have an advantage.

The M.M.I. also favors extroverts who typically possess superb interpersonal skills at the expense of the more introverted applicant, one with less “real world” experience or an applicant with fewer resources who may have less experience navigating ethical discussions.

Thus, the multiple mini interview may “weed out” talented applicants who have the compassion and capacity for great “people skills” but have not had the time or opportunity to nurture them.

The article didn’t mention that the M.M.I. format often assesses applicants’ ability to work in a team by having them complete simple tasks together or with an “interviewer,” who plays the role of a team member.

JESSICA FREEDMAN
New York, July 11, 2011

The writer, an emergency physician, is president of MedEdits Medical Admissions, a consulting firm, and the author of “The Medical School Interview.”

To the Editor:

Sadly, medical schools are still seeking ways to identify students who have people skills. High science scores and faculty interviews by research academicians, traditionally the two major selection criteria, by their nature fail to find good communicators.

Forty years ago, I enrolled in medical school. We were encouraged to major in a humanities field to provide us a well-rounded experience and prepare us to add empathy and communication to our medical toolbox.

I majored in English literature. Ninety-five percent of my fellow students were physics, biology, math or chemistry majors.

Increasingly, we see that medical schools are accepting students who majored in nonscience subjects. This is a trend that is advantageous to a profession where diagnosis and treatment are an equal measure of art and science.

But while speed ethics tests are at best an intriguing experiment, at worst they are the latest gimmick. Medical schools might try looking at the whole person.

BERNARD J. MANSHEIM
Daufuskie Island, S.C., July 11, 2011

The writer is a retired internist and infectious diseases specialist.

To the Editor:

The notion of choosing medical students for their social skills is unrealistic. First, select brilliant students, and then cultivate their social skills.

Charm won’t save a patient’s life. The approach suggested by Virginia Tech Carilion, the country’s newest medical school, will foster mediocrity.

ROBERT A. FISCHL
Stamford, Conn., July 11, 2011

The writer is a retired plastic and reconstructive surgeon.

To the Editor:

I am surprised that anyone would trust mini interviews for medical school applicants to reveal “character flaws” that might lead doctors to bully nurses and fail to listen to patients. Many potential bullies will be slick enough to be polite and receptive in an artificial situation where they know that their personalities are being assessed.

I suspect that the stressful mini interviews might screen out not bullies, but mildly awkward people who would be fine when dealing with real patients and nurses.

FELICIA NIMUE ACKERMAN
Providence, R.I., July 11, 2011

A version of this letter appeared in print on July 18, 2011, on page A18 of the New York edition with the headline: Test Medical Students on People Skills?.
