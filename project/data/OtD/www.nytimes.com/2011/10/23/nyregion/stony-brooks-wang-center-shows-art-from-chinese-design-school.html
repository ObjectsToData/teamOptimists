Trash Reinvented

Barton Silverman/The New York Times

REUSE OR NEW USE Lu Kaiquan painted musical notations on condoms pulled over laboratory tubes.

By KARIN LIPSON
Published: October 21, 2011

STONY BROOK UNIVERSITY is known for its scientific enterprise, but the 8-foot-tall robot lurking these days in the Charles B. Wang Center was not conceived as a high-tech wonder.

Its creator, Wei Zekai, a 23-year-old art student from Shanghai, was aiming mostly for old rather than new: His genial, super-size metallic man, part of an exhibition of works by art and design students from China, is made of materials that include hosing from a washing machine, the innards of an outdated computer, a DVD player, bicycle parts and other discarded objects he found on the streets of Shanghai and at a city recycling center. Where others might see debris, Mr. Wei saw the makings of an art project that would be a commentary on contemporary culture.

With the rapid development of China, “there’s a growing amount of electronic waste we see in daily life,” Mr. Wei said early this month as he tinkered with his robot, called “Superman,” before the show opened.

Working nearby were some of the 13 other students from East China Normal University in Shanghai who, with representatives from the faculty and the administration, had come to mount the show, “Recycling and Reincarnation: An Exhibition of Industrial Design, Installation and Mixed-Media Art,” which is on view in the Wang Center’s lower lobbies through Nov. 6.

With more than 50 pieces by at least 70 artists, “Recycling and Reincarnation” showcases the work of students attending the School of Design at East China Normal University. Additionally, one piece, “First Banquet,” was created at the Wang Center in collaboration with Stony Brook art students. (The title, with its promise of more intellectual nourishment to come, may suggest a nascent trend: Samuel L. Stanley Jr., the president of Stony Brook University, and Yu Lizhong, the president of East China Normal University, have signed an agreement committing the universities to future academic exchanges.)

The recycling theme was chosen for its international resonance, said the exhibition’s curator, Wang DaZhou. An artist and industrial designer with a home in Great Neck, Mr. Wang (no relation to the Wang Center’s namesake) founded the School of Design in Shanghai; he spends about half the year there as a professor.

“Recycling and Reincarnation” grew out of conversations between Mr. Wang and Sunita S. Mukhi, the director of Asian and Asian-American programs at the Wang Center. (They met several years ago while serving on the Suffolk County executive’s Asian-American Advisory Board.)

The Wang Center, which focuses on Asian and Asian-American cultures, has never before had an exhibition from China, Dr. Mukhi said. Yet China has a significant presence on campus; more than a third of all international students at Stony Brook are Chinese citizens, said William Arens, the dean of international academic programs and director of the Confucius Institute at Stony Brook University, a language and cultural group and a co-presenter of the exhibition.

China has long sent engineering and science students to Stony Brook, Dr. Arens said. But “the show is a reflection of an interest in cooperating with American universities in areas which include the arts and humanities,” he said.

The interwoven concept of recycling and reincarnation was the idea of Mr. Wang, according to Dr. Mukhi. “It’s a green idea, and it’s also a spiritual idea; it has a layered meaning,” she said. By reusing old objects, “you’re rejuvenating them, giving them life.”

In her work, “Retained,” Luo Yi, 23, symbolically gives new life to old, torn snapshots by placing them in Petri dishes (the shallow containers in which biologists grow cell cultures). “The Petri dishes are a way to recover and create memories, like cells,” said Ms. Luo, who, like most of the artists, spoke largely through Stony Brook student interpreters and Mr. Wang.

The installation of Chen LiLi, featuring vintage clothing draped over horizontal bamboo poles, recycles her own childhood in a sense. Until the age of 10, she lived with her mother, father and grandmother in two rooms of a “lane house”— Shanghai’s distinctive three-story structure built along narrow courtyards. Many such traditional homes have given way to high-rises in modern Shanghai.

When she was a child, Ms. Chen said in halting English, she would often pass beneath a colorful panoply of laundry, drying on bamboo poles that straddled the windows facing one another on her lane. Now 25, Ms. Chen has adapted that recollection for “Relations,” her piece on display.

The “relations,” Mr. Wang further explained, are the sense of community that flourished, albeit at a loss of privacy, in the old lane houses. “At the time, if you cook noodle soup, you can hand it to your neighbor across the window,” he said. The idea of Ms. Chen’s work is “to bring that social relationship of that old time to today.”

The exhibition, with its emphasis on found objects, features some unusual materials and transformations. For instance, camel’s hair is rubbed into linen batting in an installation by Mao Xin Ru, and a discarded toilet becomes an homage to the Dadaist artist Marcel Duchamp in a work by Lu Kaiquan.

In another piece, called “Music School,” Mr. Lu, 26, has employed what must be the most surprising medium in the exhibition: a collection of condoms, pulled over laboratory tubes. Around each condom, the artist has delicately painted a musical score from either the East or the West.

“He has tried to come up with a new form of life — a more cheerful life,” Mr. Wang said. Mr. Lu had his own, brief explanation. With their musical notations, the condoms are “like a concert,” he said.

“Recycling and Reincarnation: An Exhibition of Industrial Design, Installation and Mixed-Media Art” is on view through Nov. 6 in the Zodiac and Skylight lobbies of the Charles B. Wang Center, Stony Brook University; stonybrook.edu/wang. Exhibition is free. Monday to Friday, 9 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday, 9:30 a.m. to 8 p.m.
