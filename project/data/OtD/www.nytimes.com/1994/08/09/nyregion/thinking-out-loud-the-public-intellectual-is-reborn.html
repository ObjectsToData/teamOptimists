Thinking Out Loud: The Public Intellectual Is Reborn

By JANNY SCOTT
Published: August 9, 1994

It was one more attack on the condition of universities, this one on the cover of The Atlantic Monthly, that drove the young professor to wander off the academic reservation and fire back from a decidedly non-scholastic publication, The Village Voice.

The professor, Michael Berube, had no idea how his unorthodox behavior might affect his chances of getting tenure. But the time seemed ripe for a good polemic, rather than another juiceless article in a scholarly journal.

Professor Berube, who now writes regularly for The Village Voice while teaching English at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, is one of a growing number of scholars who are trying to reach beyond the boundaries of academic life to speak to broader audiences on broader issues.

Professors in fields like English are putting aside their ponderings of prosody in Tennyson and their ruminations on post-structuralism and deconstruction to take up subjects like race relations, biotechnology, sexual equality and the future of higher education.

Some are firing off manuscripts to mainstream magazines. A few are appearing on the op-ed pages of daily newspapers. Others have started publications aimed at encouraging scholars to stop talking just to each other and start speaking with policy makers and the well-educated public.

Like Professor Berube, many are liberals roused to action by the recent conservative assault on trends in education. But the new vogue also reflects a wider dissatisfaction with what many describe as the introversion and specialization of academic life in recent decades.

"There is unmistakably a rapidly growing interest in something that we could call the public intellectual," said Thomas Bender, a New York University a historian who is the author of "New York Intellect" (Alfred A. Knopf, 1987), an intellectual history of the city. "I think the public wants it, to some extent. Certain academic scholars want to be public intellectuals as well." And, he said, universities have learned they can no longer afford to neglect the outside world.

The notion has a special appeal in New York, where the likes of Lionel Trilling and Irving Howe once shaped the city's cultural life, and where some have seen that generation's passing as proof that the species was dying out.

It also appeals to scholars on the left, feeling bruised by the assault from the right. Conservatives, on the other hand, call it left-wing wishful thinking. They say public intellectuals never went away; they just became conservatives working out of think tanks.

"There's a certain lamentation on the left that left-wing intellectuals aren't as numerous or sparkling as they once were," said Irving Kristol, the neoconservative commentator and editor. They may be trying for a revival, he said, "but I don't think they've been all that successful."

Hilton Kramer, editor of The New Criterion, a conservative journal of cultural criticism, said: "The great mistake is to identify public intellectuals with academics. Most of the most serious intellectual discourse for some years has not come out of the academy. The academy is intellectually dead."

Some academics, too, have their doubts. They say scholars should stay out of politics and stick to what they do best. One skeptical professor described one new public-intellectual journal as "a bulletin board for fashionable academics to talk about things they know little about." If Not TV, What?

Others say few outlets remain open to intellectuals.

"Television seems only to want intellectuals who will scream at one another about politics," said Stanley Katz, the president of the American Council of Learned Societies, an umbrella group of organizations of scholars. "If there were an Edmund Wilson, do you think what's-her-name at The New Yorker would be running him? I don't think so."

Not surprisingly, there is disagreement about the definition of public intellectual (and the definition of intellectual and the definition of public). But many say such a person uses both deep knowledge and broad speculative ability to speak with authority on a wide range of issues of the day.

Some invoke the names of Jean-Paul Sartre and Vaclav Havel. Others point to the cover of The New York Review of Books. Oft-cited examples are Henry Louis Gates Jr. and Cornel West, who have moved far beyond the boundaries of their academic disciplines to write for wide audiences on race and other issues.

"It's true that people like me have been there all along," said Morris Dickstein, a professor of English at the Graduate School of the City University of New York who is also a cultural critic and author. "But what was fashionable in the university was something else."

This year, the public intellectual is on the agenda of the New York Institute for the Humanities, a collection of heavy-thinking New Yorkers, which is planning a conference in December to explore issues like the ethical responsibilities of intellectuals and their place in politics. Intellectuals on the Air

The National Humanities Center in Research Triangle Park, N.C., has applied for financing for a radio series on public intellectuals that would air over 18 months on 300 stations. It is to include conversations with such publicly visible academicians as Helen Vendler, Stephen Jay Gould and Professor Gates.
