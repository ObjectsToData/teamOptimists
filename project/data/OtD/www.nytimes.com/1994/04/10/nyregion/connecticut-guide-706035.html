CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: April 10, 1994

HARTFORD'S SYNAGOGUES

An extensive tour of Hartford's historic synagogues will be led on Thursday by David F. Ransom and Mary Donohue, architectural historians of the Connecticut Historical Society. The excursion, planned by the Jewish Historical Society of Greater Hartford and the Hartford Architecture Conservancy, will begin at 9:30 A.M. at the Jewish Community Center, 335 Bloomfield Avenue in West Hartford.

Included in the tour are drive-bys of the synagogues in Hartford's North End and Blue Hills neighborhoods, and visits to the former Emanuel and Agudas Achim Synagogues, the Charter Oak Cultural Center (formerly Beth Israel Synagogue) and Congregation Beth Israel.

Transportation by motor coach and a kosher lunch of turkey, tuna salad or egg salad are included in the fee of $26. The tour will end around 3:30 P.M. For more information or to register, the number is 236-4571. YALE'S TAKE ON VASARI

Giorgio Vasari, 1511-1571, was a painter, architect and biographer of Italian Renaissance artists. Now, he is the subject of an international symposium and exhibition to be held in New Haven at Yale. All events are open to the public free of charge.

"Vasari's Florence: Artists and Literati at the Medicean Court" begins with an exhibition on Thursday featuring drawings, prints, books and manuscripts of Vasari and his contemporaries. It may be seen through May 15 in the Yale Art Gallery at Chapel and York Streets, Tuesday through Saturday from 10 to 5 and Sundays from 2 to 5.

A keynote address address by John Shearman, a professor of art history at Harvard, will be given in the gallery's auditorium on Friday at 5:15, followed by a reception at the Yale Beinecke Library, and preceded by talks on the Renaissance artists from 3 to 4:30.

The symposium will continue on Saturday from 9:30 to 5:30 and on Sunday from 9:30 to 1. Eighteen specialists from universities and arts institutions in Florence, Paris, Vienna, the Netherlands, Britain and the United States, will lecture and participate in panel discussions at the gallery or at the Whitney Humanities Center. For a complete schedule or other information, 432-2977. OF GREGORIAN CHANTS

A working seminar on Gregorian chants, scheduled on Saturday from 1 to 6 in New Canaan, is meant primarily for organists, choirmasters, choir members, cantors and clergy, but anyone interested in this venerable musical genre is welcome.

Gerald Holbrook, choirmaster at St. Birgitta's convent in Darien, will conduct the program at St. Aloysius Church, 31 Cherry Street in New Canaan. The final session will consist of the singing of a Solemn Mass. Members of the public who are not parishioners may attend the singing of the Mass free of charge at 5:30, when the regularly scheduled Parish Mass will be taking place.

Learning to recognize neumatic notation, the use of solfege, conducting of chant and interpreting the rhythms, expressions and dynamics of the musical scores are some of the elements to be included in the afternoon workshops.

Admission to the seminar is $10 in advance, $15 at the door. Call David Thomas, director of music at St. Aloysius, 966-0020, for reservations or more information. 'MARY SILLIMAN'S WAR'

"Mary Silliman's War," a film about a Revolutionary War couple, set in the Town of Fairfield, will be shown as a benefit for the Connecticut Historical Society on Saturday evening in Davies Auditorium at Yale.

The title character and her husband, Major Selleck Silliman, were residents of Fairfield and American patriots at the time of the 1779 Tory raid on Connecticut. Major Silliman was kidnapped and imprisoned by the British for a year, leaving his pregnant wife to run the farm, give birth and cope with the invasion on her own.

Based on "The Way of Duty: A Woman and Her Family in Revolutionary America," a biography by Joy Day Buel and Dr. Richard Buel Jr., the film was shot in 1992 in Nova Scotia, where locations could be found that more nearly approximated 18th-century Connecticut than Connecticut itself.

Running for 93 minutes, in color, it was produced by Stephen Schecter and Barry Cowling for Heritage Films and Citadel Films, with the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Nova Scotia Development Corporation. Eventually, it is expected to be shown on cable and public television.

Partially tax-deductible tickets to the screening at 8:30 and a dessert reception afterward are $25. Reservations for cocktails at 6:30, followed by dinner, the screening and reception, are $100. More information and tickets are available at 236-5621. LEARNING ABOUT AIDS

A national traveling exhibition called "What About Aids?" aimed at people under the age of 20, may be seen through April 30 at the Science Center of Connecticut, 950 Trout Brook Drive in West Hartford.

Explicit as it is, it is nonthreatening and thought-provoking, promoting conversations among families and emphasizing the importance of prevention, since a cure seems many years away.

Models, graphics, photographs and computer presentations show how the immune system works, how it is compromised by H.I.V., and how scientists identified the virus and learned how it spreads.

A dice game played by visitors reveals how risky behavior multiplies the chances of getting the virus, and taped interviews with victims of the disease explain what it is like to live with H.I.V. and full-blown AIDS.

What can be done by individuals to protect themselves and what science is doing to find a cure is also covered, and there is a section where visitors can write personal memorials to people they knew with AIDS. In addition, more than 50 quilts made by Connecticut children, prison inmates and other volunteers will be on display. The quilts are made for infants born H.I.V.-positive or with other life-threatening diseases.

Connecticut is 6th in the nation in the number of AIDS cases per capita. There were 4,866 cases in 1993, of which 982 were in the 13 to 19 age group and 112 were pediatric. AIDS is now among the 10 top causes of death among children 1 to 4 years old.

The Science Center is open Tuesday through Saturday from 10 to 5, Sunday from noon to 5. Admission is $5 for adults, $4 for children 3 to 15 and elderly individuals. Admission will be free on weekends for young people from 12 to 20 years of age. The number for more information is 231-2824. SACRED MUSIC AT YALE

Yale's Institute of Sacred Music, an arm of its Divinity School, will present a symposium in celebration of its 20th anniversary today through Tuesday on the New Haven campus.

Special events begin today with an exhibition of art by students and faculty at 3, followed by a reception, a banquet and, at 8, an organ recital by Thomas Murray. Banquet tickets cost $25.

The symposum will culminate in a performance of Mahler's Symphony No. 8 on Tuesday at 8 in Woolsey Hall, with the New Haven Symphony Orchestra, the Yale Camerata, Yale Glee Club, New Haven Chorale, Southern Connecticut State University Chorus and Boys Choir of Trinity Church. Concert tickets are $25.

Highlights on Monday include a luncheon at 11:45 ($15) and an alumni panel discussion, "Called by the Spirit," at 3:15. A discussion of the Mahler work will be held on Tuesday at 10:30 A.M. by music faculty members, and a lecture titled "Religion and Literature" is slated for 2 P.M. on Tuesday.

The full fee, including banquet, luncheon and concert is $75, attendance solely at symposium discussions is $15. Most events will take place at the Yale Institute of Sacred Music, 409 Prospect Street. For more information or to register, the number is 432-5180. ELEANOR CHARLES
