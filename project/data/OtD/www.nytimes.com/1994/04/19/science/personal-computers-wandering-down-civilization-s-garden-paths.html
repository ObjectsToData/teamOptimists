PERSONAL COMPUTERS; Wandering Down Civilization's Garden Paths

By PETER H. LEWIS
Published: April 19, 1994

IN the course of Western civilization, April 19 has been something of a day off. It is St. Alphege (pronounced ALF-edge) of Canterbury's Day, commemorating the Archbishop of Canterbury who went to his eternal reward in 1012, axed in the head after refusing attempts to ransom him from the marauding Danes on the ground that the money should go to the poor. On the same date a scant 869 years later, in 1881, Queen Victoria's favorite Prime Minister, Benjamin Disraeli, joined St. Alphege in eternity and in doing so created the legacy of Primrose Day, a holiday observed by his party in England.

Since today's schedule is so light, historically speaking, it might be a good opportunity to explore other avenues of Western civilization that we may have avoided in school. For owners of Apple Macintosh computers, one of the more enjoyable ways to do so is with Culture 2.0, subtitled "a multimedia guide to Western Civilization."

Culture 2.0's daily cultural almanac, which yielded the information about today's date, is just a small feature of a much more ambitious program. Culture 2.0 endeavors to be nothing less than a tour guide of Western Civilization, naming and explaining the key people, events and ideas that shaped our society throughout history.

Culture 2.0 is an unusual program, earning praise not so much for fancy technical tricks (it is based on Apple's Hypercard software technology, which was long ago eclipsed by fancier ways to display information) as for the richness of its content and wit.

Content is often forgotten in the headlong rush toward the so-called information superhighway. Plutocrats, technocrats and politicians are spending lots of time and money debating the best schemes for pumping bits and bytes into our homes, schools and offices. But in the end it really will not matter whether the bits arrive on a computer diskette, a CD-ROM disk or a coaxial cable linked to the television or telephone.

What really matters is the content of the raw data, the value that can be added to the stream of factoids by seeing links and influences.

In the case of Culture 2.0, the content that matters is the selection and interpretation of events in Western culture by Walter Reinhold, a professor of music history and humanities at New York University. Professor Reinhold, N.Y.U.'s Teacher of the Year in 1992, has managed to condense about 20 years of his popular lectures into several hundred screen-size segments.

The program uses the hypertext technology to allow the user to jump from one screen, or concept, to another, instead of being forced to follow some rigid timeline. One might pursue the evolution of music from ancient Greece through the Renaissance and Baroque eras, or follow a more meandering path from Shakespeare to Disraeli to Dizzy Gillespie.

The premise is that learning comes from being able to see relationships among discrete facts, and that one cannot truly understand a facet of history out of context. Art, music, religion, philosophy and science all contribute to an understanding of the history and politics of a given time, and Culture 2.0 brings them all together.

The "multimedia" designation in the subtitle of Culture 2.0 is an overstatement, unless one counts a few computer beeps and some grainy pictures and maps as multimedia. To see the full flower of Culture 2.0's multimedia features -- the ability to see Michelangelo's art in full color, or to hear Mozart in CD-quality sound -- one must have a laser videodisk player and purchase additional software. That is the weak link in this program.

Culture 2.0 has many links to these laserdisk and CD data bases, but the average user may be frustrated that the best images and sounds are not included with the main program. For example, if the discussion turns to a specific work of art, there may be a symbol on which one may click. Nothing happens; that is, nothing happens unless you have the optional National Gallery of Art laserdisk. It is available for $99.99 plus $5 shipping from VPI/AC Video Inc. of New York City; telephone (212) 685-5522.

Culture 2.0 itself costs $249 and can be ordered from Cultural Resources Inc. of Cranford, N.J.; telephone (908) 709-1574. A separate workbook that suggests questions for classroom discussion is $45.

Although it is out of the price range of most home users, schools and libraries may be interested in another product, the $595 Western Civilization laserdisk from the Instructional Resources Corporation of Annapolis, Md.; telephone (800) 922-1711. The price includes Macintosh or Windows software that allows teachers to construct presentations using graphics, video and sound clips from the material. Retreating From the Field

Cultural Resources is a small company that cannot compete on the same level as the Microsoft Corporation, which has millions of dollars with which to acquire licensing rights to famous artwork. But it is not just the small companies that are struggling to compete against Microsoft.

The Lotus Development Corporation of Cambridge, Mass., confirmed last week that it was no longer developing major application software for Apple Macintosh systems. Lotus is the maker of the 1-2-3 spreadsheet used by millions of businesses, .

It is never a good sign when a major software developer abandons a machine. However, Lotus has never had much success with the Macintosh, and thus its retreat will not alarm most Macintosh users. Besides, the Power Macintosh's ability to work with Microsoft Windows applications means that if someone absolutely must have Lotus 1-2-3 capability, the Power Mac can provide it quite adequately.

More troubling is that Lotus may have simply surrendered to the overwhelming dominance of the Microsoft Corporation, which, despite all the attention paid to its Windows products, is by far the leading maker of Macintosh programs.
