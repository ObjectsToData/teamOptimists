Is Western Civilization Worth $20 Million?; The Gift Is Huge and Welcome, but Its Restriction to Western Studies Fuels a Debate

By JACQUES STEINBERG,
Published: December 12, 1994

Yale University called a news conference on April 17, 1991, to announce an extraordinary gift: Lee M. Bass, the youngest of four billionaire brothers from Fort Worth, had pledged $20 million to his alma mater to further its study of Western civilization.

The centerpiece of the Bass program was to be a rigorous, yearlong undergraduate course that would analyze virtual every major development in Western history, from ancient Mesopotamia to Nazi Germany. Conceived by Mr. Bass, Benno C. Schmidt Jr., then Yale's president, and Donald Kagan, the dean of Yale College, the double-credit course was to begin in the fall of 1993 and was intended to make a powerful statement -- that multiculturalism might be laying siege to the study of the West on other campuses, but not at Yale.

Nearly four years after that grand announcement, however, the course has yet to be designed, let alone offered to students. And half the Bass gift -- the $10 million that was to be spent on the new course and its teachers -- sits untouched in a college account, while fierce disagreement continues over how it should be spent.

The dispute says much about the perils of trying to expand a Western history curriculum in an era when debates about multiculturalism divide campuses. It also illustrates the risk of accepting a major donation with strings attached at a time of immense budgetary constraints.

The matter has become a cause celebre among conservatives at Yale, who feel the delay in using the money has been engineered by liberals on campus intent on undermining the teaching of a traditional curriculum. In an effort to diffuse emotions, Yale's current president, Richard C. Levin, is to fly to Texas on Tuesday to meet with Mr. Bass.

Yale is hardly the only institution to stir criticism by accepting a gift from a donor for a pet project. Earlier this fall, for example, Ahmet Ertegun, the founder of Atlantic Records and son of a Turkish diplomat, gave $3.5 million to Princeton University to start a Turkish studies program, prompting an editorial in The Daily Princetonian that questioned whether there was "demonstrated student interest" in the subject.

Citing other gifts to Princeton -- including a half-million dollars from the Ruling Prince of Lichtenstein for research on self-determination -- the student editors wrote that "the specificity of these recent donations creates the impression that wealthy benefactors can purchase a professorship to help further their agenda."

Kevin Whitelaw, a senior who is the editorial chairman of The Daily Princetonian, added: "I'm not trying to say that you can buy a program at Princeton, but I must admit that the way this has happened, you could almost make that conclusion."

Such tensions between donors wishing to dictate how their money will be spent and school officials trying to retain maximum control of that spending arise all the time at universities, said Peter Buchanan, the president and chief executive of the Council for the Advancement and Support of Education, a nonprofit advocacy group.

"If you're on the university side," said Mr. Buchanan, a former development officer at Columbia University, "it seems to me that you try to make sure you don't make commitments or tie people's hands in ways that are going to turn out to be a real problem."

At Yale, the delay in carrying out Mr. Bass's wishes can be partly attributed to the loss of the program's two champions, Dean Kagan and Mr. Schmidt, within 14 months of accepting the gift. Dean Kagan, a vociferous conservative, had repeatedly clashed with most of the faculty, which he accused of engaging in "mutual massage" with a generally liberal student body. He resigned the deanship in April 1992 to return to teaching history and classics at Yale.

A month later, during a virtual faculty revolt over steep budget cuts that he had supported, Mr. Schmidt followed suit. Mr. Schmidt, who left to become president and chief executive of the Edison Project, did not return phone messages left with his assistant. Professor Kagan declined to be interviewed.

Leaders of some conservative national and campus groups contend that the same "liberal" faculty members who had pressed for the resignations of Dean Kagan and Mr. Schmidt are leaning on Mr. Schmidt's successor, Mr. Levin, to quietly modify the Bass course.

But many of the Yale faculty members said the problem was not one of ideology, but was a question of need: there are, they said, already more than 100 undergraduate courses at Yale that deal with broad periods of Western history or thought, from Religious Studies 277A (History of Christianity in the Ancient World: Jesus to Augustine) to Humanities 221A (Medieval Europe: From 1000 to 1492).

It is now up to Mr. Levin, an economics professor who became Yale's 22d president in July 1993, to figure out what to do with the grant. Presented last fall with an outline of the Bass course by a faculty committee appointed by Mr. Schmidt, Mr. Levin rejected it, saying the course would have tied up too many of Yale's best faculty members in an effort that would benefit a limited number of students.
