G.O.P. Sounds Confident, But Unity Proves Elusive

By JAMES DAO,
Published: January 8, 1994

Though New York State Republican leaders publicly brim with confidence about their chances of unseating Gov. Mario M. Cuomo this fall, their party remains disunited in ways that remind some of the fiasco that resulted in their crushing loss to Mr. Cuomo in 1990.

At last count, 12 men were vying for the Republican nomination, but none have been able to unite the party's key factions. And while leaders of the Republican and Conservative parties have talked about uniting behind a single challenger, tensions within and between the two parties have hamstrung their search and created the appearance of disarray among the anti-Cuomo forces.

"I'd be less than candid if I didn't admit that we've had problems, problems that could impair the chances of a Republican candidate for Governor," said the Staten Island Borough President, Guy V. Molinari. "It's been more a question of whose candidates will be out there than who is the best candidate for our party." 'Might Look at It'

Many Republicans think that the best candidate would be United States Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, who has said he would consider entering the race if no one can unify the Republicans.

Republican Party leaders say the large number of potential candidates is a sign of their party's vitality and Mr. Cuomo's vulnerability. Recent public opinion polls show the Governor's approval rating hovering at 34 percent, an all-time low for him. The Republicans also point to their party's victories last year in the New York City mayor's race and the New Jersey Governor's race as evidence of voters' dismay with incumbent Democrats.

And they say that 1994 is very different from 1990, when Mr. Cuomo was soaring in opinion polls and the Republicans couldn't find anyone to challenge him. At the last minute, they selected a little-known economist, Pierre Rinfret, who was trounced by Mr. Cuomo and almost beaten by the Conservative Party candidate, Herbert London, who is again a candidate this year.

"It's a strength not a weakness to have so many candidates," said William D. Powers, chairman of the state Republican Party. "You don't have one person talking about the horrendous Cuomo legacy. You have 14 or 15 out there." Who's the Mystery Candidate?

But to some Republicans, there are unsettling similarities between the 1990 and this year. Earlier this week, for instance, Mr. Powers said he was trying to encourage an unnamed millionaire to pursue the party's nomination.

Word of the "mystery candidate" caused concern, and chuckles, in some Republican circles. "Sounds like Pierre part-two," said Jay Severin, a Republican political consultant not involved in the race, noting that Mr. Rinfret had been touted in similar tones.

Mr. London, a professor of Humanities at New York University, who is considered the leading Conservative Party candidate again this year, said the failure of the two parties to unite could lead to a repeat of 1990.

"I do think the parties look somewhat foolish. Every day they trot out another name," he said. "It looks to the man on the street as if there isn't much order, as if there is internal factions, as if the Republican Party is not geared up for victory."

In October, many viewed state Senator George Pataki, 48, of Peekskill, to be the leading Republican candidate because he appeared to have the support of Mr. Powers and Senator D'Amato, who is powerful among both Republicans and Conservatives. Mr. Pataki was seen as a strong choice in some circles because he is a fiscal conservative, says he supports abortion rights and has only a small Legislative record to attack.

But Mr. Pataki's star dimmed when another important Republican, the Senate majority leader Ralph J. Marino, of Oyster Bay, said he would oppose any candidate supported by Change-New York, a conservative group that had tried to unseat Mr. Marino in 1992. The group had played an important role in helping Mr. Pataki win his Senate seat.

As an alternative to Mr. Pataki, Mr. Marino and Mr. Molinari have urged Representative Gerald B. H. Solomon to run. Mr. Solomon, a Republican-Conservative from Glen Falls, will decide whether to join the fray in the next several weeks, said Dan Amon, his spokesman.

"It's almost becoming comical that these party leaders are trying to dictate who the candidate will be," said Richard M. Rosenbaum, a former state Republican chairman who is running. "It reminds me of the gang that couldn't shoot straight."

Among the rest of the pack, four candidates have said they plan to run in the September primary regardless of what the party does at its nominating convention. They are: Mr. London; Mr. Rosenbaum; Evan G. Galbraith, a former Ambassador to France; and J. Patrick Barrett, a former state Republican chairman and co-founder of Change-New York.

Mr. Rosenbaum and Mr. Galbraith have been building campaign warchests for months, while Mr. Barrett is a millionaire who says he is prepared to spend large amounts of his own money on a campaign.

The others considering the race are: Ronald S. Lauder, the driving force behind the New York City term-limits movement; former Representative Bill Green; Clarence D. Rappleyea, the Assembly minority leader; state Senator Roy M. Goodman of Manhattan; state Senator Dean G. Skelos of Rockville Center, and Robert L. King, the Monroe County Executive.

But overshadowing the entire pack is Mr. D'Amato. To some, his unwillingness to rule out a run represents an attempt to help Mr. Pataki by keeping other candidates from entering the race. Others suggest the state's junior Senator has supported weak candidates to make sure he remains the state's strongest Republican. Mr. D'Amato denies both charges.

"Al wants to be number one," said Julian Gingold, an adviser to Mr. Galbraith. "If he had 100 percent of state control before, now with the election of Giuliani he has only 60 percent."
