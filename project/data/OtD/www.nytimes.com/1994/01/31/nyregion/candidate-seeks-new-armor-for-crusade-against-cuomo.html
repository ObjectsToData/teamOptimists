Candidate Seeks New Armor For Crusade Against Cuomo

By JAMES DAO,
Published: January 31, 1994

It was a Herbert London kind of crowd: middle class and brimming with discontent about high taxes, failing schools, illegal immigrants, welfare mothers and Ford Foundation liberals.

Chilled by the freezing rain outside, they looked in no mood for moderate nostrums -- and the man seeking the Republican and Conservative party nominations for Governor did not disappoint. He would cut taxes deeper than Christine Todd Whitman. He would sentence repeat felons to life in prison after two convictions, not three. He would slash the state work force, put every able-bodied welfare recipient to work and reintroduce capitalism into this, the last socialist state.

I'm not running as a moderate to tinker at the margins," Mr. London said, looking bashfully preppie in his double-breasted blue blazer as the crowd of 150 applauded approvingly.

As he enters the fourth year of his poorly financed, some would say Quixotic campaign to oust Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, Mr. London has made such town hall meetings weekly rituals, traveling from Levittown to Jamestown ginning up supporters and converting skittish moderates to his brand of conservatism: William F. Buckley with a twist of Ross Perot. A Conservative Favorite

Though many Republicans would like to dismiss him as a fringe candidate, Mr. London, 54, is clearly more than that. With the polls showing the Democratic incumbent more vulnerable than ever, and the Republican and Conservative parties resolved to support one candidate, Mr. London has the largest Conservative following, thanks to his strong third-place finish on the Conservative Party line in the 1990 gubernatorial race and his extensive campaign work since then.

Moreover, the fractious Republican Party leadership has failed to unite around one candidate, buying time for a glib maverick like Mr. London to build support among rank-and-file members. Even if he is not the final choice of the leaders -- and most think he will not be -- Mr. London could bring enough support to the party's spring convention to insure that the nominee and platform are to his conservative liking, lest he wage a primary battle.

"He has the potential to pull the field to the right," said Lee T. Miringoff, director of the Marist College Institute for Public Opinion in Poughkeepsie.

What has brought the Republicans and Mr. London to this splintered juncture is an overabundance of enthusiasm: 14 men have expressed interest in challenging Mr. Cuomo and five, including Mr. London, have been actively campaigning across the state. Cuomo at Low Point

Some say the large field is a reflection of Mr. Cuomo's weakness: his approval rating is its lowest in his 11 years in office. But many others say the inability of party leaders to winnow the field -- it has grown almost weekly -- is evidence of deep rifts dividing the Republicans.

Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, a powerhouse among both Conservatives and Republicans, is widely viewed as supporting State Senator George Pataki, a Conservative-Republican from Peekskill. But Mr. Pataki has run afoul of another influential Republican, Ralph J. Marino of Oyster Bay, the Senate majority leader, because of his ties to Change-NY. The anti-tax group tried to oust Mr. Marino in a 1992 primary.

Mr. Marino's antipathy toward Change-NY could hurt Mr. London and two other candidates, J. Patrick Barrett and Evan G. Galbraith, because all were instrumental in the group's creation.

At the other end of the spectrum, Richard Rosenbaum, the most successful fund-raiser of the field, as well as former Representative Bill Green and State Senator Roy M. Goodman, are considered too liberal, particularly on abortion issues, to win approval from the Conservatives. D'Amato in Wings

None of the rest have generated enough interest to vault into front-runner status. And hovering above the fray is Mr. D'Amato, who has left open the possibility of running if no one else unites the party.

Mr. London, the John M. Olin Professor of Humanities at New York University, stands out as a constant in this inchoate field. After nearly finishing ahead of the Republican, Pierre Rinfret, in 1990, he took a two-week vacation and then began running for 1994. Since then, he estimates that he has conducted over 200 town hall meetings.

As he crisscrosses the state dressed in his heavy black overcoat and black fedora, he wears the somber look of a preacher. Indeed, he refers to his campaign as a "mission" or "crusade." And he sounds almost messianic when he vows never to repeat the mistakes of 1990, when Republican leaders promised him support only to endorse Mr. Rinfret.

"I was foolish," he says of his decision not to challenge Mr. Rinfret, whom he calls "the man whose elevator didn't stop at every floor."

Among Republicans, the rap against Mr. London today is much the same as it was in 1990: that he cannot raise money and alienates moderates with his strong anti-abortion stands. Sees No Fund Problem

Mr. London says that he would have no trouble raising money once the field cleared and he was the nominee. But as of Jan. 15, he had raised only $53,722 and had only $3,992 in his account. Mr. Cuomo's war chest is almost $3 million.
