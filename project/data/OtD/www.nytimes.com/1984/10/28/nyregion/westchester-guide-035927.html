WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: October 28, 1984

POCANTICO TREASURE

An exhibition opening at the Greenburgh Public Library Thursday and continuing through Nov. 30 provides an overview of a little-known treasury of 14,000 cubic feet of documentary records contained in the Rockefeller Foundation Archives in Pocantico Hills - a research center for scholarly pursuits opened nine years ago.

''This is a good chance for us to publicize the humanities division of the foundation,'' said Claire Collier, a foundation archivist and organizer of the exhibit. ''It will cover things that were supported by the Rockefeller Foundation from 1929 to 1969 in the United States.'' Material the center receives each year is closed for use for about 20 years.

The purpose of the foundation, said Miss Collier, is ''to help start programs . . . get them on their feet. Or to fund short-term research projects.'' It provided initial grants for the establishment of the Berkshire Music Center, a regional theater at the University of North Carolina, The Actor's Studio, Public Radio, the Museum of Modern Art film collection and a dance notation bureau where George Balanchine, Agnes de Mille and others deposited written records of their choreography.

It financed visits to this country by German and Japanese journalists after World War II to study the workings of a free press, and during the war provided crash courses in Russian, Malay, Hindi, Japanese and other languages to military personnel.

In the single randomly chosen year of 1951, Miss Collier said, the humanities division distributed $1.6 million out of a total of $21.1 million spent on all programs, including medical science, natural science, social science, agriculture, public health, a general fund and administration.

''Anyone with a serious purpose such as writing a book, a thesis, a magazine article or an article for speculation may call 631-4505 and make an appointment to use the archives,'' she said. Also available are Rockefeller family records, records of their philanthropies both defunct and extant, and a history of Rockefeller University, founded for graduate scientific research in 1901 at 68th Street and York Avenue in Manhattan. More than 15 Nobel Prize winners were produced there, according to Miss Collier, including Dr. R. Bruce Merrifield, this year's winner in chemistry.

The Greenburgh Library is at 300 Tarrytown Road, Elmsford, and the exhibit can be seen from 10 A.M. to 5:30 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday, 12:30 to 5 P.M. Sunday. 7TH ENSEMBLE ENCORE

Bernard Rose will be guest pianist with the Saxon Woods Chamber Players, opening their seventh season at 3 P.M. today in the meeting house of the Westchester Ethical Humanist Society, 7 Saxon Woods Road in White Plains.

Four-handed piano works by Schubert, Ravel and Milhaud; the Prokofiev Sonata for Flute and Piano in D major, Dohnanyi's String Trio and a Passacaglia for Violin and Cello by Handel-Halverson are on the program.

Members of the ensemble are Paul Dunkel, the flutist and recently appointed conductor of the New Orchestra of Westchester; Eugene Moye, cellist; Joseph Rabbai, clarinetist; Nardo Poy, viola, and the group's directors Jack Katz, violin; and Marsha Cheraskin Winokur, pianist.

One of a series of five concerts will feature a commissioned piece by Alvin Brehm, to be presented in March.

Tickets at the door are $8.50, $7.75 for the elderly and $6.50 for students. For subscription and other information call 948-1120. GYMNASTIC DISPLAY

More than 100 young women from New York and New England will take part in the Golden Apples Invitational Gymnastics Meet from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Saturday in the Byram Hills School.

The morning will be devoted to Class I compulsory events, the afternoon to Class II and III intermediate and compulsory events.

''This has nothing to do with the United States Gymnastics Federation,'' said Debbie Savoie, publicist and mother of Denise Savoie, one of the gymnasts. ''Six teams of up to 20 members will be involved in exhibition gymnastics rather than competition.''

Among the more-promising members of the four-year-old Golden Apples Team of Westchester are Holly Joyner, who competed in the Gymnastics Federation regionals and eastern nationals last year; Jaime Lee, Tara Roberts, Miss Savoie, Melina Aronstein and Allison Bates - all 12 to 17 years old. Their coach is Karen Hemberger, an all-American gymnast from the University of Massachusetts.

Tickets at the door are $3, or $1.50 for children. ON THE GRAPHIC ART

A free talk by one of the country's leading printers of artists' quality graphics - Kenneth Tyler - will be given at 11:30 A.M. Tuesday in the Visual Arts Building, Room 1019 of the State University of New York campus at Purchase.

Mr. Tyler was the founder in 1965 of Gemini, Limited, in Los Angeles, and in 1974 of Tyler Graphics, Limited, in Bedford. He has distinguished himself by inventing innovative printing presses and printing techniques that expanded the scale and variety in which graphic artists work.

Among the artists who have availed themselves of his workshop facilities are Robert Motherwell, David Hockney, Ellsworth Kelly, Frank Stella, Roy Lichtenstein, Nancy Graves and Helen Frankenthaler.

Call 253-5014 for more information. COURSE ON AIDING AGED

The Westchester Chapter of the American Red Cross has given sensitivity training for working with the elderly to more than 175 high school students, 60 percent of whom have been placed in volunteer work around the county in the last two years.

A seminar on the subject of ''Youth and Elders Working Together'' will be held for interested students from 9 A.M. to 3 P.M. Friday in the Red Cross Building, at 106 North Broadway in White Plains.

Topics to be covered include ''normal aging process,'' ''clarifying personal feelings about elders,'' ''intergenerational relationships'' and ''volunteer responsibilities.''

The course is taught by Dr. Reva M. Greenberg, consultant in gerontology at Fordham University; Carol Reiman, health consultant; and June McCaffrey, Red Cross volunteer. Also participating will be members of the Red Cross Retired Senior Volunteers Program.

Students seeking placement afterward may so do through the Red Cross. ''We have agreements with eight or nine nursing homes, senior centers, New York Hospital-Cornell Medical Center, and other agencies for the elderly around the county,'' said Ann Miller, a spokesman for the Red Cross, ''Once the students are placed, the agency trains them in the specifics of the job that they will be performing.''

Registration for the seminar costs $7, including lunch, materials and a Red Cros Youth-Elders Certificate. Tuesday is the deadline for registering. Call 946-6500 for more information.
