WESTCHESTER JOURNAL

Published: May 20, 1984

SCHOOLCHILDREN nationwide are rushing to aid the restoration of Miss Liberty as the venerable statue approaches its 100th birthday Aug. 5.

Westchester schools have already sent 68 sepaarate checks, but none that could match the $2,370 that the students of the Farragut Middle School in Hastings-on-Hudson presented last Wednesday.

In a ceremony at the base of the huge copper statue attended by all 250 Farragut students, faculty and school officials and aides of both the French Cultural Service in New York City and the United States Congress, the Farragut Middle School band and chorus performed a ''Salute to America.''

Two seventh graders, Beverly Schmidt and Marybeth Shewmake , told the Liberty Island superintendent, David Moffitt , of their original plan to raise $250. ''We had $250 the first day,'' Beverly explained. In her presentation, Marybeth added, ''When we started to ask our parents, neighbors, teachers and businessmen for contributions, we learned how deeply they all felt about the Lady with the lamp.''

Allowances and snack money were added to the results of neighborhood canvass drives and the $150 check one enterprising young man persuaded his father's law firm to donate.

Under the direction of the middle school humanities chairman, Albert Barouh , students wrote essays about the statue's significance in their own lives. Recalling stories of her grandfather's arrival in New York City from Russia 67 years ago, a sixth- grader, Heather Schiffer , wrote, ''He became a lawyer and president of a corporation,'' and she added: ''He always told me that only in America could a poor boy from the ghetto achieve such success and live with such freedom.'' Mary Diasparra , a school secretary, initiated the school drive after receiving a letter announcing the creation of The Daily News Charities for Liberty Fund.

''My parents emigrated from Italy in 1929, and the statue meant a great deal to them, as it does to me,'' Miss Diasparra said. During World War II, Miss Diasparra's mother requested United Statescitizenship and was asked whether, if women were drafted, she would fight against her native country. Mrs. Diasparra is said to have replied:

''As much as it would hurt me, 'Yes.' This is now my country and my children's country.'' - Tessa Melvin

Frances S. Berkey has lived in Scarsdale 27 years and, she says, she has given 100 parties each of those years: ''You name it,'' she said, brown eyes flashing. ''I've given little political dinners for people like Carl Vergari , Malcolm Wilson ; I've given New Year's Eve parties for 300 people in my basement.''

But recently the woman once dubbed by a reporter ''the Perle Mesta of Westchester'' had a chance to really relax at a gala. She was among those honored at the Casita Maria 50th Anniversary Fiesta at the Pierre Hotel in Manhattan. The event, which drew 550, was a benefit for Casita Maria, a nonprofit agency affiliated with the Archdiocese of New York. With settlement houses in the South Bronx and East Harlem, Casita Maria offers family counseling and activities for the elderly, as well as nutrition, recreation and tutoring programs to 500 people a day.

Guests clad in sequins, chiffon and tuxedos danced to the music of the Stuart White Orchestra and ate asparagus, sauteed chicken and ice cream pie under the shocking-pink balloons, streamers and gold sequins that covered the hotel's grand ballroom. They also watched as Mrs. Berkey and others - among them, Anthony Quinn, Chita Rivera and Javier Perez de Cuellar , Secretary General of the United Nations - were awarded medals of honor.

''Thank you most sincerely,'' Mrs. Berkey said as Cristina Ramos Toosie , president of Casita Maria, draped a red ribbon hung with a gold medallion around her neck. ''It just so happens I am very happy at what I am doing.'' Mrs. Berkey has done various volunteer jobs for Casita Maria, established a scholarship fund for the agency and serves on its board of directors. Born in Cuba to Puerto Rican parents (she prefers not to say how many years ago), she is married to Benjamin Berkey , founder of Berkey Photo. Over the years, she has organized hundreds of galas, including several debutante balls. Her parties have aided a variety of organizations, including the Scarsdale Congregational Church, where she is a member, and Camp Tranquillity, a camp in Earlton, N.Y. for underprivileged Jewish children.

''I like festive things,'' she said, patting the gardenia pinned to her dark hair. ''My style is to have lots of flowers, and, oh yes, I am also famous for certain foods, like my Spanish paella.'' She paused. ''My philosophy in life is - do good. Also, when my friends have a good time, I have a good time.''

Was the fiesta, which guests paid $200 each to attend, more or less elaborate than the parties she has thrown at her own house?

''The same, I think,'' she said, laughing. ''The same.'' - Lynne Ames

A new newspaper for the 80,000 regular Metro- North commuters will begin appearing on station platforms and in Grand Central Terminal tomorrow. The publication, Commuter Express, now is circulating a ''premiere issue'' at 47 suburban stations and plans to produce 26 a year. The paper is to be supported by advertising and distributed free. Frances Freeman-Dorf , a publicist who will be editing the publication, said it would be devoted to the ''commuting and post-commuting interests of a vital, prestigious community and will take much of its editorial coverage from rider-reader suggestions.''

The 20-page preview issue features information such as how to leave Grand Central in bad weather without getting wet, what the new Stamford, Conn., station will look like, a survey of rider complaints and tips on shopping for film in midtown New York City.

In explaining the aim of the newspaper, Mrs. Freeman-Dorf said:

''While our community's residents can live as far as 50 miles from one another, they are a homogenous, contiguous group, indeed. Essentially, ours is a unique, involuntary community with a population in excess of 80,000 (regular rider-citizens). Many of our citizens spend as much time in 'our community' as they spend awake and relaxed in the place where they pay taxes. As a community newspaper, we'll keep you up-to-date on 'government affairs' at Metro-North, both good and bad.''

She said her staff of five would publish features on commuting and profiles of ''famous, not-so-famous and infamous'' commuters and of the people who help them commute.

''Since we've appointed ourselves as your voice,'' Mrs. Freeman-Dorf said in the newspaper, ''it's only appropriate that we also ask you what you want of us - something we'll do on a recurring basis, beginning with a questionnaire in our next issue.'' - Martin Gansberg
