HOW THE GOPHERS GO FOR GOLD

By Patrick Marx; Patrick Marx contributes to The New York Times from St. Paul, Minn.
Published: August 19, 1984

TWO decades ago the University of Minnesota, with more than 45,000 students on six campuses, raised less than $650,000 from private sources and was the last in the Big 10 in that category.

But in 1969, a chance question by a local foundation director to Malcolm Moos, then the university's president, about the level of foundation contributions helped prompt university officials to initiate one of the most aggressive and successful fund-raising machines in the country, which Robert Odegard, the head of the university's fund-raising program, came to call an ''elephant hunt.''

As a result, the University of Minnesota has grown in private gifts to the point where, over the last decade, it has ranked among the top 20 fund-raising schools in the nation. In 1982-83, the last year for which complete figures are available, it was the only publicly supported institution in the top 12. In that year it listed more than $62.7 million in private donations, putting it third behind Harvard and Stanford, according to the Council for Financial Aid to Education.

When Mr. Moos could not answer the foundation director's question he decided to ask for himself at the university's donations office. Arriving there, he discovered a small cubbyhole so disorganized that it could not account for any contributions, let alone raise much money.

Public universities in Minnesota were receiving adequate funding through the State Legislature, but after reflecting on how much would be available from private donations, Mr. Moos decided to organize a development office to exploit such sources. One of his first acts was to hire Mr. Odegard, who was to pursue corporate and foundation money.

Mr. Odegard moved quickly to hire several fund-raisers, and went to the business community to form a board of directors composed of the area's top businessmen. These included Donald Dayton, then head of the major Middle Western retail chain, the Dayton and Hudson Company; Bernard Ridder, an executive of the Knight-Ridder newspaper chain; Curtis Carlson, owner of several hotels, and Raymond Plank, president of the Apache Corporation, an oil and gas exploration company.

''Even though a lot of these people didn't go here,'' Mr. Odegard said, ''they recognized what we do for their businesses. For the most part we're dealing with corporations that are located here which have a need for University of Minnesota graduates.''

When C. Peter Magrath was named University president in 1974 upon Mr. Moos's retirement, he took a personal interest in fund-raising. ''It was one of my earliest discussions as president-elect,'' said Dr. Magrath, a native of Brooklyn and president of the State University of New York at Binghamton from 1972 to 1974. ''I met with the heavy hitters on the foundation board and they told me they were committed to raising money, but they wanted an operation they knew would work for them.''

DR. MAGRATH, who recently submitted his resignation and will soon become president of the University of Missouri, entertained potential donors at his home and went directly to business leaders to sell them programs that appealed to their self-interest. In 1976, for example, he and Mr. Odegard established several semi- autonomous development offices at the central Minneapolis campus. ''It was really a pioneering idea,'' Dr. Magrath said. ''From that we were able to break into a sound marketing strategy. They really identified with their schools and that's where we really tapped into the entrepreneurial talent of each school.''

The management school and the Institute of Technology were especially favored even during the recession, but the colleges of liberal arts and humanities did not do as well. The College of Liberal Arts lost over $1 million in public funds, costing 52 full-time faculty positions and 49 full-time teaching assistants - losses that were not balanced by an infusion from private sources.

In 1978 David Lilly was named dean of the management school. Mr. Lilly had important business connections from his term on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors and as chief executive of the Toro Company, which makes lawn mowers and snow blowers. He hired three fund-raisers, established six teaching chairs funded by private corporations on a 10-year renewable basis and expanded the faculty mostly from corporate and foundation donations. When he resigned last year he had personally delivered more than $11.3 million in business support, adding greatly to the rate of growth of private donations.

According to David Merchant, a management school vice president, ''In 1978 about 95 percent of our budget was from the Legislature. Now 60 percent of it is from public sources.''

But even the spectacular increase in public funds didn't shield the university from a recession-driven retrenchment three years ago, though private help cushioned the impact for the high-technology fields in the school system. The Institute of Technology and the School of Business went virtually unscathed compared to other colleges in the system.

Tuition was raised 27 percent for the University's students and faculty salaries dropped 19 percent adjusted for inflation, when the Legislature in the 1982 fiscal year trimmed $26.5 million from the University budget. The Legislature acted in the face of recessionary economic conditions in which Minnesota faced a $600 million budget deficit.

That the vast majority of private funds has been for the high-technology schools doesn't disturb Dr. Magrath, who reasons that he will fund those areas where there is a willingness to give, putting emphasis on areas of high growth. ''I start with the premise,'' he said, ''that the university is inescapably part of this society, and therefore must inescapably be involved in the thrust of that society.''

Though the University of Minnesota has taken a dominant role in fund-raising among the state's institutions of higher education, the fund-raising programs of the state's 17 private colleges have not been seriously hurt. Private colleges tend to rely on alumni, foundations dedicated to maintaining private education and special benefactors who are attracted to specific programs or capital projects.

''Minnesota places a priority on higher education, and people recognize that private colleges play a role in this major public responsibility,'' said Larry Osnes, president of the Minnesota Private College Council. ''In addition, the quality of the University's graduate programs is highly dependent on private colleges. The relationship with the University is very strong now as part of a long tradition, and that tends to balance things out.''

Mr. Osnes said, however, that private colleges would become concerned if the balance were disturbed. ''It tends to put slightly out of balance funding for private colleges, especially in light of the cuts in public funding,'' he said. ''If the public institutions go full bore into private philanthropy it would put serious strains on the private colleges. It's an issue, but the long-term relationship would suggest these issues can be handled.''
