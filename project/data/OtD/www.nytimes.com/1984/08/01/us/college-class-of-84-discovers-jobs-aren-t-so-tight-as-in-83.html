COLLEGE CLASS OF '84 DISCOVERS JOBS AREN'T SO TIGHT AS IN '83

Published: August 1, 1984

With the job recruiting season now over, college officials who try to help graduates find employment to match their education are saying that things turned out substantially better than last year, which one counselor remembers as the worst since World War II.

Two recent surveys found marked improvement in the employment picture for the college class of 1984. Employers were eager to hire graduates not only in engineering and computer science but also in the previously difficult area of social science, the results indicated.

Mathematics teachers have long been in demand, but now English teachers are finding jobs much more quickly, according to one report, compiled by Michigan State University.

Another report, released last week by the College Placement Council of Bethlehem, Pa., found a 26 percent rise in the number of jobs offered this year over last year.

Nevertheless, some students have discovered that finding the job they want is harder than they expected. Some aspiring professionals have temporarily turned to the typing-and-shorthand market to make ends meet. Upswing Called 'Dramatic'

The report by the council, which monitors the college employment situation several times a year for employers and colleges, cautioned that ''a number of variables that have nothing to do with the level of recruiting activity'' could influence the number of offers in a single survey.

''However,'' the council continued, ''the upswing in this report is so dramatic that it appears to reflect a much improved job outlook for the class of '84.''

At the same time, the council found many employers were ''holding the line on starting rates.'' Job openings may have increased significantly but not enough to create the competition for graduates that drives up salaries. Those who received higher salary offers were getting only 2 to 5 percent more than last year's graduates, the council said.

''As usual,'' the council reported, ''petroleum engineers received the highest salary offers, averaging $29,568 a year. Although well above the averages for all other disciplines, this figure dropped 4.1 percent since last year.'' Salaries Show Increase

As for graduates in social sciences and the humanities, the council said their salary offers, about $17,570, were 10 percent above last year's. And students in these areas received ''significantly more offers'' than last year.

Computer science majors, always in high demand, received an average salary offer of $24,552, according to the council.

The council's survey was based on information from 187 job placement offices at 162 colleges and universities. The number of job offers was the total from Sept. 1, 1983, to June 8, 1984.

The survey by Michigan State University was less enthusiastic, however.

As of June 30 it found only a 3 percent increase in employment for this year's graduates.

''That's good, but it's not 10 to 20 percent,'' said John D. Shingleton, director of placement at the university. His figures apply only to Michigan State graduates, ''but we're a pretty good index of the nation,'' he said. 'We're on the Upswing'

''The significant thing,'' Mr. Shingleton said, ''is that we've plateaued out and we're on the upswing. The curve has changed. Last year was the worst year since World War II. This year is much better.''

Mathematics and science teachers were easy to place, he said, and even English and social science teachers were able to find jobs if they were willing to move.

He said that last year 13 percent of the university's graduates went unemployed. He expected the figure would be 10 percent this year.

For some students, like Ricky M. Hunt of Davison, Mich., that statistical improvement meant finding a job was much easier.

Mr. Hunt, who could have graduated last year, took an extra year to finish because he participated in a work- study program. Some of his friends who graduated last year could not find jobs.

When he graduated in June, Mr. Hunt got a job with a division of the General Motors Corporation. He had already worked for G.M. in the work-study program. Statistics No Help to New Yorker

But the statistics made little difference for Karen Klein. She graduated from New York University in February and has found breaking into the job market at the point of her aspirations nearly impossible.

She earned a master's degree in counseling, and her aim is to get into an employee assistance program in the New York City area. ''Well, there are jobs available,'' she said, ''but not in my field.'' She had one offer in her specialty, which is in the area of alcohol abuse, in Burbank, Calif.

To support herself, she temporarily became a secretary. Friend of a Friend Is a Help

Knowing the right person made all the difference for Suzanne V. Schuette, who majored at Michigan State University in social science and employment relations.

Last year one of her friends in the class of 1983, with a 3.97 grade average, languished for eight months without a job. This year ''I went four weeks without an interview during the height of job interviewing on campus,'' she said, adding, ''You feel like it's opportunity slipping through you hands.''

But her break came when a friend of a friend of the family reported that a company was hiring. ''It does work out,'' Miss Schuette said, ''but you have to keep pursuing it.''
