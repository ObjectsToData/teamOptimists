U. OF NEW MEXICO EMBROILED IN DISPUTE ON NEW PRESIDENT

Published: August 26, 1984

ALBUQUERQUE, N.M., Aug. 24— The selection of a new president for the University of New Mexico has set off a dispute at the institution.

The new president, John C. Elac, a 52-year-old economist, was selected by the Regents. He is the director of the general studies division of the 42-nation Interamerican Development Bank. The division is the organization's research arm.

Some of the university's 23,000 students and faculty members have questioned the secrecy in the search for a new president, as well as Mr. Elac's qualifications.

This week the Associated Students of the University of New Mexico, the student government, and a third of the voting faculty passed resolutions of no confidence in the Regents. On Thursday, Pauline Turner, president of the faculty senate, met with Mr. Elac and gave him a senate committee letter asking that he not to accept the post.

The university offers major programs in the humanities, the social sciences, Latin-America studies, the law, medicine and some physical and biological sciences. It has not had a permanent president since 1982, when William E. Davis left to become chancellor of the Oregon State System of Higher Education.

Last year the search for a successor ended after dissent in university circles and indecision on the part of the Regents. They named John Perovich, the university's vice president for finance, as president, but he agreed to take the post for just two years.

At a news conference Thursday, Mr. Elac said he was surprised by the dispute. ''I am saddened by the amount of concern, very troubled by it,'' he said. After meeting today with Albuquerque business leaders who support his selection, Mr. Elac said he would announce his decision next week.

Violation of the Law

At a meeting Tuesday, faculty members said the Regents had violated the state's law requiring open meetings in the search process. Mr. Elac was interviewed by some of the Regents in Denver in July, but the Regents' chairman, Henry Jaramillo, denied that the Regents had ever met with Mr. Elac as a group, as had been reported.

The law requires that all gatherings of public officials where a quorum is present must be open to the public except those dealing with personnel.

Some faculty members say they may seek a restraining order in court if the Regents go ahead with Mr. Elac's appointment, according to Jane Slaughter, an associate professor of history who is vice president of the Faculty Representation Association.

Miss Turner said that members of the university staff, as well as students and members of the Albuquerque business community, interviewed Mr. Elac and another candidate and were sworn to secrecy. Miss Turner said she was not permitted to solicit questions for the candidates from colleagues, or pass information on the meeting to the rest of the faculty.

''You cannot have a great university in secrecy,'' David Darling, a professor of education, said in supporting a censure motion this week. ''Those two terms don't go together.''

Mr. Jaramillo said of the search process: ''It's not secrecy. It's confidentiality and the right to privacy.''

Mr. Elac was born in Chile and is a specialist in Latin-American affairs. He was named a director of the Panama Canal Company in the Johnson Administration. He received his doctorate in economics at the University of California, Los Angeles, and taught there for two years.

But some faculty members say that most of Mr. Elac's career has been in research and that he has not had extensive experience in university teaching or administration.

Gilbert Merx, director of the university's Latin-American Institute, said the Interamerican Development Bank research division was ''a dumping ground for those who can't make it in operations.'' 'We Need Someone Mean'

Anthony Hillerman, a journalism professor, said of Mr. Elac: ''I'd like him as a friend, but we need someone mean, nasty, arrogant, overbearing in his self-confidence and not afraid to make enemies. I don't agree that we necessarily need someone with a lot of university experience, but if he'd been spending his time reorganizing Bolivia and straightening out General Motors, it would be different.''

Mr. Jaramillo says the Regents were not necessarily looking for a teacher or someone with university experience. ''If a person spends 10 or 15 years in a classroom, he's not likely to be able to manage a university of this size with a $300 million budget,'' he said.

Faculty members also cited Mr. Elac's 20-year friendship with Robert Cox, the president of PA Executive Search, a New York concern the Regents hired to help find a president.

Mr. Elac denied that that friendship had led to his nomination and said he could bring to the job ''work I have done with interpreting the problems of development, especially human resources development.''

Miss Slaughter said that the university was caught between being run like a business and being involved in education. The university, she said, has ''never resolved that because we've not had leadership that can bridge that gap either in presidents or in Regents, and particularly in Regents.''
