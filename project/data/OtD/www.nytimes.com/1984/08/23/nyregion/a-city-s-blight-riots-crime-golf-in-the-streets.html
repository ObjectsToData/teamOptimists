A CITY'S BLIGHT: RIOTS, CRIME, GOLF IN THE STREETS

By JAMES BROOKE, Special to the New York Times
Published: August 23, 1984

ALBANY— The city's chief executive feuded with the City Council. A shooting by a policeman touched off a race riot. Crackdowns failed to stop prostitution, smuggling and illegal dealing in intoxicants. Immigrants balked at learning the language and petitioned for bilingual services.

The story sounds familiar - a chronicle of life in Manhattan. But the time is the 1650's, and the characters are Dutch colonists, New York's founding fathers.

Ten years ago next month, Dr. Charles Gehring, an expert in 17thcentury Dutch, started a lifetime project of translating the New Netherland archives of the Dutch West India Company stored at the New York State Archives here.

Scorched by fire, stained by water and written in crimped handwriting, the documents were long ignored by historians, who instead depended on English sources for information on New York's beginnings. In 10 years, the linguist has translated about a third of the 12,000-page archive, going through five prescriptions for eyeglasses in the process. If his eyesight holds up, Dr. Gehring, who is 45 years old, hopes to complete the project around the year 2000.

New York historians say this first decade of translations of letters, wills, laws, patents, court records and company council minutes has given new insights into New York's earliest social history: crime and punishment, race relations, the role of women, drinking habits, sports such as bowling and golf, and attitudes towards sex and homosexuality.

''It is extremely important what Dr. Gehring is doing,'' said Wendell Tripp, editor of New York History, a quarterly publication of the State Historical Association.

With financing of $40,000 a year from the National Endowment for the Humanities, several Dutch institutions and the New York State Library, Dr. Gehring toils with a magnifier and a 40-volume Dutch dictionary in a corner office high atop a marble building in the Empire State Plaza. Prints of Dutch milkmaids and tavern scenes surround his desk.

There he is immersed in a world where messengers needed an hour and half to ride over hilly, country from Wall Street to the farming village of Haerlem, where New Amsterdam's ferrymaster was not obliged to cross the East River to Breuckelen on stormy days, where the sails were off the windmill at Battery Park.

It was Holland's golden century, and the small seafaring nation had colonies and trading posts from Brazil to South Africa to Ceylon to Indonesia to Japan. From 1626 until 1664, when the colony surrendered to the British, New Amsterdam was the seat of power for Holland's semi-official West India Company.

From the director general's gabled house at the corner where Water and Whitehall Streets now meet, Peter Stuyvesant tried to hold together this empire. As mayors and governors of Manhattan were to discover over the next four centuries, a major stumbling block was the City Council.

One burning issue, Dr. Gehring said, was to build a wall to keep out the New Englanders, a growing English-speaking minority seen as a social and political menace. They often refused to learn Dutch and pressured for bilingualism in courts and commerce.

''Everyone agreed the wall should be built,'' said Dr. Gehring.''But no one wanted to lose his time or resources. The City Council said it was up to the company to pay for defense.''

General Stuyvesant mortgaged his farm in the Bouwerij and paid for a six-foot-deep trench and accompanying breastwork, which was built where Wall Street now lies. But the City Council refused to maintain the wall. Gun emplacements rotted, and General Stuyvesant angrily withdrew his cannon to the company's fort, which fronted on the parade ground, now Bowling Green.

Court records show the underside of any society, and Dr. Gehring's translations show that the first New Yorkers were given to drunken brawling, smuggling guns, stealing pigs, selling beaver pelts on the black market and liquor to the Indians, seducing their neighbors' wives and cheating in commerce. Patrolling the Harbors

Beaver pelts were more important than Dutch currency in early Manhatttan, and the West India Company had a monopoly on the fur trade. Without much success, General Stuyvesant repeatedly sent company boats to patrol the harbor for smugglers.

Smuggling reached such high levels that in 1652 General Stuyvesant fired his schout, or top police officer.

Soon after, the man, Hendrick van Dyck, caused problems by shooting an Indian woman whom he found stealing peaches from his garden at Cedar Street and Trinity Place.

The shooting set off a racial explosion. Indians from Long Island and New Jersey burned almost all Dutch farms in their area, killing several settlers and kidnapping 160. Golf Forbidden in Streets

Some estimates calculate that one out of four buildings in New Amsterdam was a tavern. The Dutch were so fond of their beer that General Stuyvesant had to issue a temporary ordinance in 1654 forbidding the use of wheat to make beer until a bread shortage had passed.

Drinking often went hand in hand with the favorite Dutch sports, bowling and golf. In Beverwyck, now Albany, so many people were hurt and so many windows broken that an ordinance was issued forbidding golf games in the streets.
