LONG ISLAND JOURNAL

Published: January 15, 1984

MOTORISTS who have noticed new electronic signs on the Long Island Expressway will have to wait more than a year before the signs are given a green light to operate.

The signs are part of a project called I.M.I.S. (Integrated Motorist Information System), which will alert drivers to traffic bottlenecks and suggest alternate routes throughout the Island. When completed, it will be the largest computerized traffic system in the country, said Joe Contegni, I.M.I.S. project director.

The delay has been caused in part because the system's designer, the Sperry Corporation of Great Neck, is still programming the computer software for the project. In addition, L.K. Comstock, the contracting company, hasn't finished installing cables that will coordinate the signs, and the main I.M.I.S. control room in Hauppauge, where the signs' messages will originate, has yet to be completed.

After the system is hooked up, Sperry will test out the project for several months before turning on the power by the spring of 1985. The cost will exceed $35 million.

Nearly half of the system's 74 signs will be on the expressway. Other signs will dot the Northern State Parkway, the Cross Island Parkway, the Grand Central Parkway and Jericho Turnpike.

''Motorists will be able to maintain higher speeds,'' said John Zentgraf of the State Department of Transportation, ''and we'll be able to move more cars.''

''We can no longer build additional expressways to solve the congestion problems in our urban areas,'' said Philip Zove, Sperry's program manager for the project. ''Instead, we must make the best of the roadway networks that we have, and that is exactly what I.M.I.S. is designed to do.''

The Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s birthday won't go unnoticed today on Long Island, at least at two local libraries, which have scheduled special commemorative programs.

At 2:30 P.M. at the Allard K. Lowenstein Public Library, 111 West Park Avenue in Long Beach, Jerrold Pope, a baritone who has appeared with the Boston Symphony and the Cincinnati and Santa Fe Opera Companies, will perform Handel, Schubert, Barber, Gershwin and spirituals in a free concert dedicated to Dr. King's memory.

The Port Washington Public Library, 245 Main Street, is screening ''King: A Filmed Record, Montgomery to Memphis,'' the documentary that chronicles the civil rights leader's campaign for equality and justice from 1955 to 1968. The free program is at 3 P.M.

Although a controversial plan to establish a group home for the mentally disabled in Woodmere finally has been approved despite strong opposition from some segments of the community, the renovated hostel has not opened on schedule because of a temporary delay in state funds, according to Kenneth Feiffer. Mr. Feiffer is executive director of the South Shore Association for Independent Living, the nonprofit agency sponsoring the project.

Mr. Feiffer said the hostel would open in two weeks. He added that part of the payments would come from clients' Social Security funds, with 44 percent coming from the state's Office of Mental Health.

''There have been both negative and positive reactions in the community,'' Mr. Feiffer said. ''While we cannot pretend that all the residents want us here, as time goes on, we are getting more and more support from local people, including offers of money and furniture.''

The location of a home for 10 emotionally disturbed young adults (half of them to come from the Five Towns area) became a matter of contention among neighbors in 1982 when the state announced plans to establish a home on Irving Place, a street where a number of health-related centers already exist.

While there are now 45 halfway houses for retarded and mentally disturbed people in the bicounty area, ''it is still an issue that people tend to have strong feelings about,'' said John Pessala, a deputy attorney for the Town of Hempstead.

Mr. Pessala said that the town's court fight against the home, in which it maintained there was an oversaturation of such facilities, was ended by a recent court order that confirmed the decision of the State Commissioner of Mental Health to permit the hostel to open.

''There is no further role for the town to play now under the provision of the mental hygiene law,'' Mr. Pessala said.

Stacia Smales has resigned as executive director of the Riverhead- based East End Arts and Humanities Council. After 15 months on the job, Miss Smales will leave on Feb. 1 to become vice president of Creative Theater Marketing, a New York City organization working with Broadway and Off Broadway theater.

''I enjoyed my tenure here,'' Miss Smales said, ''and will miss my involvement with developing arts. But this offer was just too good to turn down.''

During her tenure, Miss Smales said, she ''almost eliminated'' the council's accumulated deficit and, in addition to continuing arts programming and exhibition policies, expanded the council's services to local artists. These services include the establishment of a research library under the auspices of the Center for Arts Information, which will open soon.

Miss Smales's departure means that now two eastern Long Island arts organizations are looking for executive directors. Guild Hall in East Hampton has still not filled its vacant post.

The Bridgeport and Port Jefferson Steamboat Company will extend its season through the winter on a month-by-month basis. For the last 15 years, the ferry service had stopped in mid-October, but this season, it had been extended until Jan. 3. However, skiers and other weekend passengers have requested a further extension of service.

''Basically, this is experimental - we're trying to make a workable year-round schedule,'' said Frederick Hall, assistant general manager for the ferry service. ''The ferry will be running through January, and at the beginning of February, we'll make a decision about running for the month, depending on weather and demands.''

During the winter, the ferry has been making three trips a day across Long Island Sound from Fridays through Mondays. It is closed on Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Thursdays in the winter.

The 280-foot Grand Republic is licensed to hold up to 300 passengers in the winter, and it is heated. According to Mr. Hall, although there were icy spots in the Sound during the December cold spell, there has been no problem with navigation.

Mr. Hall said the full ferry schedule would resume in late March or early April.
