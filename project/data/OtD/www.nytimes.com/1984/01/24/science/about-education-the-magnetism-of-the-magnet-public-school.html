ABOUT EDUCATION; THE MAGNETISM OF THE 'MAGNET' PUBLIC SCHOOL

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: January 24, 1984

IS excellence compatible with

equality? Not if equality means

sameness, ruling out different

paths to excellence. Unfortunately, because sameness is easier than variety to administer, school authorities often denounce alternative routes as a threat to equality. This may be why a number of principals in New York demanded last week that students should not be allowed to pick the magnet schools they want to attend. Students, the principals urged, should be assigned randomly to those schools.

Magnet schools are the latest device to inspire students by giving them choices. They usually attract students by concentrating on particular fields, such as the arts, science, mathematics, writing or international studies. Not surprisingly, these schools are being challenged as a threat to equality.

New York City's Schools Chancellor, Anthony J. Alvarado, is an advocate of magnet schools. In fact, he made much of his reputation by creating a variety of magnet schools when he was district superintendent in East Harlem. He is known to be planning a series of ''theme'' high schools in the tradition of the High School of Music and Art. Last year, the High School for the Humanities was inaugurated in lower Manhattan.

But growing numbers of principals complain that magnet schools skim off the best of their students. If the magnet schools are to continue, they say, students should be assigned to them, to make the schools representative of the population.

Chancellor Alvarado conceded that magnet schools sometimes may hasten the deterioration of some existing schools. But, he added, those schools should probably go out of existence anyway. Magnet schools, he said, stimulate competition and thus may improve the entire system.

Public education has never resolved the conflict between its ideal of diversity and the administrative desire for standardization. This is why the comprehensive high school remains so attractive as an institution that tries to contain under one roof everything any student might want - required academic subjects, electives, and shop courses - serving rich and poor, black and white, bright and slow.

In practice, the comprehensive school finds it difficult to serve all of its diverse clientele. As a result, more students drop out and more parents look to the private schools. ''People talk about the comprehensive high schools as if they were all alike,'' Mr. Alvarado said, when many actually run their own programs to attract students with special interests.

Magnet schools address both the dropout problem and the flight from public education. A survey of magnet schools, conducted by James H. Lowry & Associates for the United States Department of Education, shows that in the past decade more than 1,000 such schools have been created in more than 130 urban districts. Many school people and parents, the authors report, see these schools as ''an attractive model for improving education'' and a successful approach to racial integration. They often shore up public confidence in education. Crucial to their success: an innovative, ''entrepreneurial'' principal and a carefully selected teaching staff, committed to spending extra time with students.

Although at first the costs of magnet school ran about $200 higher per student, the difference has declined to an average of $59 a year later, according to the study.

The study found that even though most magnet schools do not select the brightest students, the quality of education and the students' achievements improve. What does appear indispensible, however, is voluntary attendance.

At a time when the schools face demands for better quality and fewer dropouts, it is difficult to argue against schools which succeed on both counts. Yet opposition to special schools in general has never died. In New York, the targets have often been the schools with selective admission, such as the Bronx High School of Science, Stuyvesant, Brooklyn Technical High School and, more recently, John Dewey High School in Brooklyn. In the more distant past Townsend Harris High School, on the City College campus, whose roster of alumni gave it a nationwide reputation for excellence, fell victim to just such opposition and budget-cutting by Fiorello H. La Guardia, Mayor of New York City from 1934 to 1945. (The impulsive Mayor once admitted that when he made a mistake it was ''a beaut.'' Townsend Harris loyalists still consider its suppression a notable example, and the school's revival, this time in Queens, is being considered.)

Opposition to selectivity - whether by the school or the students - dies hard. At a recent meeting in North Carolina, several school administrators and school board members wondered whether the North Carolina High School of Science and Mathematics violates democratic principles. The school, perhaps the most daring venture in public education today, is the first public boarding school. It draws the brightest juniors and seniors from all over the state and gives them free education, room and board. It has been successfully integrated.

What about the charge that such special schools unfairly favor those lucky enough to attend them, leaving the rest of the schools worse off? The objection has merit if the goal of public education is to make all schools as much alike as possible. But if, as supporters of magnet and selective schools claim, the goal is to seek the most effective ways to excellence, the comprehensive school model is of questionable merit. The magnet schools' success could serve as an incentive to the remaining comprehensive schools to seek their own paths to better education and to become more attractive to students. Advocates of some selective public schools point out that able students reinforce each others' strengths. They warn that to eliminate elite institutions is to deprive individuals of opportunity and the nation of vital, fine-tuned talent.

Ideological and administrative arguments are likely to be drowned out by demands that all public schools must compete with the best education offered by the best schools, public or private. In such a competitive arena, public school administrators may have no alternative but to make peace with the idea of a system that offers students a wide variety of choices. Speaking for those who believe in such options, Lew Smith, principal of John Dewey High School, said he was ready to take a stand on the matter. If students have made Dewey their first choice, after investigating what the school has to offer, he said, he wants those students' choices to be honored. In what sense could a school be considered a ''magnet'' if students come to it by being assigned rather than by being attracted?
