NEW JERSEY GUIDE

By Frank Emblen
Published: September 14, 1986

ENGLAND'S POET LAUREATE

When Ted Hughes was named Poet Laureate of England in 1984, it came a bit of a surprise because of his relative youth. He was 54, a whippersnapper as poet laureates go.

What does the honor bring besides honor? The Poet Laureate becomes a member of the Queen's Household and receives l$:100 a year and a case of wine, an amount unchanged since John Dryden was named the first official Poet Laureate in 1670.

Next Sunday, Mr. Hughes will open Seton Hall University's 1986-87 Poetry-in-the-Round series with a reading, the start of a two-day visit to the United States. It will be not only his first appearance in the United States since being named Poet Laureate, but also his first in 10 years.

Mr. Hughes, who was educated at Cambridge, is a former rose gardener, zoo attendant and night watchman at the Rank film studios in Pinewood. He was once married to the American poet Sylvia Plath, and was in the news in July for his lyric offering for the Prince Andrew-Sarah Ferguson wedding (''The Honey Bee and the Thistle'').

His specialty is poems about animals and birds, and therein lies a connection to New Jersey. Many of Mr. Hughes's poems were inspired by the drawings of Leonard Baskin, a native of New Brunswick.

It was Mr. Baskin, indeed, who conceived the subject of ''Crow'' - a ''ragged, murderous scavenger concerned mainly with his own survival'' - a volume of poems published in 1971 and hailed as comparable to ''The Waste Land.''

The lecture begins at 8 P.M. in the Theater-in-the-Round on Seton Hall's South Orange campus, 400 South Orange Avenue. Tickets, at $8, are available by sending a self-addressed envelope and a check (made out to Poetry-in-the-Round at Seton Hall) to the Department of English, Seton Hall University, South Orange, N.J. 07079.

Information: (201) 761-9388 or 678-1680. VAUGHAN IN WATERLOO

Speaking of legends, Sarah Vaughan, who began singing for audiences more than 40 years ago in her native Newark, will give the closing concert of the season at Waterloo Village Saturday evening.

Following a concert in June at Avery Fisher Hall, Stephen Holden wrote in The New York Times:

''Miss Vaughan's voice is a wonder of nature, richer and stronger today than it was 30 years ago.''

Tickets for the 8 P.M. performance are $18.50 and $15, and are available by calling the box office or from Ticketron.

The village is at Exit 25 of Interstate 80, near Stanhope. Signs show the way. Information:(201) 347-4700. 'DAMN YANKEES' George Abbott is spending his 100th year writing a new musical, rewriting another and - at the Paper Mill Playhouse in Millburn - directing a new production of ''Damn Yankees,'' which he wrote with Douglass Wallop. It opened on Broadway in 1955. Mr. Abbott's Broadway legend began in 1913, when he started as an actor. He also has been a playwright, producer, director and play doctor and, in 1983, directed ''On Your Toes,'' which he wrote and which opened originally in 1949.

''Damn Yankees'' will open Wednesday at the Paper Mill, and is probably as sure a bet as any first-night gambler can make.

Orson Bean, Alyson Reed and Davis Gaines star. The score, by Richard Adler and the late Jerry Ross, includes ''Whatever Lola Wants,'' ''You Gotta Have Heart'' and ''Two Lost Souls.''

By the way, the new musical Mr. Abbott is working on is about Dracula; at last report, it had a working title of ''Irwin'' and a rock music score. The one he's updating is ''Broadway,'' which was Mr. Abbott's his first big hit.

''Damn Yankees'' will run through Oct. 26, with performances at 8 P.M. Wednesday through Sunday, at 2 P.M. Thursday and at 3 P.M. Saturday and Sunday.

The Paper Mill is on Brookside Drive in Millburn. Information: (201) 376-4343. PIANIST AT BROOKDALE Christopher O'Riley, the pianist, will open Brookdale Community College's Performing Arts Series Saturday night on the campus in Lincroft.

A recipient of the Avery Fisher Grant, Mr. O'Riley is a winner of the Young Concert Artists International Auditions.

Tickets are $6, $7 and $8. The concert begins at 8 o'clock in the Performing Arts Center. Information: (201) 842-3335. WIESEL IN HACKENSACK ''He is the closest thing we have in the Jewish community to a superstar,'' a rabbi once said of Elie Wiesel, the writer. Who would argue?

Mr. Wiesel will speak Thursday evening at Orrie de Nooyer Auditorium in Hackensack as part of Fairleigh Dickinson University's Presidential Lecture Series. His topic will be ''When the Unthinkable Happens: Implications of the Holocaust for the Nuclear Arms Race.''

Mr. Wiesel was born in 1928 in Sighet, in the Carpathian Mountains of Rumania. Before he was 16 years old, he had been a prisoner in two concentration camps, Auschwitz in Poland and Buchenwald in Germany.

While at Auschwitz, he saw his father murdered. His mother and younger sister also died.

Mr. Wiesel recounted the horror of the camps in his memoir ''Night.''

After World War II, he became a correspondent in Paris for a Tel Aviv newspaper, and in 1956 a staff writer for The Jewish Daily Forward in New York City.

Mr. Wiesel is now Andrew Mellon Professor in the Humanities at Boston University and chairman of the United States Holocaust Memorial Council.

The lecture is being sponsored by the university, and free tickets can be ordered by telephone. Information: (201) 460-5082. AFRICAN FESTIVAL Rides on elephants and camels will be part of the African Festival that the State Museum in Trenton is presenting next Sunday.

The festival, from 1 to 5 P.M., will include a slide program and demonstration on the pottery of the Ipetumodu of Nigeria by Winnie Owens-Hart, a ceramic artist and associate professor at the College of Fine Arts at Howard University in Washington. She has taught the craft at the University of Ife in Nigeria.

This and other craft demonstrations will be based on the museum's African exhibition. Kobla Mensa Denta will present a program on the Akan style of woodcarving drums, furniture and tools. He has offered similar programs for the American Museum of Natural History in Manhattan.

Byard Lancaster of Philadelphia, a music educator and jazz musician, will play the flute and saxophone during the festivities.

African foods prepared by Jessica Granville of Lawrenceville will be served at 1:30 P.M. at a sit-down luncheon that includes a show of modern and traditional African fashions. Reservations, at $10, are necessary for this.

The festival will take place in the vicinity of the museum on West State Street, with parking available behind the planetarium. The museum shop will feature crafts and sculpture from many African countries.

Information: (609) 292-6300 or 292-6308.
