CHARITY DONATIONS SET A RECORD IN '85

By KATHLEEN TELTSCH
Published: May 7, 1986

CHARITABLE contributions reached a record of $79.8 billion in 1985, and for the first time in 16 years individual Americans gave more than 2 percent of their personal income to philanthropy.

The growth in contributions from individuals, corporations and foundations was reported yesterday in ''Giving U.S.A.,'' the annual study by the American Association of Fund-Raising Counsel, widely regarded as an authoritative source on philanthropic trends.

Giving by individuals went from $60.6 billion in a single year to $66 billion, helped along by the increased number of ''baby boomers'' born in the postwar years who have now reached their mid-30's, according to Dr. Ralph L. Nelson, a Queens College economist who devised the association's methods of measuring individual gifts.

''The significant increase reflects the larger number of Americans now in the 35-to-65 age group, the prime years of giving,'' he said. ''These are the big earning years, and people's attitudes about giving are positive. They have families, they have established homes and these psychological factors are important in determining how much they give to charity.'' And because the baby boom birth rate peaked in 1957, he said, ''we can look forward to more Americans in the prime giving years.''

A second positive element was the upward movement of the stock market, Dr. Nelson said, because ''people give more when they feel wealthy.''

Corporations and foundations also gave more money away in 1985, the association said. The two categories ran ''neck and neck'' for second place, each contributing about $4.3 billion, the association's report said.

Giving by American business showed a 13.1 percent increase. But the report also pointed out that corporate giving was expected to show ''only moderate growth for the remainder of the 1980's.''

The prediction of a considerably slower growth is based on the expectation of the drop in oil company profits as well as steel and heavy manufacturing generally, according to Anne Klepper, senior research associate of the Conference Board, a business research organization that supplied some data on corporate giving to the association.

The rise in foundation giving in 1985 reflected the increased value of the assets held by the country's 24,000 grant-making foundations, again because of the rise in their stock holdings. Under Federal law, foundations are required to pay out annually the equivalent of 5 percent or more of their assets.

Giving by individuals represented 82.7 percent of all charitable contributions, and bequests added another $5.1 billion.

As in the past, the largest share of all philanthropic giving went to religious orgnizations, which received $37.7 billion in 1985, about 6.5 percent more than in 1984.

Giving to health causes and hospitals totaled $11.2 billion, narrowly passing the $11 billion for education. Social service organizations received $8.5 billion, a record high. The increase reflected the rise in contributions through the United Way, which raised $2.3 billion last year.

Giving to the arts and humanities climbed to $5 billion, a 13.1 percent increase over 1984. Contributions in the category of civic and public affairs went up to $2.2 billion.

An increase in giving for overseas assistance was also reported, reflecting the outpouring of contributions for famine relief including the televised concert Live Aid.

The 1985 publication of ''Giving USA'' was issued this year by the association's recently established public service affiliate, the Trust for Philanthropy.
