FAMILIAR VOICES RELATE HISTORY OF LOWER EAST SIDE

Published: February 9, 1986

It took some prodding, but Nancy Core, a senior at Seward Park High School, finally got her mother to talk about her past, about what it was like to move to New York from Bayamon, Puerto Rico, and make a new life on the Lower East Side.

''She opened up and started to talk and, forget it, I couldn't stop her,'' Miss Core said. ''I learned a lot of things I didn't expect. My mother went through a lot.''

Dozens of students at Seward Park, at Grand and Ludlow Streets, have conducted similar interviews with relatives and neighbors in the last year and a half as part of a special humanities course. The result is a growing collection of informal oral histories that give voice to the rich and varied heritage of the Lower East Side.

''I constantly tell the kids that their oral histories will become the texts of the class, that they are contributing to the history and literature of the Lower East Side,'' said Jessica Siegel, an English teacher who created the course, ''Our Neighborhood: The Lower East Side Experience,'' along with a social-studies teacher, Bruce P. Baskind. A Snowy Arrival

The histories are the accounts of immigrants, and many are poignant.

Eli Hadjis recounted the snowy day he arrived in New York from Greece, ''happy because we were starting a new life for ourselves and hoping to achieve the American Dream, which I read about in Athens as a child.''

Mui Fong recalled tasting her first burger and shake at the airport cafeteria, and later finding work in a Chinatown sweatshop. Jaya Baudari described the loneliness of being a young, scared girl far from her native India.

Eunice Quinones, who came from Puerto Rico as a young bride, revealed that it ''took me so long to get adapted to the way of life here that I sometimes feel I haven't yet adapted.''

Such voices are one reason Miss Siegel brings tissues to the last class of the term. ''Like clockwork, the last day I burst into tears,'' she said. Recipes and Remembrances

The teachers intend to have the oral histories published, along with a book of Lower East Side recipes and remembrances collected by the students.

The recipes reflect the ethnic diversity of the neighborhood. Yina Hor's snails in black bean sauce shares billing with Edith Lopez Caban's surullitos, Gladys Marshburn's corned beef and cabbage, Carmen Rosado's lasagna, Rosina Winfield's chitlins, Alla Kashmanian's paskha and Nina Hadjis's halvah.

The brief remembrances have a flavor all their own. ''It's funny,'' Monserrate Medina told his daughter, Yolanda, ''because I am 51 years old now and I still wake in the middle of the night to eat some arroz con dulce, just as I did when I was a young boy.''

The course itself traces the patterns of ''life, labor and love'' on the Lower East Side, from the days when Indians camped there through the arrival of the Dutch, English, Irish, Italians, Jews, blacks, Puerto Ricans, Chinese and others.

It also examines the conditions that led people to leave their homelands, what they expected to find here, and how they used their cultures to survive. Breaking Down Stereotypes

The ultimate aim of the course, however, is to lead students to a deeper understanding of their neighbors, their families and themselves.

''I used to worry about the stereotyping of Chinese people,'' said Sue Lee, a senior who came to New York from Hong Kong at the age of 3, ''but I never worried much about stereotyping of other ethnic groups.''

The course, she added, ''taught me about my friends, the people I go to school with every day - how often do you learn about blacks, Hispanics and Chinese in history books?''

''It helps open your mind,'' said Moses Torres, who noted that students in the class ''bring up things from their own lives as examples of their cultures.'' Spinning a Web

The course meets daily in two sections - Mr. Baskind teaches history and Miss Siegel focuses on literature.

''I may start off in Africa or Latin America,'' Mr. Baskind said, ''but somehow I'm going to spin a web for them that ends up on the Lower East Side.''

Miss Siegel has culled stories, poems, plays and snippets of literature related to the Lower East Side from city and university libraries and the Library of Congress.

On a recent afternoon she led her class in a discussion of the short story ''Spanish Roulette,'' by the Puerto Rican writer Ed Vega. The story, about a young Puerto Rican torn between his traditional culture and the mainstream culture he has absorbed, seemed to strike a chord with several students.

''A lot of people go through that,'' Mr. Torres said.
