IF YOU'RE THINKING OF LIVING IN

IF YOU'RE THINKING OF LIVING IN; HAWORTH

By GENE RONDINARO
Published: January 26, 1986

IN eastern Bergen County, where many municipalities have been permitting their last tracts of open land to be developed intensively to gain more tax revenues, the Borough of Haworth is holding fast to its traditional pattern of low-density zoning.

Within its 2.5 square miles, single-family homes of almost every size and style grace quiet, shaded streets, while its 3,500 residents go about their daily routines without fear of congested thoroughfares, bustling commercial strips, fast-food emporiums and other disturbing characteristics of urban sprawl.

''Haworth is a beautiful single-family community just 45 minutes from Broadway.'' said Mayor John Johl, a 28-year resident. ''Before we moved here in 1958 my wife and I were looking for a community with a small-town flavor, good schools and a place where our daughters could ride their bikes on uncrowded streets. Today, people move here for pretty much the same reasons.''

The preservation of this small-town ambiance through strict zoning is of prime concern to Haworth residents who feel such measures - which limit most new construction to a third of an acre per unit - protect property values and their way of life from the encroachments of apartment and condominium developers.

Of specific concern is the future of the western section of Haworth, where forested watershed areas, owned by the Hackensack Water Company, could be sold for such development. The utility recently declared 18.5 acres off Lake Shore Drive near the 3.5 billion-gallon Oradell Reservoir as surplus and has encouraged development through its real-estate arm.

Sixty-seven acres of this watershed property has been leased by the Haworth Golf and Country Club for an 18-hole course - available to nonmembers for a fee - and the private White Beeches Golf and Country Club holds about 30 acres acres nearby.

Although no formal applications for development have as yet been presented to zoning bodies, they are expected soon.

''It is a difficult problem,'' conceded Mayor Johl, who said he believed cluster development set in remote areas could be provided under the existing zoning and, at the same time, preserve much of the natural forested areas.

Despite fears of development, Haworth remains today much the way it has been for the last 30 years, with homes set far back off the narrow streets and an almost pervasive quiet. The community still is heavily wooded because only parts of it were cleared for farming in its early days.

The community - bordered on the north by Closter, the east by Demarest, on the south and west by Dumont and Oradell and on the west by the Oradell Reservoir - is an affluent bedroom suburb with a median family income of $43,024, according to the 1980 census.

Its semirural amenities and stock of houses in many styles have not gone unnoticed by prospective home buyers, who have made Haworth one of the most sought-after and expensive residential communities in Bergen County.

Single-family houses - among them ranches, contemporary split levels, Tudors and huge well preserved Victorians in the older sections off Sunset Avenue - go from about $170,000 to $550,000, with an average price of about $235,000.

''Some contemporary-styled homes with huge lots along the fairways of White Beeches Golf Course would go for a lot more,'' said Carl Steinmetz, who with his wife, Mildred, run Steinmetz Agency Realtors on Terrace Street. Rentals of single-family homes, although rare, range from $800 to $3,000 per month.

The average stay of residents in Haworth is over 20 years, according to brokers, limiting available listings.

The owner of a $235,000 home, depending upon age, size and location, would pay about $3,000 in annual property taxes.

ALTHOUGH historical records are imprecise, Haworth is believed to take its name from Haworth, England, a hamlet made famous by the Bronte sisters.

But the first European settlers were not English, but Dutch farmers, who carved out arable fields from thick forests. These farms were raided frequently by Loyalists during the Revolution. The rural tranquility of the area remained undisturbed until the last decades of the 19th century, when a new wave of settlement was set off by thecompletion of the West Shore Railroad. Among the new settlers were New York businessmen who commuted to the city. Haworth experienced its greatest growth in the decade following World War II.

Today's newcomers are, for the most part, business, legal and health-care professionals attracted to the community by its natural beauty and easy accessibility to the city. The most famous current resident may be the actress and model Brooke Shields, who resides with her mother in an elegant brick Tudor-style home off Haworth Avenue.

''Haworth is wealthy, but not ostentatious,'' said Mayor Johl. ''Our residents take pride in their homes and their property but are not into gaudy excesses. We take more pride in our schools.''

At the corner of Haworth Avenue and Valley Road, the Haworth Elementary School with an enrollment of 342 children, is the only one in the borough. It features a comprehensive curriculum with special emphasis in the arts and humanities. An author, a poet and an artist meet regularly with students during the academic year and more than 22 computers augment regular classroom instruction.

The school offers a low 17-1 teacher student ratio and over 95 percent of its 26 full-time faculty members hold advanced degrees.

''On average our children are two years above grade level in math and verbal skills,'' said Dr. William P. Dorney, Haworth's Schools Superintendent.
