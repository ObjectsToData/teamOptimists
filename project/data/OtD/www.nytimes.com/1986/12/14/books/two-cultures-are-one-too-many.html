TWO CULTURES ARE ONE TOO MANY

BY G.S. ROUSSEAU; G. S. Rousseau, who teaches English at the University of California, Los Angeles, is chairman of the trustees of the newly-formed Society for Literature and Science.
Published: December 14, 1986

LANGUAGES OF NATURE Critical Essays on Science and Literature. Edited by L. J. Jordanova. 351 pp. New Brunswick, N.J.: Rutgers University Press. $30.

IT is not random that this book appears just as the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching has issued the strongest criticism ever leveled at undergraduate education in America. Like its predecessors, the Carnegie report alleges that the humanities are in a quagmire of contradictions and inconsistencies brought about, in part, by professors who fail to reflect seriously on the methods of the humanities and their relation to other domains of knowledge. In the words of Ernest L. Boyer, president of the Carnegie Foundation, a rift between ''careerism and the liberal arts'' has developed that threatens to obliterate the humanities altogether.

The underlying pattern for this distinction lies in C. P. Snow's notion of ''two cultures,'' which separated the sciences from the arts. But the contributors to ''Languages of Nature: Critical Essays on Science and Literature'' view that contrast as false and arbitrary. Collectively they maintain that if these boundaries were removed, some (but not all) of the tensions described by Mr. Boyer and his cohorts would be diminished. As the volume's editor, L. J. Jordanova, remarks: ''The distinction between science and literature is, for the contributors to this volume, a debatable one that deters rather than enhances historical understanding.''

Ms. Jordanova, a historian at the University of Essex in England, offers a concrete program for the new, developing subject ''literature and science'' in her introduction. Like Raymond Williams, the recently retired Cambridge University historian who has written a foreword to this volume, she laments that ''virtually everything in our culture conspires to reinforce a separation between the study of science and the pursuit of the humanities.'' Instead she argues for broad ''interdisciplinary studies'' that salvage contemporary literary criticism from the pathetic solitude in which it lingers: ''Where history of science has been becoming more concrete, separating itself from its philosophical ancestry, literary studies, by contrast, have been going in the opposite direction, becoming more abstract in the wake of the impact of structuralism and psychoanalysis.'' She then outlines a concrete research program that includes the literary analysis of scientific texts, strategies to treat the scientist as a writer and observations on what she calls the ''boundaries and dichotomies'' between the sciences and the humanities.

Ms. Jordanova explains why images of life, nature and human nature gave rise to a series of analogies and metaphors in the last two centuries that were socially and ethically charged, and to what detriment literary criticism has recently divested itself of earlier social and ethical concerns. She also suggests the types of historical discriminations that must be made if we are to rectify current trends. The interdisciplinary program she advocates could be implemented in colleges and universities tomorrow, were it not for rigid departmental structures that have kept scientists and humanists apart. But she pays less attention to scientists, wishing instead that they would acknowledge that science is inherently language-bound. If they did, she believes that ''the history of science and the literary study of language'' could soon be brought together. uf703> The seven essays that follow illustrate what happens when this confluence occurs. Following A. O. Lovejoy, the American historian of ideas who wrote a famous article demonstrating 18 major uses of the word ''nature'' during the Enlightenment, the Oxford literary scholar A. E. Pilkington shows that nature was an ''ethical norm'' then as well as an esthetic one. In a brilliant essay that relates French literature to the development of the biomedical sciences, Ms. Jordanova anatomizes nature by focusing on its sexual and erotic dimensions. James Rodgers, an associate professor of the humanities at the Lawrence Institute of Technology in Southfield, Mich., discusses Laurence Sterne's ''Tristram Shandy'' and ably argues that the author, widely read in physiology and psychology, rebelled against the possibility of a ''scientific language'' that would rest on ''verbal fixity.'' Maureen McNeil, a lecturer at the Center for Contemporary Cultural Studies at the University of Birmingham, England, focuses on the poetry of Charles Darwin's grandfather, Erasmus Darwin, a medical doctor and a prolific natural philosopher whose verse intertwined the concerns of science, literature and the new ideology of the Industrial Revolution.

The three essays dealing with the 19th century dwell exclusively on ''great authors'' (Darwin, George Eliot, Jules Michelet), despite the contributors' aim to diminish the gulf between major and minor writers. Gillian Beer, already known for her literary criticism of Darwin, explores ''evolutionary narrative'' in ''The Origin of Species'' and demonstrates on how many different linguistic levels Darwin construed man and the category ''human nature.'' Sally Shuttleworth, who teaches English at the University of Leeds, examines the cultural assumptions in George Eliot's ''Silas Marner,'' especially Victorian theories of physiological psychology, and relates them to the evolutionary debates that immediately followed publication of Darwin's book in 1859. Roger Huss, a specialist in 18th- and 19th-century French literature, discusses Michelet, the prolific French historian-patriot and popularizer of science, claiming that in both realms nature is ''a fundamental subject of Michelet's ideal national curriculum.'' This is a very different view of Michelet from the one found in Edmund Wilson's ''To the Finland Station,'' in which popular science enters into Michelet's life only because ''it was one of his new wife's enthusiasms.'' IF the authors had extended their meditations to the 20th century, they would have discovered further protean transformations in the languages of nature, as competing systems (Freudianism, quantum physics, structuralism and so on) became dominant. But the significance of these essays lies less in tracing the evolution of the idea of nature than in expounding a method that demonstrates to what extent all writing, not merely the writing of accepted literary works, is language-bound.

Acceptance of this condition entails a priori agreement that writing about science necessarily contains contradictions - multiple readings of the same phenomena, generated by the contexts in which those readings occur. And this postulate almost always threatens scientists who continue to believe that science is truth, or at least that science represents a constant attempt to reconcile those contradictions. Yet not one of the contributors to this volume is a practicing scientist, let alone a distinguished one, and the debate about the extent to which ordinary science is language-bound will probably not advance very far until distinguished scientists enter into it.

The significance of ''Languages of Nature,'' then, is that it opens up a field of research just at the moment when the humanities seem to have been depleted of meaningful projects, and at a time when the very concept of the humanities as a valid academic subject has come under sharp attack in diverse quarters.
