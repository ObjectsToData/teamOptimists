RHODES CHOICES GOOD NEWS AT YALE

By MICHAEL FREITAG
Published: December 14, 1986

AFTER a disappointing result last year, administrators at Yale University were pleased last week when they learned that four of the university's students have been named Rhodes Scholars.

Yale's four recipients of the prestigious scholarship, which provides two or three years of expense-paid study at Oxford University in England, were the most from any one college or university in the United States. A total of 32 American students from 24 schools were named Rhodes Scholars on Dec. 7. They will join Rhodes Scholars from 17 other countries at Oxford next fall.

In the United States, scholars were chosen from eight regions on the basis of intellectual attainment, character, leadership and physical vigor. The winners from Yale, all seniors, were: Michael S. Barr of Chevy Chase, Md., Robert M. Dow Jr. of Joliet, Ill., Susan M. Pepin of Tulsa, Okla., and Jacob Weisberg of Chicago.

None of this year's 32 Rhodes Scholars are residents of Connecticut, though Miss Pepin was born and raised in Wallingford and attended Guilford High School. Her family moved to Tulsa after she enrolled at Yale.

While university officials tend to downplay their significance, the awarding of Rhodes scholarships, as well as other academic awards such as the Fulbright and Marshall scholarships, are often viewed as indications of an institution's educational abilities. Last year, Yale had no Rhodes Scholars, while its traditional Ivy League rivals fared well. Harvard University had six recipients and Princeton University had four. This year, Harvard had one and Princeton two.

Yale's result this year is more typical than last, according to a university spokesman, Walter D. Littell. Over the last 21 years, he said, the university has had 73 Rhodes Scholars, an average of 3.5 a year. The university's highest total was seven in the 1971-72 school year.

''Yale is certainly pleased with the success of its students,'' said the assistant dean in charge of the university's career services, Susan Hauser, ''but we don't really care about competition.''

Unlike some schools, Ms. Hauser said, Yale does not believe in coaching its students for specific career opportunities. Nonetheless, she said, Yale students seem to earn their fair share of academic awards.

This year's group of Rhodes Scholars from Yale reflects the individuality of the awards, Ms. Hauser said. Although all four recipients share a strong academic record, they have highly diverse backgrounds and interests.

Mr. Barr, 21 years old, is a history major who is writing his senior essay on the black consciousness movement in South Africa. He was elected to Phi Beta Kappa this year.

His interest in South African affairs, Mr. Barr said, has led him to participate in the Coalition Against Apartheid, a group that has actively protested Yale's investments in companies that do business in South Africa. He also was a member of Yale's Committee on Investor Responsibility.

Because of his concern for the blacks of South Africa, Mr. Barr said, he was hesitant to pursue the Rhodes scholarship. The award is named for Cecil J. Rhodes, who created the award in 1902 to promote world understanding. But some have contended that Mr. Rhodes, a British colonial financier, helped create the racial problems that now exist in South Africa.

''I thought about that a lot before I applied,'' Mr. Barr recalled. ''But the scholarship is more open to women and to people of other skin colors and backgrounds than it used to be. And it is letting people go on and do good things for other people.''

Mr. Barr said he planned to earn a second Bachelor of Arts degree at Oxford. ''I've had a lot of history, and I wanted to take a step back and take in a more philosophical perspective,'' he said.

Mr. Dow, 21, said his extracurricular activities were what probably led to his receiving the Rhodes scholarship. In addition to being a member of Phi Beta Kappa, he is a freshman counselor and headed a few groups in his residential college, including the student government and the intramural athletic program. He also wrote for the school newspaper and yearbook and participated in several intramural sports.

Mr. Dow, who is majoring in both history and political science, is writing his senior essay about the economics of fencing in the 19th-century Middle West before the invention of barbed wire.

''It's kind of an obscure topic,'' he acknowledged, ''but I think it's worth doing, and it's certainly original.''

The essay, which concerns itself with people's reaction to changes in their environment, derives from a desire to write about the Middle West, he said. Mr. Dow's parents own an insurance company in Joliet. ''My whole family lives there,'' he said. ''I was the first person in my family to go to college outside the county.,'' he said.

After he finishes studying international relations at Oxford, Mr. Dow said he planned to return to Joliet to pursue a career in government.

Until she received the Rhodes scholarship, Miss Pepin was always planning to be a doctor.

''I've always imagined myself as going straight on to med school,'' she said. ''But now, since I can do anything I want at Oxford - it's kind of like being in a candy store - I am reconsidering my options.''

Miss Pepin, 21, is majoring in molecular biophysics and biochemistry. In addition to being a freshman counselor this year, she has volunteered at Yale's student-to-student counseling service and the Yale-New Haven Hospital emergency room. Her essay for the Rhodes scholarship (applicants must submit an essay, a transcript and eight recommendations) was about her volunteer work at Stanford University's Children Hospital, where she assisted terminally ill children and their families.

Mr. Weisberg, 22, said he was grateful for the opportunity to reassess his career goals. A humanities major and aspiring journalist, Mr. Weisberg was an editor of the Yale Daily News, the campus newspaper, and the Yale Political Monthly, a political magazine.

Last year, he took a leave of absence to write for The New Republic. His articles, several of which were featured on the magazine's cover, discussed current politics and cultural issues.

One of the reasons he is looking forward to studying political thought at Oxford, Mr. Weisberg said, is that it will give him a chance to engage in ''serious reading and thinking'' without any major distractions.

''This is an escape to a place where the pressure of the outside world is not felt as heavily,'' he said.

But even the year he has already spent outside of college was not as tense as the meeting at which the finalists for the Rhodes scholarship were told of their fate.

''It's that combination of being extremely nervous and terribly bored at the same time - that is what war must be like,'' he said.
