EFFORT GROWS TO CREATE SENSITIVE DOCTORS

Published: April 8, 1986

''When they ask me, as of late they frequently do, how I have for so many years continued an equal interest in medicine and the poem, I reply that they amount for me to nearly the same thing.'' WILLIAM CARLOS WILLIAMS

AT the end of a day crammed with labs, lectures and clinical rounds, a group of students at Mount Sinai Medical School gather in a conference room at the hospital to discuss contemporary fiction.

During the hour sandwiched between a 90-minute physiology lab and a two-hour oncology lecture, medical students at the University of Massachussets reflect on the paintings of Rembrandt and the writings of Michelangelo.

An increasing number of medical schools are modifying their curriculums to include such subjects as art and literature in an attempt to make young doctors more sensitive to the social and psychological needs of their patients.

The trend is an outgrowth of concern that doctors today have lost touch with the human side of caring for patients. Critics complain that revolutionary technological advances threaten to obscure the more humanistic aspects of the medical field.

''Computers may begin to take over more and more of the diagnostic, technical and scientific aspects of medicine, but there will always be a need for physicians practicing the art of medicine,'' said Dr. John Stephenson, president of the Forum for Death Education and Counseling, a national group based in Ohio. ''In this context, the physician will be treating the whole person - not just a collection of symptoms - but a person who has feelings and dreams and fears. Art and literature can serve to sensitize the doctor to these very important aspects of human life.'' Experimental Program Backed

In a recent call for medical-education reform, Harvard University's president, Derek Bok, criticized the ''lack of attention paid to the nonscientific side of medicine.'' He gave his support to the development of a new experimental curriculum at Harvard Medical School that teaches doctors to ''understand the emotional, psychological and cultural underpinnings of human behavior, including the interweaving of mind and body in illness and health.''

The Association of American Medical Colleges now promotes the humanities in medical school, with the hope that such courses will instill in physicians a greater sense of ethics and human values.

An increasing interest in the humanities is already reflected in medical school admissions. Dr. August G. Swanson, director of academic affairs at the Association of American Medical Colleges, said today's English majors experience a higher rate of acceptance than those who have majored in the biological and physical sciences.

As many as 15 percent of American medical schools offer courses in literature, according to a 1984 survey by MD magazine. These courses, most of which are elective, are based on the premise that literature can give better human perspective to physicians. ''Dying, disease, anguish, pain, birth, hope, courage, love - the absolute substance of literary work are also the integral aspects of medical work,'' says the course description, written by Barbara Berg, a professor of medicine and literature at Mount Sinai.

Works ranging from Brian Clark's ''Whose Life Is It, Anyway?'' to Ken Kesey's ''One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest'' have elicited impassioned discussions from her students, Professor Berg said. Vehement debate over a patient's right to die and the physician's role in that decision have forced students to come to terms with their own feelings about difficult ethical issues. The students often identify with the characters they are discussing and ''this gives them a chance to express their deepest feelings, hopes, fears and anxieties about being doctors,'' she said.

For Ron Golden, a fourth-year student in Professor Berg's class, the material fostered an ''understanding of the experience of illness as more than just a pathological process, and the healing experience as more than the relief of symptoms.''

''Literary works such as 'The Bell Jar' by Sylvia Plath increased my awareness of the experience of illness through the consciousness of the patient,'' he said. ''In 'Awakenings,' Oliver Sacks demonstrates that the physician can do more than record a medical history, he can explore and document a life history and illuminate the human story of suffering and recovery. Clearly, these areas are crucial to the humane practice of medicine.''

Robert Coles, who has taught medicine and literature at Harvard Medical School for seven years, agrees that literature gives students a missing perspective. His reading list includes George Eliot's ''Middlemarch'' and Tolstoy's ''The Death of Ivan Ilyich.'' But his personal favorite is William Carlos Williams, whose ''Doctor Stories'' offer him a source for introspection.

Many professors insist that visual arts can also play an important role in training medical practitioners. Sandra Bertman, who set up a medical humanities program at the University of Massachusetts nine years ago, presents images of the physician in literature, art and pop culture, using film and video clips, paintings and even greeting cards.

''Art is a way of seeing,'' she said, ''and so much of the medical encounter is based on keen observation and a critical eye.''

She turns off the sound when she shows students the documentary ''Dying,'' hoping to develop the students' ability to interpet nonverbal behavior. She cites Rembrandt's ''Anatomy Lesson'' and similar works by Leon Hovespian, a contemporary Massachusetts painter, as moving illustrations of the anatomist's relationship to his subject.

''Rembrandt makes us look not only at the body but at those looking at the body,'' she said, explaining that she shows her class details of the people standing around the body, forcing students to focus on the emotions of the physicians rather than the technical aspects of the anatomy lesson -in effect, reversing the way students are usually taught in medical school.

In ''On Death, Dying and Dissection,'' one of three required humanities courses at the University of Massachusetts Medical School, Professor Bertman prepares students to meet their first patients - the cadavers they will dissect in anatomy lab. She asks them to read excerpts from ''The Agony and the Ecstasy,'' Irving Stone's fictionalized biography of Michelangelo. One excerpt describes the young artist's reaction on seeing a cadaver he is to secretly dissect to learn more about the human body. Professor Bertman explores the artist's feelings of pity, fear and excitement, emotions so universal among first-year medical students.

According to Dr. Stephenson, president of the death education forum, medical students quickly learn to desensitize themselves to the dead body and to death. ''This is where they lose that human context,'' he said. ''Death becomes a scientific process and not the end of a human life.''

Professor Bertman attempts to challenge the young student's need to detach himself, forcing him to confront his true feelings. And each year, at the conclusion of their anatomy course, veterans of her class hold a memorial service for the human beings who dedicated their bodies to medical education.
