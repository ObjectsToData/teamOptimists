SCHOLARLY DISCIPLINES: BREAKING OUT

By COLIN CAMPBELL
Published: April 25, 1986

BRYN MAWR, Pa.— Two years ago a dozen professors at Bryn Mawr College got together and formed a private study group of a kind that has been popping up on campuses around the country.

The professors had discovered that they were all feeling frustrated by the narrowness of their separate departments, which included literature, philosophy, anthropology and art history.

They wanted to ask bigger questions than their specialties usually encourage them to ask. They wanted to know how other disciplines interpret the human world of language, knowledge and culture. Most of all, they wanted to acquaint themselves with some unscientific European thinkers whose writings have influenced several fields but who are rarely considered crucial to any single academic department.

Out of such yearnings has come a current of change that is affecting the way academics think, write and teach. Some believe it is challenging the basic structure of American universities. ''Groups and committees are being formed all over the country to break down the departmental system,'' said Steven Levine, an art historian who is a leader of the Bryn Mawr group, the Committee on Interpretation. New Journals and Courses

From the University of California to the University of Virginia, at Stanford, Wesleyan and other respected schools, specialization-weary faculty clubs and other voluntary associations are devoting serious study to the same thinkers that have inspired the Bryn Mawr group: grand philosophic ''structuralists'' like Freud, Marx and the anthropologist Claude Levi-Strauss; and such ''post-structuralists'' as the historian Michel Foucault, the critic Roland Barthes and the philosopher Jacques Derrida, who have tended to treat the structuralists' abstract, comprehensive, elaborately organized models of human behavior (along with many other received meanings) as constructed myths.

In the last few years some of these American groups have generated influential journals and new courses. And schools including Yale, Johns Hopkins, the University of Pennsylvania, the University of Chicago and the University of California at Santa Cruz have developed entire new programs based on cross-disciplinary thinking.

The University of California at Berkeley provides a good example of how such ideas grow. In 1980, Stephen Greenblatt, a professor of English at Berkeley, helped organize an informal study group of people from various departments. ''It seems absurd to think that the simple act of getting together to discuss things should be so surprising, but it is,'' he said.

Before long he and his friends had founded a quarterly journal, Representations, now widely regarded as one of the hottest newer journals around.

In a recent issue devoted to American culture between the Civil War and World War I, the shackles of narrow specialization are nowhere to be seen. It includes an article by Michael Rogin, a political scientist, on D. W. Griffith's movie ''The Birth of a Nation''; a piece on Civil War photographs by Alan Trachtenberg, a professor of English and American studies at Yale; a literary-psychological-economic study of misers and the fiction of Frank Norris entitled ''The Gold Standard and the Logic of Naturalism,'' by Walter Benn Michaels, a Berkeley English professor, and other articles focusing simultaneously on literature and the arts, politics and historical fabric of the era. Interest in 'Human Sciences'

Somewhat comparably, the literary journal Critical Inquiry, published by the University of Chicago Press, has grown deeply interested in the politics that lurk in art and in the symbolic elements that lurk in politics, history, science and society.

The European writers who have figured most prominently in these developments are not easily lumped together. One thing they have in common is an interest in a wide range of cultural phenomena: the ''human sciences'' generally, as the Europeans say. They are likely to move without apology into linguistics, history, art, literature, anthropology, philosophy, politics and criticism. Essentially, ''they're all talking about language and they're all talking about the paradigms of not being able to express oneself except in somebody else's words,'' Professor Levine said.

Ideas like these run across the grain of the typical American university, subdivided into departments modeled on those of Germany's 19th-century universities.

This system, combined with rapid changes and apparent increases in knowledge, has fostered tremendous specialization. And specialization, say the academics who have taken an interest in the new interdisciplinary groups, has become a crippling problem.

''Many faculties throughout this country and Europe,'' said Ralph Cohen, a professor of English at the University of Virginia, ''recognize the inadeqaucy of the present division of the university into departments.'' 'Great Points of Intersection'

At Virginia, Professor Cohen and about 15 others, including musicologists, philosophers, lawyers, historians, art historians, literary critics and religious scholars, gathered together more or less off campus.
