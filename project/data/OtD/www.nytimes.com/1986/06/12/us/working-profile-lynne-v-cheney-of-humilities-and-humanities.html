WORKING PROFILE: LYNNE V. CHENEY

WORKING PROFILE: LYNNE V. CHENEY; Of Humilities and Humanities

By LESLIE MAITLAND WERNER
Published: June 12, 1986

WASHINGTON, June 11— ''Describe me as quite tall!'' said Lynne V. Cheney, who measures 5 feet 2. But even as she issued that self-mocking request, it was clear that the new chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities needed no added height to give her stature.

In her new position, which she assumed a few weeks ago, Mrs. Cheney will dispense more than $125 million a year to various intellectual ventures, which gives her a large and powerful voice within scholarly circles. It is a job to which she brings a long string of academic, literary and political credentials and connections as well as a firm conviction that ''in a democracy you need an informed citizenry.''

Mrs. Cheney succeeded William J. Bennett, now Secretary of Education, as head of the endowment, which was established by Congress in 1965 to support scholarship, research, education and public programs in the humanities. Under her, she says, the endowment will continue to emphasize the basics in education and to advocate improved teaching of the humanities at the high school level.

''Citizens of a democracy,'' she contended, ''have to be able to think critically and judge wisely, and that's what the humanities teach us.''

She also wants to focus attention on American heritage and tradition. ''The endowment must look for ways to foster a greater awareness of our heritage of history, literature and philosophy,'' she said, noting that the endowment will stress study of the Constitution as the bicentennial of the signing of that document approaches.

Mrs. Cheney, wife of Representative Dick Cheney, Republican of Wyoming, sailed through hearings on her nomination and was confirmed by the Senate unanimously. But she added a few words of her own to a Samuel Johnson quotation to describe the confirmation experience: ''Nothing concentrates a person's attention like the prospect of being hanged in a fortnight or confirmed by the United States Senate.''

In fact, in treatment befitting the wife of a member of Congress, the hearings were something of a celebration of her credentials rather than an inquiry into her past or her plans. Those credentials include a bachelor's degree with highest honors from Colorado College, a master's from the University of Colorado and a doctorate in 19th-century British literature from the University of Wisconsin, which she earned in 1970, writing a dissertation on the poetry of Matthew Arnold. Before being named head of the endowment, a job that pays $72,600 annually, she taught, wrote and was a senior editor of Washingtonian Magazine.

Mrs. Cheney is a native of Casper, Wyo., where her father was a civil servant and her mother a deputy sheriff. Her husband, a conservative, was formerly chief of staff to President Ford and was elected to Congress in 1978. The Cheneys have two daughters and live in suburban McLean, Va., where Mrs. Cheney takes long, brisk walks for exercise three or four times a week.

At the endowment, whose staff of 250 has offices above the maze of shops and eateries in the newly renovated Old Post Office Pavilion, Mrs. Cheney will have the last word on which requests for study and research grants will be honored each year.

The applications - there were 7,524 last year - first go through a system of peer review that draws scholars from all over the country, then are sent to the National Council on the Humanities, whose 26 members are appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate for six-year terms. 'I Don't Sense Any Fat'

Several appointees to the council have been criticized in recent years as lacking qualifications beyond their active support for President Reagan. But Mrs. Cheney says she has been impressed with those she has met.

Will she request reduced financing for the endowment, as Mr. Bennett did?

''The agency I found when I got here was very lean,'' she replied. ''I don't sense any fat here at all.''

According to a brochure on the endowment, the term ''humanities'' includes history, philosophy, languages, linguistics, literature, archeology, jurisprudence, art history and criticism, ethics, comparative religion and aspects of the social sciences. Through the humanities, the brochure continues, each generation confronts timeless questions: ''Who am I? What is truth? What can I know? What is my destiny? What is noble, and what is base? What is the difference between right and power?''

Accordingly, the projects sponsored by the endowment are wide-ranging. In 1985, for example, there were grants for translations of a Marathi poet and the inscriptions in the tomb of Khesu the Elder in the Western Delta of Egypt, grants to programs designed to further the appreciation of the multiethnic cultures of Queens and Nassau County, N.Y., and grants for an exhibition on 18th-century American household objects.

Why should the Federal Government play such a role?

Mrs. Cheney replied by citing a letter written by John Adams to his wife, Abigail, in 1780.

''He said,'' she recounted, ''that he would study legislation and administration so that his sons - or daughters, I might add - might study 'mathematics and philosophy, natural history and naval architecture, navigation, commerce and agriculture in order to give their children a right to study painting, poetry, music, architecture, statuary, tapestry and porcelain.' ''

''A free people,'' Mrs. Cheney continued, ''must be an educated people.''
