ATHLETICS AS USUAL

By Gordon A. Craig; Gordon A. Craig, the J.E. Wallace Sterling Professor of Humanities Emeritus at Stanford University, is the author of ''The Germans.''
Published: June 15, 1986

HITLER'S GAMES The 1936 Olympics. By Duff Hart-Davis. Illustrated. 256 pp. New York: Harper & Row. $16.95.

FOR the Western democracies, the 1930's were years of economic depression and domestic turmoil, and it is understandable that their governments and peoples found it difficult to think seriously about matters beyond their own boundaries. Hence the failure to come to grips at an early stage with the menacing new regime that took power in Germany in January 1933. To the majority of Americans, Hitler was not a source of concern until the very end of the decade, when they reacted to his existence almost with surprise, like the woman in James Thurber's 1938 cartoon who asks, ''Who is this Hitler and what does he want?'' In Britain, the Hitler problem was held at bay by irony and humor - as in David Low's endless strip ''Hit and Muss,'' about the adventures of two vaguely familiar dictators who own a tandem bicycle, and in J. C. Squire's mocking verse: What's the use of Goering?

He never was worth while. So pack all your Goebbels in your old kit bag, And Heil! Heil! Heil! or by bland Government assurances that, as far as the country was concerned, such a problem did not exist.

This complacency, however, always had an undertone of unease, induced by the behavior of the new ruler in Berlin. His brutal treatment of Jews and political opponents made thoughtful people question the possibility or propriety of having normal relations with anyone so contemptuous of law and the standards of civilized life. His Saturday Surprises in 1935, in which he repudiated the arms clauses of the Versailles Treaty, strengthened these doubts, and his dispatching of armed units into the demilitarized Rhineland in March 1936 struck many foreign observers as the prelude to a dangerously adventurous foreign policy.

In these circumstances, Germany's invitation to the youth of the world to attend the 1936 Olympic Games in Garmisch-Partenkirchen and Berlin raised moral and political questions of the greatest delicacy for Western governments. Could they refuse the German invitation without creating an international incident that would increase the already formidable number of problems with which they had to cope? Could they accept it without seeming to condone the domestic crimes of the Hitler regime and to overlook its breaches of international law, thus encouraging it to new outrages? On these questions the spirits were divided, and the story of the bitter and protracted debate that took place before the athletes departed for Germany constitutes the most interesting part of Duff Hart-Davis's book, ''Hitler's Games: The 1936 Olympics.''

This is not to say that the Games themselves are inadequately treated, for in this respect Mr. Hart-Davis's account compares very favorably with Richard Mandell's ''Nazi Olympics'' of 1971 and is better written. A former journalist, the author of many works of fiction and nonfiction and currently an editorial adviser for The Sunday Telegraph, Mr. Hart-Davis is particularly good on the planning and construction phase of the Games, in which Hitler himself played an interesting role and the end result of which impressed and sometimes overwhelmed foreign visitors. The author has lively accounts of the winter Games and the unexpected triumph there of the British hockey team, of the torch run from ancient Olympia to Berlin and the grand opening of the summer Games on August 1, and of the exploits of such outstanding athletes as the American Jesse Owens, the New Zealand metric miler Jack Lovelock, the Korean Kitei Son, who won the marathon for his country's occupier, Japan, and the German hammer thrower, Karl Hein. He also has a fine chapter on ''entertainments,'' which includes a description of a party given by Joseph Goebbels on the Pfaueninsel in Berlin at which, in a not untypical example of Nazi overkill, the fireworks display almost deafened the guests.

But essentially this is a book about the politics of the 1936 Games, and as such it is a sad story. For although the Western governments were urged by any number of individuals and groups to take the line that Germany's domestic policies made it an inappropriate location for the Olympics, and although an American diplomat urged that taking that line would represent ''one of the most serious blows which National Socialist prestige could suffer'' and would weaken Hitler's influence over German youth, they refused to take any responsibility for the decision whether or not to go to Berlin. Mr. Hart-Davis tells us that the standard Foreign Office response to letters demanding a boycott of the Games was that ''the question of British participation . . . is left entirely in the hands of the [ British Olympic Games Committee, ] a private organisation with whom His Majesty's Government has no connexion.'' A similar position was adopted by the United States Government. THE national committees came to be dominated by people, like Avery Brundage in the United States, who believed that sports should be unaffected by politics and who convinced themselves that they could persuade Hitler to agree with them. Not even the blatant exclusion of Jewish athletes from competition for places on the German team - which George S. Messersmith, the United States Consul General in Berlin, said could be ignored by the American committee only at the cost of ''betraying its obligations towards American athletic organisations and American youth'' - deterred them in their determination to be represented at Berlin. And in the end they had their way, to Hitler's great advantage.

In this respect, the stubbornest person in Mr. Hart-Davis's cast of characters, and the blindest to Nazi violations of the Olympic spirit, was the Belgian aristocrat Count Henri de Baillet-Latour, the president of the International Olympic Committee. With an assurance that bordered on effrontery, he simply ignored views contrary to his own, arguing that the campaign of opposition to participation in the Games was based on assertions ''whose falsity it has been easy for me to unmask.'' After Hitler's Rhineland coup in March 1936 had started new talk of a boycott, Baillet-Latour told a press conference that only an armed conflict would prevent the holding of the Games in Berlin. When his persistence was rewarded, he attended a grand dinner given by Hitler for Olympic notables and sat next to the wife of the Nazi Youth Leader, Baldur von Schirach. Mr. Hart-Davis reports that she remarked on how well ''the great festival of youth, peace and reconciliation'' was going. Baillet-Latour answered, ''May God preserve you from your illusions, Madame! If you ask me, we shall have war in three years.''
