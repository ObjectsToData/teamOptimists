An Intellectual Utopia Proves to Be a Work in Progress

By LYNDA RICHARDSON
Published: July 2, 2000

After a year hidden away at the New York Public Library, Paul Berman, a journalist and cultural critic, was finally ready to meet his public, with an impressionistic lecture that touched on religion, sex and Spanish imperialism.

The hourlong talk by Mr. Berman, one of the first 15 fellows at the library's new Center for Scholars and Writers, focused on two Latin American poets, Sor Juana Ines de la Cruz and Ruben Dario. He recited long quotations of poetry in Spanish, sometimes passionately, then offered his own English translations. He did not use notes. It was almost like performance art.

When it was over, no one in the small audience asked any questions. At earlier lectures by library fellows -- one about the letters of Arturo Toscanini, the Italian conductor, another about the children's books of William Godwin, an anarchist political philosopher -- there had been excited chatter and raised hands.

Mr. Berman, 50, the author of ''A Tale of Two Utopias: The Political Journey of the Generation of 1968,'' could not explain the reaction, just his lecture. ''It was presented as a work of art,'' he said. ''It was presented as an adventure in poetry. It was not a polemic. People were puzzled. What can I say?''

The awkward moment was an extreme example of the difficulties faced by the library's new center as it tries to find a public face. In a somewhat utopian effort to create an intellectual community, 15 fellows were thrown together in a renovated suite of offices on the northeastern corner of the landmark Humanities and Social Sciences Library at 42nd Street and Fifth Avenue.

The one-year residency, one of the more generously endowed humanities fellowships in the country, comes with a stipend of $50,000 and a housing allowance for out-of-towners. The fellows included novelists, historians, journalists, university-based scholars, a doctor and a musician.

The public had only a few opportunities to learn what the library fellows were up to. Aside from the series of five free lectures, including Mr. Berman's in early May, the fellows mostly kept to themselves, though early on they did ask one another: how would the center make use of its prominent address? What would be its public face?

Other humanities institutes have faced these kinds of questions. ''It's a paradox,'' said Jocelyn Carlson Baltzell, former associate director of the New York Institute for the Humanities, a sort of lunch club for intellectuals in Greenwich Village, and now the associate director of the Los Angeles Institute for the Humanities.

''Maybe it's mixing two irreconcilable aspects of intellectual life,'' she said. ''In medieval terms, there is a life of action and a life of contemplation, two separate lives. How could you possibly blend them? You get a hybrid.''

The library fellows devoted their days to contemplating, reading and writing in glass- and oak-paneled offices equipped with computers and louvered hardwood blinds that snapped shut for privacy. They shared a telephone and met over a buffet lunch in the lecture room every day. Offerings from the library's vast collections were delivered at least twice daily on a rolling cart.

The time seemed to melt away. On the last official day of the program on May 26, they swapped practical advice on how to download documents onto computer disks. They packed their books and boxes and traded e-mail addresses. One of the more important memos circulating was a list of the best pastry and coffee shops in Vienna, compiled by Christian Fleck, a sociologist from Austria. Some leftover Champagne materialized at lunch. There was a tinge of sadness.

Francine Prose, a novelist who is writing a book on the lives of women known principally for having been muses to well-known artists, said the intellectual dividends of the program would become apparent from work produced by the fellows. ''For me, the great beauty of it was the ability to do my work,'' she said. ''Sure, it's nice if all that other stuff happens. But every moment I got to be in my office working is a real blessing.''

D. Graham Burnett, a historian, found that having the time to read in solitude was precious indeed.

''The process of scholarship is really reading,'' said Mr. Burnett, who had a futon delivered to his office because he does not read well sitting up.

The center's leading donors, Dorothy Cullman and her husband, Lewis B. Cullman, insisted that writers be included to spice things up. They gave $10 million.

Twelve fellows were named by an anonymous selection committee after a rigorous application process. Three were chosen by the center's director, Peter Gay, a Yale historian. The applicants had to demonstrate that their work would benefit directly from access to the library's collections.

In announcing the first class of fellows last year, the library's president, Paul LeClerc, said he had envisioned the center ''becoming a great intellectual spark plug in the life of New York.''

In a recent interview, Mr. LeClerc said he would not abandon the spark plug metaphor. ''A certain amount of sparks happened this year, and we'll see more in the future,'' he said.
