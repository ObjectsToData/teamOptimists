The Nation: Confessional; Crime, Punishment and the Passions of Miranda

By LINDA GREENHOUSE
Published: April 16, 2000

AS the Supreme Court prepares to revisit the Miranda decision, in a case to be argued Wednesday, there is something oddly appropriate about the timing.

Miranda, of course, was a case about confessions, and 34 years after the court raised the bar for obtaining confessions from criminal suspects, not only do people who receive the famous warnings still bare their souls, but the country is awash in confessions of all kinds.

Confession -- by almost anyone to almost anything -- appears to be a national obsession. There are memoirs on the best-seller list, revelations of appalling intimacy on television. Political leaders, or would-be leaders, are expected to admit to all manner of personal transgressions: Barely two years ago, the country was mesmerized by President Clinton's grudging path toward confessing to what, by the end, everyone already knew.

So it should be no surprise that after all these years, long after other landmarks of the Warren Court's criminal procedure revolution were comfortably absorbed by the criminal justice system, Miranda v. Arizona still stirs passions, as the foot-high stack of briefs filed in the pending Supreme Court case attests. True, 80 to 90 percent of suspects waive their Miranda rights and talk anyway, and many police departments have long since made peace with Miranda. But the decision still casts a long symbolic shadow.

As Miranda's reappearance before the Supreme Court demonstrates, the passions unleashed in 1966 never really went away; they just kept flowing underground, kept alive by the political right largely out of sight of the liberal establishment that had considered the question closed long ago. The new case is the result of a tenacious search for a vehicle that might push the Supreme Court to resolve the question of whether a long-ignored 1968 law supplanted Miranda. The argument is being made by an otherwise obscure University of Utah law professor, Paul Cassell, who was only 6 years old when Miranda was decided but who has since built a career as its mostly widely published critic, along with the Washington Legal Foundation. Professor Cassell says Miranda has unduly handicapped the police in pursuing criminals, although social scientists on the other side have raised serious questions about the validity of the statistics he offers to back up his argument.

Congress intended the 1968 law, known as Section 3501, to replace the Miranda warnings, in federal cases, with an inquiry by the trial judge into whether a confession was voluntary in light of ''all the circumstances.'' This is essentially the case-by-case test the Supreme Court found unworkable two years earlier when it decided Miranda. Section 3501 and Miranda are mutually exclusive; by the time the court decides Dickerson v. United States early this summer, only one of them will be left standing. (While a decision in favor of Section 3501 would apply only to federal cases, it would free states to adopt their own statutory substitutes.)

That the battle over Miranda still rages is evidence of ''the whole cultural unease, the ambivalence, about confession and what we expect and want from it,'' said Peter Brooks, the Chester D. Tripp Professor of Humanities at Yale University, who has written an illuminating book on the role of confession in western thought. ''Troubling Confessions: Speaking Guilt in Law and Literature,'' which is to be published next month by the University of Chicago Press, examines confession's deep roots in religious, literary, legal and cultural tradition. Confession is ''one of the most complex and obscure forms of human speech and behavior,'' Professor Brooks writes, in part because it serves a dual role, leading simultaneously to punishment and absolution, discipline and redemption. Confession is supposed to serve the interests of the state even as it meets the needs of the soul.

The book examines confession in the context of the church, the analyst's couch and the novelist's pen, as well as in judicial opinions, which Professor Brooks reads as literary texts and places in the company of Dante, Rousseau, Dostoevsky and James Joyce. He quotes Joyce's depiction of Stephen Dedalus, in ''Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man,'' returning home after confession, ''conscious of an invisible grace pervading and making light his limbs.''

That urge to feel the cleansing power of confession, Professor Brooks says, ''must make us suspicious of its use for other purposes: purposes that seek punishment rather than absolution, or, even, that claim that absolution and purification pass through punishment, rather than penance.''

THE urge is so strong that false confessions have long troubled the criminal justice system, as the court noted in the Miranda decision. Aside from the constitutional and human rights arguments against coerced confessions, there are substantial questions about whether such confessions are reliable; a lawyer's presence offers protection not only against compelled self-incrimination but also against false confession.
