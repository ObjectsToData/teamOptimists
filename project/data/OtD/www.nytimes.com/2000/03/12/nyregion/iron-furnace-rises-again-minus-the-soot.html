Iron Furnace Rises Again -- Minus the Soot

By FRANCES CHAMBERLAIN
Published: March 12, 2000

CONNECTICUT'S Northwest corner is characterized by pastoral scenes, winding roads and scenic farmland. It's hard to picture that at the turn of the last century there was a sprawling network of blasting hot iron furnaces, billowing soot, trains loaded with pig iron, and an industry that made part of Litchfield County look anything but pastoral.

Today, you have to imagine much of this -- wooden buildings have collapsed or burned, gigantic stone furnaces have collapsed, forget the trains and few signs remain of what was once a thriving business.

Along Lower Road in East Canaan, one of three former furnaces in that area has been restored and gives the public an opportunity to see how such a furnace used to operate. In fact, enough interest has arisen around the remains of the iron industry to create ''The Heritage Trail,'' seven sites that tell the story of Connecticut's booming turn-of-the-century iron industry. In the Housatonic watershed, there were 44 furnaces over three states, about 19 in Connecticut.

Fred Hall, Canaan's town historian, said he remembers, as a young child, going out on business with his father, a stonecutter and blacksmith. His father had been called make a repair across the road from the Beckley Furnace, which closed in 1918.

''Someone invited me to come over to the furnace,'' Mr. Hall recalled. ''And I stood by the hearth as the hot iron began running out. I could feel the heat of it, like a volcano.''

The temperature in the casting shed probably reached 140 to 150 degrees as the iron poured into troughs. From there it would be cooled and transported to the foundry in Lime Rock for final transformation into something useful like cannons and railroad wheels.

While the industry started in other areas, it was in the region from Stockbridge, Mass., down to Dover Plains, N.Y., that it really became successful, because here there was water power, hardwood forests to make charcoal and hills containing carbonate rock that could be quarried for lime, which removed the impurities from the iron. Wrought iron was first produced in forges shortly after the Pilgrims landed, but in 1644 the first blast furnace began producing tools, nails, cooking utensils, and plows.

Connecticut produced bog ore, which develops from bacterial action in bogs, until 1731, but the discovery of limonite iron in Salisbury triggered the growth of a huge industry. In 1734, the first forge was built in Lime Rock, and for the next 190 years, the iron industry determined how life would be lived in this state.

The Beckley Furnace in Canaan would have eventually fallen into irreparable decay if it weren't for the efforts of a group of volunteers. The Committee for the Preservation of the Beckley Furnace consists of 10 people, including Mr. Hall, Dr. William Adam of Canaan, whose family used to operate the business in the 18th and 19th centuries, and Edward M. Kirby of Sharon, a retired school administrator and historian.

Mr. Kirby combined a serious interest in science with history. ''My field was really geology,'' he said. That led to an interest in the important rock formations that allowed Northwest Connecticut's iron industry to prosper. ''They needed this local rock formation to get lime,'' he explained. ''Most came from Salisbury. There was a belt of iron ore and wood from north of Pittsfield to Dover, New York, called the Salisbury Iron District. It has a certain kind of manganese that was shock resistant and therefore perfect for cannons and railroad car wheels.''

Today, there are fields waiting for an archaeological dig, all in the hopes that unearthing things will help piece together how the furnace worked. Memories like those of Mr. Hall and old pictures have helped reconstruct part of the history, but iron furnaces were such a common part of life that people didn't always notice the day-to-day routine or make a record of it.

On the other side of Lower Road, across from the Beckley Furnace, trains unloaded lime ore and charcoal, which was ferried across the road by a small suspended bridge to be fed into the top of the furnace.

The furnace started out at 32 feet high, then was later raised to 40 feet. To make a ton of iron, 138 bushels of charcoal had to be fed into the 40-foot-high opening. The furnace was fired up with the assistance of huge bellows, pumping tubs and water power from the Blackberry River, just a few yards away.

Even the language of the furnace sounds quaint today. ''The tuyere was a nozzle that shot blasts of hot air into the furnace,'' Mr. Kirby explained. ''The tuyere reduced the amount of charcoal and time to get the furnace up to a productive heat.''

The bustle pipe, which came around the furnace from the back, shot air into two tuyeres. In addition to the tuyere arch, and tuyere, there was a ''downcomer'' to the tuyere, and the bottom of the bosh, where the blast furnace shaft tapered inward to meet the hearth and crucible. Eleven salamanders, dense iron that couldn't be melted down again, were found on the site, each weighing about 10 tons.
