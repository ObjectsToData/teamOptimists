THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 19, 2000

Photo History

The 19th-century daguerreotype process will be the subject of a discussion, ''Shadows on Silver: The Art and Technology of the Daguerreotype,'' on Thursday at 6 p.m. at the Connecticut Historical Society in Hartford. The program is an adjunct to ''A Durable Memento: Augustus Washington, African American Daguerreotypist,'' on view through May 2.

William Robinson, author of ''A Certain Slant of Light: The First Hundred Years of New England Photography,'' will serve as moderator. Panel members include Alan Trachtenberg of Yale, Roger Watson of the George Eastman House Museum, Lucy Bowditch of the College of St. Rose, and Matthew Isenberg, a collector and author.

The method of producing images without a negative, invented in 1839, was chosen by Augustus Washington as his life's work. Out of some 49 of his daguerreotypes believed to be in existence, 33 are in the exhibition.

Washington was born in New Jersey to a free black father and an Asian mother. He was accepted at Dartmouth College in 1843 at the age of 21 but, after a year, lacked the money to continue. He turned to photography to support himself (charging 50 cents to $10 a sitting) and by 1846 had opened a daguerreotype studio in Hartford that flourished for six years. Washington managed to capture the patronage of many Hartford notables including Eliphalet A. Bulkeley, founder of the Aetna Insurance Company, and Lydia Sigourney, the poet.

Washington died in Liberia in 1875, after emigrating in 1853 with his family and serving in the Liberian legislature.

The Historical Society at 1 Elizabeth Street is open Tuesday through Sunday from noon to 5. Admission to the panel discussion is free with general admission of $6, $3 for students and older people. The number to call for more information is (860) 236-5621.

Out of Doors

Paugussett State Park in Newtown is to be explored in the first Western Connecticut Orienteering Club Meet of the new year. Registration and starts at all levels from novice to expert will take place between 10 a.m. and noon. The fee is $5.

Orienteering means using a map and compass to go it alone in the wild. Participants choose one of the routes indicated on the map. At each color-coded checkpoint flag they punch a score card to verify their progress. Those who wish to compete must run the course and punch the cards in the shortest time. (So far, participants have gotten lost but have always been found and shepherded home.)

Maps are distributed at the site, instruction and compasses are available, and more information may be obtained by calling the leader, Tony Muffatti, at (203) 798-1231.

Fruit and Flowers

Still lifes by Terry Donsen Feder and Lisa Macbride will be on view through April 1 at 100 Pearl Gallery in Hartford.

Ms. Feder uses watercolor on plaster to create fruit and floral images flanked by brightly colored panels containing excerpts from texts on art theory and language. She is also exhibiting colored-pencil drawings of mysteriously arranged objects. The artist teaches composition, literature and American studies at the University of Hartford.

Watercolors are Ms. Macbride's medium as well. Her subjects are marionettes within stage settings, exquisitely drawn and delicately colored but filled with tensions and traumas. Ms. Macbride, trained at the Rhode Island School of Design, has exhibited works in New England, New York and South America, received grants from the Connecticut Commission on the Arts and the Greater Hartford Arts Council, and been a resident at the Yaddo Colony for the Arts.

The gallery, at 100 Pearl Street, is open Monday through Friday from 9 a.m. to 8 p.m., Saturday from 9 to 3. The number to call for more information is (860) 233-1932.

Using Yeast, Making Soup

Mary Ann Esposito's 11-year-old cooking show on public television, ''Ciao Italia,'' is carried nationally in the United States as well as in England, Japan, Canada and South Africa. Ms. Esposito, author of several books on Italian cookery, will lead a workshop based on her book ''What You Knead'' at The Silo in New Milford on Friday from 7 to 10 p.m.

The workshop will focus on three types of dough that require the use of yeast, demonstrating how to tame the tricky substance to produce baked goods from plum kuchen to tomato tarts to English muffins. The fee is $80, $150 for two.

Children might like to sign up for a workshop to be held next Sunday from 1 to 3:30. Under the guidance of Gary Goss, author of ''Blue Moon Soup, A Family Cookbook,'' they will be in charge of their own pots. The fee for a team of child and parent is $60. The number to call for more information is (860) 355-0300.

Film Festival

Twenty-two films, documentaries and short films will be shown through April 2 during the Fourth Annual Jewish Film Festival presented by Trinity College, the Greater Hartford Jewish Community Center and the Wadsworth Atheneum in Hartford, supported by the Connecticut Humanities Council.

Saturday's opening at 7:30 at the Atheneum is ''The Giraffe'' followed by a reception with the film's director, Dani Levy. Among next Sunday's offerings is ''Silence'' and ''Karoussell,'' to be screened in the Cinestudio at Trinity at 2 p.m., and ''The Life and Times of Hank Greenberg'' at the college's faculty club at 7:30.

The number to call for a complete schedule and directions is (860) 236-4571.

Sacred Spaces

Alexander Gorlin, a New York City architect, winner of the Rome Prize in architecture, and expert on the role of architecture in religion, will deliver a talk on ''The Architecture of Sacred Spaces'' today at 4 p.m. at the Aldrich Museum of Contemporary Art in Ridgefield.

The talk relates to the current exhibition at the Aldrich, ''The Impact of Judeo-Christian Religion on Art at the Millennium.'' Admission is $10. The museum is at 258 Main Street and the number to call for more information is (203) 438-4519.

Prints on View

An exhibition of exceptional prints can be seen at the Creative Arts Workshop in New Haven through March 31. Curated by Barbara A. Harder, printmaker and head of the department at the workshop, the 11 artists in the show were chosen for their expertise in bending the process to their individual wills. The images may be realistic or abstract, reflecting the originality and technical skill of the maker.

Among the artists are Lynne Allen, faculty member at Rutgers University; Vijay Kumar and Judy Mensch, founders of the Manhattan Graphics Center in New York City; Wayne Miyamoto, chairman of the art department at the University of Hawaii, and James Reed, a master printer and faculty member at the State University of New York at Stonybook.

Other exhibitors include Anne Queeney McKeown, faculty member at UConn Stamford and associate printer at the Graphic Arts Center in Norwalk, who will present a talk on Friday at noon, and J. Catherine Bebout, head of the print department at Montclair State University in New Jersey, who will lecture on March 31 at noon.

The Creative Arts Workshop at 80 Audubon Street is open Monday through Friday from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m., Saturday from 9 to noon. The number to call for more information is (203) 562-4927. ELEANOR CHARLES
