PLAYING AROUND THE NEIGHBORHOOD

By ANDREA DELBANCO
Published: October 22, 2000

COOPER SQUARE

Drug Center Photographs In Black and White

In 1964 Lisl Steiner, a celebrated photojournalist for Time-Life, became interested in a shelter for drug addicts in Harlem. The shelter was founded and run by John Cram, a descendant of Peter Cooper, the 19th-century manufacturer and philanthropist.

Ms. Steiner spent an afternoon photographing the residents, and the resulting series of black and white photographs are on view in an exhibition at Cooper Union, ''John Cram in Harlem -- Photographs of Lisl Steiner.'' It was organized by Peter Buckley, an associate history professor at Cooper Union.

''John Cram in Harlem -- Photographs of Lisl Steiner,'' tomorrow through Friday 11 a.m. to 7 p.m.; Humanities Gallery of Cooper Union, Albert Nerken School of Engineering, Eighth Street near Third Avenue; free; (212) 353-4272.

CENTRAL PARK

Boo, Mateys! All Aboard For Pumpkin Sailing

Jack-o'-lanterns of 10 pounds or less are eligible for the 10th Annual Pumpkin Sail at the Harlem Meer today, sponsored by the Central Park Conservancy. The lighted pumpkins are set afloat, as shown in the photo at left by Sara Cedar Miller. Sailing starts at 5:30. Registration ends at 4:15; sailing spaces are limited.

Participants should dress warmly and in costume. Children 5 to 12 who are dressed up can participate in a parade led by Frankenstein and other costumed characters at 4:30 around Harlem Meer. John Colligan will spin spooky stories, and refreshments will be served.

Halloween Parade and Pumpkin Sail; today, 4:30 p.m. to 6:30 p.m.; Charles A. Dana Discovery Center, inside park at 110th Street and Fifth Avenue; free; (212) 860-1370.

MIDTOWN

Children of the Nightshade: Favorite Poisonous Plants

The Horticultural Society of New York is offering an ''evening of eerie enlightenment'' Wednesday with talks by Cassandra Danz, author of ''Mrs. Greenthumbs Plows Ahead: Five Steps to the Drop-Dead Gorgeous Garden of Your Dreams'' (Crown Publishers, 1998), and by Deirdre Larkin, a horticulturist.

Ms. Danz will talk at 6 p.m. on ''Know and Grow Your Poisonous Plants -- Lethal Favorites from Mrs. Greenthumbs.''

Ms. Larkin will follow at 6:30 p.m. with ''Forked Roots and Poison Berries -- Plants, Magic and Witchcraft.''

The lectures mark the opening of an exhibition of black and white photographs taken by Margaret Casella. Her works reveal the fine (though hairy) details of root crops, like the image of a dandelion shown below.

Horticultural Society of New York, Margaret Casella photographs: opening event Wednesday 6 p.m. to 9 p.m.; exhibition through Nov. 24, Mondays through Fridays 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; 128 West 58th Street; opening night lectures $10, reception and exhibition admission free; to register call (212) 757-0915, ext. 200.

ALSO AROUND TOWN

CELEBRATING DOROTHY DAY -- Pax Christi Staten Island, a community group, and Wagner College present ''A Saint for Staten Island: An Evening With Dorothy Day,'' with short videos and a panel discussion about Day, a pacifist and advocate of the poor who was a founder of the Catholic Worker movement in the 1930's; Thursday 7 p.m.; Room 4, Spiro Hall, Wagner College, (near Clove Road Exit of Staten Island Expressway); free; (718) 390-3309.

NATURE'S WAY -- The New Life Expo Symposium for Natural Health features seminars and presentations on topics like holistic heart health, herbs for the immune system, women's health care, yoga and cancer treatment; today 11 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Hotel New Yorker, 34th Street and Eighth Avenue; tickets begin at $10; to register, (212) 787-1600.

FLAX TIME -- Flax will be spun into linen using traditional tools at a demonstration by the Prospect Park Alliance and the Lefferts Homestead Children's Historic House Museum; today 2, 3 and 4 p.m.; at the museum, Prospect Park, on Flatbush Avenue near Empire Boulevard; free; (718) 965-6505. ANDREA DELBANCO
