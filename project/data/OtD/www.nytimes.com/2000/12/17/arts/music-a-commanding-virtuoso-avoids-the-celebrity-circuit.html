MUSIC; A Commanding Virtuoso Avoids the Celebrity Circuit

By ANTHONY TOMMASINI
Published: December 17, 2000

THE pianist Russell Sherman is rather miffed that at this stage of his eminent career he is still considered by some to be an eccentric interpreter. ''I think of myself as a compassionate conservative,'' he said during a recent conversation in New York. ''I respond radically to the score and nothing but the score.''

Over the years his playing has often divided listeners. But the way he views it at 70, the division among listeners is ''between those who have imagination and those who don't.''

Mr. Sherman, who will perform Liszt's daunting ''Transcendental Etudes,'' all 12 of them, tomorrow evening at Alice Tully Hall, admits to being ''somewhat defensive.'' He is genuinely discouraged that this charge will not go away. In his work he has tried to counter what he sees as a dismaying penchant among performers of the last 50 years for music-making that places an inordinate value on unity, flawlessness and the interpretive ''straight line,'' as he calls it, rather than on range, spontaneity and the ''sheer variety which overpowers and delights us, and which I embrace.'' So strongly does he feel this that Mr. Sherman followed up his comments with a long letter sent from his home in Lexington, Mass., a few days later.

''I think that musical performances should be free,'' he wrote, ''should invite danger, should tell a story, should court the 'madness of art,' should in every way reveal the characteristics and visions of the composers.''

There is not much music that courts danger and madness more directly than Liszt's. And Mr. Sherman, boasting an incomparable technique, an enormous palette of colors and a bold imagination, has won great respect for his performances of Liszt: in his estimation, a noble and misunderstood genius.

Tall but slight of build, with wispy strands of white hair and long, delicate fingers, Mr. Sherman does not cut the figure of a commanding virtuoso on stage. And in person, with his verbal dexterity, rich vocabulary and soft speaking voice (soft enough to confound any interviewer's attempt to tape him), he seems more a bookish professor than a dynamic Lisztian. But it was Mr. Sherman's astonishing cassette recording of Liszt's ''Transcendental Etudes'' that brought him to international attention in 1974.

Born in New York, Mr. Sherman was only 11 when he began formative studies with the Polish-born pianist and composer Edward Steuermann, a Busoni student who was still an intimate of Schoenberg at the time. Steuermann was a principled and self-effacing musician who conveyed to Mr. Sherman the sense that a musician's work should be a source of joy and discovery.

At 15, Mr. Sherman entered Columbia University, where he studied humanities and immersed himself in literary criticism. After graduation, he was quickly repelled by the idea of making a career as a virtuoso. Instead he became active in New York's new-music scene. In 1959 he essentially stopped performing professionally, moved west and began teaching, first at Pomona College in California, then at the University of Arizona. In 1967, he accepted a position at the New England Conservatory in Boston at the invitation of the composer Gunther Schuller, then its president.

When, in the mid-1970's, his recording of the ''Transcendental Etudes'' started to circulate, he was suddenly inundated with requests to play concertos and recitals. Clearly, he could have had big-time success on the touring circuit. But when he found himself at the Hollywood Bowl playing the Grieg Piano Concerto conducted by, of all people, the early-music specialist Christopher Hogwood, after only one rehearsal, he knew that he had to get out.

Though that cassette recording of the ''Transcendental Etudes'' is no longer available, an even greater version, Mr. Sherman's 1990 account on Albany Records, is.

Liszt began composing a set of etudes in 1824, at 13, and they were published two years later. In 1838 he recomposed the works and published them as ''Grandes Etudes.'' The ''Transcendental Etudes,'' Liszt's third reworking, date from 1851. Many pianists find them nearly impossible to play, especially all at once.

Most etudes, by definition, provide a specific technical workout, like Chopin's etude in double thirds. Liszt's ''Transcendental Etudes'' are visionary and fantastical works. ''The poetic idea is central,'' Mr. Sherman writes in his insightful program note for the Albany recording, ''and the virtuoso elements become so many layers to orchestrate the poetic content.''

''So many layers'' does not suggest the half of it. In these harmonically daring and thick-textured works, Liszt was not just venting his musical imagination but also pushing the piano itself to previously unknown realms of sound and effect.

For all the talk of Mr. Sherman's interpretive eccentricities, his reading of the ''Transcendental Etudes'' is striking for its cogency and directness. The untitled Second Etude, in A minor, is a sort of crazed battle music built on the Beethovenian dot-dot-dot-dash victory motive; Mr. Sherman dispatches it with rhapsodic sweep and mischievous glee. The fiendishly difficult ''Mazeppa,'' a wild horse ride of an etude, chock-full of leaping chords and keyboard-sweeping runs, gallops by with a ferocious relentlessness in Mr. Sherman's account. Yet he manages to bring a foursquare, dancelike brio to the music at the same time. And he makes the forest murmurs and twittering butterflies of ''Feux Follets'' seem like an anticipation of every coloristic device in the piano music of Debussy and Ravel. Whenever Mr. Sherman plays these works, Liszt emerges as the pathbreaking master the pianist believes him to be.
