Numbers For Magic In Spain: 01/01/01

By EMMA DALY
Published: December 31, 2000

''Happy Millennium!'' shines the sign, picked out in white lightbulbs on a bridge over the Paseo de la Castellana, the tree-lined avenue that leads south into the heart of Madrid. Stores are selling out of candles molded into the shape of XXI.

But these are not leftovers, not hand-me-downs from December 1999, for in Spain, most people agree, the 20th century ends this weekend, and with it, the second millennium.

As the Spanish daily El Pais wrote: ''January 1 has everything: it starts the week, the month, the year, the century, the millennium. Its numbers are magic, 01/01/01.''

The official State Society for Transition to the New Millennium has based its lengthy program of cultural activities in 2000 and 2001, dismissing Dec. 31 1999 as a populist fiesta for those who simply could not wait for the scientific truth.

''We have chronological advisers who explained it all very clearly,'' said Prof. Luis Miguel Enciso, president of the society. ''But we have always known two things: that the key date was 2001, but that a collection of zeros has such charm and attraction that people would decide on their own that the millennium fell in 2000.''

A brochure produced by the society approvingly quotes the American scientist Stephen J. Gould: ''It does not matter what public sentiment prefers, 1900 was part of the 19th century and 2000 will be the year that concludes the 20th century, and not the start of the next millennium.''

In June 1999 (several years after London, for example, had begun its ambitious millennial program), the Spanish government set up the society, also known as Spain New Millennium, with a budget equivalent to $167 million and a mandate to spend it on events of cultural importance (and some fun).

There was no desire to emulate the grandiose public building plans under way in London. Instead, the Spanish have organized exhibitions, conferences and competitions aimed at uniting ancient and modern. ''This is a meeting point between the past and the future, between science and technology, symbol of the future, and the humanities, symbol of the past and present,'' said Professor Enciso, who used to teach modern history at the Complutense University in Madrid.

Among what he views as the highlights of the Spanish effort are an exhibition of the past 1,000 years of Spanish history that will go to Brussels in February, and a show called, ''The Keys to 20th-Century Spain,'' which will include many artworks dealing with the Civil War, the Franco dictatorship and the restoration of the monarchy. It will also go on display in February in the new arts and science museum in Valencia.

Clearly celebrating with culture rather than risky projects like the Millennium Dome in London, the Madrid regional government is promoting the Festival of the Millennium, which offers Spain's newest flamenco sensation, Sara Baras, appearing with the soprano Ainhoa Arteta. And City Hall has planned a series of 64 concerts under the banner, Madrid Thrills!

''The city of Madrid has tried to commemorate, through music, the start of a century that will, without doubt, be one of fusion, of meetings and mixing of cultures,'' said Mercedes de la Merced, the mayor's deputy. ''Madrid wants to start the new millennium by being welcoming, not by being wrapped up in itself.''

By tradition, a Spaniard greets the New Year by eating 12 grapes at the stroke of midnight, as sounded by the bells of the clock tower in the Puerta del Sol in Madrid, and 2001 will be no exception. Half an hour later, the city plans a fireworks display: nearly a ton of explosives set off from seven points around the city.

On New Year's Day, Catalan comedians and other performers will invite citizens to ''Wake Up to the Millennium''; they will give pieces of paper to passers-by so they can make a wish before placing the papers in a balloon to be released to the heavens.
