By LAWRENCE VAN GELDER
Published: December 14, 2000

NEWS

Harlem in Mind

It's back to the Harlem Renaissance in the exhibition that opens tomorrow for a run through Jan. 16 at the Apollo Theater, 253 West 125th Street. Accompanied by music of the period, the free show illustrates how African-American artists and intellectuals in the 1920's and 30's found inspiration in African traditions and culture in developing American art, literature and music. ''A Stronger Soul Within a Finer Frame: Portraying African-Americans in the Black Renaissance'' includes more than 250 rare original books, posters, letters, photographs, artworks, recordings and related materials.

Halcyon Views

''Fresh Woods and Pastures New'' is a title taken from the last line of Milton's pastoral poem ''Lycidas.'' Beginning on Saturday this title applies to a new exhibition of works by Dutch artists like Rembrandt, Jan van Lievens and Jacob van Ruisdael at the Worcester Art Museum in Massachusetts. The show comprises 40 drawings that take the viewer for a stroll in the Italian countryside or among Roman and medieval ruins or into the humble surroundings of a farmer. Some drawings are preparatory studies for paintings, while others are finished products. ''Fresh Woods and Pastures New: 17th-Century Dutch Landscape Drawings'' is on view through Feb. 25.

Boys of Christmas

Winding up a 34-city North American tour, the Vienna Choir Boys are bringing Christmas music and other works to the metropolitan area. The 10- to 14-year-olds will be singing at 8 p.m. on Saturday at the New Jersey Performing Arts Center in Newark and at 7:30 p.m. on Sunday at Carnegie Hall.

On the Move

Raymund A. Paredes, 58, associate vice chancellor of the University of California at Los Angeles, has been appointed director of the creativity and culture program of the Rockefeller Foundation. The program promotes diverse expression in the arts and humanities and fosters the preservation of cultural heritage in developing countries.

A Little Music

What better occasion than the observance of the 230th birthday of Ludwig van Beethoven than to present ''Ode to Schroeder: The Art of the Toy Piano''? If the name Schroeder tinkles a tiny bell, it was he, of course, as a member of Charles Schulz's ''Peanuts'' gang, who regarded Beethoven as his inspiration. ''Ode to Schroeder'' will find the toy piano virtuoso Margaret Leng Tan at the keyboard, playing seasonal music at 1:30 and 3 p.m. on Saturday and Sunday (widely regarded as Beethoven's birthday) at the Children's Museum of Manhattan, 212 West 83rd Street.
