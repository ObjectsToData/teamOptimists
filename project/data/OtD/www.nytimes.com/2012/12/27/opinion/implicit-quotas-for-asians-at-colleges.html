Implicit Quotas for Asians at Colleges?

Published: December 26, 2012

To the Editor:

Connect With Us on Twitter

For Op-Ed, follow @nytopinion and to hear from the editorial page editor, Andrew Rosenthal, follow @andyrNYT.

“Asians: Too Smart for Their Own Good?,” by Carolyn Chen (Op-Ed, Dec. 20), paints a troubling picture of stigma and exclusion. Indeed, a system by which Asian students are denied access on account of racial stereotype would be abhorrent and unlawful.

That sounding alarm should be tempered by a view to the larger picture, however. In recent years, complaints of anti-Asian bias at Ivy League schools resulted in extensive investigations, but no findings of such bias. More important, there remains a strong Asian presence at selective universities untethered to Asian representation in the population.

Without further evidence, a claim that quotas against Asian overachievers are “implicit, but very real” overshadows more searing truths about educational equity for Asian-Americans: that many come from communities pervasively underrepresented in higher education; that Asian-Americans have their highest enrollment in chronically underserved community colleges; and that notions of “merit” limited to certain standards of academic performance negatively affect Asian students, too.

THOMAS L. MARIADASON
New York, Dec. 20, 2012

The writer is a staff attorney in the Educational Equity Program of the Asian American Legal Defense and Education Fund.

To the Editor:

Carolyn Chen makes a compelling case that admissions quotas at elite colleges for Asians are wrong, although her evidence that they actually exist is somewhat tenuous. What is troubling about her article, though, is that she states that we need to maintain affirmative action for certain other ethnic and racial groups.

Either you oppose race as a factor in admissions or you do not. It is hypocritical to state that race considerations should be used to achieve certain diversity you find desirable, but race should not be a factor if it results in diversity that you do not support.

To move any further down the path toward a race-blind society, there should be no consideration of an applicant’s race in the college admissions process, period. If the racial composition of elite schools changes, so be it, but the integrity of the putatively merit-based admission process will be restored.

ANTHONY BOLZAN
Boxford, Mass., Dec. 21, 2012

To the Editor:

The Argentine author Jorge Luis Borges describes a seemingly coherent classification system that in reality divides animals into arbitrary categories: embalmed, belonging to the emperor, stray dogs, those that have just broken a water pitcher.

The racial categorization system that we persist in using is as arbitrary as Borges’s despite its “scientific” aura. Who are Latinos? There are Mexicans of Native American heritage, Argentines of Eastern European Jewish roots, Peruvians of Chinese ancestry. And who are blacks? Is President Obama black?

Who exactly are “whites”? “Whiteness” became a prevalent category with the racialization of slavery. In the United States today, “white” tends to refer to people of Northern European ancestry. Scientists have suggested that the term be abandoned as both racist and not useful, and that identifications be based on geographic origin and migration history — as in Asian-American.

EDNA AIZENBERG
New York, Dec. 20, 2012

The writer is professor emeritus of humanities and Hispanic studies at Marymount Manhattan College and the author of a book about Borges, “The Aleph Weaver.”

A version of this letter appeared in print on December 27, 2012, on page A26 of the New York edition with the headline: Implicit Quotas for Asians at Colleges?.
