Spare Times: For Children, for Feb. 3-9

By LAUREL GRAEBER
Published: February 2, 2012
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

BAMkids Film Festival

American cinephiles often complain about the lack of good foreign films in theaters. And they’re adults. When was the last time your multiplex had subtitled cinema for 7-year-olds?

That’s where children’s film festivals come in, and the Brooklyn Academy of Music takes on the challenge this weekend with 73 works from 26 countries. Its 14th annual BAMkids Film Festival includes six features, all in their original languages. For those too young to follow subtitles, the festival has a solution: Actors read them aloud.

“We lower the sound of the soundtrack,” explained Suzanne Youngerman, director of the academy’s education and humanities department. “You can still hear it if you’re someone who can understand the language, but the subtitle reader’s voice is really louder.”

BAMkids, which typically caters to those 10 and under, offers three animated features this year: “Elias and the Treasure of the Sea” (Norway, recommended for ages 5 to 9), whose hero is an honest rescue boat in conflict with the big-shot ships of Polar Harbor Fishybusiness; “Mamma Moo & Crow” (Sweden, ages 4 to 7), about a cow-crow friendship; and “The Great Bear” (Denmark, ages 7 to 10), involving siblings, a hunter and the beast of the title.

The live-action features are “The Star Talers” (Germany, ages 8 to 10), from a little-known Brothers Grimm story; “The Magicians: The Secret” (Netherlands, ages 7 to 10), focusing on a father-son magic act; and “Morrison Gets a Baby Sister” (Netherlands, ages 6 to 8), about that great cross-cultural subject, sibling rivalry.

“I think in the U.S., films are so hampered by needing to have huge marketing budgets to even be shown,” Ms. Youngerman said. She added, “In foreign countries they’re able to really focus on issues and circumstances and characters that speak to children.”

But that’s not to say that BAMkids, organized by Nicole Dreiske, executive director of the International Children’s Media Center, lacks American films. Its six shorts programs include “Naked Mole Rat Gets Dressed,” an adaptation of Mo Willems’s picture book; “Orange City,” above, in which a friendly snail dispenses oranges to a variety of animals; and two films by Sandra Boynton: “One Shoe Blues,” in which B. B. King stars, and “Philadelphia Chickens,” with the brothers Kevin and Michael Bacon.

BAMkids also offers discussions with directors, live entertainment and voting for its awards, the BAMmies. Those who can’t read the ballots can check off appropriate symbols: little faces ranging from smiley to scowling.

(Saturday and Sunday, 10 a.m. to 4 p.m., BAM Rose Cinemas, 30 Lafayette Avenue, at Ashland Place, Fort Greene, 718-777-3456; schedules at bam.org; $12 per screening or performance; $9 for 65+ and 12 and under; $7 for BAM Cinema Club members.) LAUREL GRAEBER

For Children

‘The African Drum’ (Friday and Saturday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Shadow Box Theater’s multimedia presentation of traditional African tales  includes a talking leopard, a wise loon and a resourceful turtle. All are shadow puppets that appear to be frolicking in the grasslands, along with Kijana, the young heroine. (Through March 8 at various sites.) Friday at 10:30 a.m. and Saturday at 11 a.m., auditorium of P.S. 3, the Bedford Village School, 50 Jefferson Avenue, between Bedford and Franklin Avenues, Bedford-Stuyvesant, Brooklyn (entrance at the back of the school, on Hancock Street). Tuesday at 10 a.m., and Wednesday and Thursday at 10 and 11:30 a.m., Hostos Center for the Arts and Culture, 450 Grand Concourse, at 149th Street, Mott Haven, the Bronx. Information: (212) 724-0677, shadowboxtheatre.org; $10 in advance; $15 at the door; group rates available. Reservations advised.

‘The Amazing Max and the Box of Interesting Things’ (Saturday) Max Darwin, a k a the Amazing Max, promises not only interesting things, but also some interesting phenomena: levitation, strange appearances and disappearances, props that seem to move on their own. They’re part of this 50-minute magic performance, which includes audience participation. (Through March 10.) At 4:30 p.m., Manhattan Movement & Arts Center, 248 West 60th Street, (212) 239-6200, theamazingmax.com; $29.50 and $49.50.

‘Angelina Ballerina the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) That winsome white mouse who does all her scurrying in toeshoes has returned to the McGinn/Cazale Theater. Vital Theater Company’s musical adaptation draws on the books by Katharine Holabird and Helen Craig and the PBS series “Angelina Ballerina the Next Steps.” The show, by Susan DiLallo and Ben Morss, features Angelina and her fellow students doing modern dance, the Irish jig and hip-hop, as well as ballet. At 3:30 p.m., 2162 Broadway, at 76th Street, fourth floor, (212) 579-0528, vitaltheatre.org; $29.50; $49.50 for premium tickets; $25 lap seats for children under 1 available at the box office on the day of performance only.

‘Archaeology Zone: Discovering Treasures From Playgrounds to Palaces’ (Friday; Sunday through Tuesday; and Thursday) Children will step into the shoes of an explorer like Indiana Jones in this permanent exhibition at the Jewish Museum, but the adventures will be purely scholarly. Still, there is plenty of excitement in analyzing artifacts like a jar handle, a clay jug and a bangle and figuring out the purpose behind ancient pieces like a Greek helmet and a bull-shaped vessel. This interactive show, for ages 3 to 10, also includes a recreated room from the Ottoman period (about 1900), where young archaeologists can dress in costume. From 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; until 8 p.m. on Thursdays; until 4 p.m. on Fridays; 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street, (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org; free with admission: $12; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for under 12 and members.

Backstage Tours at ‘The Fantasticks’ (Monday through Wednesday, and Friday through Sunday) The producers of “The Fantasticks” think it’s time to introduce the next generation of theatergoers to their show, the longest-running musical in the world. They have initiated a program for young audiences that will take them behind the scenes after performances, where they can tour the backstage area, meet cast members (Aaron Carter is now starring), learn how the show is put on and take pictures. And, of course, have their first experience with Tom Jones and Harvey Schmidt’s fable about love. Performances are Monday, Tuesday and Friday at 8 p.m.; Wednesday at 2 p.m.; Saturday at 2 and 8 p.m.; Sunday at 3 and 7:30 p.m.; Jerry Orbach Theater, Snapple Theater Center, 210 West 50th Street, Manhattan, (212) 921-7862, fantasticksonbroadway.com. Free with the purchase of tickets: $51.50 to $76.50; $160 for a family four-pack. Tour reservations are required and should be made through the box office.

Ballet for Young People: Storytelling at New York City Ballet (Saturday) How can you tell a tale without words? Ballet companies do it at almost every performance, and this program, created for children ages 4 and older, shows young audiences how dancers build a narrative with movement. Members of City Ballet will perform excerpts from works by George Balanchine, Jerome Robbins and Alexei Ratmansky, and those attending will help create their own story ballet. At 11 a.m., David H. Koch Theater, Lincoln Center, David H. Koch Theater, Lincoln Center, (212) 496-0600, nycballet.com; $20.

‘The Berenstain Bears Live! In Family Matters, the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) The most famous bears since the three in the Goldilocks story are now onstage in an open-ended run of this adaptation of three of the titles in the long-running children’s book series by Stan and Jan Berenstain. The show, by Michael Borton and Michael Slade, offers a pleasant hour for small theatergoers, with a pop-flavored score and actors who inhabit their fuzzy roles enthusiastically. But like the books, it’s rather tame and tidy; after this many years of a formula, even the Berenstain cubs are starting to show their age. Saturdays at 11 a.m. and Sundays at 2 p.m., Marjorie S. Deane Little Theater, 10 West 64th Street, Manhattan, (866) 811-4111, berenstainbearslive.com; $34.95 to $59.95. (The most expensive tickets include wearable bear ears and foot-of-the-stage seating.)

Broadway Playhouse: Stephen Sondheim (Sunday) Merkin Concert Hall’s Broadway Playhouse series introduces great musicals to the next generation of theatergoers. At this concert it is also introducing a great musical maker: Stephen Sondheim. Hosted by Sean Hartley and directed toward ages 4 through 11, the series includes singalongs, interactive games and fully staged musical numbers. Here they will come from four shows for which Mr. Sondheim wrote the music and lyrics: “Into the Woods,” “Sweeney Todd,” “Follies” and “A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum.” At 11 a.m., Merkin Concert Hall, 129 West 67th Street, Manhattan, (212) 501-3330, kaufman-center.org; $20.

‘The Bully’ (Friday through Sunday) Vital Theater Company has again revived this ever-topical musical, by John Gregor and David L. Williams, which refreshingly avoids the trap of bullying its own audiences with heavy moralizing. Instead, the many pitfalls of the typical middle school relationship of victimizer and victim are revealed through wit, humor and a killer of a sly twist. (Through Feb. 26.) At 11 a.m. and 1 p.m., Vital Theater, 2162 Broadway, at 76th Street, fourth floor, (212) 579-0528, vitaltheatre.org; $25 and $30.

‘Cenicienta/Cinderella’ (Saturday) The heroine could easily lose her glass slipper dancing the tango in this Latin-flavored musical from Teatro SEA, which specializes in Latino theater for children. Presented in an easily understood combination of English and Spanish, the production also features surprises: a dwarf who narrates and an unexpected character who fills in for the ill fairy godmother. (Through Feb. 25.) Saturdays at 3 p.m., Los Kabayitos Puppet and Children’s Theater, Clemente Soto Vélez Cultural Center, 107 Suffolk Street, at Rivington Street, second floor, Lower East Side, (212) 529-1545, teatrosea.org; $18; $15 for children.

‘Cinderella: A New Version of the Oldest Story Ever Told’ (Friday, and Monday through Thursday) Not every ’‘Cinderella” tale has a glass slipper, a pumpkin-turned-coach or even a beleaguered young heroine. This production from the Circle in the Square Theater School, for ages 5 through 10, presents alternative versions from cultures in Africa, India and China. Adapted by David F. Eliet, they include a story in which a magic fish functions as the fairy godmother and another in which the protagonist is male. (Through Feb. 10.) At 10:30 a.m., Circle in the Square, 254 West 50th Street, Manhattan, (212) 307-0388, circlesquare.org; $5. Reservations required via phone, the Web site or an e-mail to admissions@circlesquare.org.

Family Saturday: Valentines en Français (Saturday) Whether you think of it as Family Saturday or Samedi en Famille, this new program from the French Institute Alliance Française offers opportunities for fun and some immersion in French. The theme this month is Valentine’s Day, and little ones (ages 2 to 5) can participate in holiday-theme arts and crafts and cookie decorating at 3:30 p.m. in Le Skyroom (no knowledge of French required). At 3:30 p.m. in Tinker Auditorium, the Cinékids film series, for ages 5 and older, will screen “The Three Robbers,” an animated French story about an orphaned little girl who leads three thieves to establish an orphanage with their ill-gotten gains. (This film is not subtitled.) For all ages, from 3:15 to 5 p.m., the Haskell Library will offer an open house with French storytelling, snacks and more crafts. Le Skyroom and Haskell Library, 22 East 60th Street, Manhattan; Tinker Auditorium, Gould Hall, 55 East 59th Street, Manhattan; (800) 982-2787, (212) 355-6160, fiaf.org; $10; $7 for members. The open house is free.

‘The Festival of the Vegetables’ (Saturday and Sunday) Children don’t always welcome vegetables, but they should make an exception for these: they’re stars in a theater-dance-music piece about the produce-filled dreams of a toddler who falls asleep in a grocery store. Written by Michael Kosch and choreographed by his wife, Rachael Kosch, the show, for ages 5 and older, is back for its sixth season with characters that include a King Potato, a String Bean Fiddler and the dancing Duncan Broccoli. (Through Feb. 19.) Saturday at 11 a.m. and 6:30 p.m.; Sunday at 11 a.m.; Metropolitan Playhouse, 220 East Fourth Street, East Village, (212) 995-5302, metropolitanplayhouse.org; $15; $10 for children 12 and younger.

First Saturdays Family Fun: ‘Gates of Equality’ (Saturday) This one-man show by Stanton Wood celebrates the life of a leader who helped open those gates. Presented by Urban Stages in honor of Black History Month, the play mixes fictional scenes with excerpts from the speeches and news conferences of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., portrayed here by Michael Green. Tracing his path from childhood through the civil rights movement, the show introduces its intended audience — children 6 and older — to his ideas and philosophy. At 4 p.m., Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture, 515 Lenox Avenue, at 135th Street, Hamilton Heights, (212) 491-2040, schomburgcenter.org; free; registration, via phone or at schomburgcenter.eventbrite.com, is required.

‘Freckleface the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) The title has changed, but the show hasn’t. Based on the actress Julianne Moore’s picture book “Freckleface Strawberry,” about a 7-year-old redhead who earns that nickname from her teasing friends, this delightful production has come back onstage at a new theater and with a new cast. The musical transcends the usual themes about celebrating the differences that make us special with a winning score and inventive comedy, as well as an acknowledgment of the unfunny aspects of its young heroine’s situation. (The run is open-ended.) At 1 and 3 p.m., Manhattan Movement & Arts Center, 248 West 60th Street, (212) 239-6200, frecklefacethemusical.com; $45 to $85.

‘Gazillion Bubble Show: The Next Generation’ (Friday through Sunday) Children love bubbles, and this interactive show promises not just a gazillion but also some of the largest ever blown, along with light effects and lasers. The stars are the members of the Yang family: Fan and Ana Yang and their son Deni and others, who rotate as M.C.’s for the production. Audience members may even find themselves in bubbles of their own. Friday at 7 p.m.; Saturday at 11 a.m. and 2 and 4:30 p.m.; Sunday at noon and 3 p.m.; New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, gazillionbubbleshow.com; $44.50 to $89.50; lap seats for ages 2 and under are $20, at the box office only.

‘Giggle, Giggle, Quack’ (Saturday and Sunday) If the title has a familiar ring, that’s because it’s a sequel to “Click, Clack, Moo: Cows That Type,” Manhattan Children’s Theater’s most popular show in 2009. (Both productions are adaptations of books by Doreen Cronin.) In this tale, turned into a musical by James E. Grote and George Howe, Farmer Brown has gone away, leaving his animals in the care of his brother, Bob. (The originality in these stories doesn’t lie in the names.) Bob is warned to watch out for Duck, a troublemaker (and maybe also a giggler). (Through Feb. 12.) Saturdays and Sundays at noon and 2 p.m., Manhattan Children’s Theater, 380 Broadway, at White Street, fourth floor, (212) 352-3101, mctny.org; in advance, $18; $16 for children. At the door, $20.

‘The History Mystery’ (Saturday and Sunday) Tada! Youth Theater has revived this musical about three children who voice a typical complaint about learning history: that it’s all about “boring old white men.” But when their imaginations take them back in time, they discover otherwise. The show’s creators — Janine Nina Trevens (book), Eric Rockwell (music) and Margaret Rose (lyrics) — introduce them to some interesting young people, including two named Eleanor Roosevelt and Martin Luther King Jr. (Through Feb. 19.) At 2 and 4 p.m., 15 West 28th Street, second floor, Manhattan, (212) 252-1619, Ext. 128, tadatheater.com; $25; $15 for 16 and under; some limited tickets at $20 and $8.

‘Jim Henson’s Fantastic World’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) The man behind the Muppets — and so many other feats of puppetry, film and imagination — is being celebrated in this exhibition at the Museum of the Moving Image, which has been extended through March 4. Containing over 120 artifacts, the show includes Kermit the Frog and Bert and Ernie Muppets; excerpts from Henson’s experimental films and his Muppet movies; and animations, storyboards, props and photographs spanning his career, from the 1950s television show “Sam and Friends” to “Sesame Street” and beyond. On Saturdays and Sundays, guided tours of the exhibition are at 2 p.m. This Saturday at 2:30 p.m. the museum will hold a workshop with the puppeteer John E. Kennedy to make a sheep puppet from a sweatshirt sleeve. Recommended for children 8 and over, the workshop will start with a screening of Mr. Kennedy’s short film “The Sure Sheep” and end with the young participants performing with their flock. This Saturday and Sunday at 1 p.m. the museum will also screen “Muppet Show” episodes featuring Elton John and Paul Simon. 35th Avenue at 37th Street, Astoria, Queens, (718) 777-6888, movingimage.us. Museum hours: 10:30 a.m. to 5 p.m.; until 8 p.m. on Fridays; until 7 p.m. on Saturdays and Sundays. Free with admission: $12; $9 for 65+ and college students; $6 for ages 3 to 18; free for under 3; free to all on Fridays from 4 to 8 p.m. Sweatshirt sheep workshop on Saturday: $16; $5 for members of Red Carpet Kids (includes museum admission).

‘The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe’ (Saturday) If the children of the Pevensie family could travel to the magical land of Narnia through something as simple as a wardrobe, why shouldn’t two hard-working actors be able to play them and all the characters found there? That’s what Erin Layton and Andrew Fortman attempt in Off Broadway Family Theater’s first production, le Clanché du Rand’s adaptation of C. S. Lewis’s classic novel. Saturdays at 11 a.m., St. Luke’s Theater, 308 West 46th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, narniaoffbroadway.com; $35.

‘Little Dragon Tales: Chinese Children’s Songs With the Shanghai Restoration Project’ (Saturday) The dragon is an ancient creature, but it undergoes some modernization in this program at Museum of Chinese in America. Dave Liang, part of the ensemble Shanghai Restoration Project, will perform signature Chinese children’s songs as electronic music, as he does on his “Little Dragon Tales” album. Families who would like to spend the entire day immersing themselves in the Year of the Dragon can first participate in a walking tour through Chinatown, learning about new year customs and traditions. Walking tour at 11 a.m.; “Little Dragon Tales” at 1:30 p.m.; Museum of Chinese in America, 215 Centre Street, between Howard and Grand Streets, Lower Manhattan, (212) 619-4785, mocanyc.org. “Little Dragon Tales” is $7; $4 for ages 3 and older; free for members and under 2. The walking tour is $18; $15 for students or 65+; $8 for members; free for under 5. Tour reservations are required via phone or an e-mail to education@mocanyc.org.

‘Little Miss Muffet’s Monster-Sitting Service’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) This Little Miss Muffet, Molly, a descendant of the original, is certainly a lot braver: not only does she not fear spiders, but she’s also willing to go into business with the very one that sat down beside her ancestor. In this new production from the Swedish Cottage Marionette Theater in Central Park, by Doug Strich and Emily Fellner, Molly and Al, the spider, start a baby-sitting service for infant monsters just as a huge winter storm is heading their way. (Through Aug. 26.) Friday, and Tuesday through Thursday, at 10:30 a.m. and noon; Saturday and Sunday at 1 p.m.; 79th Street and the West Drive, Central Park, (212) 988-9093, cityparksfoundation.org/swedish_cottage.html; $8, or $5 for under 12. Reservations required.

Lunar New Year Festival (Saturday) Dragons, a few lions and lots of children will take over the Metropolitan Museum of Art in this celebration of the Lunar New Year, the Year of the Dragon. In the morning highlights will include a “Sesame Street” puppet show about new year traditions; a Chinese zodiac and dragon scavenger hunt; and a workshop to make a giant dragon for the Great Hall balcony. After a noon lion dance up the museum’s front steps, the fun will continue with a lantern-making workshop, a calligraphy demonstration and more dance and musical performances. From 11 a.m. to 4 p.m., (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org/lunarnewyear. Free with suggested museum admission: $25; $17 for 65+; $12 for students; free for under 12 and members.

Material Lab at the Museum of Modern Art (Friday through Monday, and Wednesday and Thursday) No matter how much talent artists have, they need the right materials to create their work. This new interactive space at MoMA invites families to explore a wide range of mediums that are reflected in the museum’s collection. The stations in the Material Lab include a drawing table; a collage table; Discovery Boxes, with surprise materials within; Cornell Boxes, filled with found objects, in the spirit of the work of Joseph Cornell; and a digital painting experience using new technology from Microsoft. (Through June.) Friday, 10:30 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Monday, Wednesday, Thursday, Saturday and Sunday, 10:30 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Lewis B. and Dorothy Cullman Education and Research Building, (212) 708-9400, moma.org. Free with museum admission: $25; $18 for 65+; $14 for students; free for members and for ages 16 and under (and free on the first Thursday of every month); also free on Friday evenings from 4 to 8.

‘Monkey King: A Story From China’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) This Saturday, the Children’s Museum of Manhattan is welcoming back a mischievous hero: Monkey King, the star of this exhibition, which has traveled many miles for years, just like the character it centers on. The interactive show, inspired by the Chinese epic “The Journey to the West,” recreates many of the Monkey’s adventures, like fighting the Bull Demon at a station that resembles a video game. Much of the fun teaches Chinese values: cooperation, perseverance, bravery and self-sacrifice. (Through May 2013.) Hours: daily except Mondays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; to 7 p.m. on Saturdays; the Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street, (212) 721-1223, cmom.org. Free with admission: $11; free for under 1 and members; free from 5 to 8 p.m. this Friday.

‘Moon Over Manhattan’: Lunar New Year Family Day (Saturday) The Year of the Dragon isn’t just a Chinese phenomenon; cultures all over Asia celebrate the lunar new year, and Asia Society and Museum will investigate different countries’ traditions throughout this festival. The activities will include a music-and-storytelling performance; a lion dance; a kung fu demonstration; and workshops in making shadow puppets and Year of the Dragon ceramic charms. From 1 to 4 p.m., 725 Park Avenue, at 70th Street, (212) 517-2742, asiasociety.org; $12; $7 for members (free for Dual Family members); $5 for students, 65+ and children.

New Museum First Saturday for Families: Artist Workshop With Nicolás Paris (Saturday) Nicolás Paris, an artist from Bogotá, Colombia, should have no trouble connecting with children: he was trained as a schoolteacher. A participant in the New Museum’s forthcoming Triennial, “The Ungovernables,” he will explore the many facets of drawing — including its connection to learning — in this program for visitors ages 4 to 15. At 10 a.m., 235 Bowery, at Prince Street, Lower East Side, (212) 219-1222, newmuseum.org; free.

New York Family Camp Fair Tour (Saturday) Finding the right summer camp can be as complicated a process as finding the right school. New York Family magazine and the American Camp Association try to make the decision easier by holding annual fairs. Many camps, both day and sleep-away, will be represented at this event, offering options for ages 3 to 17. From noon to 3 p.m., Bank Street School, 610 West 112th Street, Morningside Heights, newyorkfamilycamps.com; free; registration advised.

The Paper Bag Players: ‘Laugh! Laugh! Laugh!’ (Saturday and Sunday) The title captures the usual reaction of small theatergoers to a show by the Paper Bag Players, who specialize in delighting those 3 to 8 with musical skits incorporating their signature oversize props and costumes of cardboard and paper. This all-new hourlong show includes bits about the first day of school and a little girl on a tricycle who’s a superhero. (Through Feb. 12.) At 2 p.m., Kaye Playhouse, Hunter College, 68th Street, between Lexington and Park Avenues, (212) 772-4448, kayeplayhouse.hunter.cuny.edu; thepaperbagplayers.org; $15 to $30.

‘Pinkalicious, the Musical’ (Saturday and Sunday) In Elizabeth and Victoria Kann’s adaptation of their children’s book, the pink-obsessed title character, a little girl, finds out that sometimes being in the pink can be too much of a good thing — especially when eating too many pink cupcakes has turned her pink from head to toe. (John Gregor wrote the score and some of the lyrics.) At 11 a.m., Manhattan Movement & Arts Center, 248 West 60th Street, (212) 579-0528, pinkaliciousthemusical.com; $29.50; $49.50 for premium tickets.

‘Shababa the Concert’ (Sunday) The annals of pop music have had Sha-La-La’s and Sha Na Na, and now there’s Shababa. This concert derives from the weekly program of the same name at the 92nd Street Y, which uses music and singalongs to help children celebrate the spirit and values of Shabbat, the Jewish Sabbath. Honoring the new CD “Shababaland,” this event will feature the Shababa Mamas, the children’s group the Miracle Makers, the lovable sloth puppet Coco and a new mystery puppet. From 3 to 5 p.m., 1395 Lexington Avenue, (212) 415-5500, 92y.org/shababa; $20 and up.

‘ “West Side Story” and Other Music of the Americas’ (Saturday) “Skyscrapers bloom in America,” Anita sings in “West Side Story,” and so do music and dance. This concert from the Little Orchestra Society, part of its Happy Concerts for Young People series, for ages 6 through 12, will highlight dance music from North and South America with works by Leonard Bernstein, Alberto Ginastera and Aaron Copland. Children from the Dancing Classrooms Youth Dance Company will show some of the moves this music inspires. At 11 a.m. and 1 p.m., Avery Fisher Hall, Lincoln Center, (212) 971-9500, lincolncenter.org; $15 to $50. LAUREL GRAEBER
