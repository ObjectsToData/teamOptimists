Ireland begins campaign to improve higher education

Connect With Us on Twitter

Follow @nytimesworld for international breaking news and headlines.

Twitter List: Reporters and Editors

Officials in Ireland have taken the first steps in an effort to transform the country’s higher education system, asking the heads of all institutions to submit detailed reports explaining mission statements, mandates and goals.

The process will ultimately promote collaboration and possibly encourage mergers, said Malcolm Byrne, spokesman for the Higher Education Authority.

“It is a significant reform of the system,” Mr. Byrne said last week in a telephone interview.

As part of a national education strategy announced by the Irish government last year, the authority is hoping to eliminate duplicate programs.

“It will focus energies and resources better to meet the needs of students, employers and the community,” said Diarmuid Hegarty, president of Griffith College Dublin and former chairman of the Higher Education Colleges Association.

Each institution has six months to present its report, “setting out its strategic intentions as to where it proposes to position itself within the Irish higher education system,” according to the public call published by the authority Feb. 13.

“We are working to ensure that the student that emerges from the higher education system is a world-class graduate,” Mr. Byrne said.

— CHRISTOPHER F. SCHUETZE


Revised exam in U.S. for medical school entrance

The body that administers the Medical College Admission Test, the examination required for entry into most U.S. medical schools, has announced plans to release a revised exam in 2015. The new MCAT will cover the social and psychological sciences, in addition to biology, chemistry and physics. It will also eliminate the current writing section.

“For over 80 years the MCAT has been the key test used to judge applicants for medical school,” said Dr. Darrell G. Kirch, the president and chief executive of the Association of American Medical Colleges, which administers the exam. “It was time to take a deep look at the MCAT and what it assessed so we could be confident that it was helping us pick the best doctors for the future of health care,” he said during a teleconference on Feb. 16.

Adding two new sections covering the psychological sciences and aspects of the humanities is the first major change to the exam since 1991. The test also adds an hour and a half to the MCAT’s previous 5-hour length.

The association says that growing body of evidence it has complied shows that doctors’ having a wider understanding of the social and cultural aspects of health leads to improvements in patient care, and that conclusion had prompted the changes.

— STEPHANIE NOVAK
