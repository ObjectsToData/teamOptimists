A Patron’s Passion for History on Display

Suzanne DeChillo/The New York Times

Roger Hertog put the New-York Historical Society on a path to modernity.

By ROBIN POGREBIN
Published: November 8, 2012

ABOUT six years ago, Roger Hertog opted out. He quit the business activities that had amassed him considerable wealth. He left almost all the many boards he served on, including the New York Public Library and the New York Philharmonic. The only thing he stuck with was the New-York Historical Society, in part because he is chairman of that board but also because he remains personally invested in the society’s mission: to relate the history of the country — through the prism of New York — from its founding through the present.

“The American story is an important one, flaws and all,” Mr. Hertog said in a recent interview at his spacious Fifth Avenue corner office overlooking Central Park. “The American story is a birthright. Every young person should know something about the history. It reveals broad lessons for today and tomorrow.”

Having overseen the $100 million campaign that the society just concluded and the $65 million renovation of the society’s building on Central Park West, completed last year, Mr. Hertog has concentrated his efforts on helping solidify the institution, which a decade ago teetered on the brink of bankruptcy.

“He’s been exceedingly generous,” said Louise Mirrer, president of the society.

In addition to giving more than $10 million to the society since joining the board in 2003, Mr. Hertog created its Chairman’s Council, composed of supporters who contribute $5,000 to $25,000 a year. Members are invited to the society’s annual Weekend With History, which features seminars with historians and writers. Participants have included Ron Chernow, Sean Wilentz and Henry Louis Gates Jr.

During the weekend, the society awards its American History Book Prize of $50,000 — also a Hertog idea — to an author of the best book on American history for a general audience, as judged by a panel that has included the historians Kenneth T. Jackson and Ric Burns. The winner is also named “American Historian Laureate.”

Despite an aura of formality — in spectacles and a bow tie — Mr. Hertog, 71, has a down-to-earth directness. He has no publicist and answers his own e-mail. In an interview, he gamely responded to every question, including those about whether his conservative politics — shared by several of his fellow trustees — had influenced the society’s programming.

Mr. Hertog was chairman emeritus of the Manhattan Institute, a conservative research organization, and served on the board of the American Enterprise Institute. He was also a backer of the right-leaning New York Sun newspaper.

When the society mounted a $5 million exhibition on Alexander Hamilton in 2004, some worried that the show was politically motivated — Hamilton has been a hero to conservatives because of his role as the father of American capitalism when he was the nation’s first secretary of the Treasury. Mr. Hertog contributed $2 million toward the Hamilton exhibition, along with two other board members: Lewis E. Lehrman, who ran as a Republican in the 1982 New York State governor’s race, and Richard Gilder, a leading fund-raiser for Republican and conservative causes.

“There is never a discussion of politics. There isn’t a bent, a movement — this is a museum,” Mr. Hertog said. “I know that was a fear, but I think it wasn’t fair, and it doesn’t matter because there is no evidence of it at all.”

He developed a passion for history out of his own personal pain — except for his parents and two aunts, he lost his entire family in the Holocaust. He has no grandparents, uncles or cousins. And he spent much of his adolescence growing up in the Bronx wondering why. “I was obsessed as a young kid with trying to understand what happened to this large a family — why did all of those Jews have to die?” he said. “I got really intrigued with history, with war, with what went on in the country politically.”

His parents — an auto mechanic and a homemaker — didn’t talk about it. And Mr. Hertog had no siblings with whom to share his questions. So he turned to books about World War II and Franklin Delano Roosevelt. The reading made him a history buff. It also made him a patriot. “I understand how fortunate I was to have been born in this country,” he said.

Mr. Hertog attended City College at night — he recalls it costing about $23 a semester. A clerical job on Wall Street piqued his interest in finance, as did reading Benjamin Franklin’s autobiography. “All of his rules of life,” Mr. Hertog said, “it was my version of a successful life.”

He was one of the founding partners of Sanford C. Bernstein & Company in 1968 and served as the firm’s president until its combination with Alliance Capital Management in October 2000. He remains vice chairman emeritus of AllianceBernstein.

He has been married for 47 years to Susan Hertog, a writer; they have three grown children and a long tradition of giving back. “We have been very fortunate in our lives,” he said, “and we wanted to give away our money over our lifetime, if it was possible.”

In 2007, Mr. Hertog was awarded the National Humanities Medal for philanthropy. In 2010 he won the William E. Simon Prize for Philanthropic Leadership.

Mr. Hertog currently spends his days as president of the Hertog Foundation, a nonprofit charitable organization, and as chairman of the Tikvah Fund, which promotes Jewish thought and ideas.

But Mr. Hertog remains very involved with the historical society, which he said had the most important archive in American history from 1825 to 1850 other than the Library of Congress. “I take an interest in all the central activities of the institution,” he said.

The society’s annual operating budget has increased to $20 million in fiscal year 2013 from $10.6 million in 2004. Over that same period, its endowment has grown to $40 million from $22 million; its board to 50 from 38 and annual visitors are up to 275,000 from 12,000 — in part because of the renovation, which made the society’s physical presence more welcoming and transparent.

“There’s a palpable feeling when one walks into this building right now that the New-York Historical Society has become a modern institution,” Mr. Hertog said, “an engaged institution.”

Mr. Hertog is particularly proud of its exhibitions, like the 2005 show “Slavery in New York” and one scheduled to begin a year from now celebrating the 100th anniversary of the 1913 New York Armory Show, which helped introduce the American public to European avant-garde painting and sculpture.

“In 1993, we were almost bankrupt,” Mr. Hertog said. “Now, 20 years later, the greatest art treasures in the world are being loaned to the New-York Historical Society. Change can come more rapidly than people believe if you’re dedicated to something that’s important.”
