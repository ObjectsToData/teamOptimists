Fear and Frustration Reign in British Tuition Overhaul

By D. D. GUTTENPLAN
Published: July 8, 2012

LONDON — “Going to university is not the same thing as buying a car.” From any other speaker such a statement might not be provocative; but this was David Willetts, the British minister for higher education, responsible for cutting direct government funding for universities and replacing it with a threefold increase in tuition.

The ceiling for annual fees paid by British or European Union students at government-funded universities in England will jump from £3,290, or about $5,150, to £9,000, starting in September.

Another shift came in June when the government announced measures designed to make it easier for smaller schools to qualify for the title of “university,” and for there to be a level playing field among different higher education providers. It will allow students at some private institutions — which can charge more than the £9,000 — access to government-guaranteed loans. These could be for anything from music institutes to for-profit colleges.

The scope and speed of changes have caused considerable anxiety. So when Mr. Willetts stepped up to the lectern at the annual conference of the Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education in June, he tried his best to be reassuring. The Q.A.A., an independent non-profit organization in charge of regulating quality at British universities, has been given a new set of responsibilities by the government.

Now, all universities who want their students to be eligible for government loans have to satisfy Q.A.A. inspectors, placing a burden on an organization that only four years ago was described by the chair of a House of Commons committee as “a toothless old dog.” More recently, the Q.A.A. has also become more rigorous, particularly in inspecting overseas programs.

Shabana Mahmood, the Labour Party’s shadow minister for higher education who also addressed the conference, said she was particularly concerned about “students who may be the first in their families to go to university.”

“These students are terribly worried about taking on debt,” she said, adding that they might be tempted by unscrupulous private providers who appeared to offer a lower-cost alternative. “I don’t want to make it easier to exploit vulnerable people. If we’re not careful in a few years we could see a sub-prime education scandal.”

Many at the conference were worried about the possibility that Britain could follow the example of the United States, where the entry of for-profit schools into the market saw many students with little prospect of ever graduating take on massive amounts of debt in return for little in the way of education.

Mr. Willetts attempted to allay such fears.

“What went wrong in the United States was the failure of regulation,” he told the delegates. He also signaled the government’s change of mind about how best to maintain academic standards. In June 2011 the government said it was considering a shift from the current system of regular inspections every few years to a “risk-based system” under which some universities might be inspected just once every 10 years, while others with good track records might be exempted completely.

Mr. Willetts said that regular inspections would be retained, but that the cycle would be stretched out from the current average of once every five years. He had not decided on the frequency of routine inspections, but he added that “once every 10 years seems to me extreme.”

Mr. Willetts said he thought it unfair that a Cambridge college that had been educating students for hundreds of years without complaint should have to go through an inspection every four years.

His main goal was “to allow more scope for risk-based intervention” — triggered by complaints from students or faculty — which might help prevent some of the scandals that have recently plagued the British system.

This year, changes to student immigration rules caused the closure of hundreds of smaller private colleges and language schools.

Paul Kirkham of the Institute for Contemporary Music Performance, which offers courses in guitar, bass, drums, vocals, songwriting and the music business, used a panel discussion on private providers to accuse the government of attacking the private sector. “These changes are the Spanish Inquisition,” he said, referring to moves requiring colleges that want to recruit overseas students to apply to the government for “highly trusted sponsor” status. “Students are adults. Students can make choices.”
