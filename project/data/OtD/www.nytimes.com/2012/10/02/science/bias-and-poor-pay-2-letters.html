Bias and Poor Pay (2 Letters)

Published: October 1, 2012

To the Editor:

Connect With Us on Social Media

@nytimesscience on Twitter.

  • Science Reporters and Editors on Twitter

Like the science desk on Facebook.

Re “Bias Is Seen to Persist for Women in Science” (Sept. 25): Shame on the researchers for excusing this persistent and insidious bias — seen in many other fields, including the legal profession — by suggesting no conscious thought was involved. There is no such thing as “subconscious” bias, only deliberate indifference and outright disdain for the valuable contributions that women make in the professional world. Diane E. Lifton

Brooklyn

To the Editor:

Nobody who’s been paying attention will be surprised to learn that both male and female professors judge the same job application less worthy when labeled female than when labeled male. The more surprising finding is that even the favored sex garnered an average salary offer of barely $30,000 to manage a university science lab. This for applicants from the graduating class of 2011, for which the National Associaton of Colleges and Employers reported an average starting salary of $41,701, and $35,503 for humanities and social science graduates. What are we to conclude about the true supply and demand profile of technical fields, especially in university employment?

Allan Fisher

Pittsburgh

A version of this letter appeared in print on October 2, 2012, on page D4 of the New York edition with the headline: Bias And Poor Pay.
