Sentence of a Murderer.

Published: February 22, 1863

TRENTON, N.J., Saturday, Feb. 21.

CHARLES LEWIS, convicted of the murder of JAMES ROWAND, of Princeton, in November last, was to day sentenced to be hung on the 3d of April next. The prisoner manifested the utmost indifference to his sentence. His counsel made a motion for arrest of judgment, on the ground of alleged illegality in the drawing of the jury. The Court overruled the motion.

Benefits and Lessons of the War.

To the Editor of the New-York Times:

The second great lesson and benefit of the war is not one that can be stigmatized as an abstraction, for it relates to a matter of the greatest practical importance. It is the inculcation of these momentous truths -- the fatal error of discouraging superiority, and the vital necessity of promoting the ablest men. That the present generation has witnessed a rapid decline in the intellectual calibre of our public men, is neither a novel nor a contestable assertion. For years it has been the wonder of every foreigner who knew anything about us, the grief of every intelligent citizen not blinded by party influences. We cannot deny the existence of a spreading conviction, both at home and abroad, that envy of superior merit and excellence was a vice necessarily evolved from our democratic institutions; and we can scarcely conceive a greater reproach to those institutions. It was the disgrace of our national policy. No one party can be justly reproached with it more than another. The Whigs might, indeed, be said to have inaugurated the doctrine of available incompetency in 1840, but the Democrats were quick enough to adopt it, and persistent enough to carry it on. This preference of mediocrity -- this hatred of superiority, or anything looking like superiority -- this desire to "strike an average" everywhere, was not confined to politics; it extended to the most absurd social details, and if it did not succeed in making a dead level of art, science and literature also, the cause may be traced to various subtile influences, the discussion of which here would involve too long a digression. (One reason may be just touched upon in passing: the verdict of excellence in such pursuits, however it may afterwards be ratified by them, cannot, in the nature of things, be first given by the people, scarcely even by a class of experts in this country alone.)

When superiority ceased to be a passport to popular favor, the best men of the rising generation were naturally diverted from public life; the progress of those already on the stage was checked, and as the old leaders died out, an interior class took their places. But these, in their turn, as the prominent, became obnoxious to popular envy, and thus the standard of statesmanship became doubly and trebly lowered. Not only were the most popular and successful candidates to be found among the second or third class of public men, but the first class was not higher than the second or third class of former generations, and when any member of it did by some accident obtain an important position, he was as likely as not to prove a failure. Such was the dearth of statesmen that when, on the wreck of old political organizations, the Republican party arose, strong in its new creed, and in the masses of honest and patriotic young men who supported it, it was all at sea for the want of a great ruling spirit. The powerful body had no head. [Here it may be objected. "But the Confederate leaders have shown ability; therefore if you deny ability to our public men, slavery is a better nurse of public merit than freedom." And this is what some of the English philo-secession writers say. But I unhesitatingly refuse to admit that the Confederates have shown great political ability. They have beaten us in domestic and foreign intrigue, which is rather the characteristic of a semi-barbarous people. Their Generals have fought pretty well with the advantages of eight years' preparation in advance, and interior lines to operate upon. Thus they show some dexterity in two species of destructive action. But constructive talent, which is the great quality and test of statesmanship, they have given no evidence of possessing. Their so-called Government, founded on mutual desolation, is only kept together by the outside pressure of war, and if it were "recognized" by everybody to-morrow, would soon require a reapplication of the same pressure to keep it from falling apart. DAVIS substantially admits this in his recent speeches; he also admits that the conflict has assumed proportions beyond his calculations, thus acknowledging the failure of his coup d'etat. English writers persist in saying that the rebellion is a success so long as it is unsubdued. I maintain that thus far it is a failure for thus far it has failed to accomplish its principal object and has been disappointed in its principal expectations. Its first and main object was to get possession of Washington and all the Border States. It has not taken Washington and has only got one-half of one of those States. Its expectations were: first, that the North would not attempt to resist it; second, that if resistance were attempted, it would be insignificant and ineffectual; third, that if the Government did succeed in starting a serious war, England and France would speedily interfere and stop it. Each successive stage of their hope has been disappointed, and still there are Anglo-Saxon men who talk of DAVIS as if he were CAESAR or NAPOLEON.]
