Sailboat With a View

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
By ALAN LIGHTMAN
Published: August 19, 2007

EARLIER this summer, on a quiet Sunday, I glided into New York Harbor by sailboat.

I had been sailing with my brother on the Promise, his 49-foot Taswell sloop. We left Annapolis, Md., two days earlier, sailed up the Chesapeake Bay, motored east through the Chesapeake and Delaware Canal, turned south into the Delaware Bay, rounded Cape May, N.J., and then headed north again in the open Atlantic until we reached the Lower New York Bay. At times, we had been beyond sight of land, surrounded by nothing but ocean, from horizon to horizon.

Magellans we weren’t, with our global positioning system, electronic charts and autopilot. Still, the fierce determination of my brother to make the trip without stopping made for a memorable experience. We did night shifts of two hours on and four hours off, we hurriedly gulped meals of canned tuna and peanut butter, and we had enough tossing and bucking in rough seas to make me violently seasick for hours.

The first landmark I saw as we entered the Lower Bay was the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge, a graceful filigree floating in midair several miles away. Then I recognized Coney Island, with its dark apartment buildings huddled close to the coast. In another half-hour, we furled our sails, turned on our motor and passed beneath the bridge, staying east of the central lanes to avoid barges and big ships.

We rounded the west shoulder of Brooklyn. Almost immediately, we could see the Statue of Liberty, turquoise and tiny from this distance, beckoning. Governors Island, a horizontal sliver of green vegetation, lay straight ahead.

And then, the most remarkable sight of all, the southern tip of Manhattan.

I have been in Manhattan many times and always felt overwhelmed by the size and intensity of the city — the honking of taxis, the crowds in the streets, the skyscrapers towering around me like ribs of a whale that had just swallowed me. But now, at this distance, seeing Manhattan from a sailboat a mile offshore, the city appeared delicate and fragile, a jewel.

The great monoliths of Lower Manhattan — the South Ferry Terminal, the World Financial Center, the American International Building, Chase Manhattan Bank, the Woolworth Building — seemed like toys stuck on a small spit of land. I felt that I could hold the city in my hand.

And I remembered a story the astronaut Jeffrey Hoffman once told me about his walk in space, the appearance of Earth from space, and how Earth’s atmosphere looked like a slender blue ribbon wrapped around a ball.

That’s what Manhattan looked like to me. And the city, from this view, had a completeness to it, a wholeness, even though I knew the twin towers were missing.

We motored along into the Hudson River and docked at a little marina. Then another amazing thing happened. I got off the boat, walked about 200 yards to the street and hailed a cab to my daughter’s apartment.

Alan Lightman, a professor of humanities at M.I.T., is the author of the forthcoming novel “Ghost.”
