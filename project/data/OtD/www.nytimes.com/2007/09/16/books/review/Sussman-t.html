Reviews by MICK SUSSMAN
Published: September 16, 2007

DOMINION OF MEMORIES: Jefferson, Madison, and the Decline of Virginia. By Susan Dunn. (Basic Books, $27.50.) Virginia’s “brilliant, forward-looking, transforming leaders,” Dunn writes, “created a nation” and then controlled the executive branch for 32 of the first 36 years of America’s constitutional government. Virginia was once the wealthiest and most populous state, but by the early decades of the 19th century, its soil had been depleted by primitive farming methods; its schools were “miserable,” according to Jefferson; and its roads were “execrable,” a disgruntled visitor said. Its constitution preserved elitist government long after most states had expanded suffrage, and its plantation aristocracy deliberately hindered the economic dynamism that was modernizing the North. Slavery’s role in the decline of the South is an old story, but Dunn, a professor of humanities at Williams College, finds fresh insights by making a case study of Virginia. She emphasizes the significance of lesser-known figures like Thomas Roderick Dew, who in 1832 published an essay that laid the intellectual groundwork for an uncompromisingly pro-slavery ideology, and Nathaniel Beverley Tucker, who anticipated secession with an 1836 novel envisioning a Southern rebellion. Dunn’s chief aim, though, is to show the complicity of Jefferson and Madison in Virginia’s stagnation. Both men strained against plantation dogma, Jefferson because of his egalitarian creed and Madison because of a wavering faith in the national government he’d helped design. Yet they both acquiesced as states’ rights radicals adduced anti-Federalist pronouncements by the two founders to trump moderate opponents. “The seeds of Virginia’s decline had been adroitly planted in fertile soil by the great patriarchs themselves,” Dunn concludes.

BUILDING A NEW EUROPE: Portraits of Modern Architects. By George Nelson. (Yale University, $45.) The architect and critic George Nelson’s essays on the state of European architecture in 1935-36. Above, the Luckhardt brothers’ design for a Berlin parking garage.

1001 NIGHTS IN IRAQ: The Shocking Story of an American Forced to Fight for Saddam Against the Country He Loves. By Shant Kenderian. (Atria, paper, $15.) An Iraqi of Armenian descent who moved to Chicago with his mother after his parents separated, Kenderian describes himself as “a guy with really bad timing.” He certainly picked an inauspicious moment to visit his father in Iraq: September 1980, seven days before Saddam Hussein attacked Iran. Trapped behind closed borders, Kenderian evaded military service by attending college. But after graduating in 1985, he chose enlistment over execution and began a three-year stint in the Iraqi Navy. His luck was no better in the sequel. After the Iran war, Kenderian’s green card was in the middle of being processed when Hussein invaded Kuwait in 1990. The bulk of “1001 Nights in Iraq,” describing his life as a soldier during the Persian Gulf war, is the stuff of a standard battle memoir: comradeship, bad food, sleepless nights and boredom interrupted by violence. But this account is distinguished by Kenderian’s wry humor and peculiar predicament. With his deep faith in Christianity and America, he was even more reluctant than most of Hussein’s conscripts. Facing a hopeless fight against an American warplane, Kenderian’s unit surrendered. As a P.O.W., he charmed his guards with his distinctively American ingenuousness; he even fell in love. Kenderian was eventually allowed to return to America, where his fortunes improved strikingly. He became an engineer, a father and a citizen. And he caught another break when he was broadcast reading from this book, which was originally self-published, on “This American Life.”

TORTURE: When the Unthinkable Is Morally Permissible. By Mirko Bagaric and Julie Clarke. (State University of New York, cloth, $53.50; paper, $17.95.) This book will convince you either that utilitarian principles justify torture in certain circumstances or that scholars in thrall to a hypothesis can rationalize almost anything. Bagaric and Clarke, who teach law at Deakin University in Australia, offer a forthright defense of a practice whose proponents often resort to euphemism. The authors draw a distinction between “consequential moral theories,” which judge an act based on its outcome, and “nonconsequential” theories, which judge an act intrinsically and are the basis for the concept of universal human rights. Bagaric and Clarke reject this latter view, declaring that “absolute right” theories “lack a coherent foundation.” These approaches are not always in opposition, they note, since human rights can function as “shortcuts” in performing a “utilitarian calculus.” To evaluate when the ends justify the means of torture, Bagaric and Clarke have devised a quasi-mathematical formula involving factors like “the number of lives at risk” and “the immediacy of the harm.” But how reliable is their ethical calculator? Rather than trusting individuals to determine when torture would maximize happiness, many enlightened societies have opted for a blanket prohibition. Such moral absolutes have a utilitarian purpose: they act as restraints against shortsighted arguments for sanctioning barbarity in the name of expediency.

THE SHORT BUS: A Journey Beyond Normal. By Jonathan Mooney. (Holt, $25.) After being classified as learning-disabled in third grade, Mooney rode the “short bus” with other special-ed students, a stigma so mortifying he later contemplated suicide. But he persevered, graduating from Brown University and writing a book with David Cole called “Learning Outside the Lines.” In his latest book, Mooney travels coast to coast in a dilapidated short bus to interview people “who had once been labeled ‘abnormal’ or ‘disabled.’ ” These portraits are endearing. He meets an eccentric, possibly autistic man who has befriended a group of mathematicians; a blind and deaf girl who curses in sign language; and a former fisherman who is considering gender reassignment. Mooney makes some discordant attempts at philosophizing in this chatty narrative (his epigraph is from Foucault: “The judges of normality are present everywhere”). And although he thinks the “medical model” of treating developmental disabilities is dehumanizing, he is vague about the “community care” approach he favors. Mooney, who suggests that reading remediation is a waste of time in some cases, suffered as a child not just because he was ostracized but also because he simply wanted to read and couldn’t. It’s not necessarily more compassionate to tolerate disabilities than to try to correct them. But it feels like bullying to pick on this book’s limitations. Mooney’s target audience is not policy makers but his fellow misfits, and his boundless empathy will surely console those who also face the worst that cruel schoolchildren and the educational bureaucracy have to offer.

REVENGE OF THE DONUT BOYS: True Stories of Lust, Fame, Survival and Multiple Personality. By Mike Sager. (Thunder’s Mouth, paper, $16.95.) Like his journalistic precursors Tom Wolfe and Hunter S. Thompson, Sager writes frenetic, off-kilter pop-sociological profiles of Americans in all their vulgarity and vitality. But compared with the new journalists, who celebrated the subjectivity of the narrator, Sager is a relatively self-effacing observer. He writes with flair, but only in the service of an omnivorous curiosity. In the articles collected here — published between 1985 and 2006, mostly in Esquire magazine, where Sager is a writer at large — the famous, almost famous and not famous all get the same Sager treatment. He is teasingly affectionate (Tom Araya of the heavy-metal band Slayer speaks like “a mixture of Valley doper, Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtle and mambo king”) and meticulous (he’s the kind of reporter who notes his subject’s shoe size). He defies expectations in pieces that lesser writers would play for satire or sensationalism: Newark teenagers who steal cars for fun, a group of professional beach volleyball players who go on strike, a Frederick’s of Hollywood model who wants to lock in a husband while she’s still young. Sager’s celebrity interviews — Roseanne Barr describing her multiple personality disorder, Ice Cube pondering race and fame — are sympathetic but not fawning. The book’s closing selection, in which Sager talks to other people named Mike Sager (a police captain, a meteorologist, a politician), is not, as it might seem, narcissistic. His conclusion ought to be read instead as a Whitmanesque ode to teeming humanity’s mystical unity: “Call us all Mike Sager.”
