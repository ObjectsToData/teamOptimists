One Man’s Musings About Assorted Influential Lives

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
  • Single Page
By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: March 20, 2007

In an earlier era, commonplace books were what blogs and My Space pages are today. They were collections of quotations, observations, clippings, proverbs, poems, personal asides and anything else that someone found worthy of saving for future reference or sharing with friends. They served, W. H. Auden wrote in the introduction to his own wonderful commonplace book, “A Certain World,” as “a sort of autobiography,” a map of the collector’s personal planet.

Jerry Bauer

Clive James

CULTURAL AMNESIA

Necessary Memories From History and the Arts

By Clive James

Illustrated. 876 pages. W. W. Norton & Company. $35.

Clive James’s massive new book, “Cultural Amnesia,” is, in essence, a very fat commonplace book, though its author has somewhat more grandiose ambitions in mind: “I wanted to write,” he says, “about philosophy, history, politics and the arts all at once, and about what had happened to those things during the course of the multiple catastrophes into whose second principal outburst (World War I was the first) I had been born in 1939, and which continued to shake the world as I grew to adulthood.”

Mr. James’s heroes (Camus, Kafka and Nadezhda Mandelstam) are here, and so are his intellectual bêtes noires, like Jean-Paul Sartre. There are chapters on famous artists (Proust, Flaubert, Diaghilev and Fitzgerald) and famous monsters of history (Mao and Hitler), and there are chapters on more obscure figures, like Peter Altenberg (a panhandler who was a “key figure” in the cafe life of old Vienna, before the advent of the Nazis) and Alexandra Kollontai, a former commissar of public welfare in the Soviet Union and “the regime’s recognized expert on women’s rights.”

At times Mr. James’s choices, arranged alphabetically, seem arbitrary in the extreme: there are no essays on Nabokov or Pynchon, no essays on Stalin, Churchill or Roosevelt, and yet there are entries on Dick Cavett, the former talk show host, and Michael Mann, the “Miami Vice” producer (humorously sandwiched between profiles of Thomas Mann and his older brother, Heinrich Mann). J. Robert Oppenheimer merits no profile, and yet Richard Rhodes, the author of “The Making of the Atomic Bomb,” is given five pages. Einstein is omitted (though he makes an appearance in an essay on Chaplin), but his cousin, the musicologist Alfred Einstein, is accorded a chapter.

As a critic, Mr. James has always demonstrated a magpie’s taste: he’s as familiar with “Dallas” and the novels of Judith Krantz as he is with the poetry of Rilke and Galway Kinnell. This volume leapfrogs from the realms of high art and intellectual history to pop culture, children’s literature and classical music. Essays on Czeslaw Milosz and Miguel de Unamuno jostle for space with portraits of Beatrix Potter and Tony Curtis; observations about Tacitus and Trotsky coexist with thumbnail sketches of Chaplin and Coco Chanel.

In many cases the portrait of the individual in question is simply a launching pad for the author’s free-associative musings, which tend to spiral around several recurrent themes: the shattering legacy of Nazism and Communism, the two totalitarian movements that overshadowed the 20th century; the dangers posed by ideologies that try to reduce the world’s dazzling complexity to simplistic formulas; and the preciousness and fragility of humanism as a cultural ideal.

If the essays are heavily weighted toward artists and thinkers who lived in Vienna before the Anschluss, that is because Mr. James seems to regard that city in the late 19th and early 20th centuries as a kind of lost intellectual Eden, “the best evidence that the most accommodating and fruitful ground for the life of the mind can be something more broad than a university campus.”

In the most compelling entries in this volume, Mr. James uses his fecund talents as a writer and critic to turn us on to the works of unfamiliar figures and to goad us to look at more famous personages from a new angle. He conjures up the aura of his subjects with a couple lines. And in chronicling the terrible suffering artists have endured in places like the Soviet Union, he makes the reader appreciate the luxury enjoyed by writers in the West, who have been able to focus on the “tragedies of ordinary life, the events that Nadezhda Mandelstam was later to subsume under her concept of the privilege of ordinary heartbreaks.”

Mr. James explicates Kafka’s weird prescience in prophesying “the nature of the totalitarian state” in his fiction — an achievement he attributes in part to the writer’s experiences as a Jew who lived “with its mechanisms of exclusion, the first part of the totalitarian state to develop.”

He excoriates Sartre for his hesitation in condemning Communism, writing that when this philosopher finally broke with the party, “he retained respect for their putatively benevolent social intentions, and was ready to say something exculpatory even if what he was exculpating was the Gulag network, whose existence, after he finally ceased to deny it, he never condemned as a central product of a totalitarian system, but only regretted as an incidental blemish.”

And he introduces us to the witty observations of the little known polymath Egon Friedell, who, he says, was “a figure unparalleled” in pre-World War II Vienna.
