COPING; An Art of Realism Amid Recession And Budget Cuts

By FELICIA R. LEE
Published: February 24, 2002

MAURINE D. KNIGHTON, executive director of 651 Arts, wants to make one thing clear: her arts organization is no victim.

No. Not even as she talks about cuts of $250,000 in a $1.25 million annual budget for a group that bills itself as Brooklyn's premier presenter of African diaspora arts. The cuts do mean that the group's 2002 main-stage season has been postponed until May, and that a $200-a-ticket gala scheduled for yesterday was canceled.

Ms. Knighton, 42, is an animated native of Mississippi who has led the organization for more than 4 of its 13 years. She insists that any setbacks stemming from Sept. 11, city budget cuts and the recession actually represent an opportunity. An opportunity for minority arts groups like 651, with its staff of seven and a loyal group of volunteers in Fort Greene, to find new ways to raise money. What is crucial, Ms. Knighton says, is to persuade more black people to buy a ticket for, say, a dance troupe as readily as they would for a church raffle.

Historically, there are many reasons that has not happened. But the times call for black New Yorkers who have a sense of self-worth and a few extra dollars to help institutions that support black artists. ''At a basic level, it is a validation of self,'' Ms. Knighton said.

She has a few strategies in mind. They include forming partnerships with profit-making arts groups and luring more people to serve on 651 Arts' nine-person board. She hopes to expand the board to 20. Ms. Knighton also wants to aggressively increase individual donations, now a small part of a budget dependent on foundation grants.

At 651 Fulton Street in the Majestic Theater, 651 Arts dwells in the same valley of cutbacks and uncertainties as most of the city's 2,000 arts institutions. They all face the gravest economic period since the fiscal crisis in the 1970's. But when big institutions catch cold, those run by minority groups get pneumonia.

''Groups of color have fewer resources and less access to money available through philanthropy,'' said Norma P. Munn, chairwoman of the New York City Arts Coalition, an arts advocacy group. ''They get a smaller amount than that given to comparable white organizations. It's more patronizing than racism.''

As artists and patrons, blacks have historically not only been excluded from mainstream institutions, but also, until recently, lacked their own. Many of the most established black cultural institutions are not that old. The Alvin Ailey dance group started in 1958, the Studio Museum of Harlem in the mid-1970's. That is one reason blacks have been more accustomed to giving to churches or schools, rather than to arts organizations, said Claude Johnson, 651 Art's director of institutional planning.

But 651 represents a second wave in black cultural institutions. For one, its mission is to reach beyond what black Americans are doing in theater, dance, music and the humanities and to represent black talent worldwide. At the same time, it wants to remain linked to its base in Fort Greene, a neighborhood known for attracting a diverse black population and nurturing black artists.

As a showcase for dance artists like Donald Byrd (''Harlem Nutcracker'' and ''Jazz Train'') and its acclaimed annual ''Black Dance: Tradition and Transformation'' festival, which brought together black dancers from places like South Africa, Paris and London, 651 had started to hit its stride.

As the predominantly black Fort Greene continues to change, though, with more whites attracted to the neighborhood's elegant brownstones and funky vibe, some wonder whether 651 will be helped or hurt.

Ms. Knighton says enough newcomers have embraced 651. Some artists and business people also think 651 can capitalize on the new élan and money. Others fear it could go the way of the black artists who put Fort Greene on the map, but were pushed out by rising rents.

''We as artists have to reaffirm how important these institutions are to us,'' Tracie Morris, a poet who lives in nearby Cobble Hill, said of 651. ''Where is the place for the innovative black artist? There has to be a way to address this issue without wringing our hands about it.''
