JERSEY FOOTLIGHTS

By Margo Nash
Published: October 6, 2002

He's Moving On

Michael Stotts, the managing director of the George Street Playhouse in New Brunswick and chairman of the New Jersey Theater Alliance, will become the managing director of the Long Wharf Theatre in New Haven in January. Mr. Stotts has been managing director of George Street for three seasons, and was managing director of the New Jersey Shakespeare Festival for nine. Mr. Stotts said he was ''very excited about the new opportunity that I have,'' but added that he would miss the bonds he forged at the theater. Mr. Stotts will step down as chairman of the theater alliance in January as well.

Learning the Lingo

Before ''The Producers,'' there was ''The Butter and Egg Man,'' a 1925 George S. Kaufman play about a motley crew that tries to produce a Broadway hit. A revival is at the Hunterdon Hills Playhouse, a dinner theater in Perryville. Butter and eggs is 20's slang for someone who bankrolls a project. Ken Schwarz, manager of the playhouse, said Kaufman's show was notable for old Broadway language like ''up blows this other bird.'' ''For the actors, it's like learning Shakespeare,'' he said. ''They have to spend hours learning how to say it.'' Through Oct. 30. (800) 447-7313.

Call to Action

ArtPride, the arts advocacy group, is planning a campaign to increase the state's arts budget in 2004. In fiscal year 2002-3 the arts allocation dropped from $20 million to $18 million. ''The governor promised he would restore funding,'' said Ann Marie Miller, director of ArtPride. ''We are going to get the arts community to advocate for a restoration of that funding and try to get beyond that, because we have been at this glass ceiling of $20 million for a long time.'' ArtPride plans to ask its 200 member organizations to go to their audiences, members and board members and ask them to contact their legislators, Ms. Miller said.

Pinelands Pride

Howard Boyd of Tabernacle has studied Pinelands flora and fauna for decades and written three books on the subject. The most recent, ''Wild Flowers of the Pine Barrens of New Jersey,'' was published last year (Plexus 2001). One of the most beautiful flowers, Mr. Boyd said, is the Pine Barrens Gentian. ''It's one of the last to bloom in autumn. It's blue and looks like a lily open to the sky.'' Mr. Boyd will be one of many presenters at today's Pinelands Month Opening Celebration in Historic Whitesbog Village sponsored by the Pinelands Preservation Alliance from 9 a.m. to 3 p.m. (609) 894-8000.

Spreading the Music

Reach stands for Resources for Education and Community Harmony, an outreach program of the New Jersey Symphony Orchestra. Small ensembles of symphony musicians give workshops and concerts in schools and other places where the symphony does not perform. They talk about music and what it is like to be a musician. Today at 2, p.m., a Reach quartet will perform Mozart, Joplin, Dvorak and Pachelbel at the Park Center for the Performing Arts in Union City. (201) 865-6980.

All Around the State

October is National Arts and Humanities month, and time for the Jersey Art Series, the monthlong event coordinated by the State Council on the Arts. There will be 19 events at museums, concert halls, theaters and other venues across the state. Audiences can hear Vivaldi's music and find out the ''Secrets of The Four Seasons'' at the New Jersey Performing Arts Center's Victoria Theater on Oct. 13; go for a candlelight tour at the Chestnut Hill Cemetery in East Brunswick, Oct. 19 and 20; or see the ''The War of the Worlds: A Radio Play'' at the 12 Miles West Theater Company in Montclair on Oct. 31. Today, the Society of African Missions' African Art Museum in Tenafly will offer free gallery tours, children's activities and African music in connection with a new exhibition, ''Cultures of Africa,'' which features ancient and modern sub-Saharan African art, including figurines from Mali that offer unique protections, says Robert Koenig, the museum's director. One protects against falling backward. Information: www.jerseyarts.com or (800) THE-ARTS Margo Nash
