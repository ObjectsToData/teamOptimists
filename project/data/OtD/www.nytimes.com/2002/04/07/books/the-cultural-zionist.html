The Cultural Zionist

By Jonathan Wilson
Published: April 7, 2002

GERSHOM SCHOLEM

A Life in Letters, 1914-1982.

Edited and translated by

Anthony David Skinner.

Illustrated. 547 pp. Cambridge, Mass.:

Harvard University Press. $35.

BETTY SCHOLEM, mother of Gershom Scholem, the unmatchable genius and interpreter of cabala and one of the world's greatest Jewish scholars, might, although she despised the ''click-clack'' of the typewriter, have appreciated e-mail. During long periods of separation from her youngest son, who sagaciously left Germany for Palestine in 1923, she punctuated his absence with a series of sprightly, witty, ironic, unsentimental, highly intelligent and, as history in the shape of Hitler and his followers bore down on her, unblinkingly courageous, keenly observed and honest letters. But her output was not matched by her concerned but busy son: she frequently had to remind him that her ratio of letters sent to those received was sometimes 3 to 1. Gershom Scholem did his best to keep up; but, as a prolific letter writer in his own right, he had many pressing correspondences going at once, including a 23-year exchange with Walter Benjamin that produced what are surely among the most important intellectual documents of the 20th century.

Most of Scholem and Benjamin's letters have already appeared in English in two volumes dedicated exclusively to their correspondence. ''Gershom Scholem: A Life in Letters'' offers new translations of a number of them and places them alongside Scholem's letters to and from family members, academics, publishers, former students and literary and intellectual friends, some of whom, like Hannah Arendt, turned into enemies. The translations from German by Anthony David Skinner (a handful of the letters were written in English) are largely felicitous, although I have to admit that it was hard to imagine Scholem writing ''I kid you not.''

From the beginning of his serious epistolary career in September 1914, Scholem was not up for much idle chat. At 16 he was writing to Werner, the youngest of his three older brothers, in a preternaturally sophisticated manner about Marxism, Socialism, Zionism, anarchy, Buber, Buddha, Hegel, Kant, Nietzsche, the politics of the day and days to come. Scholem's answer to ''wassup?'' was as far from the great American ''nuttin' '' as can be imagined. The intellectual path is quickly set; Scholem, self-taught in Hebrew, immerses himself in its ancient texts and becomes a cultural Zionist (political Zionism, advocating the establishment of a Jewish state, is not on his agenda), convinced that the way forward for Jews is via the way back, a return to the autonomous culture that they have abandoned in the course of European assimilation. He develops an early and special interest in Jewish mysticism and as his scholarly reputation flourishes, so too does a certain mystique that derives from his field of study. ''The rumor has recently begun to circulate around Munich that, using black magic, I can create mice and elephants,'' he informs his parents in the summer of 1920, only to add with an unimpeded confidence he was never to lose, ''For now, however, I can conjure up only flawless texts no one can top.''

Scholem's translation of himself from German Jew to Palestinian Jew (Gerhard to Gershom) did not come without a price. As early as 1917 his father, offended by an antiwar article written by his son for an underground Zionist paper, kicked him out of his highly assimilationist household (Christmas celebrations, but none at Hanukkah), the news of his dismissal arriving by registered letter. Soon after, Scholem constructed ''a colossal fabrication'' of mental illness to avoid army service, a performance that, for a man of his dignity and integrity, must have cost him dearly; the matter is elided in his otherwise candid memoir, ''From Berlin to Jerusalem: Memories of My Youth.''

By 1923, Scholem is writing from Jerusalem, and ''the heavy footsteps of anti-Semitism . . . always thumping behind my back'' in Germany (the letters report two fistfights Scholem has got into with Jew-baiters) have been replaced by the far less threatening irritations of daily life in Mandate Palestine: the first shock is the discovery that compared with the high-flown German Jewish intellectuals he is used to, the local literati are ''one thing only: stupid.'' +In this apocalyptic place,'' he writes to his friend, the writer and essayist Werner Kraft, ''you meet the utterly amazing phenomenon of Hebrew-speaking philistines.''

By 1933, the year Hitler came to power, Scholem was living in the Rehavia section of Jerusalem with his wife, Escha, and firmly ensconced as the first professor of cabala studies at the Hebrew University. The tumultuous dozen years that followed brought Scholem great personal misery but were also a time for him of astonishingly fruitful scholarship. You will, however, search in vain in these letters for a prolonged articulation of Scholem's feelings about either the breakup of his first marriage (which involved a rather cruel betrayal) or the death of his brother Werner in Buchenwald. His response to Benjamin's suicide on the Spanish border in 1940, an inestimable loss for Scholem, who had hoped for many years (and been given reason to hope) that Benjamin would eventually make his way to Jerusalem and throw himself into Jewish studies, is equally restrained: a brief sentence in a letter to their mutual friend Theodor Adorno, ''I don't have to tell you what Benjamin's death means for us and how vital it is for me to know, if at all possible, the details of the last days of his life.'' Whatever personal anguish Scholem felt was subsumed into his work.
