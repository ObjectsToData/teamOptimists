Study Faults Small Schools on Social Studies

By ANEMONA HARTOCOLLIS
Published: April 10, 2002

A study by two Columbia University professors has found that many of New York City's small high schools teach a diluted version of social studies that does not adequately prepare students for citizenship's demands.

The study finds that small high schools have a more caring atmosphere than traditional schools, and are better at engaging disadvantaged students. But it concludes that the teaching of social studies in small schools has been hurt by what the researchers call a less is more philosophy of curriculum.

A student in a small high school may never be exposed to important events in history, like the Civil War, World War I or the American Revolution, the report says. Instead, teachers focus on subjects they think are of interest to urban, often black and Hispanic students, like slavery, civil rights, the history of the Bronx and the Harlem Renaissance.

The study, by Margaret Smith Crocco and Stephen J. Thornton, both associate professors of social studies and education at Teachers College, comes as many of the small schools are fighting the state's requirement that they administer Regents examinations in history and other subjects. The schools argue that the exams are too sweeping and fact-based and will force the schools to abandon their in-depth approach to learning.

Avram Barlowe, chairman of the social studies department at Urban Academy, a small school on the Upper East Side, said yesterday that the report presented an exaggerated view of how history was taught at his school. He said he taught big events and dates as part of an analytical approach to history, though he might not stress memorization.

He also challenged the notion that traditional schools better prepare students to be citizens. ''What's the evidence?'' he asked. ''Their participation in electoral politics?''

The report is based in part on a review of hundreds of reports by Teachers College professors about the experiences of student-teachers they have supervised over the last few years. It is also based on questionnaires answered by 60 traditional and small schools and observations and interviews at a dozen schools.

The study says social studies classes in small schools often combine English and history under the rubric of humanities and stress reasoning and interpretation of evidence rather than a body of knowledge.

In small schools, social studies courses are more likely to be taught by young, inexperienced teachers, the study says. While enthusiastic and committed, these young teachers are often overwhelmed by the demands of creating their own curriculum, using primary sources rather than textbooks, the study says.

Dr. Crocco said yesterday that she agreed that the state was placing too much emphasis on testing. But she said her report was intended to warn that small schools might have gone too far in the other direction.

''I want to put myself somewhere in between where the Regents are with this comprehensive sweep or survey approach, which is sometimes parodied as the Plato-to-NATO approach in world history, and where the colleges are, where a history major at a very good college can come out knowing everything about the Vietnam War but nothing much about any other important period in American history,'' Dr. Crocco said.
