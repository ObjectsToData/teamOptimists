This Week

By LAWRENCE VAN GELDER
Published: November 11, 2002

All This Jazz

Where will the Metropolitan Opera star Denyce Graves, the Tony Award-winning actress and singer Patti LuPone and the pop music luminary Paul Simon be tonight? Along with the Pulitzer Prize-winning playwright August Wilson and the CBS News correspondent Ed Bradley, they will be joining Wynton Marsalis at Avery Fisher Hall at 7:30 for ''Manhattan Rhapsody,'' the musical salute to New York at the heart of Jazz at Lincoln Center's Seventh Annual Awards Gala and Benefit Concert. Information: (212) 721-6500.

Going, Going . . .

En route to decomposition, celluloid yields beauty. The evidence lies in the decaying nitrate film reels of yesteryear mined by the filmmaker Bill Morrison and the composer Michael Gordon for ''Decasia.'' This 70-minute film is to be shown tonight at the appropriately dilapidated Angel Orensanz Foundation on the Lower East Side, under the auspices of the New York Institute for the Humanities at New York University. Besides the film, a ''Fantasia''-like collage of nitrate film stock newsreel scenes, silent movies, melodramas and travelogues, the evening will offer further tribute to the aesthetic properties of deliquescent celluloid from the popular prestidigitator and dice collector Ricky Jay and the photographer Rosamond Purcell. Mr. Gordon and Mr. Jay, Ms. Purcell and the acclaimed documentary filmmaker Errol Morris will be on hand for discussion at the event, from 7 p.m. to 10 at 172 Norfolk Street, just south of Houston. Information: (212) 998-2100.

Crossroads

Grand opera and the Broadway musical intersect at 7:30 tonight, when the soprano Deborah Voigt takes to the stage of the Ford Center for the Performing Arts for arias, songs and duets with some of Broadway's leading men, including Malcolm Gets, Howard McGillin, Adam Pascal, Patrick Wilson and Tom Wopat. The benefit performance marks the 10th anniversary of Classical Action, a fund-raising program of Broadway Cares/Equity Fights AIDS. Information: (212) 840-0770, Extension 268.

Mystery and Comedy

Evolving from street entertainment, seasonal agricultural festivals and religious rituals, the spiritual Noh dramas and comedic Kyogen performances of Japan have been its most influential forms of theater for more than 600 years. In a new exhibition of the art of of Noh and Kyogen from some of the most important collections in Japan, the Los Angeles County Museum of Art is displaying wooden masks, costumes, musical instruments and painted screens and hand scrolls. ''Miracles and Mischief: Noh and Kyogen Theater in Japan'' continues through Feb. 2. Information: (323) 857-6000. LAWRENCE VAN GELDER
