THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: November 3, 2002

Open House for the Loft

Twenty-four years ago, the sculptor James Jackson Burt moved into the president's office at the abandoned Yale and Towne Lock Company, inadvertently founding the Stamford Loft Artists Association.

Today the century-old hulk of a building houses the studios of more than 60 artists who have won numerous prizes and generated substantial support from area corporations, foundations and municipal sources.

In addition to its busy program of lectures, studio tours for students, art demonstrations, poetry readings and theatrical performances, the association has held an open house each autumn for 23 years.

On Friday and Saturday, from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m., the public is invited to tour the studios, talk to the painters, sculptors, ceramicists, photographers, printmakers and collagists, among others, and perhaps purchase a work of art. There will be a mix of professionals and emerging artists.

The building is at 737 Canal Street, one block south of Exit 8 of I-95.

Information: (203) 323-4153.

Jewish Book Festival

The Greater Hartford Jewish Community Center has engaged an impressive list of authors for its Jewish Book Festival beginning Wednesday and ending Nov. 13.

Alan M. Dershowitz, a law professor at Harvard, will be the keynote speaker Wednesday at 6:30 p.m. His subject is ''Why Terrorism Works: Understanding the Threat, Responding to the Challenge.''

Some lectures during the eight-day event are free, others require tickets ranging from $10 to $20. The center is at 335 Bloomfield Avenue in West Hartford.

Information: (860) 236-4571.

Music of Ancient Iberia

King Alfonso the Wise, who ruled in Spain during the 13th century, welcomed Christian, Muslim and Jewish musicians to his court. They frequently joined in performing the king's own songs dedicated to the Virgin Mary.

Known in Spanish as Las Cantigas de Santa Maria, the music will be performed Saturday at 8 p.m. in Palmer Auditorium of Connecticut College in a program recreating the musical, poetic and spiritual ambience of 13th-century Iberia.

Three ensembles led by the Boston Camerata will play. The remaining two are the Camerata Mediterranea from France, a European counterpart to the Camerata, and the Sharq Arab-American ensemble, providing the Muslim component.

A panel discussion on the music, literature and history of Alfonso's court is scheduled at 3 p.m. in Room 210 of the Blaustein Humanities Center. Panelists include professors of Spanish and history from Yale, UConn and Holy Cross, and the musicologist Israel J. Katz. Admission is free.

The college is at 270 Mohegan Avenue in New London. Tickets for the concert range from $7.50 to $25. Reservations: (860) 439-2787.

Children's Book Fair

Adults with children in tow, or vice-versa, will have an opportunity to meet some of their favorite authors and illustrators at the 11th Annual Children's Book Fair to be held in the Rome Ballroom on the UConn campus at Storrs Saturday and Sunday from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.

There will be presentations and autograph signings, Lyle Lyle Crocodile, Hedgie, Rotten Ralph and many more storybook characters mixing with visitors, plus singalongs, crafts and storytelling.

Among the 14 authors and illustrators who will be on hand are Jane Dyer, Michael Patrick Hearn, Mary Ann Hoberman, Marc Simont and Heidi Stemple.

A lecture and book signing by Natalie Babbitt, an author and illustrator will take place Friday at 7:30 p.m. in the Thomas J. Dodd Center. Admission is free.

Information: (800) 873-2348.

Studying Religion

Upheaval in the Mideast and elsewhere in the world has generated a number of events focusing on divisiveness among religions and religion itself.

Susannah Heschel, Ph.D., an associate professor and chairman of Jewish Studies at Dartmouth will present a free talk titled ''From Rabbi to Aryan: The Political Uses of Jesus in Jewish-Christian Dialogue'' Thursday at 8 p.m. in the Quick Center for the Arts at Fairfield University.

Dr. Heschel has written several works and has lectured widely in Germany, where she was the Martin Buber Visiting Professor of Jewish Religious Philosophy at the University of Frankfurt.

Additional talks on religion include one titled ''Religion: Must it Divide Us?'' to be delivered Wednesday at 4:30 p.m. by Senator John Danforth in Saint Thomas More Chapel at Yale, 268 Park Street in New Haven. The public is invited free of charge.

The Center for Judaic Studies at UConn will present a free talk by Elliot Wolk, Professor Emeritus of Mathematics, on the subject, ''William Tyndale and the Making of the King James Bible,'' Wednesday at noon in the Dodd Center. Visitors may bring lunch; coffee and tea are provided.

A free lecture by T. Anthony Perry, Professor Emeritus of Modern and Classical Languages, is scheduled Nov. 14 at 7 p.m. on ''Medieval Jewish Commentaries on Ecclesiastes'' at the West Hartford campus of UConn.

In Old Greenwich, ''Birth and Beyond: Common Roots, Varied Practices,'' a conference and workshops for religious educators of all faiths, will be held today from 2 p.m. to 6 p.m. at the First Congregational Church, 108 Sound Beach Avenue.

Presented by the interfaith Council of Southwestern Connecticut, the conference will offer presentations of rituals practised in Christian, Jewish and Muslim faiths pertaining to birth, coming of age, marriage and death. Information: (203) 324-7816.

Government and Terrorism

''Striking the Balance: Open Government in the Age of Terrorism,'' a symposium taking place Thursday from 10:30 a.m. to 3 p.m. in Evans Hall at Connecticut College in New London, is open to the public free of charge.

Speakers include Zoe Baird, Paul Schiff Berman, Robert Gellman and Ruth Wedgwood, lawyers and/or professors of law; John Danaher, United States attorney for the District of Connecticut; J. William Leonard, director of information security oversight in the National Archives and Records Administration; Nadine Strossen, president of the American Civil Liberties Union; and Lucy Dalglish, director of the Reporters' Committee for Freedom of the Press. Information: (860) 566-5682. ELEANOR CHARLES
