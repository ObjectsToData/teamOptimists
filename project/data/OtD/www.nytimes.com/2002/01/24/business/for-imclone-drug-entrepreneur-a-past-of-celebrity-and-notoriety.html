For ImClone Drug Entrepreneur, A Past of Celebrity and Notoriety

By ANDREW POLLACK
Published: January 24, 2002

A photograph in New York magazine earlier this month showed Dr. Samuel D. Waksal standing proudly next to Mick Jagger at a holiday party in Dr. Waksal's art-filled loft in the SoHo section of Manhattan.

Dr. Waksal, the chief executive of ImClone Systems, pops up in the New York society pages as often as the business pages. He is often described as having dated both Martha Stewart and her daughter, Alexis. He squired Patricia Duff around town after her split from Ronald O. Perelman, the financier who owns Revlon. He holds monthly salons in his loft where he engages in erudite discussions with scholars and novelists.

But now Dr. Waksal, who has a Ph.D. in immunology, is at the center of a controversy over whether ImClone misled investors about the reasons the Food and Drug Administration rejected the company's application to market a new cancer drug. The House Energy and Commerce Committee said on Friday that it would investigate the company's conduct, including the fact that Dr. Waksal and his brother, Dr. Harlan W. Waksal, the company's chief operating officer, sold more than $150 million worth of shares at peak prices in the months before the drug agency's rejection.

The photo now serves as a reminder of the concerns that some investors and associates have long had about Samuel Waksal -- that he is too promotional, that his celebrity chasing and numerous outside business ventures divert his attention from ImClone and that he has used company loans as a vehicle to finance his opulent life.

''I'm not saying he should stay home and be a priest,'' said Dr. Laurence J. Blumberg, president of Blumberg Capital Management in New York, which has owned ImClone shares at times, ''but I want my management to make sure his priorities are in line with those of shareholders.''

The company's stock has lost two-thirds of its value since that Christmas party in early December, and shareholders' lawsuits have piled up.

Dr. Waksal and his brother say they have been truthful in characterizing ImClone's interactions with the F.D.A. and did not know that the agency would reject the company's application when they sold stock.

''We are confident that ImClone's conduct on these matters relating to ImClone will be shown to be entirely appropriate,'' Harlan Waksal said in a conference call with investors held yesterday by Morgan Stanley. The company hopes to address the F.D.A.'s concern and have its application accepted for review without doing new clinical trials, he said.

The Waksals have many supporters. Samuel Waksal, who is 54, is described as brilliant, gregarious and eminently likable.

''They are terrific people to work with,'' said Dr. John Mendelsohn, the president of the M. D. Anderson Cancer Center. Dr. Mendelsohn developed ImClone's drug and sits on the company's board along with Dr. Arnold J. Levine, the president of Rockefeller University, and Dr. Vincent T. DeVita Jr., the executive director of the Yale Cancer Center and former director of the National Cancer Institute.

Still, controversy has long tailed Dr. Waksal; through a spokeswoman, he declined to respond to questions about the incidents reported in this article. Some investors have been concerned about his frequent borrowing of money from the company. On Sept. 30 he owed ImClone $306,000, most of which was supposed to have been paid back three months earlier, according to Securities and Exchange Commission findings. The debt was repaid in full by November.

Dr. Waksal is not the only insider to benefit from the company. The chairman, Robert F. Goldhammer, is a limited partner in Concord Investment Management, which received $412,000 in 2000 and lesser fees in other years for managing part of ImClone's portfolio of securities. Dr. DeVita has been getting $100,000 a year as a consultant.

Dr. Waksal has been sued several times for not paying his bills. An auction house got a judgment against him in 1994 after a check for nearly $58,000 bounced. Charles Antell, an ImClone investor, sued Dr. Waksal in 1993 for failure to pay back $100,000 he had borrowed. A few days later, court records show, Dr. Waksal sent Mr. Antell two checks with a handwritten note of apology. ''Thanks for being such a good friend,'' he wrote. ''I do not deserve it I know.'' But the larger check bounced.

The Internal Revenue Service and the New York State have both filed tax liens against Mr. Waksal several times, in some cases for more than $100,000, but all of them appear to have been withdrawn or satisfied.

Dr. Waksal has pursued numerous business opportunities. He is described by other companies on whose boards he sits as having helped to found more than 15 other biotechnology companies, like Medicis Pharmaceutical and Cadus Pharmaceutical. He has a business that converts warehouse space in Lower Manhattan into condominiums. Last year he started a new venture capital fund, Scientia Health Group.

Some of his businesses have failed. Sam's Restaurant on West 52nd Street, which Dr. Waksal started with the actress Mariel Hemingway and her husband, Stephen Crisman, a movie producer, went bankrupt in 1993, apparently costing Dr. Waksal hundreds of thousands of dollars. The bankruptcy trustee sued him for taking more than $87,000 that the trustee said should have gone to creditors. Dr. Waksal denied that but settled by agreeing to pay $30,000.
