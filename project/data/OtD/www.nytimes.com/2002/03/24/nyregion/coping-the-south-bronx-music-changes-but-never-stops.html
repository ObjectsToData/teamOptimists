COPING; The South Bronx: Music Changes, But Never Stops

By FELICIA R. LEE
Published: March 24, 2002

''TO some people, the music will never sound right,'' said Ana Torres Maldonado, a cheerleader for the neighborhood of Mott Haven, a fourth-grade teacher and an unrepentant dreamer in an age of cynicism.

The music could be Ms. Maldonado's metaphor for her beloved South Bronx neighborhood, with its many churches, restaurants selling mofongo (mashed green plantains) and backyards where chickens peck.

At that moment, the soft-spoken Ms. Maldonado, her long, dark hair caught in a ponytail, quite literally meant the radio blare of tinny Latino beats. But, more broadly, she meant the clash over which cultural style prevails when struggling neighborhoods start to change.

She and a visitor had stopped at the noisy intersection of Third Avenue and 149th Street, that explosion of people and products, from African hair-braiding shops to women hawking flavored ice from small carts. Ms. Maldonado also showed off the new South Bronx that is emerging amid the old jewelry emporiums and botanicas peddling hope: a nail salon as nice as any on the Upper East Side, a dry-cleaning shop so friendly that it requires no deposit from the often impoverished residents.

Eleven years ago, before the surges in income and drops in crime were officially noted in Mott Haven, Ms. Maldonado bought a house here on a now-quiet block. She was a single parent then, with a 14-year-old daughter. After looking as far as Long Island, she settled here, where she had taught for more than a decade and had directed an after-school program at the local settlement house.

''I found out the sense of community that I was searching for was already here,'' said Ms. Maldonado, who teaches at Public School 101 in East Harlem. She started her career in 1979 as a third-grade teacher at nearby St. Jerome's parochial school.

''I'm not convinced that because I move into an area where the buildings are beautiful, block after block, that the community will be wonderful,'' she said in her soft, sure way. ''We have people here who are true survivors. They have so little money, and they stay sane.''

Of Mott Haven's 76,000 residents, two-thirds Latino and one-third black, more than half over 16 have never had a job. More than 80 percent of children live in homes with single parents on welfare, and only 35 percent of pupils in grades three through eight read at grade level.

Education is part of the battle. The official announcement is next month, but Ms. Maldonado will probably be the first principal of the Mott Haven Village Preparatory High School. It is to open in September in what is now South Bronx High School on St. Ann's Avenue.

South Bronx is a typical low-performing high school (the dropout rate in the Bronx is 60 percent) that will eventually be phased out, the building used for the preparatory school and three other small high schools that will have themes like sports and the arts.

Ms. Maldonado's new school will enroll 75 freshmen in September, adding 75 annually until it reaches 300. It is a public school, sponsored by the East Side House Settlement and supported by philanthropic foundations. Any student with college aspirations is welcome.

It's a dream come true for Ms. Maldonado, 49, the daughter of poor Puerto Rican parents who raised 10 children in East Harlem. ''We are always telling kids from these communities that they have to get out, they have to leave to become successful,'' she said, pointing out new prefab houses going up on St. Ann's Avenue. ''People don't get out of most communities. If the students see that they can use what they are learning in their daily lives, and if we can invite people from the community in to guide them, maybe we can make a difference.

''By 10th grade, the students will have internships in humanities and environmental science,'' she added. ''We know, for example, there is a high asthma rate in the Bronx. What do we do about it? We want the students to go to the hospitals, get the data, do the interviews.''

The odds are against this school becoming, say, the Stuyvesant of the Bronx. Ms. Maldonado knows that. But she keeps dreaming, keeps fighting, anyway.
