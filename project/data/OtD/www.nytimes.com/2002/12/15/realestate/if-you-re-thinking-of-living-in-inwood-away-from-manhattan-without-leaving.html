If You're Thinking of Living In/Inwood; Away From Manhattan Without Leaving

By NANCY BETH JACKSON
Published: December 15, 2002

FOR SALE: Two-bedroom, 1.5-bath Manhattan penthouse, updated kitchen, 500-sq.-ft. terrace with wide-open Hudson River views. Steps from salt marsh, primeval forest, hiking trails, kayaking and collegiate football. Asking $375,000.

THE offering sounds too good to be true, but the apartment went on the market last month in Inwood on the northern tip of the island, bordered by the Harlem and Hudson Rivers and a greenbelt of parks. The neighborhood's southern boundary with Washington Heights depends on who's defining. Residents line up in two camps: Fairview Avenue as described in the Encyclopedia of New York City and Dyckman Street, about a half-dozen blocks to the north, on Department of City Planning maps. Real estate agents seem to agree with the encyclopedia.

Ed Romanoff and Joyce A. Presutti, actors who moved from an Upper West Side studio to a $39,750 two-bedroom co-op in 1998, like to say they live in ''Washingwood'' or ''Upstate Manhattan.''

Once home to Irish and Jewish immigrants and now a Dominican enclave, Inwood increasingly draws actors, musicians, artists and young professionals seeking affordable housing and wanting to get away from Manhattan without leaving it. ''We're starting to see even more Broadway show jackets in the neighborhood,'' said Mr. Romanoff, who has lured two other show business couples to join him and his wife way uptown.

They do not come for the night life.

Lisa C. Leveque, 27, a sales agent at A. N. Shell Realty who grew up in Inwood, said: ''This is a neighborhood neighborhood. We don't have restaurants and clubs. We can still party downtown, but Inwood is the laid-back environment you need when you work 9-to-9. You need to come back to something more serene.''

It does not get more serene than Inwood Hill Park, 196 acres of how Manhattan looked before the Indians sold it to the Dutch for $24 worth of trinkets. As one version of the story goes, the sale took place under a giant tulip tree in the park, which is now the island's last natural forest. The local American Legion post is named after Peter Minuit, who brokered the deal.

Frederick Law Olmsted coaxed other city parks into artistic wilderness, but Inwood Hill is the real thing, with glacial potholes etched out in the last Ice Age and rock shelters where the Lenape cooked their oysters and clams. A five-sided earthwork fort deserted by the Americans during the Revolution has long since disappeared, as have country homes, a charity house for women and free public library of the 1800's, torn down after the Parks Department bought the land in 1916. Hiking trails lace the park, which includes a bald eagle nursery, an urban ecology center, the city's only salt marsh and a viewing spot for the Amtrak swing bridge across the confluence of the Hudson and Harlem Rivers.

On the city side of the park are tennis courts, baseball fields, a soccer field and another 27 acres of greenbelt that is home to Columbia University's Baker Field, track and boathouse. The park adjoins Isham Park, which adds 20 more acres of green. Fort Tyron Park and the Cloisters museum in Washington Heights are a 12-story elevator ride up from the A train's West 196th stop.

Broadway, cutting diagonally across Inwood, is the great divide in the real estate market. Most of the housing stock is six-story brick apartment buildings, dating from the 1930's, but west of Broadway they are more likely to have elevators and be co-ops, said Robert V. Kleinbardt, who opened New Heights Realty in 1992 after living in Inwood for a dozen years.

West of Broadway, apartment houses often have Art Deco details and interior courtyards. Buildings to the east are more likely to be rentals. They include the seven 14-story Dyckman Houses, a City Housing Authority complex, where Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, the basketball star, grew up as Lew Alcindor. Both rentals and co-ops are about 30 percent more expensive to the west.

Inwood also has several dozen two- to five-family homes. Mr. Kleinbardt can count the single-family houses without running out of fingers. ''I've sold two in 10 years,'' he said. The last was in 1997, and it was on the market for more than a year before it sold for $375,000. Today, he estimates, it could fetch as much as $900,000.

LONGTIME residents say more and more newcomers seem to be people who have chosen Inwood over Brooklyn because prices are lower and the transportation is good. The A express train, the No. 1 and 9 trains and a Liberty Lines East Side express bus lead to Midtown and beyond; the Henry Hudson Parkway and Major Deegan Expressway give quick access to getaways up the Hudson or shopping in Yonkers.

Jenn Ching, 28, a public interest lawyer who was commuting from lower Manhattan to Newark, and her partner, Jeff Lee, 29, a Brooklyn Law School student from Queens, bought a one-bedroom co-op east of Broadway in May. They paid $81,500, some 50 percent less than Park Slope apartments they had considered.

''When we came up and walked around the parks, we were just stunned, but we wouldn't have moved up here if not for the A train,'' Ms. Ching said. Her commute takes an hour and 11 minutes door to door, but she always gets a seat on the way in because Inwood is the start of the line.
