A Lost World Lives On At the Newark Library

By VICKI VASILOPOULOS
Published: December 15, 2002

WHILE browsing at a bookstore in Springfield four years ago, Peter Markos came upon ''Newark's Little Italy: The Vanished First Ward,'' by Michael Immerso. It made him think that someone should do something to recognize Newark's Greek neighborhood, where he grew up.

He decided to do it himself. The result is ''Remembering Newark's Greeks: An American Odyssey,'' an exhibition of photographs and memorabilia at the Newark Public Library that documents the Greek-American presence in the city in the first half of the 1900's.

The show, which runs through Dec. 31, was curated by the retired Mr. Markos, 67, a former vice principal at a high school in Roselle, and his childhood friend Angelique Lampros, a retired teacher and administrator at elementary and high schools in Maplewood and Union City. Charles Cummings, Newark's city historian and the assistant director for special collections at the library, oversaw the project.

Greeks began arriving in Newark in the early part of the 20th century, Mr. Cummings said. At first, they worked in menial jobs -- as waiters, bootblacks, ice men and vegetable vendors. Eventually, they became small-business owners. The library show documents about 150 Greek-owned businesses in the city from the 1920's to the 1960's, from restaurants and luncheonettes to florist shops, movie theaters, coffee distributorships and bakeries. Describing the Greek immigrant mindset, Ms. Lampros said, ''Even if you had to work longer hours and harder, you knew it was yours.''

Greeks settled in the city's central third ward, which encompassed High Street (now Martin Luther King Boulevard), West Market Street, Springfield Avenue and Roseville Avenue. The heart of the neighborhood was St. Nicholas Greek Orthodox Church, on Martin Luther King Boulevard, the first Greek Orthodox church in New Jersey.

After World War II, as the population became more affluent, Newark's Greeks moved to the suburbs. Today, the Greek section of town is a thing of the past. In the 1930's, there were about 8,000 Greeks in the city, Mr. Cummings estimated; the 2000 census lists 192 Greeks out of 61,522 in the state. Indeed, because much of its congregation has scattered, with many people reluctant to travel to Newark, St. Nicholas church, known as St. Nick's, plans to move to Roseland.

At first, Mr. Markos, who lives in Branchburg, wanted to write a book about this lost world, even though he had never written a book before. He enlisted the help of Ms. Lampros, who lives in West Orange. As children, the two attended Greek school together at St. Nick's.

They canvassed for photographs, documents and information, placing notices in church newsletters around northern New Jersey and in the magazines The Greek-American and The Orthodox Observer, which reach Greeks nationwide. Using the mailing lists of St. Nicholas and St. Demetros Greek Orthodox Church, formerly in Newark and now in Union, they sent questionnaires to Greeks who had settled in Newark, along with first- and second-generation descendants across the country with roots in Newark.

They collected hundreds of photographs, and spent three years taping and transcribing oral histories. Then they applied to the New Jersey Historical Commission for a grant to publish the book. To their dismay, they were turned down.

Early on, they talked with Mr. Immerso, who wrote the book about Newark's Italian neighborhood that had inspired Mr. Markos. That book had its genesis as an exhibition at the Newark library. Mr. Immerso suggested that they speak to Mr. Cummings at the library, who helped put together that show as well as one on Newark's Jews.

Mr. Cummings was interested in their idea and suggested that, like Mr. Immerso, they do an exhibition at the library first. He helped them procure a grant from the New Jersey Council for the Humanities for the project.

The show documents the struggle of Greeks to assimilate while preserving their language, culture and Orthodox faith. While celebrating the humble lives of these immigrants, it also exalts the achievements of some, among them A. A. Adams, formerly Adamopoulos, the owner of a chain of movie theaters in New Jersey who raised $4 million through the sale of war in World War II, and Nicholas A. Antonius, a cardiologist and medical director of St. Michael's Hospital in Newark.

The show has evoked bittersweet feelings in people like Gus Stathis, a resident of Montville who was born in Newark and who, like many, has attended the show more than once. ''The house I was born in no longer exists,'' he said. ''It stimulates the mind, the memories.''

Mr. Markos and Ms. Lampros still hope to write their book. They have also set up an endowment to acquire and preserve materials for the library about Greeks in New Jersey.

Mr. Cummings paid tribute to the dedication of Mr. Markos and Ms. Lampros. ''Each of them, for three and a half years, did nothing but work on this night and day,'' he said.

They say their work was a labor of love. ''We wanted to honor our parents and our grandparents to save it for future generations, to save it for scholars,'' Ms. Lampros said.
