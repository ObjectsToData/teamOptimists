They'll Be Hanged

By FRANCINE PARNES
Published: September 22, 2002

JOANNE KELLAR BOUKNIGHT, an architect from Cos Cob, Conn., who thinks our lives would be far more pleasant if our closets were well organized, once observed a squirrel perform its own version of household storage.

''It was early fall, when we are pelted for days with what seem like billions of acorns from our oak tree,'' she said. ''I was watching this wacko squirrel frantically looking for a place to put an acorn. 'Do I put in the leaves, in the grass or elsewhere?' Finally, he began digging a seemingly random hole.''

All that Ms. Bouknight could think was, ''And you're going to find that one next spring?''

''We've all done the same kind of thing,'' said Ms. Bouknight, author of ''Taunton's Home Storage Idea Book'' (October 2002) by Taunton Press in Newtown, Conn., which publishes many books on home topics. ''There's a psychology to the ending of summer, a new-year feeling in September, when you put away bathing suits, beach towels and flip-flops. But then spring comes, and we think, 'The beach stuff, where did I put it? In the duffel bag, under the bed or in the laundry room?' ''

How we organize our stuff behind closet doors can range from stockpiled clutter and chaos to Zen-like minimalism and quietude.

No longer just the organizational nerve center of domesticity, closets have evolved into a form of self-expression. Many homeowners have gone well beyond the old-time clothes rack to create bedroom-sized custom closets, status symbols to be flaunted. Others are rethinking their closets as personal retreats that hold workstations or favorite objects as well as clothes.

''People call me 'Mr. Organized,' '' said Jeffrey Nathan, a chef and restauranteur from Livingston.

The nickname might be an understatement.

In Mr. Nathan's closet, 20 pairs of shoes are lined up and stuffed with 20 pairs of matching socks. Each hanger holds a complete outfit: pants, jacket, shirt, necktie and belt.

''Many people have shirts and pants hanging separately in the closet,'' Mr. Nathan said. ''I can't handle that. It's a tremendous amount of time and work to walk around your bedroom in a 360-degree circle to find your clothing for the day. My wife thinks I'm a nut, but I think I'm successful because I know where everything is.''

Mr. Nathan keeps index cards in the breast pocket of each hanging sports jacket, listing details about coordinating items. His previous system, photographing the entire outfit at the store at purchase time, was less precise, and failed altogether when he forgot a camera, he said.

''I once bought a tremendous amount of clothing, had it altered, took it out of the bags and said, 'What am I doing, what went with what?' I had purchased a couple thousand dollars of clothing, had taken no picture, and then had no idea what went together. It was overwhelming.''

Mr. Nathan is not the only one reining in a tremendous amount of clothing.

''Today's closet has to handle a colossal variety of clothes, much more specialized for different activities than our great-grandparents' limited weekday and Sunday clothes,'' Ms. Bouknight said, referring to categories including casual wear, work wear, evening wear and sports clothes. ''Clothing has diversified, but also we acquire more things today and have to find space for them.''

Less extensive wardrobes of earlier eras went with the smaller closets typical of many older homes. But many expansive free-standing storage pieces made grandiose statements.

Edward Tenner, a former visiting humanities lecturer at Princeton University, recalled the massive storage pieces at the Princeton home of Albert Einstein, where the ornate cabinetwork reminded him of items in German museums.

''I thought it odd of Einstein, famous for despising European bourgeois pomp, to let it take over so much of his house,'' Dr. Tenner said. ''Perhaps the American closet was a declaration of independence from such massive objects built to last for generations. Every move could be a new beginning, and a new closet would be waiting.''

Betsy Devine, a writer and engineer who throughout the 1990's owned and lived in Einstein's furnished house with her husband, Frank Wilczek, a physicist, recalled one bedroom, without a closet, that had a 16-foot-long clothing-storage piece, elaborately carved with an inlay of flowers, fruit and faces of Greek gods.

''It was huge,'' Ms. Devine said. ''We couldn't even imagine how it got through the door. It took up so much space that it even blocked a door. We thought of it as Satan's idea of storage.''

Closets evoke other sinister connotations.

''When I think of what's in a closet, there's the obvious, 'Is there a skeleton or body in there?' '' said Naomi Rand of Upper Montclair, who happens to be a mystery author. ''There are probably 10 bodies in my closet. Me and every stage of my life.''

She ran through her closet's golden oldies: childhood equestrian clothes, Salvation Army hippie gear, her red (!) wedding dress.

''A closet is just a closet, but it's also a trip down memory lane,'' Ms. Rand said. ''It's all the sizes I used to be, all the stuff I used to look good in, all the people I once was. Your closet is your private space. You keep all that nostalgia hidden away where no one can see it.''

Hidden away? Not necessarily.

Many closets today are showpieces, said Dorothea Braginsky, a professor of psychology at Fairfield University in Connecticut.
