Beyond the Briefly Inflated Canon: Legacy of the Mysterious 'W. S.'

By WILLIAM S. NIEDERKORN
Published: June 26, 2002

''W. S.''

It was those tantalizing initials in the byline on the title page and on the dedication of the poem ''A Funeral Elegy'' that gave Donald W. Foster, a professor of English at Vassar College, the first piece of evidence that the reference was to Shakespeare.

And it was those initials that led Richard Abrams, professor of English at the University of Southern Maine, to gather historical evidence to support Professor Foster's theory.

Both scholars have recently recanted their Shakespeare theory, conceding that a new analysis of the text of the elegy, which was written for William Peter, a murder victim, points to the 17th-century dramatist John Ford as the author, a position on which scholars are now in general agreement.

But what about W. S.?

''I have not tried to identify him,'' Gilles D. Monsarrat, professor of languages at the University of Burgundy, in Dijon, France, says in his article in The Review of English Studies that toppled the theory. He conjectures that W. S. paid for the printing of the elegy. ''I suppose he was a young Devonshire gentleman (not a poet), who had, like William Peter, been to Oxford,'' he writes.

Brian Vickers, the director of Renaissance studies at the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Zurich, whose book ''Counterfeiting Shakespeare: Evidence, Authorship and John Ford's Funerall Elegye'' is due from Cambridge University Press in August, makes no conjecture about W. S.

''At the moment that's a black hole,'' he said in a telephone interview, joking that he would leave the question to ''anyone who really wants to go out and look at the Oxford University archives,'' find out who flunked out, ''and correlate that against Devonshire country family archives.''

Professor Abrams has spent a good part of the last seven years on historical digging. Some of his research has involved checking records to see if any other candidates with the initials W. S. presented themselves.

''I've gone through as many West Country documents as I've been able to,'' he said. ''I've constructed huge genealogies, I have a database with something over 10,000 names with a great deal of information on people, but I haven't had my head turned by any other W. S.'s I've been encountering.''

''My thought is that W. S. would have been taken by many readers for William Shakespeare,'' he said, adding that there might be evidence involving Shakespeare, even with the authorship ruled out.

Professor Abrams has not yet presented the evidence, but in an interview he described it as having to do with connections between the Shakespeare family of Stratford-upon-Avon and the Peter family of Devonshire, in particular William and his brother, John, to whom the dedication of the elegy was addressed.

''Although I can't put William or John Peter in a room with Shakespeare, I can put people who undoubtedly knew Shakespeare in a room with people whom John Peter undoubtedly knew and spent time with,'' he said, including someone who paid a bereavement call on John Peter. ''It's an astonishing number of connections, and that I'll be publishing pretty soon.''

Professor Abrams wanted to stress his own ''puzzlement about the evidence.'' He said, ''If Foster and I were led astray here I think that may in some intent have been the purpose of John Ford in writing this poem, to suggest Shakespeare.''

Daniel L. Wright, professor of English at Concordia University in Portland, Ore., and director of the Edward de Vere Studies Conference, echoed that thought: ''It may be an attempt to suggest the work is by Shakespeare,'' he said. ''It may be someone whose initials were indeed W. S. There are a number of ideas. I don't think any of them are worthy of endorsement.''

He noted that the elegy was not the only period text by ''W. S.'' and that the other texts ''are in no sense agreed upon by a majority of scholars to be William Shakespeare's.''

Professor Foster, too, now sees the evidence of the initials W. S. -- and title page attributions generally -- in a new, dimmer light compared to internal evidence, including, he said, ''language and prosody and spelling and borrowings and so forth that we associate with a particular writer.''

He concluded: ''This really represents a kind of revolutionary moment, because what I'm willing to assent to is that the internal evidence that Ford wrote the poem is stronger to my mind than the evidence of stationers and initials and so on.''

Publishers of the editions of Shakespeare's works that included the elegy after Professor Foster's initial conclusion are inclining toward a revolutionary moment, too.

Stephen Greenblatt, general editor of ''The Norton Shakespeare'' and a professor of English at Harvard, said, ''My own inclination and I imagine my associates' will be to take it out.'' The funeral elegy has played the role of introducing students to authorship questions, he continued, adding that the play ''Edward III'' might be a better candidate for that role in a future edition.

The poem will remain in the next printing of ''The Riverside Shakespeare'' with a change in the introduction to reflect what has happened, said Michael Gillespie, editor in charge of literature titles for college courses at Houghton Mifflin. It will be removed from the next edition, he added.

David Bevington, a professor of the humanities at the University of Chicago and editor of ''The Complete Works of Shakespeare,'' published by Pearson's Longman division, could not be reached for comment, but a Pearson spokesman said that Professor Bevington would probably consider removing the elegy.
