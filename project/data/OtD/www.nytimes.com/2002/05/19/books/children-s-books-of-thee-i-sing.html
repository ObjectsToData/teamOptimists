CHILDREN'S BOOKS; Of Thee I Sing

By Alexander Stille
Published: May 19, 2002

AMERICA

A Patriotic Primer.

By Lynne Cheney.

Illustrated by Robin Preiss Glasser.

Unpaged. New York:

Simon & Schuster. $16.95.

(All ages)

LIBERTY!

Written and illustrated

by Allan Drummond.

Unpaged. New York:

Frances Foster Books/

Farrar, Straus & Giroux. $17.

(Ages 4 to 8)

ABRAHAM LINCOLN

By Amy L. Cohn and Suzy Schmidt.

Illustrated by David A. Johnson.

Unpaged. New York:

Scholastic Press. $16.95.

(All ages)

There may be a truce in the culture wars -- if children's books provide any clue as to what is happening in the country at large. Patriotism, not surprisingly, is a big theme in a number of titles dealing with American history since Sept. 11, and patriotism has a way of healing or, at least, papering over other differences.

Perhaps most indicative of this truce is Lynne Cheney's ''America: A Patriotic Primer'' -- as notable for its moderate, inclusive tone as for its flag-waving. The wife of the current vice president, Cheney played the role of point person of the cultural right during her tenure as chairwoman of the National Endowment for the Humanities from 1986 to 1993, excoriating the multicultural political correctness of institutions like the Modern Language Association. ''America,'' which wraps blacks, American Indians, immigrants, feminists and even the disabled in red, white and blue, is an expression of the new ''compassionate conservatism'': Norman Rockwell meets Jesse Jackson's Rainbow Coalition.

Written with a page for each letter of the alphabet, ''America'' hits some of the obvious patriotic notes -- A is for America, C is for Constitution, W is for Washington. But it contains a few surprises as well -- S is for Suffrage, T is for Tolerance and N is for Native American. The text reaches out to include and recognize the contributions of women and minorities in ways that the cultural right has often objected to in the textbook battles of recent years. H, for example, features 12 American heroes. Five -- John and Abigail Adams, Nathan Hale, Clara Barton and Sam Houston -- are fixtures of old-fashioned American textbooks beloved of the Daughters of the American Revolution. But there are five women, three blacks and one Indian (Chief Joseph) and a surprising number of liberal icons: the social reformer Jane Addams, Franklin and Eleanor Roosevelt.

What the book may show is that notions that were bitterly divisive several years ago -- diversity, multiculturalism, inclusiveness -- are now so widely accepted as to have become entirely standard. Cheney's ''patriotic primer,'' for good or ill, looks a great deal like many of the grade-school social studies texts of recent years: a well-intentioned if occasionally awkward attempt to satisfy various constituencies, to honor the usual dead white males (the founding fathers et al.), while opening the pantheon to a wider, multihued group.

A typical example is the page that includes both E and F. E is for Equality, and F stands for both Freedom and Flag. American flags surround the double page, with captions about saluting and folding the flag, while another border of text contains an ''Equality Time-line,'' which charts the gradual expansion of rights in American history from the 13th Amendment of 1865 to the Voting Rights Act of 1965 and even the 1990 Americans With Disabilities Act. The accompanying picture shows a happy array of different racial and ethnic groups, including a child in a wheelchair. (Not long ago, this might have been denounced as tokenism.)

Cheney writes in her introduction that ''few stories are more heartening than the way that the idea of equality, which was the basis'' for the founding fathers' actions, ''has expanded over the subsequent two centuries, including more and ever more of us in the phrase 'We the people.' ''

Cheney sounds an equally conciliatory note on the subject of immigration, which looked in the mid-1990's as if it might become one of the nation's hottest and most divisive political issues but appears to have lost much of its sting in recent years. ''Our children should also know that you do not have to be born in this country to be an American,'' she writes.

Many conservatives will be happy to see that G for God gets a two full pages, a break from the usual one-letter-per-page format. But some may be surprised to see figures like the dancer Martha Graham, the architect I. M. Pei, the radical suffragist Susan B. Anthony and the antislavery protofeminist Sojourner Truth getting their day in the sun too. Given her efforts at inclusion, we can perhaps forgive Cheney for awarding her former boss, Ronald Reagan, the last quoted words in the book (''I know that for America there will always be a bright dawn ahead'').

The watercolor illustrations, by Robin Preiss Glasser, are lively, cheerful and imaginative. Surely, however, Cheney could have come up with something better than ''Z is the end of the alphabet.''

Because of the primer format, Cheney can't give more than a brief reference to any single figure or idea. Two other picture books, ''Liberty!,'' written and illustrated by Allan Drummond, and ''Abraham Lincoln,'' by Amy L. Cohn and Suzy Schmidt, with illustrations by David A. Johnson, strum the chords of patriotism by dealing with a single figure or event.

''Liberty!'' is about the unveiling of the Statue of Liberty. Although the text is rather thin (''I am free . . . and you are free''), the watercolors are highly attractive. The story concerns a boy who is supposed to give the signal to Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi, the statue's creator, to pull the veil from Lady Liberty's face at the opening ceremony. When a French girl in the crowd sneezes and the boy offers her his handkerchief, Bartholdi mistakenly takes this as the signal -- but the ceremony works out fine just the same. A potentially more interesting story line is suggested by one full-page illustration that shows a boat of suffragists who actually did stage their protest near the island that day.

''Abraham Lincoln'' is a brief illustrated biography of our 16th president. While it is pleasant enough, and nicely illustrated, the text is disappointing. The authors have chosen a folksy, colloquial tone that is supposed to capture the language of the 19th-century frontier. ''My, he's strong,'' they write. ''Strong enough to plow and plant 'longside his pappy.'' It ends up seeming affected, as well as discordant with the language of the man who was perhaps our most eloquent statesmen.

Far more is made of Lincoln's long arms and legs than of his political career, which is compressed to a few unsatisfying sentences: ''Now look at Abraham, all dressed up and on his way to the White House. Abe had tried -- and failed -- to win a United States Senate seat. But, by golly, the speeches he gave about slavery and the country's future made him so famous that he found himself elected president just two years later.''

The text comes alive when the authors allow Lincoln to speak in his own words. ''I am a slow walker, but I never walk backward,'' he responds to those who wanted him to revoke the Emancipation Proclamation. Given the vivid beauty of Lincoln's language, a book about him that included more of his prose and less of the authors' might have worked better.
