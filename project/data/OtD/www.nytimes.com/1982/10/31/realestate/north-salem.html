NORTH SALEM

By FRANKLIN WHITEHOUSE
Published: October 31, 1982

THERE are no traffic lights on the 100 miles of roads that wind t hrough the hills and fields of North Salem, just stop signs. The n earest supermarket and movie theater are in neighboring towns. And c able television has yet to be hooked up.

This sparsely settled town in northern Westchester County exults in its rural setting. Macouns and Cortlands are in now from the apple orchards along Hardscrabble Road, and the frosts of autumn add an early morning sparkle to the fields of the town's horse farms.

Some people say in jest that there are more horses than people in this town of 4,563, which was settled early in the 1700's. There are, in fact, a score of farms on which horses are bred, jumped and shown, and many private homes have their own barns out back.

One of the half dozen commercial stables, Far West Farms on Route 124, was purchased in 1981 and renovated by Paul Newman, the actor, and his wife, Joanne Woodward, the actress, who live just over the border in Connecticut.

The town has more than 60 miles of bridle paths, many of which cross the rolling meadows and stone walls of estates whose owners grant permission for riders to pass.

About 100 families belong to the Goldens Bridge Hounds, whose mounted members, clad in ''pinks'' and swooping after baying hounds, hunt foxes twice a week in the fall and winter.

The houses that dot North Salem's 42 square miles represent nearly all styles of American architecture, from Federal and Georgian Colonial to Greek and Italianate Revival. Some of the large estates in the eastern part of town occupy more than 100 acres and have sold recently for as much as $600,000.

Three-bedroom ranch houses that were built on two acres for $30,000 in the Lake Hawthorne section in the 1960's and 1970's sell now for $100,000, according to the town historian, Richard de Frances, who lives there.

North Salem is bounded on the east by Ridgefield, Conn., on the north by Putnam County, on the south by the Town of Lewisboro and on the west by the Town of Somers. North Salem's two principal hamlets, Croton Falls and Purdys, lie close to the western boundary along Interstate 684, which runs roughly north and south.

Salem Center, where municipal offices occupy a white clapboard house with a Dutch gambrel roof built about 1770, is some 55 miles north of Manhattan by car on I-684 and the Hutchinson River Parkway.

''If you want elbow room, you've got to come this far north,'' said Jerome Billingsley, a real-estate broker whose family has land investments here. About 300 residents make the 90-minute commute to New York City from Conrail stations at the two hamlets. Others commute by car to corporate offices in the region, such as I.B.M. in Armonk, Pepsico in Purchase, Texaco in Harrison and Union Carbide in Danbury, Conn.

Change has come slowly to North Salem. One section of Route 121, a local thoroughfare, appears to be unchanged when compared to an 1895 photograph, Mr. de Frances said, save for paving. Yet, with several residential, commercial and industrial developments either under way or in planning stages, the pace of development is quickening.

One New York City investing group is considering building a group of $500,000 homes on lots of at least 10 acres on a 172-acre tract east of Route 124. Don McGrath, a real-estate agent from Katonah and the group's representative, would not identify the investors, who plan to include a barn with a stable. He said only that ''their passion is horses.'' THE group recently dropped its option on a 188-acre site on H ardscrabble Road in the belief that obtaining rezoning from i ndustrial to residential use would take too long. One developer who p lans to take advantage of that industrial zoning is Edward W. Kelly o f Katonah. He expects to build an industrial park on the 200 acres o f the Outhouse Orchards that he recently purchased. Many of the p each trees and apple trees that surround his buildings sites are to b e saved, however, and the orchard is to be leased back to the p revious owners, Wayne and Drew Outhouse.

Easements for horseback riding on the property of others, which had to be approved by the New York Secretary of State, were written into land titles in Stonewall Farms, a subdivision of 31 homesites around the intersection of Finch Road and Vail Lane.

Mr. Billingsley, the real-estate agent who began selling the land in 1979, said that the first lots had sold for between $30,000 and $50,000 and now go for ''more than $70,000.'' They range from three to 23 acres, with the average about 10 acres. A dozen homes have been built so far.

Multiple dwellings, which now exist only in small numbers in Purdys and Croton Falls, may be on the way. Last summer, the town lost its final appeal of a State Supreme Court finding that its zoning was ''exclusionary.'' Robert Marsecca, an investor from New York City who brought the suit on the zoning ordinances, soon will seek approval of a plan to build about 84 units on a 21-acre site between Route 22 and I-684, according to Stuart R. Shamberg, the Mount Kisco lawyer who represents him.
