MORE TOP STUDENTS SHUNNING PH.D.

By FOX BUTTERFIELD, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 16, 1982

CAMBRIDGE, Mass., March 15— The number of top students who earn Ph.D. degrees and become professors has been declining sharply in the United States in recent years while the number switching to more lucrative careers in business, law, medicine and engineering has been increasing.

Despite this, the overall number of graduate students in the arts and sciences has not substantially declined, and the universities are turning out almost five times more doctorates than the number of available teaching posts, a trend that a number of educators view as a cause for concern.

William G. Bowen, the president of Princeton University, said, ''The essence of the problem is this paradox: There are too many Ph.D. candidates and not enough quality.'' Shortage of Scholars Feared

Mr. Bowen, like other educators, is worried that a shortage of scholars may be emerging. ''Over the long term,'' he said, ''we may face the possibility that the quality of teaching and research will diminish and entire fields of knowledge may be weakened.''

''It would be tragic,'' he said, ''if we were to go through a period when the ablest candidates were discouraged from getting a Ph.D. when, over all, the number of Ph.D.'s is still too large for the available jobs.''

The decline in the number of the brightest students going to graduate school has been dramatic. Surveys Indicate Trend

Lewis Solmon of the Higher Education Research Institute of the University of California at Los Angeles has found from surveys that in 1964 about 75 percent of the students who graduated in the top quarter of their college class continued their education in the arts and sciences while only 20 percent went to law, medical or business school. By contrast, in 1979 more than half of the top graduates chose professional schools and only a third chose the arts and sciences.

At Harvard, 77.2 percent of the seniors who graduated with highest honors went on to graduate school in the arts and sciences in 1964. But in 1981, the figure was only 30.8 percent.

The reasons for this dropoff, educators say, are clear: declining financial aid for graduate studies, the shrinking academic job market and low university salaries. Graduate fellowships, financed by organizations like the Ford Foundation, began to dry up several years ago, even before President Reagan's proposed elimination of Guaranteed Student Loans for all graduate students.

To the surprise of some educators, the total number of doctoral students has declined only slightly, to 31,319 last year from a high point of 33,756 in 1973, according to the National Research Council in Washington. Sharp Differences Between Fields

These figures, however, do mask sharp differences between fields. At Harvard's Graduate School of Arts and Sciences, the number of first-year graduate students in the humanities has slipped 39 percent in the last decade, as against a 9 percent decline in the sciences.

The humanities, including English, philosophy and the classics, have been particularly hard hit by the reduction in the number of new college teaching posts.

The Modern Languages Association in New York has found that in the last few years an average of only 40 percent of those students who received Ph.D.'s in English and foreign languages have received full-time college teaching posts with jobs that could lead to tenure. Another 20 percent have found temporary teaching appointments, and the other 40 percent have ended up outside college teaching. Job Crisis Called 'Brutal'

Gary Gerstle, a sixth-year graduate student at Harvard in American history who hopes to finish his doctoral dissertation this summer, is facing the job crisis now. ''It's brutal,'' he said.

He said there were only about 15 job openings nationwide this year in American history for which he was qualified. He has sent applications to all 15. So far, all but three have rejected him. The others have not replied.

Mr. Gerstle said he had heard that there were up to 100 applicants for each of the job openings. Even if he did get an appointment, the starting salary for an assistant professor in history is only $15,000 to $19,000, depending on the school.

That is not much considering that he has given up six years of earning power to go to graduate school and has had to spend much of his time working and teaching to help finance his studies. A Grim Situation

Mr. Bowen calculates that the grim situation for academic employment will get even worse before a possible improvement in the middle to late 1990's, mainly for demographic reasons.

In his 1981 annual report, ''Graduate Education in the Arts and Sciences: Prospects for the Future,'' Mr. Bowen noted that the number of people 18 to 24 years old will decline more than 23 percent from 1978 to 1997. From these figures he projects a rather steady fall in college enrollment, from 8.25 million in 1981 to just over 7 million in 1996. This would be an overall decline of 15 percent nationally, with perhaps 30 percent in some parts of the country, forcing some colleges to close. Faculty Makeup Is a Problem

Educators have warned that the situation will be worse if President Reagan's proposed cuts in aid to education are passed by Congress.

Compounding the problem for new Ph.D. holders is the fact that most colleges have a high proportion of relatively young tenured professors, a result of higher education's rapid expansion in the 1950's and 1960's.

Taking all these factors together, Mr. Bowen estimates that over the next 15 years there will be only 100,000 academic positions to be filled. By comparison, that many jobs were filled in the three peak years of 1965-1967 alone. And, if the country's graduate schools continue to produce Ph.D's at last year's rate of 31,319, 470,000 people would be chasing those 100,000 jobs.

The key task, in Mr. Bowen's view, is to reduce the quantity of graduate students while encouraging those of the highest quality. One positive development, he said, was the Mellon Foundation's creation this winter of a fellowship program for outstanding doctoral candidates in the humanities.

In response to the changed job market, some institutions are developing alternative doctoral programs designed to be more practical and help students find employment.

Carnegie-Mellon University in Pittsburgh, for example, has several new Ph.D. programs in English that train students to give writing courses or to teach business executives how to prepare more comprehensible written materials.

Some professors insist that the decline in the quality of new graduate students is not cause for alarm. Professor Solmon, at U.C.L.A., said that only a tiny fraction of intellectuals do all the creative work anyway and added, ''Are we really worse off having more bright lawyers and doctors than English teachers?''
