POLISH AIRWAVES: AIDE ASSERTS GOVERNMENT DEMANDS ONLY 'UNDERSTANDING'

Special to the New York Times
Published: January 6, 1982

WASHINGTON, Jan. 5— Following are excerpts from domestic Polish radio and television broadcasts as transcribed and translated from the Polish by the Foreign Broadcast Information Service, a United States Government agency:

Unionists Consulted 7 P.M. Tuesday

(1 P.M. Tuesday, E.S.T.) P.A.P. has learned that talks with activists of N.S.Z.Z. Solidarity, industrial trade unions, autonomous trade unions and other union associations are under way in the office of the minister in charge of trade union affairs. The talks p ertain to methods of satisfying the social needs of the working peopl e at a time when activities of trade unions have been suspended for the duration of the state of martial law.

Issues pertaining to the future of independent and self-managing trade union activity proceeding from constitutional principles of Poland's system and statute provisions of these unions are also being dealt with during the talks.

Czyrek Sees Envoys 6:21 P.M. Tuesday

(12:21 P.M. Tuesday, E.S.T.) On Jan. 5, Foreign Minister Jozef Czyrek, Politburo member and a Centralk Committee secretary, received a group of diplomatic representatives of Western and third world countries.

During the meeting, Minister Jozef Czyrek informed the heads of diplomatic missions about the Polish stand toward some international issues and the situation in Poland. He also expressed his satisfaction resulting from the fact that the majority of countries have been showing understanding to the developments in Poland and stressed the significance of economic aid that Poland receives, especially from the U.S.S.R.

It is with surprise, regret and objection that Poland meets the stands of some governments that on the one hand declare their friendliness to the Polish nation and on the other avail themselves of political pressure and even economic restrictions threatening the interests of the Polish society.

Taking part in the meeting were the Ambassadors of Afghanistan, Argentina, Australia, Austria, Bangladesh, People's Republic of China, Egypt, Finland, Nigeria, Norway, Pakistan, Portugal, the United States of America, Syria, Switzerland, Sweden, Thailand, Tunisia, Turkey, Zaire, the charges d'affaires of Albania, Algeria, Brazil, Ecuador, Iran, Malaysia, Morocco, Uruguay, Venezuela, the member of the popular committee of the popular bureau of the Libyan Jamahiriyah and the deputy permanent representative of the P.L.O.

Rakowski Makes Speech 7:32 A.M. Tuesday

(1:32 A.M. Tuesday, E.S.T.) The national press today carries the text of a speech by Deputy Premier Mieczyslaw Rakowski delivered on 23 December last year at a meeting between the chairman of the Military Council of National Salvation, Army Gen. Wojciech Jaruzelski, and representatives from the world of science. We shall acquaint you with excerpts from this speech.

We read at the beginning that ''what I have to say will be unpleasant and painful to some of those present here.'' ''In fact, I am addressing not only those gathered here but the entire Polish intelligentsia, from the humanities and technology, and, perhaps above all, to the intellectual vanguard of our nation.''

Next, Deputy Premier Rakowski spoke about the first post-August months when he could afford the luxury of being merely a witness and observer of the authorities' activity. ''My privileged position ended in February 1981,'' we read futher, ''when I accepted the general's proposal to assume the function of Deputy Premier in his Government.

''I was one of the co-authors of the agreement on a political system which would reconcile the diverse interests of Polish society, which would create conditions for building better socialism and a better Poland, a Poland that enjoys international prestige both in the East and West.

''I believed that my work in the Government,'' Deputy Premier Rakowski continued, ''would enable me to develop efficient action aimed at transforming those socioeconomic structures that were out of date. I was convinced that it would be possible to find a platform for cooperation between the authorities and N.S.Z.Z. Solidarity that would be profitable for the country. I must say quite frankly that while I joined the Government with such intentions, I was really convinced that it would be possible to create this partnership, that I would gain support from the people who more or less determine the state of social awareness.

''Now I must admit frankly, after these 10 months of participating in the Government, that there has only been a cup of bitterness on my ministerial desk and nothing else. The source of this bitterness was the fact that during all this period I was almost completely alone, that much too rarely did I enjoy the support of a few of those who marched in the vanguard of t he front of renew al.''

Referring to the discussion that was going on, Deputy Premier Rakowski said: ''My friend Professor Markiewicz has said here that we should not demand conformist attitudes from scientists. Well, through the whole 10 months no one demanded conformity from scientists, and that is not what we expected from the creative milieu. Not once did the general demand that. None of us did . The only thing we demanded was understan ding for the requirements of the Polish raison d'etat.
