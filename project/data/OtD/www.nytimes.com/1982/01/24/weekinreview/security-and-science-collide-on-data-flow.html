SECURITY AND SCIENCE COLLIDE ON DATA FLOW

By PHILIP M. BOFFEY
Published: January 24, 1982

The exchange of ideas between nations is not easily controlled through export fees and licenses. So, while the Reagan Administration is raising the alarm over the flow of America's advanced scientific information to the Soviet Union, it is not clear how the Administration can stop it, or that it merits stopping. Indeed, many American scientists consider attempts to curb scientific interchange unpardonable, and dangerous, censorship.

Administration officials have been arguing that unclassified sources, including scientific journals and meetings, exchanges of students and scholars, and Government depositories of technical data, are gold mines of information with potential military importance for the Soviet Union. Thus, last week the State Department p l aced Stanford University off-limits to a visiting Sovie tscientist, Nikolay Umnov, when the university said it coul d not enforce restrictions on his movements and access to inform ation as required by the Government.

Other Government voices are becoming more insistent. Speaking at the annual meeting of the American Association for the Advancement of Science (A.A.A.S.) earlier this month, Adm. Bobby R. Inman, Deputy Director of the Central Intelligence Agency, warned: ''In terms of harm to the national interest, it makes little difference whether the data are copied from technical journals in a library or given away by a member of our society to an agent of a foreign power.'' Frank C. Carlucci, Deputy Secretary of Defense, warned the association that Russia is exploiting scientific communications and exchanges in an ''orchestrated effort'' aimed at ''gathering the technical information required to enhance its military posture.''

Such statements suggest that what Admiral Inman called a ''confrontation between national security and science'' is at hand. If so, it will be a conflict in which battle lines are not easily drawn. Restricting trade in high technology goods alone is a thorny problem, raising questions of whether export prohibitions will do more harm to Western economies or to the Soviet Union. Such issues took center stage last week when the United States urged its allies, represented in Paris at the Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls, to increase the types of high technology equipment banned for export to the Communist world, including items needed by the Soviets to build their natural gas pipeline from Siberia to Western Europe.

A comparable effort to block the flow of ideas and data raises not only questions of practicality and national self-interest, but strikes at the very structure of scientific communication and, in the United States, involves constitutional issues of free speech.

Interestingly, the extent to which scientific communication can threaten national security has never been established. In a letter to the A.A.A.S., for example, Mr. Carlucci pointed to the potential for harm in scientific exchanges, but gave few examples of actual damage. He noted that American scientific articles are published in journals for all the world to read, whereas Soviet articles are subject to censorship before publication. He also complained that the United States sends humanities students to Russia on academic exchange, while the Russians send over physicists whose work is of potential military value.

The most dramatic information leak Mr. Carlucci cited, however, occurred in the late 1970's and involved Russian scientists who studied fuel-air explosives and an advanced aerodynamic vehicle at American universities, and then, he said, pursued similar studies on returning home. There was also the case of a Hungarian researcher who reportedly gave the Soviet Union information on magnetic bubble memories, important in advanced computer designs, gleaned from visits to American laboratories.

Many American scientists doubt that the information leak is quite the hemorrhage portrayed by defense and intelligence officials. They point out that the Federal Government has restricted scientific exchanges in the past -in 1980, for example, the Carter Administration denied visas to Soviet bloc scientists who wanted to attend conferences on bubble memories or lasers - and will do so in the future to express disapproval of Soviet diplomatic or military maneuvers. Federal regulations already prohibit disclosure of technological data that have direct military application. ''Any suggestion that universities are behaving irresponsibly or are a source of leakage of scientific or high-technology information is a distortion of the facts,'' said Marvin L. Goldberger, president of the California Institute of Technology.

Of Benefits and Beneficiaries

Frank Press, president of the National Academy of Sciences, disputes the notion that the Soviet Union gains disproportionately from bilateral exchanges. ''I couldn't get American scientists to waste their time educating a Soviet counterpart,'' Dr. Press said. ''So by and large, we cooperate only in areas where we are equal or almost equal. And these are not areas involving classified material.'' In a letter to Mr. Carlucci, William D. Carey, executive officer of the A.A.A.S., said the United States has much to gain through bilateral exchanges, since American scientists can in this way keep posted on ''Soviet excellence in fields that matter to us, including metallurgy, condensed matter physics, theoretical physics, astrophysics, geophysics and cancer research.''

More important, many observers argue that the Soviet Union's main sources of Western scientific data are articles in journals and trade publications. And the dissemination of these materials, they note, would be extraordinarily difficult to restrict without infringing free speech. The National Security Agency and academic mathematicians have launched an experimental program of voluntary restraints in the field of cryptography. The small community of specialists in this field submit their papers to the agency for comment before publication. There is an appeals procedure, with the final decisio n on whether to disseminate a paper remaining with the scientist. Th us far, some 25 papers have been reviewed. Bu t the system has not really been put to the test, since no paper has been deemed a security problem.

Admiral Inman believes that a similar voluntary system might work in fields such as computers and other electronic gear, lasers, crop projections and manufacturing procedures, where, he suggests, potential harm to national security should be examined from the moment a scientific undertaking is considered. Admiral Inman has urged the research community to devise remedies before Congress imposes legislative restrictions.

The nation's leading scientists, however, doubt the need for or legality of mandatory restrictions on unclassified information. ''If you want to protect information, you have to classify it,'' says Dr. Press. ''If you try to restrict it in other ways, it's probably unconstitutional.''
