Connecticut Guide; LECTURES ON ISLAM

By Eleanor Charles
Published: January 24, 1982

A series of eight weekly lectures dealing with specific aspects of Moslem culture will begin at 7:30 P.M. Thursday at the Hartford Seminary, 77 Sherman Street. Supported by a grant from the Connecticut Humanities Council, the talks are open free to the public. They represent an effort ''to create better understanding of the Moslem world,'' according to Dr. Byron L. Haines, chairman of a study group on Chr istian-Moslem Relations of the National Council of Churches.

''We are not interested in pushing Islam as a religion, nor in offering solutions to the problems of the Middle East,'' Dr. Haines emphasized, ''but we are trying to eliminate prejudice.''

Beginning in 1890, when the Duncan Black Macdonald Center for Islamic studies was founded there, the Hartford Seminary has been ''one of the foremost centers in this field in the United States,'' Dr. Haines said. He is associated with the institution in the division of continuing education.

''Too often,'' Dr. Haines said, ''the Islamic world has been represented by the stereotype of the Arab Bedouin, and Moslems are identified by what we perceive to be backward, ignorant and uncivilized attitudes.''

''We are very conscious of the world situation, and we have tried to bend over backwards to be faithful to the Connecticut Humanities Council mandate,'' Dr. Haines continued. In that regard, he said many Jewish leaders in the area were taking responsibility for organizing the series. Dr. Peter Breit of the University of Hartford and Dr. Stephen Trachtenberg, president of the university, are chairmen of the project.

Leaders of the project are negotiating with Syracuse University Press for publication of the material at the conclusion of the series, anticipating its use as a sccial-studies textbook in secondary schools and by the general public.

The first lecture, by Dr. William A. Bijlefeld, professor of Islamic studies at the Hartford Seminary, is titled ''On Being Moslem: The Faith Dimension of Moslem Identity.''

Subsequent programs on Islamic education, science and art, the condition of women in the Moslem world, Moslems in the United States and elsewhere, and political and ecomomic aspects of Islam will be addressed by professors from Harvard and Princeton Universities, the State University of New York and Temple University.

A trip to the Islamic exhibition at the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York will be conducted Feb. 18 at cost. For reservations call Dr. Ellison Findly, 527-3151, extension 474. For more information on the series, call 232-4451. ART EXHIBITIONS

The Yale Center for British Art, at 1080 Chapel Street, New Haven, is featuring a collection of antique containers for those ''very serious cookies'' advertised on television by Peek, Frean & Co. Wednesday through March 7.

Huntley & Palmers, Carr's and other brands are also represented in this ''British Biscuit Tins'' show, which ranges from 1868 designs to tins commemorating the coronation of King George VI in 1937.

Biscuits, a staple of the British diet for centuries, enjoyed great popularity among seafarers in the 1700's. Later they became a favorite snack on railway trips and at tea-time to stave off hunger pangs while waiting for fashionably late dinners.

As packaging and production methods improved, so did printing processes, enabling manufacturers to offer illustrations from ''Ivanhoe,'' reproductions of Turner paintings, scenes of historical significance and, in later years, adaptations of Art Nouveau and Art Deco designs. Many boxes were made in the shape of soldiers, telephones and cameras, to be used as toys after the biscuits had been consumed.

At the Yale Art Gallery across the street, two unusual exhibitions go on display Thursday. ''German Drawings of the 60's'' is the first such show to be seen in the United States, consisting of 130 works by 11 artists from the cultural centers of Berlin and Dusseldorf.

Efforts to evolve a new Germanic style may be seen in examples of Expressionism, pop and conceptual art. Some of the pieces are heavily tinged with social and political overtones, particularly those by artists who grew up in East Germany and emigrated to the West. The show will remain until March 28.

A second exhibition opening Thursday and running until Aug. 30 is a collection of 30 stone sculptures from Hindu or Buddhist temples, carved between the 3d and the 13th centuries.

There is no admission charge at either museum and hours are identical: 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesdays through Saturdays, 2 to 5 P.M. Sundays. RENAISSANCE DANCES

The Art of Courtly Dancing in the Renaissance will be the subject of a progam at the Yale Art Gallery Saturday. Dr. Ingrid Brainard, director of the Cambridge Court Dancers, in Massachusetts, will give a slide talk at 2:30 P.M. and two workshops at 11:30 A.M. and 3:30 P.M.

Paintings of the 15th and 16th centuries will tra ce the differences b etween Flemish, Burgundian, Elizabethan, Italian and Shakespearean styles in dance and dress.

''No experience is necessary for the workshops,'' Dr. Brainard said, ''but soft-soled ballet or at-home shoes should be worn. Sneakers are too heavy and they don't bend.'' Dr. Brainard is expected to bring one or more costumes reproduced from paintings of the period.

Among the workshop dances are the Pavane, Gailiarde, English country dances and the French Branle. ''All of them were written down in dance treatises from 1430 on, she noted, ''in a shorthand of letters, or line notations.''

She researched the subject extensively for her dissertation at the University of Gottingen in West Germany. One of the first individuals to produce a scholarly work on early dance, in the 1950's, Dr. Brainard believes that ''the United States is 30 years ahead of Europe in dance scholarship. It's a serious field here.''

There is no charge for the program. BARCZYK CONCERT

Cecylia Barczyk, the 31-year old Polish cellist who has been publicizing on television talk shows her worldwide concert tour to raise money to help the Polish people, will give a performance at 8:15 P.M. Saturday in New Milford High School.

The tour will begin at Carnegie Hall in New York on Feb. 5, when Miss Barczyk will be joined by Lorin Hollander and Ward Davenny, pianists; Aldo Parisot, who was her teacher at the Yale School of Music; Roman Totenberg, violinist, and a cello ensemble from Yale.

Miss Barczyk is seeking political asylum in the United States. She recently formed a committee for human rights and democracy in Poland among musicians and university faculty members.

Saturday's concert will include a performance of the Haydn Concerto with the Connecticut Chamber Orchestra under the direction of Sayard Stone. Nonsubscription tickets will be available at the door. For more information call 354-5593. Eleanor Charles
