CONNECTICUT GUIDE; PHOTOS OF CONNECTICUT

By Eleanor Charles
Published: September 12, 1982

More than 200 historic and contemporary photographs of Connecticut have been brought together for an exhibition illustrating the state's architectural and topographical variety and the evolution of some of its urban and rural locations. Titled ''Vital Signs - Connecticut,'' it may be seen Friday through Nov. 15 at the Old State House, 800 Main Street, Hartford.

Locations singled out for special attention are downtown New Haven for its historic architecture; Frog Hollow in Hartford as an example of an urban residential neighborhood; Greenwich for its suburban juxtaposition of country lanes and corporate monoliths; New London, Essex and Stonington representing various types of maritime communities; and Litchfield and Windham Counties for their changing rural landscapes.

Historical photographs from private and public collections are combined with the recent work of Tom Zetterstrom, Harriet Gans and Robert Maass. Pictured are mills, mansions, railroad stations, churches, barns, docks, skyscrapers and people, among other things.

The exhibition was produced by the Connecticut Trust for Historic Preservation with financing from the Connecticut Humanities Council. Admission is free. Hours are 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. daily, noon to 5 P.M. Sunday. BEEKEEPING SCHOOL

Of the 200,000 hobbyist beekeepers in the United States at least 1,700 are registered in Connecticut, according to Dr. Larry Conner, an agriculturist and entomologist who will conduct a beekeeping school for beginning and active beekeepers for six weeks beginning Thursday. The classes will be held from 7 to 9 P.M. weekly until Oct. 21 at the Cooperative Extension Service of the University of Connecticut, Route 6, Stony Hill, Bethel.

Dr. Connor estimates the cost of starting a colony at between $100 and $200. Expenditures include wooden hive equipment, protective clothing, wax and a few books and implements. During peak season in June a good colony will consist of 40,000 to 60,000 worker bees, 1,000 drones and one queen. A colony may produce 100 pounds of honey in a year when rain and sun have been favorable for nectar-secreting plants.

''The motivation for most people to become beekeepers is to pollinate their fruit trees. Others use no sweetener but honey for eating, canning and cooking,'' said Dr. Conner. ''It's much like any other pet-type activity. I've known people who talk to their bees, although I suspect that does them more good than it does the bees.''

''All you are doing is providing a better home than the bees are likely to find in the wild. It's important to remember that they are wild animals and approach them with respect.'' He noted that some stocks may be selected for gentleness.

''They will drink from the dog dish or birdbath when there is no other water available. A leaky hose is good. And they must be fed sugar in fall or spring when there is little natural food around. In winter they breed and raise new bees inside the hive.''

A $50 fee covers all materials and text books, and spouses may take the course for half-price. Information: 797-4176. WILLIAM BLAKE SHOW

Not since 1939 when the Philadelphia Art Museum presented an exhibition of William Blake's paintings has the English fantasist's work been seen in a major exhibition in this country.

The Yale Center for British Art will put 250 of Blake's drawings, prints, watercolors and illustrated volumes on display Wednesday through Nov. 14. Also included in the exhibition are some works by his contemporaries: Richard Brother, Henry Fuselli, William Sharp and Samuel Palmer, for purposes of comparison and to set a perspective for the times.

Drawn from the center's collection and the private collection of the center's founder and benefactor, Paul Mellon, the show also includes pieces on loan from the National Gallery of Art in Washington, the Pierpont Morgan Library, the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston, the Library of Congress, the Philadelphia Art Museum, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the British Museum and the Tate Gallery in London.

Among them are 20 of Blake's watercolor and tempera illustrations for the Bible and designs for the works of Milton, Gray, Young and Dante. A gallery talk will be held at 2 P.M. Thursday. Admission to the center, 1080 Chapel Street, is free. Hours are 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday, 2 to 5 P.M. Sunday. Information: 432-4594. NUCLEAR LECTURES

A series of free lectures dealing with ''Human Conscience in the Nuclear Age'' will be given Monday through Thursday in the Campus Center Oak Room and Loyola Chapel of Fairfield University, North Benson Road, Fairfield. Nuclear weapons and disarmament are the subjects at noon and 4 P.M. tomorrow; the economics of the arms race and how it affects the poor are the topics at noon and 4 P.M. Tuesday, with an additional talk on the biomedical effects of nuclear war at 7:30 P.M. Nuclear war and the gospel, civil disobedience, religious cults, the church and disarmament are among other issues to be explored in the remaining days.

Speakers have been gathered from Cornell University, Mount Sinai Hospital in New York, Georgetown University, members of the clergy and representatives of various organizations advocating disarmament. Information: 255-5411. RUSSIAN FILM FESTIVAL

In conjunction with an exhibition of paintings and sculpture by two Russian emigre artists, the Jewish Community Center, 335 Bloomfield Avenue, Hartford, will present a Russian film festival beginning at 4 P.M. today with ''The Shooting Party,'' a recent adaptation of a Chekhov story.

''Unfinished Piece for Player Piano,'' to be shown Oct. 24, was released in New York City last spring. ''The Twelve Chairs'' will be shown Oct. 3, ''Anna Karenina'' Nov. 7 and ''Crime and Punishment'' Dec. 12. Tickets are $3 at the door, or $12 for the series.

The works on exhibition are by Boris Landa, a Leningrad painter who came to this country in 1975, and Leonid Sokov, a sculptor who has been in the United States since 1980. The gallery is open from 9 A.M. to 9 P.M. daily except Saturday, 9 A.M. to 4 P.M. Friday. Admission is free. Information: 236-4571. Eleanor Charles
