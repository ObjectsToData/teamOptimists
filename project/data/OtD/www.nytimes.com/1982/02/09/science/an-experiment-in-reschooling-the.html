AN EXPERIMENT IN RESCHOOLING THE

Published: February 9, 1982

SCHOLAR By GENE I. MAEROFF

DR. CLARA ALEXANDER, an English professor at Medgar Evers College in Queens, part of the City University of New York, is studying computer science, a field in which enrollments are booming while majors in her own discipline are becoming an endangered species.

She is one of about two dozen faculty members, mostly from areas of low enrollment, learning the rudiments of some of the most popular fields in higher education - computer science, data processing and basic writing - so that they can teach introductory courses in those fields.

''I don't expect to move out of English totally, but I am not averse to change,'' said Dr. Alexander, who has tenure and is 52 years old. ''I believe in growing and I don't want to develop a civil service mentality.''

The City University is hoping other professors will show the same interest in expanding their usefulness as Dr. Alexander. The new faculty development program in which she is participating is a $60,000 pilot project designed to enable people to work in more than one field.

Mary Rothlein, associate dean for faculty on the headquarters staff of City University, the institution did not pretend to transform people into instant experts in new fields. But, she said, it was reasonable to expect that ''those who have already mastered the knowledge'' in one area can learn enough fairly quickly to teach introductory courses in another field.

Various colleges within the City University, acting on their own, have already begun programs to retrain faculty members. At Brooklyn College, for instance, 13 faculty members from other departments teach introductory courses in computer science this year, after taking summer courses to prepare for the assignments.

Colleges and universities around the country are faced with the problem of having to shift faculty members from fields of low student demand to areas of greater demand, About 65 percent of the nation's faculty members are tenured, according to the American Association of University Professors. These people are essentially frozen into their jobs for as long as they choose and many are in literature, foreign languages, history and o ther disciplines in which the interest of students is l agging.

In the City University, for example, tenured faculty members account for more than 95 percent of City College's history department, 94 of Brooklyn College's philosophy department and 89 percent of Hunter College's Romance language department.

Trying to persuade professors to shift to new fields with surging enrollments has been one response to the mismatched distribution, but most institutions have had limited success in encouraging such changes.

Furthermore, in spite of claims by the City University, some scholars question whether a few courses can adequately train a faculty member to teach in a discipline for which others have been preparing for a lifetime.

''It has been difficult to move faculty members, especially at the later stages of their careers, from one part of the house to another,'' said Thomas M. Stauffer, a vice president of the American Council on Education. ''It is a fine idea, but it doesn't seem to work very well because most faculty members have concentrated their teaching and research in one area and with their overspecialization it is hard to divert them elsewhere.''

But if the humanities are the most awkwardly overtenured departments, will they respond proportionately to proposals for a cademic retraining? Irwin Polishook, preside nt of the faculty union at the City University, said: ''It may be the thing is too new. I believe we will eventually get people from al l disciplines. It takes a while for the program to build.''

City University is deliberately starting its faculty development program with volunteers who are successful and secure. All are tenured, many with lists of distinguished accomplishments to their names, and none are threatened with dismissal as a result of the dwindling enrollments in their fields.

Dr. Alexander, who earned her doctorate in the teaching of reading at the University of Massachusetts, is coordinator of the English program at Medgar Evers and acting chairman of the humanities division.

Officials felt the project would be seen most favorably if professors were not participating out of desperation, and the chance to gain new knowledge to apply in their own fields is apparently an attractive feature of the program.

Raoul Camus, a music professor at Queensborough Community College, for example, has been involved in a computer project in which he has been helping to categorize 38,000 American tunes from the 18th century.

''I admit there is some self-interest here because I see this helping me in my musical scholarship,'' the 51-year-old music professor said. ''But the college is also having trouble finding enough people to teach introductory computer science and I would like to help in that way, too.''

Nevertheless, Mr. Camus, a widely recognized expert on American music, remains somewhat uneasy about the program. ''The title of 'faculty development' makes me a little upset because I don't want my participation to imply I'm a failure in my field or that I'm turning my back on music,'' said Mr. Camus, who is conductor of the Queensborough Symphony Band, jointly sponsored by the community and the college.

Another participant, Joan Sayre, a professor of psychiatric nursing at Hunter College for 17 years, will be learning how to teach basic writing. She says she thinks the experience will make her a more valuable member of the faculty.

She is already teaching a course in expository writing to nursing students, using topics related to health. She has not received any formal training to teach the course, however, and that is what she hopes to get from the faculty development program.

Those participating in the program, which began last week and will last for one year, have been given one less course to teach. They are meeting weekly for two and a half hours in seminars taught by fellow City University professors.

''We would hope that a year from now,'' said Dr. Rothlein, ''those who have participated in the program will begin teaching one or more courses in their new discipline.''
