SPOKEN WORDS TRACE PATH OF HISTORY

By DIANE COX
Published: July 4, 1982

IN a tape-recorded account of his 45-year career as a machinist, Michel Bilger of Watertown describes his lengthy apprenticeship in the 1930's at the Peugeot factory in France. It was a huge, 19thcentury workplace with machinery run by overhead belts and pulleys, a place where apprentices began by mastering a file, a hammer and a chisel.

In the 40 years that followed, Mr. Bilger, who now works on the latest in automated machinery, has been a part of the transformation of industry from the age of craft to the age of high technology. His account of what those changes meant to him and of how he adapted his skills to them offers an invaluable view of labor, industry and technology in the 20th century from a point of view sometimes overlooked: that of the worker himself.

Mr. Bilger's observations might not have gone any further than his own family had it not been for the University of Connecticut's Center for Oral History. The center has set out to preserve the history of those people who do not usually leave written records behind, and to fit those individual experiences into the larger picture of the history of Connecticut.

Begun in the late 60's as the Oral History Project, the center was originally charged with recording the memories of prominent citizens of the state, usually political figures, and with recording the university's history.

While interviews with politicians and members of the university's faculty and administration are still part of the center's work, the arrival of Dr. Bruce M. Stave as the center's director in 1979 turned it toward examination of groups of citizens with common experiences.

Dr. Stave points out that this is the direction that the study of social history itself has taken in the last decade, and it parallels his own work as a professor of urban history.

''With prominent people, an oral history interview is filling in gaps of what is already known about them, which is considerable,'' he explains. ''With unknowns, you are getting the whole story, probably the only time it will be heard.''

The center's most ambitious project to date, ''Connecticut Workers and Changing Technology, 1930-1980,'' is a two-year study financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities. Interviewers have talked to 150 workers, some of whom were embittered and frightened by the erosion of job skills and loss of jobs in traditional manufacturing industries, some of whom were excited to be part of the burgeoning high technologies and many of whom were reasonably content with their lot.

When completed, it is hoped that the project will have produced an extensive chronicle of the working life of Connecticut in the 1980's; a book, written by the project co-director, Robert Asher; a series of radio programs; several articles and a pamphlet.

One of those interviewed was John J. Driscoll, president of the Connecticut State Labor Council, A.F.L.-C.I.O, who praises the project as ''a very worthwhile investigation into a subject which has perhaps been too long neglected by researchers.''

Others interviewed for the project have ranged from workers at high-technology concerns such as Pratt & Whitney to workers at concerns whose techniques have varied very little over the last century, such as the Deep River Lace Factory in Deep River.

Other center projects have included studies of the state's many ethnic groups, the first generation of fully enfranchised Connecticut women, Holocaust survivors, the millworkers in Willimantic and the unsuccessful attempt to build a new town in Coventry.

Two others are on female graduates of the university and the practice of history by historians who do not have academic appointments. Still in the planning stages is an examination of Connecticut residents who were part of the W.P.A. artists' project and another on the effect of the Bill of Rights on various groups of citizens.

Choosing which projects can be done effectively is a major part of Dr. Stave's work. He finds himself on the receiving end of a stream of phone calls and visitors with ideas about what to do next, most of them predicated on the imminent death of an elderly citizen who is invariably described as ''fascinating.''

Though a sympathetic and patient listener and a ready dispenser of advice, the 45-year-old Dr. Stave is on the lookout for projects, whether large or small, that have a specific focus on some aspect of the history or development of Connecticut.

Other factors have to be considered, too, such as the commitment of other parties involved. Since Dr. Stave usually co-directs a project with the person who proposed it, the ability to stay on the course is an important one, especially since most projects are financed by grants with strict rules of accountability.

Since Dr. Stave is the center's only permanent staff member, he is dependent on grant money to provide the interviewers, transcribers and clerical help needed for each project.

''Oral history is labor-intensive and expensive,'' he says. When the grant ends, so do those jobs. As a remedy for the problem of turnover, the center, with the help of the university, has established a professional transcribing service, Tapescribe, which is managed by Sharon Youland. Dr. Stave hopes it will become selfsupporting and a permanent adjunct of the center.

Just as important as the transcribing of tapes is the interviewing itself, which again means new people on each project. Since good interviews are often determined by the interviewer rather than the interviewee, a certain amount of training is necessary before sending them out into the field.

Dr. Stave believes that an interview is an agreement between two parties with the subject matter known in advance, no hidden microphones, no trick questions and a non-adversary approach. Interviewers must familiarize themselves in advance with information about the project, formulate specific questions and stick to the subject at hand. He particularly warns about wandering far afield and avoiding the generalized ''vacuum cleaner'' approach for which oral history interviews are sometimes criticized.

Interviewers must also be prepared to deal with the emotional effects of memories that may have been repressed for a long time, as in the case of a successful doctor, interviewed for the study of the state's ethnic groups, who became distraught when describing the anti-Semitism he had encountered at a major university in the United States.

Tapes and transcriptions of oral history interviews will soon be available in the archives of the university's library. The work of the center and the archives often dovetail. Two former members of Congress from Connecticut - Chase Going Woodhouse and Robert Giaimo - have donated their papers and photographs to the archives and both have been subjects of lengthy interviews, Mrs. Woodhouse as part of the study of newly enfranchised women and Mr. Giaimo in an interview with the former director of the oral history project, Morton Tenver.

The use of archives to complement taped interviews illustrates a principle of Dr. Stave: that oral history does not stand alone and should be used in conjunction with written material to get the most accurate picture possible. He finds that memory is often the ''fond deceiver,'' as Oliver Goldsmith called it, and needs a corrective from other sources.
