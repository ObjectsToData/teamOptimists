GOING OUT GUIDE

By Richard F. Shepard
Published: May 8, 1982

ON VIEW

The New York Public Library has such vast collections that it need only reach into its pockets to pull out an exhibition that will make something new out of some things old. The evidence of this is hanging on the walls as a new show in the Stokes Gallery, in the corridor of the third floor of the main building at 42d Street and Fifth Avenue.

''Melville, Whitman and New York'' is a delightful combination of word and picture that gives a fresh look at our city in the 1800's. Herman Melville and Walt Whitman lived in town at the same time, although they never met each other, which is not unusual in New York life. However, much of their work is stored in the library for the benefit of latter-day scholars. And, of course, the library has limitless pictures of every kind on every topic.

What you can see on the walls are the words of the writers as captions to bird's-eye views, prints and photographs, scenes of Manhattan Island, of the streets and people and vistas unique to it.

Among items under glass to be seen are Melville's manuscript of ''Hawthorne and His Mosses'' and a leaf from ''Typee,'' as well as first editions of his novels. Also Whitman's own copies of editions of ''Leaves of Grass,'' and of his first and only novel, ''Franklin Evans or the Inebriate,'' a temperance best-seller that he later deprecated.

The exhibition is presented to celebrate the start of the Library of America, a new publishing venture of the library financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Ford Foundation that will feature collected works of prominent American authors in hardcover editions.

Open free from 10 A.M. to 9 P.M. Mondays through Wednesdays, from 10 to 6 Fridays and Saturdays. Information: 930-0720 (but not Saturdays). ART ON THE ROCKS

There is a six-foot sculpture of a person, in ice, reposing in a refrigerator at the Waldorf-Astoria, but it is only in temporary residence and not designed as a centerpiece for a wedding party. This evening it will be moved to the Center for Inter-American Relations, 680 Park Avenue, at East 68th Street (249-8950), where, as a work of art, it is the centerpiece for a 20-hour art show starting at 8 P.M.

The piece was made by Leopoldo Maler, an Argentine artist, who is also represented at the center by such works as manikin heads floating in two liquid-filled bins and a wash basin on a pedestal approached by a red carpet. In the exhibition are other items by Regina Vater of Brazil and Jonier Marin of Colombia, all part of the center's First International Performance Cycle.

About that piece of ice and the 20 hours: the artist hasn't quite said what will happen during this time, but the smart money believes that the heart of the artistic event will consist of watching it melt. The center advises you to bring sleeping bags and pillows. Light refreshments will be served.

Admission is free, as it is to the exhibition on regular days, through May 16, from noon to 6 P.M. daily except Monday. TWO-TONE

Two works by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, each reflecting a different mood and a different stage of his career, will be performed tonight at 8 o'clock by the 160-voice Collegiate Chorale at Carnegie Hall, 57th Street and Seventh Avenue (247-7459).

One, ''Vesperae Dolennes de Confessore,'' was written, very buoyantly, in 1780, when the composer was 25 years old. ''Requiem'' came 11 years later, a moving somber work.

The first piece has a lyrical aria for soprano and chorus and will be sung by Hei Kyung Hong, born in Korea, a graduate of Juilliard and a winner of the 1982 Metropolitan Opera auditions. Other singers on the program are Philip Creech, Metropolitan Opera tenor; Alteouise DeVaughn, mezzo-soprano; and Arthur Woodley, bass.

Admission: $5 to $14. Richard F. Shepard
