WESTCHESTER GUIDE; GLASS ART EXHIBIT

By Eleanor Charles
Published: May 2, 1982

A comprehensive display of contemporary glass art may be seen through June 3 at the Westlake Gallery at 210 East Post Road in White Plains. The staff is well versed on the various techniques of fuming, etching, sandblasting, cutting, slumping, litticino, plateglass construction, electroplating, neon and the uses of blown glass.

More than 35 well-known artists in the 20-year-old American studio glass movement are exhibiting. Among them are David R. Huchthausen, Robert Hurlstone, Tom McGlaughlin, John Nygren, Paul Seide and Josh Simpson. At least 300 objects will be on view.

''The accent is on collectibility and the fine-art aspect,'' said Cathy Westlake, owner of the gallery, ''not on function.'' Sizes vary from one-and-a-half-inch paperweights to sculpture and neon pieces several feet long and wide. Prices range from $60 to more than $4,000.

''Most of these artists work in rural areas where the fire laws are less strict and the fuel costs are lower,'' Mrs. Westlake explained. A glass furnace is more expensive and more temperamental to operate than a clay kiln. The use of silver to make yellow and gold to make red is also a major investment. ''Some very sophisticated chemistry is required - the use of color is distinctly American,'' Mrs. Westlake said.

Many styles, from representational and abstract to pop and minimalist are represented. The gallery will be open from 1 P.M. to 5:30 today but regular hours are 9 A.M. to 5:30 P.M. daily except Sunday. For more information, call 682-8123. FLOWER-MARKET SALE

A visit to the New York Botanical Garden in the Bronx for the annual flower-market sale is an ideal way to spend some of the Mother's Day weekend. The event takes place from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Friday through next Sunday under a large yellow and white striped tent on the lawn.

In addition, a stroll through 11 glass-domed pavilions in the nearby conservatory offers an opportunity to see thousands of unusual plants from many climates and corners of the world.

Modest prices will prevail at the flower market for hanging baskets, many varieties of geraniums, cactuses and succulents, African violets, multiple orchids on stems, bromeliads, vegetables and perennials, all grown in the Botanical Garden greenhouses or in the private greenhouses of Botanical Garden Council members. Experts will be on hand with instructions for care.

There is a $2.50 charge for parking on the grounds, but some street parking is available free. Admission to the conservatory is $2.50; children, students and the elderly are charged 75 cents. One free admission to the conservatory is a bonus for paid parking.

For more details, call 220-8700. CLIMATE-ORIENTED

A solar house and earth-integrated greenhouse designed by Jeff Vreeland and featured in five pages of the summer-fall issue of Better Homes and Gardens is one of six climate-oriented homes in a tour that will be continuous from 10 to 4 today. Operated by The Katonah Gallery, the tour includes innovative homes in Cross River, South Salem, North Salem, Pound Ridge, Katonah and Mount Kisco, all within a few minutes of the gallery. An introductory talk will be given at 10 A.M. by John Milnes Baker, an architect.

Among the features of the various houses are a computerized greenhouse, earth berming or a build-up of earth for insulation, a woodburning furnace, a freestanding fireplace based on an 18th-century design, a wind-deflecting 12-sided house built into a hill and a wide range of fireplace heatilators and solar-panel installations.

The tour is an adjunct of the current gallery exhibition, ''Shelter: Models of Native Ingenuity,'' and it costs $17. A map is included and the six homeowners will be available to answer questions. Call 232-4988 or 232-4343 for more information. MOTHERS/DAUGHTERS

''Women often complain about relationships with their mothers,'' said Miriam Lubow of Wainwright House at 260 Stuyvesant Avenue in Rye. ''It's important for them to work out those relationships in order for them to be responsive to other women.''

This subject will be explored in a workshop called ''Mothers and Daughters: Taking the First Step,'' to be held from 7:30 to 10 P.M. Friday and 9:30 A.M. to 4:30 P.M. Saturday at a cost of $45 a person. Mothers and daughters are encouraged to participate together, but it is not necessary. Women must be 17 of age or older to enroll.

The workshop will deal with such topics as dialogues, journal keeping, imagery, music, movement, sharing and play - all relating to issues of unfinished business between mothers and daughters. It will be conducted two psychotherapists, a mother and her daughter. Julie Firman practices privately in New York City and is a member of the International Transactional Analysis Association in Manhattan and the Foundation for Religion and Mental Health in Briarcliff. Her daughter, Dorothy, is director of the Synthesis Center in Amherst, Mass.

To register or to obtain more information, call 967-6080. TALK ON CHINESE LIFE

Fox Butterfield, chief of the New York Times Peking bureau from 1979 to 1981 will speak on ''Life in China Today: Surviving the Revolution,'' at 4 P.M. tomorrow in the library of Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers.

The lecture is part of a yearlong seminar on international relations sponsored by the National Endowment for the Humanities, and it is free to the public.

Mr. Butterfield is a graduate of Harvard University who spent three years in Taiwan on Fulbright and Ford Foundation fellowships in the 1960's. He is co-author of ''American Missionaries in China'' and now heads the Boston bureau of The Times.

Call 337-0700, extension 412 for more information. WORKING WITH CLAY

Throughout this month the Mid-Westchester Y.M.-Y.W.H.A. will hold its third yearly Clay Arts Festival consisting of three one-day lecture-demonstrations and two three-day workshops by noted artists in the field, beginning today with Elsbeth Woody, a sculptor known for large-scale works.

A workshop in traditional Japanese techniques will be held by Makoto Yabe May 12 through 14, and Robert Sedestrom will lead a moldmaking workshop May 24 through 26. Marilyn Fox and Dale Zheutlin are the remaining lecturers May 16 and 23.

Fees are $25 for lecture-demonstrations and $55 for workshops, all held from 10:30 A.M. to 4:30 P.M. in the ''Y'' at 999 Wilmot Road in Scarsdale. Phone 472-330 for information and registration. Eleanor Charles
