PLEAS MADE TO CONGRESS FOR FUNDS FOR THE ARTS

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY, Special to the New York Times
Published: May 22, 1982

WASHINGTON, May 21— Performing artists and their allies made their annual trek up Capitol Hill today to plead with Congress for more Federal funds for the arts, their mission more urgent this year in view of the Reagan Administration's efforts to hold the line on almost all spending.

''I feel the Federal Government should really be sold on this,'' said Itzhak Perlman, the violinist. ''Why do we have to bargain? The arts are part of the fiber of American society and should get Federal support.''

Rise Stevens, the former Metropolitan Opera singer, recalled that she had testified at Congressional hearings in 1965, when the National Endowment for the Arts was established. She said, ''It's rather disturbing to hear the same arguments today'' from the opponents of Federal subsidies for the arts.

''If the endowment pulls back, all the arts will suffer - and it is going to be like dominoes, with each one falling by the wayside,'' Miss Stevens added. Project of Former Prisoners

Colleen Dewhurst, the actress, told of endowment support for a project that allowed former prison inmates in New York to find an outlet that had previously been closed to them and, finally, to succeed in a play performed in a Lower East Side theater.

''Theater is the heart and soul of what the country is,'' Miss Dewhurst said. ''Cuts in Federal funds will end the chance for writers to try and fail, for actors to try and fail, for directors to try and fail. Art is entering the arena when you do not know the result.''

The witnesses, however, were preaching to a congregation of the already converted, the House of Representatives Appropriations Subcommittee on the Interior, whose chairman, Representative Sidney R. Yates, keeps putting more money in the arts budgets than the Administration wants to spend.

The case for reduced spending was made at another hearing of Mr. Yates's subcommittee this week by Frank S.M. Hodsoll, chairman of the endowment. Reductions Are Minimized

Mr. Hodsoll told the panel that the requested cuts were not as drastic as they may have appeared because, he said, Government financing accounts for 5 percent of all financing for the arts.

The reception accorded Mr. Hodsoll then was not as cordial as that given today to the performers. Mr. Yates, a Democrat of Illinois, began the hearing by chiding Mr. Perlman over Mr. Yates's inability to find a recording by Mr. Perlman of the Beethoven violin sonata in F after having heard Mr. Perlman perform it on television.

The violinist responded that it should be available because he had made such a recording 10 years ago. ''It's on London-Decca, which will get a telephone call from me tomorrow,'' Mr. Perlman told the chairman. Praise for Miss Dewhurst

Mr. Yates treated Miss Dewhurst similarly, declaring that she had made a ''beautiful statement'' after she asserted that the theater, which is dependent on public funds, represented the great art of America, much different from television, which she said assumed that it had an audience of 12-year-olds.

In a discussion of financing with Lucien Wulsin, chairman of the Colorado Council on the Arts and Humanities, Mr. Yates made clear that he would seek Congressional approval of more than the $100.8 million for the arts endowment sought for next year by the Reagan Administration, although it will be less than the $143 million in the current budget.

Shouting ''Bravo!'' when Miss Dewhurst finished testifying was the actor-folk singer Theodore Bikel, another witness, who was appearing as a member of the National Council on the Arts, the endowment's advisory board, and as president of Actors Equity.

If thes endowment's funds were cut, Mr. Bikel said, ''the established places will survive, but the marginal won't - the Off Broadway, Off Off Broadway and regional theaters like those in Washington.''

This was so, he said, because, ''the private sector finances yesterday's successes rather than tomorrow's promise.''
