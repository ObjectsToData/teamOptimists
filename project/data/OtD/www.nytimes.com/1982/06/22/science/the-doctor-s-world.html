THE DOCTOR'S WORLD

LAWRENCE K. ALTMAN, M.D.
Published: June 22, 1982

THE scene: A medical school professor is lecturing to a virtually empty classroom and a spinning tape recorder. As the talk ends, one of the few students present retrieves the recorder and prepares to transcribe the lecture and to distribute copies to classmates who stayed away.

The story may seem apocryphal. But students at several American medical schools told me it does occur occasionally. Students, in these cases, believe they are studying more efficiently by cutting boring lectures, and using that time to read textbooks and transcripts of the lectures.

Dr. David E. Rogers describes the same phenomenom in the latest issue of The Pharos, a journal published by the Alpha Omega Alpha Honor Medical Society in Menlo Park, Calif. Medical students poured out their frustration in telling him about ''being lectured to death'' by a faculty that taught poorly and with whom they developed little personal contact.

Dr. Rogers, a former dean of Johns Hopkins Medical School who now heads the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation in Princeton, N.J., said he was not concerned about the caliber of medical students or faculty members. Both are good, he and many others said. Rather, they criticized the medical education system.

One of his concerns is that too many facts are being crammed down the throats of young doctors, a problem that is particularly vexing because medical facts change so rapidly. Moreover, learning to be a doctor requires personally structured time to mix with wise, older physicians. That, according to Dr. Rogers, is not being done enough.

Dr. Rogers proposed that medical school faculties, rather than taking punitive steps against the students, ''simply take the meat ax to the medical curriculum.'' To start, he would simply reduce the hours of lectures by 40 percent and let the young doctors decide how to use the free time.

Dr. Lewis Thomas, chancellor of Memorial-Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, has accused medical school admission committees of playing a major role in the de-emphasis of the humanities in premedical education. To correct the situation, he proposes a more traditional approach - courses in English, history, philosophy and the literature of foreign languages.

Dr. Thomas's point seems to have been underscored in a new report by Dr. David P. Yens and Dr. Barry Stimmel of the Mount Sinai School of Medicine in New York. Students who did not major in a science as undergraduates ''performed as well or better than traditional science trained students on almost all performance measures,'' they said in the June issue of the Journal of Medical Education.

Yet, the pressures of getting into medical school have had their effects on the colleges. One college president told me that cliques of premedical students have had a devastating impact on the rest of the student body. Many feel under so much pressure to do well in the sciences that the chief purpose of college for them seems to be, not to get a liberal education, but to get into medical school.

Dr. Carleton B. Chapman, of the Albert Einstein Medical College in the Bronx, has proposed a national commission to reform medical education because it ''is intellectually deficient, wasteful of money and time, and in urgent need of overhaul.''

Earlier this month, Dr. Daniel C. Tosteston, the dean of Harvard Medical School, joined the list of leading physicians who want to revamp the education of young physicians. Among his proposals are that medical schools place greater stress on the need for compassion and understanding in treating patients.

Each generation of physicians wants to leave its imprint on medical education, but only a few doctors have been able to do something about it. One, Dr. Thomas Wakley, founded the Lancet in London in 1823, in part to reform British medicine. At that time, medical students paid professors about five pounds a lecture for required courses. Dr. Wakley pirated those lectures, publishing the texts transcribed by medical students.

In 1910, a report by Abraham Flexner forced many American medical schools to close and to put the training of young doctors on a more scientific footing. Some of Dr. Flexner's glory has been tarnished in recent years amid criticism that medicine has overemphasized the science at the expense of the art of the profession. Some critics now look at medical school as more of a trade school than a source of a professional education.

These days, it seems that you cannot read a scientific journal without finding a serious critique of American medical education. Dr. Lester S. King, a former editor of the Journal of the American Medical Association who is now at the University of Chicago, has referred to the situation as the ''mess'' of American medicine.

Although much of the criticism has come from within the medical and educational professions, the problem is one for the public. The taxpayer pays a large part of the costs of training doctors.

For younger and middle-aged Americans, today's medical students will be the doctors caring for them when they are old. One frequent complaint is that as medicine has become dependent on technology, too many doctors stress technology at the expense of human feelings. Some medical leaders believe part of the problem is that too few professors are serving as role models in teaching younger colleagues how to relieve anguish by the laying on of hands. To the patient, it is not just the doctor, but also the staff, that has been infected by the callous, matter-of-fact approach.

It is no longer possible for any physician to know everything in medicine, no matter how good his memory may be. Memorization has been, and always will be, an integral part of medical education. But some critics contend that there is too much emphasis on memorization, which they say stifles intellectual thought and does not contribute to the powers of observation and problem-solving that are crucial in patient care and research.

Dr. Fred T. Valentine, a specialist in infectious diseases and immunology at New York University who is one of the best teachers I know, said he believes the least important aspect of a lecture is to transmit facts. The main purposes, he said, ''are to stimulate the enthusiasm of students for the subject; to correlate information and to explain lines of reasoning because the better one understands a subject the less he has to memorize; and to teach what is not known as well as what is known.''

Many people who are not familiar with the medical system are surprised to learn that few medical schools pay professors simply to teach, as in a high school. Most medical school professors teach as a sideline; their basic salary comes from research grants or from income derived from caring for patients in private practice.

It is not the students, but the system that is the focus of the debate over reforms in medical education. Even critics of the present system express optimism about the newest medical school graduates. As one highly respected physician said, ''If you've got bright students, they'll survive no matter how you teach them.''
