PUBLISHING: BILINGUAL EXHIBITION OF LATIN AUTHORS

By EDWIN McDOWELL
Published: April 26, 1985

A couple of years ago, Isaac Goldemberg was invited to read his poetry at a weeklong fair in Washington devoted to Latin American books. ''On my way home to New York,'' the Peruvian-born author recalled, ''I said to myself, 'If they can get a turnout of 300 to 400 a day in Washington for Latin books, why can't we have something similar in New York?' ''

That question will be answered next weekend, when the ''Feria del Libro Latinoamericano - the ''Latin American Book Fair'' - is held in New York University's Loeb Student Center, at Washington Square. Some 75 publishers and small presses from Latin America, Spain and the United States - plus one from Jerusalem that publishes books in Spanish - will exhibit their books from May 3 to May 5.

Some 30 leading Latin authors are scheduled to participate in bilingual readings from their works, including Ernesto Cardenal, the poet and Minister of Culture of Nicaragua, Luisa Valenzuela from Argentina and Reynaldo Arenas, the poet and novelist who came to the United States with the last major group of Cuban exiles. There will also be documentary films from Venezuela, El Salvador and Nicaragua, a program of Andean music, and panel discusions on Central America, Latin American women writers and literature and politics in Latin America.

''We think of the fair as not only an exhibition of books but also a cultural festival,'' said Mr. Goldemberg, who has lived in New York since 1967.

Books by some of the 500 Spanish-language authors who live in the New York area will be exhibited at the fair, and eight tables will be devoted to children's books in Spanish and English. There will also be two performances for children, one by the Don Quijote Children's Theater, the other a play based on a Mayan legend, by the Bubbles Players, a Hispanic children's theater company from Queens.

The fair has several aims: To show New York's growing Latin population the range of Spanish-language books available from the many foreign publishers, some 60 of whom are scheduled to attend. And to introduce non-Latins to Spanish and Portuguese literature, through the translations that will be exhibited by such American book publishers as Avon, Curbstone Press, Harper & Row, Farrar, Straus & Giroux, Ediciones del Norte, Alfred A. Knopf, New Directions, Lawrence Hill, the University of Nebraska Press and the University of Oklahoma Press.

''People have begun saying that the boom in Latin American literature has come to an end in the United States,'' said David Unger, a Guatemala-born poet and translator who is another prime mover behind the fair. ''But that viewpoint is a reflection on the economy, not on the quality of Latin writing. Maybe there won't soon be another 'One Hundred Years of Solitude,' in terms of sales, but this is an attempt to refocus attention on Latin American writing.''

The fair is financed in part by the National Endowment for the Arts, the New York State Council on the Arts, the New York Council for the Humanities and Con Edison. It has also attracted a dozen or more sponsors, including New York University, the Center for Inter-American Relations, the Poets and Writers organization, the New York Public Library, the Coordinating Council of Literary Magazines and the Translation Center at Columbia University.

''But the whole thing developed from the grass roots, with the artists and dramatists themselves, not with official pump priming from above,'' said Alexander Coleman, a professor in New York University's Spanish and Portuguese Department. ''We've needed this kind of fair for years, and it's just amazing it hasn't been done before.''

If it succeeds, Mr. Goldemberg and Mr. Unger plan to make it an annual event, beginning in 1987. ''There are more than two million Latin Americans in greater New York, including almost 200,000 exiles from Central America,'' Mr. Unger said. ''This kind of fair can break down their isolation, and help direct attention to the rich vein of Latin literature.'' Oracle Press Publishes Two Volumes of Biography Oracle Press of Baton Rouge, La., recently published ''Shipping Out'' and ''Millwork,'' the first two volumes in a fictionalized biography written by Alfred Levinson, a former teacher and resident of Sag Harbor, L.I. The books were simultaneously published in London by Signford Ltd. Both the American and British editions were underwritten by Michael Palin, the British actor who is also a member of the Monty Python comedy team.

During a visit to New York last fall, Mr. Palin explained that he had established Signford about a half-dozen years ago to publish books that struck his fancy but that had little commercial appeal. During that time, he has published several books, including two by the British artist Chris Orr, and ''Travelogs,'' a volume of poems by Mr. Levinson. He met Mr. Levinson at the home of a mutual friend in London in 1975, Mr. Palin explained, and ''when Monty Python came to New York the following year, he gave us advice about Americanizing the show for a U.S. audience.''

Mr. Palin said he was deeply impressed by the fictionalized memoirs of Mr. Levinson, who has written plays and poetry. ''They're quite radical memoirs about growing up in the McCarthy era,'' the actor said, ''but they are very direct and very honest.'' After deciding to have Signford publish them in England, he sought a co-publisher in the United States and eventually discovered Oracle, a four-year-old concern that has published some 30 books, mainly about the theater, that appealed to its founder, C.J. Stevens, a retired New York professor.

Under the co-publishing arrangement, Mr. Palin paid for the printing of the $14.95 hardcover books and Oracle is to pay for advertising and distribution. The jackets for both books, in both the British and American editions, were prepared by James Campus, a designer who had worked for Monty Python. ''In the film world, I'm very wary about putting any money into anything,'' Mr. Palin said. ''And I'm not expecting these books to be a financial success. I'm backing them out of friendship, but I really believe they deserve to be published and read.''
