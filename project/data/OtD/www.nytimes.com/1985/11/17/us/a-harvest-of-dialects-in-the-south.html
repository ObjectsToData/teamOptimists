A HARVEST OF DIALECTS IN THE SOUTH

By WILLIAM E. SCHMIDT, Special to the New York Times
Published: November 17, 1985

ATLANTA, Nov. 16— It was somewhere along Thomas Creek, not far from Bristol, Tenn., that Lee Pederson first learned about tommytoes. ''You don't really call them tommytoes, do you?'' Mr. Pederson asked an old farmer.

But the farmer said he did, a reply that was later to lead Mr. Pederson, a linguist at Emory University here, to turn up nearly 500 other people, mostly old-timers from the hills of Tennessee and Georgia, who said that was just the word they used when they wanted to describe cherry tomatoes.

For Mr. Pederson, the discovery of that usage was just one more clue in a 17-year effort - so far - to construct the Linguistic Atlas of the Gulf States, the most thorough scholarly survey ever made of the way Southerners talk.

The study is made up of 5,200 hours of tape-recorded conversations involving 1,211 Southerners in Alabama, Georgia, Tennessee, Mississippi, Arkansas, Louisiana, Florida and east Texas. It is financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities and Emory University and is to be published in four or five volumes, including a set of maps and legends, over the next four years.

'Merry' and 'Mary'

By plotting the geographic dispersal of usages like tommytoes, or mapping the places where people call a dragonfly a snake doctor, Mr. Pederson and his researchers have broken down the historic roots of the basic Southern drawl into as many as 13 separate dialects.

Each is marked by differences in grammar and pronunciation, as well as vocabulary, that shape a kind of code, tying the speaker to the hills or the plains or the piney woods where he was born and ''bred up.''

There is, for example, the homophony of ''merry'' and ''Mary,'' which typifies the South Midland accent common in the hill country of east Tennessee and upper Georgia.

And across what was once the rich cotton-growing landscape of Georgia, Alabama and the lower South, where people speak with the more leisurely drawl of the Plantation Southern dialect, the consonant R, in words like butter or bird, is more likely sounded like ''Uh.''

Mr. Pederson, a Chicagoan, concedes that many of these differences are, in a way, relics, remnants of an earlier and more isolated rural South. Since World War II, urbanization, the penetration of the mass communications media, the expansion of the Interstate highway system and the growing mobility of Americans are helping to reshape the way Southerners speak.

Three Essential Factors

''In one sense, the region's rich variety of dialects is disappearing, only to be replaced by social dialects,'' said Mr. Pederson. As a result, urban speakers in major Southern cities like Atlanta, Jacksonville and Birmingham, are tending to sound more alike, say, than people who live 30 miles from Atlanta in rural Georgia.

But just because the language of the South is changing, Mr. Pederson says, does not mean it is losing its special flavor.

Aside, perhaps, from New England, there is no region of the United States with a richer variety of dialect and usage than the South, a fact that Mr. Pederson traces to three essential factors.

First, the South never received the waves of Continental Europeans that swept the more industrial North, which means that the essential speech patterns drawn from the region's original Scotch and Irish ancestry have been less diluted.

Second, the endurance of those language patterns, coupled with a lower literacy rate, helped foster a strong oral tradition in the region, reflected not only in the South's penchant for oratory but the writings of great storytellers like William Faulkner and Erskine Caldwell.

'On Their Mother's Knee'

''In the North, people learned about their language and their history from teachers, or in books,'' said Mr. Pederson. ''In the South, youngsters more often learned on their mother's knee.''

Finally, in the years following the Civil War, the South found itself isolated from the rest of the country, a circumstance that Mr. Pederson said created a kind of garrison mentality that persisted until World War II, and helped reinforce a separate cultural identity reflected in language.

So bacon that is greasy to a Yankee is greazy to a Southerner. And down here, words like July and hotel are invariably accented on the first syllable.

Mr. Pederson's research, carried out over the years with a battery of assistants armed with tape recorders, followed a precise methodology, which was pioneered in the Linguistic Atlas of New England, published in 1939.

First, Dr. Pederson divided the eight states into 158 grid units, and speakers from each, young and old, white and black, were chosen as subjects.

Rural Resident or Yahoo

In the interviews, some of which lasted as long as five hours, the researchers drew their questions from a 107-page manual, eliciting the names of household objects, animals and foods, among other things. The interviews were transcribed, phonetically noted and entered into a computer.

As a result, Dr. Pederson now knows that, among native Southern speakers, there are 169 different terms for small wood to start a fire (like lightning wood and lighterd knots), 222 ways to describe a rural resident (as in kicker, snuff chewer and yahoo) and 73 synonyms for a cling peach (including green peach, pickle peach and yellow stone peach).

Not in the Dictionaries

Although some of the terms are in regular, everyday usage in the region, Mr. Pederson said, they do not appear in dictionaries, a reflection, perhaps, of a bias for Northern usage by dictionary publishers.

Dr. Pederson said he hoped the atlas would preserve for scholars some sort of record of a culture in transition. ''Language is the key and code of every culture,'' he said.
