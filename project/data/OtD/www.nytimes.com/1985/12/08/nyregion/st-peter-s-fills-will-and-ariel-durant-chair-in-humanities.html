ST. PETER'S FILLS WILL AND ARIEL DURANT CHAIR IN HUMANITIES

By CONRAD WESSELHOEFT
Published: December 8, 1985

JERSEY CITY— ''MY NEW teacher looked a bit ridiculous,'' the 13-year-old schoolgirl named Ariel wrote of her first impression of him.

''He was unimpressively short -five feet five and a half inches. He had nice black hair, but his pink face was blotched. He was timid, awkward, shy; too gentle to keep us in order.

''Often, as he tried to teach, we walked or ran about the room, and generally did as we pleased. I led the others in making fun of him.''

Ariel's contempt for her teacher, Will Durant, soon turned to infatuation, and he felt it necessary to offer her some advice.

''Our first love affairs,'' he wrote his pupil after she had tried to embrace him on the school playground, ''are almost always with persons much older than ourselves.

''When I was 14, I raved over a woman of 28. Most boys and girls go through such experiences, and their feelings almost always pass away with the lapse of a few months.''

But a few months later, Will and Ariel were married. He was 27 (and had forgotten the ring); she was 15 (and had brought along her roller skates).

The marriage was to last 68 years - they died two weeks apart in 1981 -and prove to be one of the most extraordinary literary collaborations of modern times.

Their most famous work is the 11-volume series ''The Story of Civilization.''

In recognition of their work, Governor Kean signed legislation last month establishing the Will and Ariel Durant Chair in the Humanities at St. Peter's College here.

The bill, drafted at the suggestion of the Rev. Edward Glynn, president of St. Peter's, will establish the chair in the amount of $65,000 annually. It will be filled by a distinguished visiting professor to be selected each year by a permanent search committee at the college.

The first to occupy that chair is the Rev. Raymond A. Schroth, former academic dean at Holy Cross College in Worcester, Mass., and a Jesuit priest. (An appreciation of Durant by Father Schroth is on the Opinion Page, xx.) Father Schroth, who is 52, said he would spend his year at St. Peter's teaching writing, conducting faculty reading groups and reading ''The Story of Civilization.''

One aim, he said, will be to ''discover Durant the individual. He seems to have been a beautiful person.''

Durant was born Nov. 5, 1885, in North Adams, Mass. - the recent legislation coincides with the 100th anniversary of his birth - but most of his childhood was spent in New Jersey.

His sister, Ethel Halliwell, who lives in Westfield, remembers their mother's shock when she learned of Will's decision to marry Ariel, a Russian Jew.

''My mother was frantic,'' said Mrs. Halliwell, who is 89 and was the youngest of the 11 Durant children. ''At that time for a Catholic to marry a Jewish girl was simply out of the question. It was a disgrace.''

Mrs. Halliwell said that it was not until the couple's first child was born several years later that her mother gave in and was able to accept Ariel.

According to his sister, young Will's interest in reading was stimulated by an older brother and by parents who, themselves unable to read, wanted more for their children.

''Mother would go into a bookstore,'' Mrs. Halliwell recalled, ''and say to a clerk that she wanted a 'good' book for her children. They would sell us Emerson's essays and Shakespeare's plays.''

As a young man, Durant developed the ambition of writing a book that would show the interdependence of history, politics, economics, art, literature and science.

''These subjects have been written up separately,'' he told an interviewer in 1922. ''My ambition is to write a complete history of the world, showing all these factors working in harmony, and giving as a result the kind of world we know.''

With a broad education and several advanced degrees to support his labor, Durant began writing ''Our Oriental Heritage,'' the first book in the series, in 1927. He asked his wife, who lacked a college education, to help in the organization and research.

Over the years, Ariel Durant took on an increasing share of the work, growing more interested and adept the closer she worked with her husband.

''You grow to become like the people you live with,'' said Mrs. Halliwell of Durant's influence on his wife.

For the seventh volume in the series, ''The Age of Reason Begins,'' Durant acknowledged the extent of his wife's contribution.

''I saw that it was a cooperative labor,'' he later wrote, ''and that simple justice required that the title page should bear both of our names. Ariel had never asked for this.''

Durant won the Pulitzer Prize for general nonfiction in 1968 for his ''Rousseau and Revolution.'' In 1977 he was awarded the Medal of Freedom.

Impish and plump, Ariel, in middle age, was known to gossip familiarly about Montaigne, Catherine de Medicis and her weakling sons, ''rare Ben'' Jonson and ''bewitching'' Disraeli, as though they still existed.

''When I am immersed in a personage of history,'' she once said, ''I live with him, eat with him, laugh and cry with him.''

The story of the Durants is, among other things, a lifelong love story.

''We have been the other half of each other for over 50 years,'' Ariel said in a speech in 1964. ''And if I ask myself what it is that has bound us and made us one, I cannot express it better than by saying, 'It was because he was he, because I was myself.' And because some inexplicable power had decreed an indissoluble union dedicating us to our work.'' Durant summed it up more simply. ''When Ariel smiles,'' he wrote in the last years of his life, ''the sun, the moon and the stars all come out at the same time.''
