REPORTER'S NOTEBOOK ON THE SCHOLARLY TRAIL OF THE NEW REVISIONISTS

By HERBERT MITGANG
Published: February 10, 1983

LITERARY revision appears to be a growing field for scholars these days. It covers an archeological dig into original notes and manuscripts, finding out how they differ from what was actually printed because of imposed editorial changes or typographical gremlins, and then reconstructing the way a book should have appeared in the first place. In the esoteric phrase of one such scholar, the aim is to discover ''authorial intentionality.''

This revisionism - it's quite often done years after a book has outlived its author, who therefore isn't around to confirm or dispute what he really meant, but it surprisingly includes living authors, too - can differ from a writer's own changes or those made with his consent by an editor.

Most books that are brought out in new editions remain just as they appeared the first time, typos and all. To save printing costs, plates frequently are reproduced from first or foreign editions. But once in a while an author gets a crack at changing even his original title. For instance, Ignazio Silone's anti-Fascist novel, written while he was living in exile in Switzerland, was originally called ''Bread and Wine'' when it was a Book-of-the-Month Club choice here in 1937. When the banned novel finally was published in Italy after the downfall of Mussolini and the creation of the Italian Republic, Mr. Silone decided the title should be ''Wine and Bread.'' He explained: ''In a democracy, wine is more important in our lives than bread.''

A new revisionist look involves Norman Mailer's ''American Dream,'' which was published serially in 1964 in Esquire magazine and was brought out as a novel in 1965 by the Dial Press. Hershel Parker, professor of American Romanticism at the University of Delaware (he coined the phrase ''authorial intentionality''), maintains that the magazine version is superior to the book version that Mr. Mailer revised himself.

''I confess that Norman Mailer did not search me out along the banks of the Brandywine and beg me to save him from himself,'' Professor Parker says in the current issue of the Bulletin of Research in the Humanities, a publication of the New York Public Library and the State University at Stony Brook. Nevertheless, he says he did minutely examine the two versions plus other material belonging to Mr. Mailer - with the author's consent - including notebooks that antedated publication.

''The author damaged it by a handful of small excisions and slight verbal substitutions during his careful polishing of the version that he had serialized in Esquire,'' Professor Parker says of ''An American Dream.'' He maintains that the novel could become an American classic - ''especially if Mailer decides to save the book from himself.''

Professor Parker notes that the novel originally used President John F. Kennedy as ''an offstage character, minor but exerting powerful influence.'' The January, 1964, issue of Esquire was set up in November, 1963, before President Kennedy's assassination. When it came time to revise the book version, Professor Parker says, Mr. Mailer made various changes, including dropping a fictional scene in which Mr. Kennedy became romantically involved with a woman and then lost her to a fellow Congressman.

Professor Parker says: ''Kennedy's death compounded the minor problems in 'An American Dream' that were already there. As he revised for the book version Mailer took the alternative of dropping Kennedy as a character and tried to move the action back to a year when Kennedy was living and was President.

''No one has mentioned any connection between the assassination and the time scheme of 'An American Dream,' but that tragedy accounts in large measure for the serial version's seeming eerily out of time and the book's containing not only anomalies but the overriding strangeness of having the story told retrospectively yet at a time when the narrator does not know when Kennedy is dead.''

Professor Parker also says that Mr. Mailer removed certain passages that seemed ''too obviously autobiographical or might seem so,'' used ''more explicit sexual language throughout'' in the book, removed expressions of ''sexual fear,'' and changed many characterizations so that ''the whole book is drained of intentionality.'' He concludes admiringly:

''Yet there may not be another man or woman of our time who has written so profound and prescient a meditation on manhood, marriage and the American Dream. It's time to give another chance to the novel Mailer wrote. I have come round to thinking that 'An American Dream' is very possibly a great novel, or that it used to be, back in 1963 and 1964, before Dial published it.''

And what does Norman Mailer himself think of Professor Parker's interpretation? Mr. Mailer said that he had glanced at the professor's article and found it interesting as a literary matter. But, he said: ''I'm not sure he's right. I think the Dial book is better. The publisher preferred to print the Esquire serial, but I devoted four months to revising it for the book. I probably made some errors in some sentences, but there is nothing wrong with making changes. Henry James rewrote all of his novels. It gives you a chance to cut out some of your darlings.''
