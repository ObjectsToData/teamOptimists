'82 GIFTS TO CHARITY A RECORD, BUT RATE OF INCREASE SLOWED

By KATHLEEN TELTSCH, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 14, 1983

In the midst of a severe recession, individuals, companies and foundations gave nearly $60 billion to charity last year, more than ever before.

But despite the generous giving, the rate of increase slowed, just as it did in the downturn in the mid-1970's, according to the American Association of Fund-Raising Counsel, a leading authority on philanthropic trends.

President Reagan has urged Americans to give more to meet community needs at a time when he is seeking to curtail Federal spending on many social programs and reduce the role of government in daily life. He reiterated that policy March 3 in a speech in Los Angeles, declaring that private initiative was ''America's most precious resource.'' He noted that despite the recession, individuals gave $48.7 billion last year, or 9.4 percent more than the previous year.

Total May Reach $60 Billion

According to the association's estimates, giving from companies and foundations brought the tentative total of charitable contributions to $59.8 billion. It said this figure could climb to $60 billion before the books were closed on 1982.

But no propect was seen by the association that private contributions could make up for the reduction in Federal financial support for the country's 300,000 nonprofit organizations active in education, health, social welfare and the arts.

And while individual donors gave 9.4 percent more, the association said this compared with an increase of 11.9 percent in individual giving a year earlier. Religious Drives Got Most of It

As in earlier years, individual donors, who account for 90 percent of all charitable contributions, gave more than half of their gifts to religious organizations. An increased share of the philanthropic dollar went also to social service agencies and to education.

The association found evidence that giving to arts and humanities organizations and to civic and public affairs groups was tempered because of a shift toward human needs. Moreover, it noted that the total of giving to the arts had been raised by a one-time J. Paul Getty gift of $1.3 billion to the museum bearing his name.

The association, which represents 28 professional fund-raising organizations, issues what are the most widely cited statistics on charitable giving. For 27 years it has published ''Giving U.S.A.,'' a handbook. This year, for the first time, the association called in a group of economists and analysts of philanthropic trends and acknowledged the need to improve the data collection. 'Hard Data Are Elusive'

''New measurement techniques are applied slowly to philanthropy, and until they are developed further, hard data are scarce, elusive and, at best, incomplete,'' John J. Schwartz, president of the association, said.

''Giving U.S.A.,'' makes tentative estimates upon a base of the 1,200 charitable organizations the association's members assist with fund-raising drives. These fund campaigns are made on behalf of nonprofit organizations, including universities and orchestras, hospitals, museums, church groups and social welfare agencies.

Before the report is completed, probably in May, additional data will be gathered from government agencies, trade associations in the arts and health fields, the Council on Foundations, United Way of America, the Conference Board, a business research group whose membership includes 3,500 leading companies, and the Council on Financial Aid to Education.

The association of fund-raising counsel gave a tentative breakdown of gifts by category: Religious organizations in 1982 received $28.1 billion; education $8.49 billion; health and hospitals $8.3 billion, and social services $6.2 billion -a gain of 10 percent - civic and public organizations, $1.37 billion; and foreign aid $2.54 billion. The arts and humanities organizations received $4.86 billion. 6 Percent Rise in This Area

In addition to the $48.7 billion contributed to charitable groups by individuals last year, foundations gave $2.7 billion and companies $3 billion, an increase of 6 percent over the year before.

The estimate of giving by companies was probably ''on the low side,'' said E. Patrick McGuire, executive director of corporate relations research for the Conference Board, which conducted its own survey of 2,500 companies. Mr. McGuire tentatively estimated giving in 1982 at $3.1 billion and said grants would be most''visible'' in communities where the corporations maintained headquarters or other facilities.

Because of the Federal budget cuts, there has been increased competition among nonprofit organizations for contributions from private sources. According to the Urban Institute, a Washington research organization, the Federal cuts would mean a loss of $25 billion from 1982 to 1985 for the thousands of nonprofit groups that depend on government grants for at least part of their support. These include schools, housing and welfare agencies, community organizations and cultural groups. 'Hard Information Difficult'

Russy Sumariwalla, executive director of the National Center for Charitable Statistics in Alexandria, Va., one of the experts who will join an advisory panel to assist the association, said: ''In these difficult times of Federal cutbacks, there obviously is increasing interest in analyzing how much money is available from alternative sources, but hard information about charitable contributions is difficult to obtain.''
