ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: September 11, 1983

WILLIAM CARLOS WILLIAMS, who was born in Rutherford 100 years ago this coming Saturday, who lived there all his life and who practiced medicine three for over 40 years, is being hailed as ''the best American poet of the century.''

So says John G. Dollar of Rutherford, a professor of humanities at Farleigh Dickinson University there, a Williams scholar and a participant in the recent Williams centennial celebration held at the University of Maine.

''What Williams did - revolutionize American poetry by writing it in an idiom that anyone could understand - was enthusiastically recognized by the literati assembling there,'' said Mr. Dollar, who then backed up his opinion by quoting the following lines from Williams's ''Selected Poems'' (New Directions, New York, $4.95): I wanted to write a poem that you would understand. For what good is it to me if you can't understand it? But you got to try hard -

A biographical essay by Mr. Dollar, a neighbor of Williams from 1955 to 1963 - the year of the 79-year-old poet's death - appears in a landmark commemorative volume published for the Maine conference: ''William Carlos Williams: Man and Poet,'' edited by Carroll F. Terrell (University of Maine, 305 English-Math Building, Orono, Me. 04469, $32.50; $15.95, including postage).

''We'll be making a bigger splash for Williams here in Rutherford for our centennial celebration,'' said Mark Hillringhouse, a 29-year-old poet from Hackensack. The events he's referring to, which both he and Mr. Dollar coordinated with the help of a grant from the Bergen County Office of Cultural and Historic Affairs, will take place at the William Carlos Williams Center at the Williams Plaza.

The center, a restored theater, is two blocks from the two-story late- Victorian house at 9 Ridge Road, where Williams lived from 1913 on, practicing medicine and writing more than 40 books of poetry, fiction, translations and critical prose. His oldest son, Dr. William E. Williams, a pediatrician, now practices there.

Next Sunday at 4 P.M., the commemoration begins with a series of poetry readings at the center by major poets, such as Allen Ginsberg, Amiri Baraka and Denise Levertrov, who, Mr. Hillringhouse says, ''consider Williams the father of modern poetry.''

That evening at 8 P.M., Mr. Dollar will lead a panel discussion, ''The Influence of William Carlos Williams on Contemporary Poetry.'' The participants will include Dr. William E. Williams, Allen Ginsberg, and Edith Heal Berrian of Rutherford, an author and friend of the poet. Admission to each event is $3. Call (201) 939-6969 for reservations and for information about the schedule of readings, music and dance performances and exhibits that end Sept. 25.

''Paterson,'' (New Directions, $5.25) the epic poem to which Williams devoted most of the last half of his career (1937-63) - the first of its five books was written in the 1940's - was meant, he wrote in its author's note, to show ''that a man in himself is a city, beginning, seeking, achieving and concluding his life in ways which the various aspects of a city may embody.''

Williams wrote in ''Paterson'' that ''anything is good material for poetry,'' and the poem contains myth and facts about the city of Paterson - historical, geographical and political references as well as newspaper items, medical reports, an interview, letters and personal experience - loosely combined in a spoken language.

''In search of Williams's sources, some people often come to Paterson and especially to its Lambert Castle where the Passaic County Historical Society has been based since 1934,'' said Barbara C. Rivolta of Paterson, the society's assistant director, adding that even Williams had visited there for his materials.

Often, the society's publications illuminate Williams's lines in ''Paterson'' and, like the following books, open wide the doors to New Jersey's archives.

''Country Walks in Many Fields'' by Joseph Rydings ($10.50), a collection of the picturesque writings of Mr. Rydings, the ''leader'' of the Paterson Rambling Club in 1904, describes the club's excursions to local attractions of natural beauty like the Passaic Falls. In ''Paterson,'' many of the historical descriptions focus on the Falls, such as the 1812 report of Sarah Cumming of Newark, who with her loving husband of two months, vanished suddenly while admiring the ''wild and romantic scenery.''

''The History of the Silk-Dyeing Industry in the United States,'' by Albert H. Heusser ($32.50), a volume handsomely bound in wine and gold, is fascinating for its biographies of the city's earliest silk manufacturers, such as Catholina Lambert, remembered for his ''great silk mills'' on both sides of Straight Street and for the castle he built - the society's Lambert Castle - ''adorning the rugged face of Garrett Mountain.''

''Up From The Cellar'' by Lini de Vries ($5.00), 420 pages, is a grim autobiographical record of what it was like to work in the Paterson silk mills in 1917 - the author was only 12 years old then - 10 hours a day, 6 days a week and of the unions' fight for an 8-hour day. The author went on to become a nurse, first teaching birth- control methods in Vineland in 1935 and then joining a medical unit in 1937 that traveled to the Madrid front during the Spanish Civil War.

The mailing address for the books is Passaic County Historical Society, Lambert Castle, Paterson, 07503; add $1 for postage.
