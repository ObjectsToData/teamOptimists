MUSIC NOTES; A PATRON REVISES HIS PLANS

By EDWARD ROTHSTEIN
Published: December 4, 1983

Paul Fromm, a retired wine importer, is one of the most important private patrons for contemporary music, having through the Fromm Foundation commissioned more than 150 compositions over the last 30 years. Each summer, Mr. Fromm has also been a co-sponsor of Tanglewood's annual Festival of Contemporary Music, a festival which has become known as ''Fromm Week.'' But Mr. Fromm said in an interview last week that the foundation will no longer be a co-sponsor of the Festival with the Boston Symphony. Moreover, during the last three months, Mr. Fromm has initiated changes in his foundation which are likely to have a strong effect on activity in the new music world. And in the midst of the reorganization he has also endowed a Fromm Foundation Visiting Professorship of Music at Harvard University, which, one semester every three years, will be given to a composer of international reputation.

The goal of these changes, Mr. Fromm said, is to ''penetrate the total musical community and be more responsive to musical pluralism in America.'' This will mean not encouraging one particular school of composition at the expense of another. In particular, the centrality of the academic modernist style in evidence at the Tanglewood Festival has been criticized. ''None of the works we commissioned for Tanglewood,'' said Mr. Fromm last week, ''has entered the repertory of new music groups around the country, which means the selection was much too narrow.'' The Fromm Foundation will continue to support a number of Berkshire Music Center Fellowships.

Thomas Morris, the general manager of the Boston Symphony, said last week that he was not ''aware of any change at this time'' in the Fromm Foundation's relationship to the Tanglewood Festival of Contemporary Music, but whatever occurs, the orchestra is ''committed to the festival and will carry it on,'' under the direction of the composer, Gunther Schuller, the artistic director of the Berkshire Music Center.

Mr. Schuller has had full responsibility for programming the contemporary programs for the Festival. But in September, he resigned from the board of the Fromm Foundation. The composer Earle Brown took his place, joining Mr. Fromm and the other board member, Christoph Wolff, the chairman of the Music Department at Harvard University. Plans were then made for reorganizing the structure of the Foundation.

An Advisory Council, composed of three regional three-member panels - for the East, the West, and the mid- West - is being established to advise the board on the variety of composers at work across the country.

The panels will suggest commissions and arrange performances through contact with new music groups in each region; each commissioned work will receive at least three performances. The panels, Mr. Fromm said, ''shouldn't reject any style, any form, any idea, as long as it indicates individuality, musical imagination and craftsmanship. Ideology should not enter into it.''

The result is likely to be a broadened interest in such compositional schools as the ''experimental'' music influenced by rock and jazz, the neo- tonal music now being written by many composers, and the work of Minimalist composers. Steve Reich has already received a commission from the Fromm Foundation and the London Symphonietta for a new work.

The East Coast panel will include the composers Charles Dodge and Joan Tower; Alvin Lucier has also been invited. The mid-West panel will include the composers Ben Johnson and John Eaton, along with Robert Morgan of the University of Chicago. And the West Coast panel will include Michael Steinberg, the artistic adviser for the San Francisco Symphony, and the composer John Adams; the composer Morton Subotnick has been invited.

''We don't want to become institutionalized,'' said Mr. Fromm about his Foundation, and, in fact, in 1972, the then 20-year-old organization also altered its esthetic position. It withdrew its central support from the abstract theoreticians then centered around Princeton University and the musical journal ''Perspectives of New Music,'' and moved the foundation's base to Harvard University. It was an attempt, Mr. Fromm said at the time, to become more involved with the communicative powers of music, and less involved with theoretical focus on its materials.

This change is meant to be similar - to find a new communicative base for new music, which has failed to be integrated into the mainstream of the musical public. There are now no plans for an organized ''Fromm Festival'': it is being replaced by attempts to have the music performed in regular concert settings.

''Serious art,'' Mr. Fromm wrote in 1978, ''does not appeal to everybody. It never did and it never will.'' Mr. Fromm indicated that in the changes now under way, he would attempt to retain seriousness without settling into academicism, and to encourage ''pluralism'' and ''vitality'' without sacrificing esthetic distinctions. Changes at Aston Magna

In another attempt to redefine the direction of a distinguished musical institution, Albert Fuller resigned last month as President and Artistic Director of the Aston Magna Foundation for Music. In 1972, Mr. Fuller began Aston Magna, which has been an important participant in the ''early music'' movement in the United States. Mr. Fuller also helped organize a series of Aston Magna ''Academies'' in Great Barrington, Mass., supported by the National Endowment for the Humanities, in which over the past six summers, visiting authorities on the arts and the history of ideas have joined the musicians in the study of such topics as ''France Under the Reign of Louis XIV.''

The resignation of Mr. Fuller at a meeting of the board in November followed a disagreement on what direction the Aston Magna Foundation should take. Mr. Fuller, in an interview, criticized the ''institutional point of view'' of the board, which ''wanted to freeze things.'' He was proposing to expand the educational and cultural activities of Aston Magna, and not limit the foundation simply to the support and performance of early music. Mr. Fuller advocated the establishment of ''satellite communities'' affiliated with Aston Magna across the country, and suggested that the Foundation sponsor him in public lecture series about the relationship between music and its surrounding culture.

The chairman of the board, Robert B. Strassler, declined to comment on the disagreements, but Marianne Carroll, the Executive Director of the Foundation, said that members of the board had objected to the plans because they would have meant ''upgrading'' the administrative activities of Aston Magna, would have cost too much money and would have blurred the goals of Aston Magna itself.

Carter Celebrated

In celebration of Elliott Carter's 75th birthday, two special concerts will be presented during the coming days. On Mr. Carter's actual birthday next Sunday, at 8 PM, at the 92d Street Y, Speculum Musicae will present an entire program of his music. The program will include the first United States performance of ''In Sleep, In Thunder'' a setting of six poems of Robert Lowell for tenor and 14 players; the world premiere of ''Changes'' for solo guitar and the composer's First String Quartet and his ''Triple Duo.'' On Monday, Dec. 5 at Carnegie Recital Hall at 8 P.M., Composers' Forum will present the pianist Claudia Stevens in a tribute to Mr. Carter. Miss Stevens commissioned five American composers to write music in honor of Mr. Carter's birthday. The works include: Shulamit Ran's ''Sonata Waltzer''; Andrew Imbrie's ''Short Story''; Robert Rodriguez's ''Reflections on 'Sensual Indulgence'''; Allen Shearer's ''Medallion'' and Vivian Fine's ''Double Variations for Piano solo. Mr. Carter's Piano Sonata will also be heard.
