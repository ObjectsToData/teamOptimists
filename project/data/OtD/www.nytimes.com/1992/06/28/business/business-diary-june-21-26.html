Business Diary/June 21-26

By Joel Kurtzman
Published: June 28, 1992

THE ECONOMY While the Other Guys Brawl, Clinton Sneaks In a Platform

While Ross Perot and the Republicans dominated the airwaves with their slugfest over "opposition research," Gov. Bill Clinton and the Democrats released drafts of their party platform. The plan -- which they call the "third way" because they see it as between laissez-faire and welfare-state economics -- proposes increased taxes on the wealthy and on corporations and a 5 percent cut in middle-class taxes -- reduced from Mr. Clinton's original proposal of a 10 percent tax cut. It also calls for a cut in capital gains taxes, new tax credits for the working poor and heavy spending on schools and highways. The tax increases will enable the Democrats to chop the Federal deficit in half by 1996. "It's clearly time to reassess and change our policies and face reality," Mr. Clinton said. Predictably, the Republicans were not impressed. The Clinton plan "has been strictly for media consumption," said Richard G. Darman, President Bush's budget director. A Presidential Prod at the Fed While economists noodle over whether lower interest rates will add spark to the economy or just fuel inflation, President Bush made his views known last week. "I'd like to see another lowering of interest rates," he said, in a statement aimed at prodding Alan Greenspan, chairman of the Federal Reserve, to push down rates. But the Federal Reserve only controls short-term rates. Longer rates -- the ones mortgages and car payments are pegged to -- are determined by the market. "Long-term rates are about where they were in November, even though the Fed has eased and eased short-term rates," said Mickey D. Levy, chief economist at CRT Government Securities. When the Fed meets this week, Mr. Bush's urgings will still be in the air. "It's poor practice for the President to jawbone the Fed. There's value in an independent central bank," Mr. Levy said. Clean Air on Endangered List? It was a curious decision for the environmental President to make. But last week, the Bush Administration gave manufacturers authorization to increase the amount of hazardous pollutants they emit into the atmosphere. The rule allows them to increase by 245 tons the amount of pollution they pour into the air without public notice or hearings. The Environmental Protection Agency issued the rule at the behest of the President's Council on Competitiveness. The rule, "carves the heart out of the Clean Air Act," said Representative Henry A. Waxman, Democrat of California. President Bush supported passage of the act. G.D.P. History Repeats Itself When the Commerce Department said last week that gross domestic product grew by 2.7 percent in the first quarter, it was a trip down memory lane. Last year, the first quarter looked promising but the economy lost its oomph by spring. "The numbers we have for May indicate a slowdown," said Norman Robertson, chief economist at the Mellon Bank. Full Disclosure on Executive Pay To prevent executives from lining their pockets with stock options and fat paychecks at the expense of the shareholders, the Securities and Exchange Commission proposed new rules for disclosing how executives are paid. The rules require companies to publish what their top executives are paid in a series of tables, graphs and notes. The S.E.C. wants them to plot those figures -- including the value of stock options -- alongside shareholder's return on investment over a five-year period. It also wants charts comparing companies' rates of return with those of their competitors. Shareholder groups have applauded the changes, while the Bush Administration opposes them as meddling. A Surprising Drop in Durables Orders for durable goods, those big ticket items that are supposed to last three years or more, fell by 2.4 percent in May, the Commerce Department said. That was a surprise to most economists. The consensus forecast was that orders for durable goods would rise. "The goods orders mean we shouldn't expect employment growth in manufacturing in the near term," said Charles McMillion, president of MBG Group, a business information firm in Washington. "Services linked to manufacturing -- software, trucking, insurance -- will also see an absence of high wage growth," he said. INTERNATIONAL

Israel's Shift in Direction By voting into power a Labor Government headed by Yitzhak Rabin, Israel will be getting a Prime Minister who says his first priority is self-rule for Palestinians in the occupied territories. Israelis will also be getting a Prime Minister who opposes new Jewish settlements in those lands. Mr. Rabin's plans are likely to please Washington. The Bush Administration held up Israel's request for $10 billion in loan guarantees because of the previous Government's pro-settlement policy. Minivans: Japan, 1; Detroit, Zip Relativism may be the scourge of the White House, but it's still in favor at the International Trade Commission. The commission decided that although the Commerce Department determined Toyota and Mazda had been selling minivans here for less than in Japan, penalties should not be imposed because their sales were still too small to hurt Detroit. The commission said its decision was based on whether American car companies suffered "material injury." Detroit, which claims Japan's dumping has hurt its minivan sales, was incensed by the decision. In a statement, the Ford Motor Company said the commission signaled to "Japan that it's O.K. to defy the international rules of trade, and, what's more, that it doesn't hurt America." Agriculture Is No Magic Kingdom As protests go, this one was pretty Mickey Mouse. Five hundred French farmers drove to Euro Disneyland near Paris to protest reforms that cut farm subsidies. The farmers parked tractors at the park's entrance and set tires on fire. Why Disneyland? "You're on American territory." said Henri Pepitas, an organizer. "They're the ones who wrote the reform."
