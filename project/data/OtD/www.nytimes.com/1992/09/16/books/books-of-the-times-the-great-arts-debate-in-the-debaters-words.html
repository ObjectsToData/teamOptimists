Books of The Times; The Great Arts Debate In the Debaters' Words

By HERBERT MITGANG
Published: September 16, 1992

Culture Wars Documents From the Recent Controversies in the Arts Edited by Richard Bolton Illustrated. 363 pages. New Press. Paperback, $19.95.

The rancorous recent debate over the arts and just how much, if at all, the Government should pay for and control them has produced the widest possible range of views. And these views all appear in "Culture Wars," a valuable documentary record of the continuing dispute over artistic freedom and the Government's judgmental role in giving grants to the arts and humanities. The book is edited by Richard Bolton, an associate professor and chairman of the art studio at the University of California, Santa Barbara. It includes 101 articles, letters, excerpts from Senate debates and assorted pronunciamentos about American cultural life, opinions that echo down to the 1992 Presidential conventions and campaign.

Among the illustrations in the book are some of the very photographs by Andres Serrano and Robert Mapplethorpe that inflamed the controversy in Washington. The debate spread across the country, affecting local and state aid for creative artists and performers. It raised fundamental questions about what was fit for people to see in museums, hear and view on stages and read in public libraries.

In a dramatic performance itself on May 18, 1989, Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, a New York Republican, tore up a reproduction of a photograph by Mr. Serrano and threw the pieces on the Senate floor, saying, "This so-called piece of art is a deplorable, despicable display of vulgarity." Senator D'Amato helped ignite a two-year battle among public officials, artists, museum directors, art critics and commentators over the budget of the National Endowment for the Arts.

The Corcoran Gallery of Art in Washington canceled its planned exhibition called "Robert Mapplethorpe: The Perfect Moment." The museum's trustees, said an article in The Washington Post on June 14, 1989, feared that the sexual content of the artist's work would jeopardize Congressional reauthorization of the arts endowment, which had partly paid for the Mapplethorpe exhibition.

The documents in "Culture Wars" show that the controversy divided Washington and the artistic world. What constitutes art, and who is to judge what is not art? The painter Helen Frankenthaler, a member of the National Endowment's advisory council, said that "quality control" was needed to raise the level of art. While she would not want to support Mr. Serrano and Mr. Mapplethorpe, she said, she felt their work should be shown once it had been paid for.

In an article on the Op-Ed page of The New York Times on July 17, 1989, Miss Frankenthaler wrote: "A country depends on its culture and cultural freedom; it is lost without it. If that healthy atmosphere is censored or dictated, the life of every citizen is at stake. Witness throughout history the results in Germany, the Soviet Union, China etc. We must not smother the expression of art anymore than we should suppress or annihilate protests and parades, all part of our unique and precious democracy."

"Culture Wars" makes a valiant effort to deliver different viewpoints. It includes articles saying the Federal Government has the right, indeed the obligation, to censor by withholding money from what officials consider obscene by artistic or community standards. Among the most interesting documents in the book are those that reprint excerpts from Congressional debates. Senator Jesse Helms has his say at considerable length.

Mr. Bolton has reached wide for documentary evidence. He includes a statement by Vaclav Havel, the playwright, when he was President of Czechoslovakia. It was addressed to "fellow artists" for Arts Advocacy Day, March 18, 1990, in the United States. Speaking as someone who had once been jailed for his art, Mr. Havel declares:

"We know first hand how essential is a fierce, independent, creative artistic spirit in the attainment of freedom. Through a long night of repression and control, the artistic community in our land helped keep alive the unquenchable flame of freedom. And artists played a central role in helping organize our final transformation to a new democratic state. An artist must challenge, must controvert the established order. To limit that creative spirit in the name of public sensibility is to deny to society one of its most significant resources."

If an office of government censorship actually existed, "Culture Wars" would never get by the thought police. It would undoubtedly be judged the sort of "rotten book" that Patrick Buchanan says produces "rotten behavior," assuming, of course, that the censors could devise a neat standard differentiating rotten books from non-rotten books.
