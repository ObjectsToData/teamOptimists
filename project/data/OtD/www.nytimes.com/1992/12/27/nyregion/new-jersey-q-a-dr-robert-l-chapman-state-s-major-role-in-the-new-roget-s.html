New Jersey Q & A: Dr. Robert L. Chapman; State's Major Role in the New Roget's

By SHIRLEY HORNER
Published: December 27, 1992

THE appearance of the fifth edition of Roget's International Thesaurus this year was a major event in the worlds of publishing and academe -- particularly so because Dr. Robert L. Chapman, the editor, took it upon himself to make some basic changes in the organization of the 1852 classic. It was, as he put it during an interview at his white Colonial house here, a "disruption of the linguistic firmament."

Dr. Chapman, 72, a scholar who has an uncanny resemblance to the extant portraits of Geoffrey Chaucer, a giant of medieval literature, spent 20 years teaching that subject at Drew University ("a five-minute bike ride away") before his retirement in 1986.

"Don't let the scholarly visage fool you," he cautioned. "I've also spent much time outside the university walls. Where I grew up in West Virginia, my father was a typewriter mechanic. My brother and I were the first in our family to go to college; during summers I worked in factories and as a truck driver."

Robert Chapman was studying at the University of Michigan in 1942 when he met and married Sarah Zeitz of Lakewood. He fought with the 86th Infantry Division in Germany and Austria in World War II, returned to the university to earn his Ph.D. in English language and literature and arrived at Drew in 1966.

Today, the couple's three sons live nearby, and thanks to one son, Douglass, a bio-statistician, Dr. Chapman, a professor emeritus in the humanities at Drew, is a grandfather of triplets. His comment, apt for a lexicographer: "There's always more than one version of everything."

In 1977, Dr. Chapman edited the fourth edition of Roget's, and 11 years later he started on the fifth, published in September by HarperCollins. In between, he wrote the New Diction ary of American Slang and compiled the Thesaurus of American Slang.

Q. What's missing from the many reviews that have praised your thesaurus?

A. It's never been identified as a New Jersey book. I'd like to accentuate, highlight and point up the fact that from our state, which never gets the credit it deserves, have come the inner workings of this thesaurus.

The major impetus is our own A.T.& T. Bell Laboratories in Murray Hill. Over the past 10 years, its people collected millions and millions of words from Associated Press and Wall Street Journal tapes and from boundless, countless and limitless publications.

I had a computer terminal in my office at Drew connected to the computer at Bell Labs, and from their linguistic wealth, I sifted, snifted, sorted and snorted and chose about 60,000 words to commend to anyone today in search of the right word -- both the new ones like space shuttle, yuppie and ecosystem as well as the old ones with new meanings like crack, boot and hacker.

Q. But your modification of Roget's work is more than adding and subtracting. The act of bravery on your part was making the layout of the book more consistent with the modern mind.

A. That's where the New Jersey influence grows even stronger. My friend Charles Courtney, a philosophy professor at Drew University, guided me to works where others had also attempted to arrange knowledge in a more natural way. Next I called Prof. George Miller at Princeton University -- he's a cognitive psychologist and author of important books about how we think.

Although Roget opened with categories of words arranged according to abstract ideas like "existence," Drs. Courtney and Miller didn't object to my belief that a more normal arrangement would conform to the development of the human individual and indeed, of the human race and that abstract thought is a relatively later achievement.

Therefore, my opening category is "birth"; my final category is "space travel." And I found that the history of the last 15 years made it necessary to add 38 new categories like computer science, fitness and exercise, and the environment.

Q. Any more New Jersey facets to this story?

A. Yes, don't forget Roget was actually a physician, and if you're going to overhaul the man's grand scheme, it helps to understand the workings of his mind.

Here, Dr. Donald Kent, a retired specialist in family practice in Chatham, came to the rescue. He'd been working for a Ph.D. in the history of science at Drew, had done research on Roget, and he put me at ease with that great personage and less in awe.

Roget wasn't too unlike me. He was also a schematizing senior citizen, was 73 when his thesaurus was published. Like him, I have wide interests; it helps when doing a book like this to be familiar with the vocabulary of many fields.

The difference I find is that he was much drier and more humorless. I write poetry. I think I'm more of a romantic.

Q. Roget wrote that he conceived his compilation to "supply" his own "deficiencies." What deficiencies led you to this work?

A. Money. As you know, college professors don't make much. From the fourth edition -- it sold more than two million copies -- I was able to buy my sailboat, which I've named Thesaurus.

Obviously, through my work, I've also attended to the deficiency of my vocabulary. I always wanted to be known as the person who comes up with the mot juste.
