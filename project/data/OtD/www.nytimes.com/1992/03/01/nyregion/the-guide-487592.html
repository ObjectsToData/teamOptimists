THE GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: March 1, 1992

EARLY MOZART

Thomas Crawford's Fairfield Orchestra, now 11 years old, will perform on Saturday at 8 P.M. in the Concert Hall at Norwalk City Hall, following its debut at Lincoln Center on Wednesday evening as part of the Mozart Bicentennial series. The 26-member period instrument ensemble will also release its first recording on the Music Masters label this summer.

The program will be much the same in all three performances, featuring Mozart's Early Piano Concertos with Malcolm Bilson as fortepiano soloist. Seven concertos will be recorded on two CD's, along with Mozart's Symphony No. 14.

Tickets for the Norwalk concert are $25 and $35, $20 for older people, and reservations may be made by calling 972-7400. City Hall is at 125 East Avenue. Those interested in attending the performance at Alice Tully Hall at Lincoln Center may do so through the orchestra, which will run buses for about $15 round trip. Concert tickets alone are $20, and a package including transportation, ticket and a champagne supper costs $100. INTOLERANCE

Leonard Dinnerstein has written many books about religious and ethnic intolerance in the United States. On Wednesday at 8 P.M. he will give a lecture, "The Rise and Decline of American Anti-Semitism: From the New Deal to Our Own Day," at the Regina A. Quick Center for the Arts at Fairfield University. Admission is free.

His book, "The Leo Frank Case," won a Saturday Review Award in 1969, and Mr. Dinnerstein is at work on a book comparing anti- Semitism and anti-Catholicism. He said that the latter was for more intense than the former until the beginning of the 20th century. The book, which is being written with a grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities, traces bigotry in America from Colonial times to the present. SOLAR ENERGY

A tour of solar-powered homes in eastern Connecticut and an exhibition of new energy technologies at the American Wharf Marina in Norwich will be offered on Saturday by People's Action for Clean Energy. A ticket, with booklets and maps, costs $12 and may be obtained by calling 693-4813.

The houses are in Lebanon, Franklin, Norwich and Preston. Among them is the first house in the state to be powered by solar electricity. Houses with passive solar energy are also on the tour, and at the American Wharf a dizzying collection of energy-efficient vehicles and equipment will be on view. They include a solar-powered electric train, solar-powered motorboats, solar electric panels for boats, solar electric water pumping devices, heat pumps and hot water heaters powered by the sun, quartz halogen lighting, a windmill, solar toys and food freezers.

This will be the group's 16th such tour, each in a different location in the state. PAINT AND LANGUAGE

Like many contemporary artists Peter Nadin is fascinated by mixing painting and words. An exhibition of his work, on view through March 29 at the Yale Center for British Art, contains pieces from six series done since the British-born artist began working in New York more than a decade ago.

In one series, the "Poetry Dispensers," the paintings contain sheets of Mr. Nadin's own poetry, while his recorded voice reads them. One of his quotes might be viewed as a summary of his philosophy: "Who, awaiting today would not pray to already know tomorrow's emptiness or return to yesterday's dream?"

In another series he paints words both subjectively and objectively, and not without sly humor, drawing a bead on Rosa Bonheur, Picasso, Henri Cartier-Bresson, and others. Mr. Nadin will give a gallery talk at 11 A.M. on Thursday.

The center, at 1080 Chapel Street in New Haven, is open without charge Tuesday through Saturday from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M., and Sunday from noon to 5 P.M. Call 432-2800 for more information. A MOTHER'S LETTERS

It was quite shocking in 1855 for a woman of barely 30, or any age, to sail away on a Mystic clipper ship to Hawaii, even with her husband as captain. But that is what Mary Stark did, leaving the couple's three children, aged 5 through 11 behind, in the care of her husband's parents. It took Mrs. Stark and her husband, Henry, a year to round Cape Horn and arrive at the port of Honolulu.

Mary's letters to her children during that eventful year were unearthed by Richard Malley, registrar of the Connecticut Historical Society, in the G. W. Blunt Library at Mystic Seaport, while he was doing other research. He will give a talk titled "The Hawaiian Odyssey of Mary Stark" at the society's Hartford headquarters, 1 Elizabeth Street, Tuesday at 8 P.M. Admission will be $4, students $2. Call 236-5621 for more information. MUSICAL ANNIVERSARIES

With the various centennials of the births or deaths of Mozart (1756-1791), Bach (1685-1750), Handel (1685-1759) and Heinrich Schutz (1585-1672) celebrated in recent years, could the tricentennial of the birth of Giuseppe Tartini not be acknowledged?

A selection of the works of Tartini (1692-1770) -- a violinist, composer, teacher and inventor who lived in Padua -- will be performed in his honor on Wednesday at 8:15 P.M. in the Trinity College Chapel, 300 Summit Street, Hartford.

Participating in the concert will be the Trinity College Community Orchestra Chamber Players, conducted by Prof. Douglas Johnson; the violoncello soloist will be Susan Moses, from Padua.

Tartini invented a violin bow and taught violin to many students. Among his compositions are 42 violin sonatas, 12 sonatas for violin and violoncello, and 135 concertos for violin and other instruments. Admission to the concert is free. ROMANCE IN ART

"Too much romance is a litle suspect, but I'm a sucker for it," said Robert Cronin, a sculptor whose work is in the current exhibition called "Matters of the Heart" at the Mattatuck Museum in Waterbury. He will be joined by other exhibitors, including Jane Sutherland, Maureen McCabe, Elizabeth MacDonald and Toshio Odato, in a panel discussion about love and romance as depicted in art at 3 P.M. today in the museum.

Human relationships from every angle will be discussed in participation with the audience. The museum is at 144 West Main Street and admission is free. RELIGION AND SEX

"Religion and Sexuality in the 90's," a round-table discussion, will explore the many new avenues of human relationships that religious institutions may consider. It will take place on Wednesday from 6:30 to 9 P.M. in the Common Hall of the Divinity School at Yale.

Underlying the program is a comprehensive study on human sexuality recently completed by a committe of the Presbyterian Church, to be presented by Dr. John Carey, chairman of the committee. Serving on the panel will be Rabbi Selig Salkowitz, chairman of a committee on homosexuality and the rabbinate; the Rev. Thomas J. Barry, of the Connecticut Catholic Conference of the Archdiocese of Hartford, and Dr. Kenneth Wapnick, lecturer in a course in miracles, a New Age religious discipline. Thomas E. Brown, a clinical psychologist and formerly a member of the Yale Divinity School faculty, will be the moderator.

Registration costs $10 at the door, and a copy of the report titled "Presbyterians and Human Sexuality 1991" is available for $6. Call 797-4320 for more information.
