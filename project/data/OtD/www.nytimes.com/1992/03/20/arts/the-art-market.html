The Art Market

By Carol Vogel
Published: March 20, 1992

To the Victor a Millet

Just how far will Sotheby's outdo Christie's to get a plum? Even when times were good, the struggle between the two top auction houses, sometimes for a single picture, was fierce. But with the spring sales in New York only two months away, obtaining great works of art appears to require a good deal of ingenuity.

On May 28, Sotheby's will be auctioning Jean-Francois Millet's 1870 "Woman Churning Butter" in Manhattan. The painting, which is expected to fetch $2 million to $3 million, is being sold by the public library in Malden, Mass., to benefit its fund drive for a building and expansion program.

The library's art collection, which includes works by Winslow Homer and Frank Benson, was acquired with money given by the shoe manufacturer Elisha Slade Converse, who not only financed the library building, but also set up a fund specifically to buy artworks. Of all the library's holdings, art experts consider the Millet special, a late example of one of the artist's two most famous images. It is also fresh to the market and therefore one of the stars of Sotheby's May sale of 19th-century European paintings and sculpture. The Millet is also a good sales tool for the auction house: by telling prospective consignors it will be in the sale, Sotheby's hopes to attract other renowned works.

Representatives of both Sotheby's and Christie's visited the Malden Public Library, which is housed in a landmark H. H. Richardson building. And both auction houses presented proposals to the library's board of trustees. What made the library choose Sotheby's over Christie's, experts say, was Sotheby's offer of a sizable donation to the library.

"Part of Sotheby's proposal did include a certain sum, a gift for the capital campaign," said the director of the library, Dina Malgeri. After checking with the head of the board of trustees, however, she declined to comment on the exact donation or the terms of the agreement.

"I don't discuss the financial transactions of our consignors," said Diana D. Brooks, president of Sotheby's in North America. "We have had a long and good relationship with the library. Two years ago, we sold a Turner for them. Over a three- or four-year period, we have given the library advice on many of their pictures, including conservation. Seven of us went up to give the board our presentation."

Mrs. Brooks said that they had offered the library an "attractive financial package" and that Sotheby's, drawing on commissions, had previously made gifts to charities or other institutions that are consignors. "But it depends on the commission arrangements," she said. "We tend to look at every situation differently and look for new ways to do things if we need to be creative."

Christopher Burge, president of Christie's in New York, said that the house had made the library a straightforward offer. "Obviously we were keen to get the business," he said. "But we never considered giving a donation to the library." A Barnes Issue in Court

Trustees of the Barnes Foundation in Merion, Pa., are organizing a traveling exhibition of about 80 paintings from the museum's collection, including works by Renoir, Degas, Monet and Cezanne. The exhibition is yet another cause of dissension between the Barnes trustees and the De Mazia Trust, which is suing the foundation for violating its charter.

This will be the first time the outstanding collection of Impressionist and Post-Impressionist works will have left its eccentric home in Merion. But the exhibition will take place only if the Montgomery County Orphans' Court, an oddly named surrogate court for the county, agrees to allow the Barnes Foundation to break the 1922 indenture set up by its founder, Dr. Albert C. Barnes, which states that none of his collection may go on tour. The trustees say they are about to file a court petition.

If the court allows the tour, the traveling show will open at the National Gallery in Washington by spring 1993. Then, the trustees say, they are hoping to send the exhibition to the Musee d'Orsay in Paris, and on to a museum yet to be determined in Tokyo, and finally to the Philadelphia Museum of Art. The show is expected to raise more than $7 million to modernize the Barnes Foundation's 1926 building, says a member of the museum's board. During the tour, the museum will be closed for renovations. Digging for Treasure

All told, 1,164 doughnuts and 1,700 cups of coffee were consumed last Sunday at Christie's East in Manhattan. It was the final day of a free appraisal weekend, three days during which 4,000 people carried in treasures ranging from a lock of George Washington's hair (undocumented) to a rare series of the photographer Alfred Stieglitz's "Equivalents" to a vest Clark Gable wore in "Gone With the Wind."

"New Yorkers are remarkable," said Kathleen Guzman, president of Christie's East. "Many people stood in line for four hours waiting to see an expert."

This was the first free appraisal weekend the auction house has held in five years. By Sunday evening, it had been consigned works with a total estimated value of $1 million. A Guggenheim in Spain
