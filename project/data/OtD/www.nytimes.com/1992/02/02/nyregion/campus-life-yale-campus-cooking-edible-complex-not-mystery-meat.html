CAMPUS LIFE: Yale; Campus Cooking: 'Edible Complex,' Not Mystery Meat


Published: February 2, 1992

Tired of flaccid vegetables and mystery meat in the college cafeteria? Then imagine a stimulating meal of Nietzsche's Nachos, I Think Therefore I Ham Salad and Multicultural Tuna, culminating with the Banana Split Personality -- enough for two.

These are just some of the less-than-scholarly offerings in "Tray Gourmet: Be Your Own Chef in the College Cafeteria," a new cookbook from Lake Isle Press written and illustrated by three 1990 Yale University graduates.

Based on reports from cafeterias at more than 40 colleges and universities nationwide, the recipes call for foods and equipment that are readily available in most of the food halls of academe. Hundreds of recipes are divided among 14 chapters with names such as "Conan the Vegetarian" and the "Edible Complex." The meat chapter includes a full page of "delicious cafeteria meatloaf recipes" -- which is blank.

Lynn Harris, a humanities major from Lexington, Mass., and Lawrence Berger, an English major from Ithaca, N.Y., said they tinkered with cafeteria food throughout their undergraduate years.

"Friends who asked incessantly for our secrets finally inspired us to write a cookbook," said Ms. Harris, a freelance writer and vice president for public relations for the Boston chapter of the National Organization for Women.

Christopher Kalb, an art major from Hattboro, Pa., signed on to design a layout and draw illustrations. Mr. Kalb won the 1988 Charles M. Schulz award, a national journalism award given by the Scripps-Howard Foundation to the most promising college cartoonist in the country.

At first, Ms. Harris said, they had difficulty convincing publishers that a market existed for their idea because cafeterias equipped with salad bars and microwave ovens -- essential raw materials for "tray gourmet" cuisine -- are relatively new.

"I'm not saying that dining halls today are like a five-star restaurant," Ms. Harris said, "but dining hall managers have really responded to student demand. Grownups have trouble with this concept. They cannot get past their nightmares of the college food of yesteryear."

What are the authors' favorite selections? Mr. Berger, a Rhodes Scholar at Oxford University, said he favors Fond-o-You Cheese Fondue. "It's the most social," he said. "People will gather around and start to speak French and read poetry and stuff."

Mr. Kalb, a designer for a newspaper in Pennsylvania, said he prefers Cliffs Oats, "the breakfast of crammers," whose ingredients include cinnamon, honey, Bilbo Baggins, Hester Prynne, Odysseus and other literary figures.

"The entanglement of the ingredients in the oatmeal establishes profound conflicts on several levels, literal and figurative, internal and external," the recipe explains. "Its glorious emergence from the microwave -- the womb of technology -- can be read as an allegory for the possibility of culinary regeneration and rebirth through technological progress."

But the book, the authors say, can be read for its practical recommendations. It includes, for example, nutrition and dieting information.

But Ms. Harris admitted, "Sometimes the title did come before the recipe," although sometimes neither made it into print -- Macroeconomics and Cheese, for example. "We really wanted to do that one," she said.
