Pop Ph.D.'s: How TV Ate Academics

Brian Raszka

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Save Article
By JEFF P. LEWIS
Published: January 8, 2006

Skip to next paragraph

Forum: Contemporary Education



Go to Special Section
Correction Appended

TODAY'S academics, many of them Gen X or baby boomers, are far more tolerant of popular culture than previous generations, says Lynn Schofield Clark, a communications professor at the University of Colorado, Boulder, whose research centers on mass media and its audiences. But she is quick to qualify, saying, "But only when it's being employed as a bridge to ideas that are relevant, timeless and important."

Perhaps nothing reflects this trend more than the eclectic dissertation topics of Ph.D.'s - research interests that eventually filter into the classroom.

In her own thesis, Dr. Clark studied religious symbols and adolescents, "analyzing the interpretive strategies that teens brought to such popular television programs as 'Touched by an Angel.' "

Here are others topics:

"Audience Reception Study of the 'Survivor' Series," Richard Crew, chairman, communications department, College Misericordia (Pennsylvania).

"From the Village Blacksmith to Mr. Good Wrench: Creating Auto Mechanics in Technology's Middle Ground," Kevin L. Borg, assistant professor of history, James Madison University (Virginia).

"Myth of Communion: Rhetorical Analysis of the Narratives of Alien Abductees," Stephanie Kelley-Romano, assistant professor of rhetoric, Bates College (Maine).

"Flash to Cash: Producing Rap Authenticity, 1979 to 1995," Jennifer Lena, assistant professor of sociology, Vanderbilt University (Nashville).

"Unveiled: The Emotion Work of Wedding Coordinators in the American Wedding Industry," Angela Thompson, professor of sociology, Texas Christian University.

"Urban Jungles: Zoos and American Society," Jeffrey Hyson, assistant professor of history, St. Joseph's University (Philadelphia).

"Lowrider Space" (as in low-riding cars), Ben Chappell, assistant professor of sociology/cultural studies, Bridgewater College (Virginia).

"Doing Gender With Santa: Gender-Typing in Children's Toy Preferences," Greta E. Pennell, associate professor of teacher education, University of Indianapolis.

"Spaghetti Dinners and Fireflies in a Jar: The Shaping of Paradoxical Places and Spaces in Disney's Celebration," Jennifer Prough, instructor of humanities and anthropology, Valparaiso University (Indiana).

"Reading Culture, Engendering Girls: The Politics of the Everyday in the Production of Girls' Manga" (Japanese comics), Andrew Wood, associate professor of communications, San Jose State University.

Correction:

Because of an editing error, a different article in the section, about dissertations dealing with popular culture, transposed the names of two authors: Andrew Wood, a professor at San Jose State University, is author of "Spaghetti Dinners and Fireflies in a Jar: The Shaping of Paradoxical Places and Spaces in Disney's Celebration." Jennifer Prough, an instructor at Valparaiso University, is author of "Reading Culture, Engendering Girls: The Politics of the Everyday in the Production of Girls' Manga."
