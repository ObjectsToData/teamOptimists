'Nicholas Miraculous,' by Michael Rosenthal

An Empty Robe

Review by THOMAS MALLON
Published: January 22, 2006

NICHOLAS MIRACULOUS
The Amazing Career of the Redoubtable Dr. Nicholas Murray Butler.
By Michael Rosenthal.
Illustrated. 528 pp. Farrar, Straus & Giroux. $35.

Forum: Book News and Reviews

IN her book "Manhattan '45," Jan Morris imagined Columbia University's aging president, Dr. Nicholas Murray Butler: "So solemn, so imposing. It does not surprise us in the least to know that he holds 37 honorary degrees, that he has been decorated by 15 governments, that he is a member of 20 clubs. He has won the Nobel Peace Prize, of course. He has been a confidant of many presidents."

Butler's widely supposed sagacity prompted The New York Times to call him, on different occasions, "the incarnation of the international mind" and "Prime Minister of the Republic of the Intellect," accolades that only add to the perplexity of his new biographer. How, wonders Michael Rosenthal, a professor of the humanities at Columbia, did Butler achieve such "instant oblivion" so quickly after his death in 1947? More to the point, a reader of Rosenthal's conscientious biography may end up asking how Butler, an empty robe far more ridiculous than "miraculous" (the book's subtitle derives from another of Butler's sobriquets), managed to hoodwink so many otherwise discerning observers for so many decades.

An Anglophile philosopher with a phony posh accent, Butler was in fact, Rosenthal explains, a "parvenu from Paterson." The Columbia College from which he took a bachelor's degree in 1882 was in those days an East Side institution of rote learning for local boys. Standards were low, and the library "never remained open for longer than an hour and a half each morning," yet Butler felt inspired to serious grade-grubbing and all kinds of extra-curricular eager-beavering. The zealous undergraduate quickly proceeded to a Ph.D. and then a position as "fellow and assistant" in the department of philosophy, ethics and psychology. His lectures were often organized to the point of dullness, but Butler pushed himself to the fore with a notion that education itself might be a fit subject for study. He helped found Columbia's Teachers College in 1887 and served for several years as its president.

During the 1890's, using the press, his personal connections and a rhetorical style as puffed as his own ambitions, Butler succeeded in a campaign to centralize the New York City public schools - in its day, a reform position that shifted their control from Tammany Democrats to liberal-minded Republicans. Up in Morningside Heights, to which Columbia moved in 1897, Butler sided with those wishing to subordinate the undergraduate college within a first-class research university. In 1902, when this sea change was irretrievably underway, he succeeded the patrician Seth Low as the institution's 12th president.

Butler began to recruit stars for his faculty, landing John Dewey but failing to get Henry James. In these days before tenure, lesser lights among the professoriate were bossed around by presidential will and whim. To establish all this, Rosenthal must reproduce some tiny tempests in dollhouse teacups, his protagonist hurling paper clips instead of thunderbolts, but each incident contributes to the reader's growing sense that Butler was less a self-made sage than a natural-born blowhard.

His most creative involvement with the undergraduate college seems to have come in searching for ways to keeps its Jewish enrollment down. He considered having applicants take physicals that would "find grounds to eliminate socially unappealing Jews smart enough to have passed the entrance examination," and throughout the 1930's he funneled Jewish students into an affiliated two-year college in Brooklyn. Its courses were "taught largely by junior faculty members from Morningside Heights," and the dropout rate was enormous. When it closed after 10 years, Butler at last gave up on "the Hebrew problem."

Rosenthal concedes his subject a "deserved reputation as a dynamic university builder," but he also supplies the reader with plenty of presidential neglect and fecklessness. In the face of the university's "severe undercapitalization," Butler was slow to adopt modern fund-raising techniques, and Columbia's successful partnership with Presbyterian Hospital came to pass only when he was removed from the negotiations. However lucrative the deal by which Columbia leased its 11 acres of Midtown property for the construction of Rockefeller Center, the university failed to protect the resulting rental income against inflation.

Correction: Jan. 22, 2006, Sunday:

A review on Page 15 of the Book Review today, about "Nicholas Miraculous," a biography of Nicholas Murray Butler, president of Columbia University from 1902 to 1945, misidentifies the city where he received the Nobel Peace Prize for 1931. The Peace Prize was awarded, as it normally is, in Oslo, not Stockholm.
