ART NOUVEAU:A RECURRING THEME

By VIVIEN RAYNOR
Published: March 8, 1981

ART nouveau writhes again - at the William Benton Museum (University of Connecticut at Storrs, through March 15). Numbering more than 100 posters and drawings, this show, called ''La Belle Epoque,'' is one of several cultural events associated with the ''Belgium Today'' program, which, inaugurated last year, celebrated the 150th anniversary of Belgium's independence. As such, it is sponsored by the National Endowments for both the Arts and the Humanities, as well as the Belgian-American Educational Foundation and other bodies, public and private, and is circulated by the International Exhibitions Foundation.

The focus is on three artists - Gisbert Combaz, Leo Jo and Privat Livemont, none of whose names are likely to ring bells with American viewers. Drawn from the collection of M. and Mme. Wittamer-DeCamps, the works were selected by their daughter, Yolande Oostens-Wittamer, who also wrote the catalogue.

Combaz (1869-1941) practiced briefly as a lawyer before becoming a designer and a teacher. Evidently well-known as an Oriental scholar, the artist produced several lithographed posters advertising exhibitions staged by La Libre Esthetique. This was one of several Belgian organizations devoted to exhibiting Belgian as well as foreign artists such as Monet, Gauguin, Cezanne and Matisse.

Early lithographs show Combaz working with a few flat decorative shapes not unlike those favored by the English Beggarstaff Brothers. He then moved into a cloisonne style of patterned images - birds, figures, land and seascapes - enclosed by a dark, unvarying line. After the year 1906, marked by a poster of a blue fishing boat on a greenish sea showered with Divisionist dots in contrasting colors, the artist seems to have concentrated on blending cloisonnism and pointillism, notably in some gouaches of oversized flowers (orchids, it appears, have been to Belgium what tulips have been to Holland).

A competent draftsman, Combaz used stock art nouveau motifs, but his technique is too burly and his colors are too rich for him to be regarded as typical of the era. His best works here are the lush designs for friezes and tiles -inventive, beautifully colored arrangements of entwined plant and animal forms.

A year younger than Combaz, Leo Jo, who was born Leontine Joris (1870-1962), was a painter of landscapes and still lifes but is, in this case, epitomized by posters and gouache caricatures, most of them undated. Plagiarizing Lautrec shamelessly, the artist concentrated on streetwalkers and their customers, along with the old, the fat or the overdressed - or all three. The cartoons, which apparently were published in magazines, come with jocular captions. What Jo lacked in originality she made up for in gusto.

A prolific poster lithographer, Privat Livemont was the oldest, the gentlest and the most art nouveau of the three. His approach resembles that of Alphonse Mucha, whose work he could have seen while studying in Paris. Not that the similarity is more than skin deep, for Livemont had a few tricks of his own. One was to model the faces and limbs of his pale maidens, who advertise cacao, corsets, cosmetics et al, and, encasing them in stylized robes, to silhouette them sharply against flat, decorative backgrounds, as if making a collage.

The exhibition contains much interesting work, including some straight life studies by Livemont, but it scarcely represents the cream of the movement, which flourished in most European countries in the last decade of the 19th century and the first of the 20th. Moreover, the catalogue, which teems with facts (some of them more suitable for a chamber of commerce publication), makes tedious reading.

All of which is rather mysterious, for though art nouveau did not originate in Belgium, it reached its highest peak there, especially in the realm of architecture. The Wittamer-DeCampses themselves own one of Victor Horta's most sublime creations, the Hotel Solvay in Brussels, built between 1895 and 1900. On the other hand, by focusing on lesser work, the show conveys all the better the popularity of art nouveau. It gives an idea, too, of what life must have been like in surroundings from which the right angle was virtually banished.

The movement in a way defies historical analysis, for though expressed mainly through architecture and the applied arts, its sinuous shapes occur in the canvases of Gauguin, Van Gogh, Toulouse-Lautrec and others. And, while it developed to some extent out of William Morris's ''socialistic'' attempts to improve the quality of mass-produced objets, it reeked of dandyism (those long-stemmed lilies were, after all, carried by the divine Oscar).

In France, especially, art nouveau was, with Symbolism and Divisionism, a reaction against the naturalism of Impressionist painting. But its images, stylized as they are, come straight from nature. Furthermore, if Japanese prints were a major influence on the style, there are forerunners like Celtic ornament and the works of William Blake to be reckoned with, not to mention the fantasies of Odilon Redon and Gustave Moreau.
