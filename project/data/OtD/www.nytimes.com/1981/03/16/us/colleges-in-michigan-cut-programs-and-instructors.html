COLLEGES IN MICHIGAN CUT PROGRAMS AND INSTRUCTORS

By IVER PETERSON, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 16, 1981

EAST LANSING, Mich., March 15— While hard times and high unemployment are pushing record numbers of young people onto the state campuses in Michigan, economic problems are forcing the state's 13 public universities and colleges to dismiss faculty members, eliminate entire programs and even to reduce enrollments.

The state's allocation for public higher education was cut by more than $100 million for this year, in the first time since the Depression that state support for higher education had declined. With a prospective state budget deficit of $150 million still facing Gov. William G. Milliken, the outlook for rescuing by the state of the beleaguered campuses is considered so slim that major institutions have begun planning to reduce their course offerings to preserve the quality of remaining programs. And these programs are threatened by budget deficits.

Nowhere in the 228,000-student system have the shocks of Michigan's economic decline been more keenly felt than here on the campus of the State University of Michigan, where the institution's president, M. Cecil Mackey, is faced with a need to cut more than $29 million from his own deficit-ridden budget this year. His board of trustees are to vote in two weeks on his proposals to eliminate the School of Nursing entirely, along with the Department of Humanities, drastically reduce the veterinarian program, and effect lesser cuts in all other departments of the campus.

Altogether, more than 70 teachers, half of them tenured, would lose their jobs along with hundreds of employees in clerical and administrative positions at M.S.U. Defending Proposed Cuts

The proposed cuts have made life hard for Dr. Mackey. He spent four hours Friday morning listening to criticism of his plans from students and faculty members at an open hearing on the proposed cuts, and Friday afternoon he was in Ingham County Court defending his plans against a faculty lawsuit charging that they violate tenure laws. More suits, particularly from nursing students facing displacement to other campuses, are expected to follow.

On other public campuses in the Middle West, where the slump in automobile, steel and rubber production is most severe, there are problems of rising costs and restricted state support. But the debate on those campuses extends primarily to how much the tuition should be raised to meet the need for more money; in Michigan, tuitions are already among the highest in the country, and although tuitions are certain to rise again, the increases will not be enough to offset current levels of overspending.

The tuition for Michigan State University's 44,000 students, for example, has been raised nine times in the last decade, from $630 a year for undergraduates in 1970 to $1,360 this year. Eliminating the campus deficit by raising fees alone, Dr. Mackey said, would require ''a horrendous increase'' that would defeat the university's effort to remain open to students of moderate means. Reductions in Programs

The administration at the University of Michigan, in Ann Arbor, expects an $11.1 million budget deficit for the current fiscal year ending June 30, and has proposed eliminating the geography program, reducing adult extension programs and eliminating 520 teaching and administrative positions.

The 46,000-student school is regarded as financially stronger than the old land-grant school here at Michigan State, however, because of its stronger, more generous and more politically active alumni association. At Michigan State, the only political intervention in Dr. Mackey's proposed cuts came from the state's black political community, which rescued the school's urban affairs program from the proposed program reductions.

Wayne State University, the public system's 44,000-student campus in Detroit, is in similar trouble. About $3.5 million will be cut from its offerings this year and $3 million more is to be eliminated next year by cutting 166 teaching positions. A court challenge prevented the administration from carrying through its plans to put its nonteaching staff on a one-week payless furlough this spring, and administrators at Wayne State complain that the certainty of similar legal challenges to pay cuts leaves the elimination of jobs as the only alternative in cutting costs.

At Michigan Technical University in Houghton, a 7,700-student enrollment will be cut 200 to 300 students next year, despite high interest in the campus's engineering and computer programs.
