PAPERBACK TALK

By Ray Walters
Published: June 27, 1981

Continued stories. This week, like many weeks, the interesting news from Publishers Row consists of sequels to tales told in the past. To wit:

Dollar gns. A year ago, we reported that Bantam Books had bought at auction, for an all-time high of $3,200,000, the paperback reprint rights to Judith Krantz's ''Princess Daisy.'' Such deals make tasty grist for the gossip columnists' mills, but many members of the book community said they thought things were getting out of hand, for they tie up great amounts of capital that might be used to publish works of literary quality that lack ''blockbuster'' ingredients.

What's happened since? That ''Princess Daisy'' sale hasn't been topped, nor does it seem likely to for a long time to come. Louis Wolfe, new head man at Bantam, attributes his house's relatively poor profit performance during 1979 in part to the large sums spent for advances and says that a ceiling will be held on future purchases. Other publishers, sobered by the current state of the economy, indicate they'll be playing the reprint-rights game more cautiously.

The biggest purchaser of late seems to be Fawcett, which paid $1,633,000 at auction for Phil Donahue's ''Donahue'' and $1,045,000 for Jeffrey Archer's ''Kane & Abel.'' Other books bought ''on option'' include Erma Bombeck's ''Aunt Erma's Cope Book'' (''near $2,000,000'') and Alistair MacBain's yet-to-be-published ''Athabasca'' (''in the seven figures'').

Three other houses cracked the million-dollar barrier recently. Erich Segal's ''Man, Woman, and Child'' went to Ballantine for ''over $1 million,'' Mary Higgins Clark's ''The Cradle Will Fall'' to Dell for ''over $1,250,000,'' and Avery Corman's forthcoming ''The Old Neighborhood'' to Bantam for $1,450,000.

Wills. Fourteen years ago, long before there was such a thing as a trade paperback best seller list, a curious ''outsized pamphlet'' claimed a space among the hardcover books on the best seller list and was acclaimed the best-selling nonfiction work of 1966, surpassing even Masters and Johnson's ''Human Sexual Response.'' During its 47-week run on our best seller list, ''How to Avoid Probate'' by Norman F. Dacey, a Connecticut estate counselor, sold 613,169 copies.

At the time, the book raised the hackles of many bar associations, for it not only told laymen how to set up their own estates without legal counsel but recounted horror stories about probate courts and lawyers growing rich at the expense of widows and children. The New York County Lawyers Association was so upset that it got Mr. Dacey convicted for contempt for ''unauthorized practice of law,'' an act later reversed.

Now, at age 72, Mr. Dacey is on the warpath with a new version, ''How to Avoid Probate - Updated!'' (Crown, $14.95), a paperback almost as hefty as the Manhattan telephone directory, swelled by hundreds of legal forms and tables. This time, as Mr. Dacey tours the country to promote its sale, he's finding a more cordial response from consumers' groups and even some law organizations. In the 10 weeks it's been in the stores, it's sold 100,000 copies.

Mr. Dacey contends that instead of writing a will -which may have to make a long, costly journey through a probate court - the ordinary citizen ought to place his assets in a ''living trust'' for future beneficiaries and name a trustee. However, even some sharp critics of the legal profession have some caveats about Mr. Dacey's book. All those forms and tables are enough to scare off all but the hardiest do-it-yourselfer. And it's doubtful that a living trust will be of much benefit to married people whose estate totals less than $175,000.

For old eyes. A few weeks ago, we reported the plans of G.K. Hall & Co. of Boston to issue softcovered editions of recent popular novels printed in large type. Eleanor Touhey Smith of Manhattan, who's a librarian as well as a senior citizen, tells us that she found this good news, but hardly the last word on the reading problems of the elderly.

''The majority of people over 65,'' she says, ''can usually have their visual problems solved with proper eyeglasses. As a librarian, I've observed that people who have been enthusiastic readers all their lives will continue to read as they grow older - the same kind of books they've read for pleasure.

''Most surveys show that the majority of older people in this country, like their children and grandchildren, are not avid book readers. It is not easy to turn nonreaders into readers when they're old, but they can be motivated to use books that contain information they need in their daily lives.'' To this end, she's prepared an annotated list of more than 270 paperbacks on health, money matters, the humanities, arts and crafts, sports and hobbies, and many other topics. The list may be obtained free of charge by any organization - senior center, club, housing project or public library - requesting it from Vincent Agnifilo, Bookazine Co., 303 W. 10th St., New York, N.Y. 10014.

Cover art. Never underestimate the lengths to which an art director will go to create a cover that will make one of his paperbacks stand out from others in the racks. As of now, the ultimate dazzler is on Pocket Books' ''Fever Pitch,'' a family saga by Betty Ferm. The glowing blue title is in what the trade calls ''metallic stamping,'' the glittering red background is in ''dayglow,'' and there's a face peering out through a gash in the cover. Put on your sunglasses when you approach it.
