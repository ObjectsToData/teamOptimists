TRIAL BUD GET PLAN WOULD CUT FURTHER INTO EDUCATION FUNDS

By JONATHAN FUERBRINGER, Special to the New York Times
Published: September 11, 1981

WASHINGTON, Sept. 10— The Reagan Administration has drafted a trial budget resolution for the fiscal year 1982 that would require further cuts in education and other programs.

According to the draft now circulating on Capitol Hill, funds for elementary and secondary education would be cut about $700 million below the reduced level already approved by Congress. Funds for the arts and humanities would also be reduced.

At the same time, the resolution would set up a mechanism, in many cases, to insure that the lower spending level would prevail in cases where the House and the Senate approved different amounts in pending appropriation bills. In other cases spending levels would be cut to those proposed by the President in his original budget package unveiled in March.

Termed 'Purely Technical'

An Office of Management and Budget spokesman, Edwin L. Dale Jr., said tonight that the draft was ''purely technical'' and had been done at the request of Congress's appropriations committees. The document, Mr. Dale said, represents ''no policy decisions.''

But Congressional sources who provided the document suggested that it could be a first step by the Administration to get billions of dollars of further cuts approved in an overall resolution. While acknowledging that the document is only a draft, the sources said the deeper cuts included could be a suggestion to Congress of what the Administration wants.

As the Administration continued today to work on the billions of dollars in budget cuts needed to close the deficit gap in 1982 and to balance the budget by the fiscal year 1984, Alice M. Rivlin, the director of the Congressional Budget Office, said that even with all the budget cuts approved or now promised by the Administration, the budget deficit in 1982 could exceed $65 billion, more than $20 billion higher than the President's forecast.

In addition, Miss Rivlin said that the Administration would face a $50 billion deficit in 1984, despite all the additional budget cuts assumed in the President's economic recovery program. Wide Reductions Feared

In testimony before the House Budget Committee, Miss Rivlin said the task of reaching a balanced budget by 1984 could be so difficult that, if the Administration stuck to its general defense spending commitment and did not make further cuts in programs like Social Security, Federal pensions and public assistance, almost all other domestic Federal programs would have to be eliminated to reach that goal.

The Budget Office projections are another indication of the difficult job the Administration faces in trying to reach its budgetary goals.

Although the deficit projections far outstrip those made by the Administration, the economic assumptions underlying the projections, except for interest rates, are not widely different and imply that even little changes in outlook could throw the Administration off track.

At an afternoon briefing, Larry M. Speakes, the deputy White House press secretary, disagreed with the Budget Office's deficit projections. ''The critical thing,'' Mr. Speakes said, ''is that they assume a passive, business-as-usual response by the Administration.'' For Continuing Operations

The draft budget resolution prepared by the Office of Management and Budget and now circulating on Capitol Hill is known as a continuing budget resolution. It is expected to be needed to continue Government spending after Oct. 1, the beginning of the new fiscal year.

Usually, the continuing resolution is used to keep the Government running at present levels for a month or so in a new fiscal year, until individual appropriation bills are approved by Congress.

But this year, officials have indicated that the Administration might use the resolution, instead of the individual appropriation bills, to make the billions of dollars of additional cuts the Administration now needs to keep to its deficit goal of $42.5 billion for 1982. In a further effort to make the resolution a real vehicle for budget cuts, the Administration draft would be effective through mid-February.

Representative Silvio O. Conte, Republican of Massachusetts, the ranking Republican on the House Appropriations Committee, said today that he thought he had blocked any Administration effort in this direction. Mr. Conte s aid in a telephone interview that he and the committee chairman, Jam ie L. Whitten, Democrat of Mississippi, had ''buried that.'' He ad ded that he planned to offer his own continuing budget resolution. The Cuts Needed This Year

In her testimony, Miss Rivlin emphasized that there was much more budget cutting needed this year than many people might have expected.

Miss Rivlin said that in addition to about $35 billion in cuts approved by Congress this summer, there were reductions of $15 billion that Congress assumed in its budget ceiling of $695.5 billion but has not yet ma de.

If these cuts are not approved, Miss Rivlin's deficit projection would rise from $65 billion to about $80 billion, she said. This means that Congress and the President, if the Budget Office forecast is correct, are faced with finding and approving $37 billion more in cuts just to get to President Reagan's $42.5 billion deficit figure for the fiscal year 1982.

The main reason for the higher deficit projections by the Budget Office is a combination of lower revenue projections and higher spending rates, especially for the military and interest-related costs. Key Factor in Interest Rates

The big difference between the Budget Office economic forecast and the Administration's is on interest rates. The Budget Office forecasts an annual average rate of 12.4 percent in 1982, for example, and 10.1 percent in 1984 on three-month Treasury bills. The Administration forecast is 10.5 percent in 1982 and 6.8 percent in 1984.

From 1982 to 1984, the higher interest assumption adds about $40 billion to the Budget Office spending and deficit projections, Miss Rivlin said.

Except for this difference, the forecasts are relatively close. The Budget Office, on a calendar year basis, puts inflation, as measured by the gross national product price deflator, at 7.7 percent in 1982 and 6.6 percent in 1984.

On unemployment, the Budget Office forecasts an average 7.3 percent rate in calendar year 1982 and 6.5 percent in 1984. The Administration numbers are 7.3 percent in 1982 and 6.2 percent in 1984.

The Budget Office also sees almost as much real economic growth, after adjustment for inflation, as the Administration. In 1982 the Budget Office expects the real gross national product to grow 3.1 percent on average, as against 3.4 percent in the Administration forecast. In 1984, the Budget Office sees 4 percent growth while the Administration projects 4.5 percent.
