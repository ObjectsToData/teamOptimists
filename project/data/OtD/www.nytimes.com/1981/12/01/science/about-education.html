ABOUT EDUCATION

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: December 1, 1981

STANFORD, Calif. ''IAM really excited about this: It looks like the dawn of a new era for university— industry cooperation,'' says James Meindl, codirector of Stanford University's new Center for Integrated Systems (C.I.S.). He was talking about what, for better or for worse, seems to be a preview of the future of the nation's research universities.

The new center will merge the interests of the university and industry in ways that the academic world might have considered unthinkable, or at least undesirable, only a few years ago - and that some academic observers still view with alarm. The center will not only train graduates in virtually all aspects of the electronic revolution and engage in research from silicon chips to computer software, but it will do much of this in the presence and with the active cooperation of representatives from private industries.

Mr. Meindl, a youthful-looking, crewcut scientist, got his first taste of electronics in 1959 as a second lieutenant at Fort Monmouth, N.J. He speaks with fascination of ''the ubiquitous silicon chip,'' the fingernail-sized device ''that is really a system'' with the ultimate capacity to contain one million to 100 million transistors in a single chip.

The center is already in operation in half a dozen temporary locations, but its 17,000-square-foot home is securely on the drawing board and scheduled for completion in June 1983. Its sponsors will be at least 16 industrial companies, each of which will pay an entry fee of $750,000 over a three-year period. Fifteen have already signed up. The initial capital of $12 million will be used to construct the central laboratory. Surrounding it will be satellite laboratories, all within two minutes' walking distance from the center, which thus will become the hub of a network of laboratories.

The sponsors already signed up are: General Electric, Hewlett-Packard, TRW Inc., Northrop, Xerox, Fairchild Camera and Instrument, Honeywell, Tektronix, Digital Equipment, International Business Machines, Texas Instruments, International Telephone & Telegraph, Intel Corporation, General Telephone and Electronics, and Motorola. In addition, the center has an $8 million Defense Department contract.

The work, particularly with silicon chips, Mr. Meindl says, promises an almost unlimited extension of computer capacity and impact. He cites as examples complex pocket calculators, the size of a credit card, the memory chip that constitutes the brain of a cruise missile, the controls of the space shuttle or new reading aids for the blind. ''There's no end in sight,'' he says.

What, in addition to a new and consistent flow of funds, will the university gain from the center? Staffed by 30 faculty members and 250 Ph.D. candidates (all of whom will retain their appointments and affiliation with their regular departments), the center will produce 100 masters degrees and 30 Ph.D.'s a year. It means new appointments of first-rate people that could not otherwise have been made, and these appointments in turn lead to new and larger contracts as well as Federal money. In addition, top-level representatives of the companies will offer seminars.

The center, says John Linvill, the other co-director, is a unique environment for graduate students because it puts them into the latest range of technological research, in contact with selected members of the industrial world.

What do the corporations get out of the partnership? ''What we can offer the sponsors,'' says Mr. Meindl, ''is enhanced access at an earlier time.'' The center will have space for full-time representatives from the 16 corporations. ''What we really offer is lead time in access to new research and to talented people,'' he added. ''What they get is a very good window on a more than $12 million-a-year research program.''

Gerald Lieberman, dean of graduate studies and research, puts it more bluntly: ''What they are really paying for is a first look at our graduate students.'' Such human talent is considered a virtually priceless asset for advanced electronics industries.

While great mutual advantages are indisputable, questions are nevertheless raised about the effect of such close proximity of the world of the scholar to that of the marketplace. Some observers worry about the growth of an academic-industrial complex and what it may do to the free exchange of knowledge and discoveries among researchers. Will industrial sponsors, with eyes on markets and profits, try to stop the free flow of research findings which they might like to keep to themselves in order to beat the competition to the punch?

Although Mr. Meindl believes Stanford is currently ahead in this area, he readily concedes that what is at issue is a national trend. But, he insisted, the center's academic integrity is protected by what he called ''one nonnegotiable feature: we must have open access to all our research as in the past, without change or modification.'' In addition, he said, he hoped that ''our research will be on the fundamental side and will not be so close to the product to make this a problem.''

Other academic observers worry nevertheless that such super-centers within any campus may distort the university's total function. Outside science and technology, and particularly in the hard-pressed humanities, the vision of a futuristic complex with almost unlimited access to money and staff adds inevitably to the already serious concern, occasionally bordering on despair, over the future of those unsponsored fields of scholarship.

Since the fates of both university science and the nation's technological capacity are inextricably intertwined, there is probably no rational way of halting developments heralded at Stanford. Two preventive steps might nevertheless safeguard the independence and breadth of the universities' future: protection of academic freedom against inroads by competitive and profit-oriented industrial forces (which spokesmen at the Stanford center insist is firmly in place) and countervailing financing by governments, foundations and corporations of the nonscientific but no less crucial scholarship on the campuses.
