SCHOLARS SIGN ON WITH SEMIOTICS

By EDWARD ROTHSTEIN
Published: October 18, 1981

When Pioneer 10 was launched from Earth in 1972, it bore a plaque meant for extra-terrestrial beings. Scientists at the National Aeronautics and Space Administration wanted the imperishable goldcoated aluminum plate to be decipherable to any scientist, of any physiognomy, living anywhere in the universe.

The plaque contains a schematic drawing of a man and a woman drawn to scale in front of a sketch of the spacecraft; the man's hand is raised in greeting. A dumbbell figure pictures the hydrogen atom, to give a measure of time and space; it is the key to a star-figure, locating Earth relative to 14 radio-wave emitting pulsars. Circles below portray the solar system; a miniature Pioneer swerves between Jupiter and Saturn, soaring into the celestial beyond.

If ever some superior intelligence on some other world comes across this little picture, peering at it with whatever sensory apparatus it enjoys, the fact of the plaque will say more about humans than the message it presumably conveys. Why, after all, should an extraterrestrial ''scientist'' even recognize the plaque as a message? Why would it translate a diagram into a three-dimensional object? Why would it organize lines to create a picture? Why would it ask the questions - like where does this come from and who built it - that the plaque presumes to answer? The plaque is based on implicit human assumptions; it is even bound to the particular culture that created it. Not everywhere on Earth would a hand be raised in greeting, would a man's hair part on the side, or a woman's hair drift down below her shoulders.

The plaque is interesting because it illustrates - on an interstellar level, perhaps - questions about every act of communication. Signs are used to convey meaning, but their interpretation is based upon unexamined, often hidden relations. There was a time when language was considered natural, linked directly to the world, the way the ''species-proof'' Pioneer language claims to be. But most linguistic signs are now viewed as arbitrary inventions. What assumptions then, does a language embody in its arbitrariness? How is language related to culture? How is it linked to the world? How, in a broader sense, is any information communicated?

Such questions have been basic to many disciplines with the development of modernist culture - including mathematics, computer science, linguistics, anthropology, music and, especially, literature. But about a century ago, a field called ''semiotics'' developed which takes the study of signs and meaning as its focus. In more recent years, the field has grown and developed, spawning movements called ''structuralism,'' ''post-structuralism,'' ''deconstructionism'' and ''inter-textuality.'' Fashion and Debate

Such approaches have become fashionable and prestigious in university life. There is a 700-member Semiotic Society of America and a larger International Association for Semiotic Studies. In universities, readings in semiotics and ''advanced'' criticism stir more excitement than readings of Flaubert or Tolstoy. But the discipline and its claims have also excited unusually impassioned controversy, stimulating heated exchanges in intellectual journals and extending, even, to university appointments; in England, Cambridge University denied a lectureship to a

''structuralist'' professor, splitting up the university community in debate. ''Semiotics'' itself is a harmless enough word, deriving from the Greek ''semeion,'' or ''sign.'' In medical terminology, it has been used to mean ''interpretation of symptoms.'' ''Semiotics,'' then, is simply the study of signs; it is interested in anything that carries meaning.

Contemporary semiotics began around the beginning of the century, with the independent work of a Swiss linguist, Ferdinand de Saussure and the American philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce. Saussure argued that linguistics would be at the heart of the study of signs. Peirce established other categories, distinguishing, for example, between the way a clock indicates the time of the day and the way a badge indicates a policeman. His system yields 59,049 classes of signs, which fortunately reduces to a more manageable 66 classes.

Systems of Culture

But such signs - linguistic or otherwise - attain meaning only when participating in systems of relations -within language, within a literary text, within a culture. The ''structuralist'' movement - represented by such writers as the linguist Roman Jakobson and the anthropologist Claude Levi-Strauss - derives from concentrated comparisons of such systems. Semiotics may study any cultural phenomenon - how advertising communicates, how an algebraic formula is given meaning, how a novel works, how unconscious phenomena are expressed in neurosis. It claims to offer the humanities a chance to become as systematic and unified as the sciences, based upon a study of culture rather than of nature.

The semiotic approach has gradually become part of advanced education in literature, anthropology, history. It is even being taught in high schools, in an elective program set up 14 years ago by Donald W. Thomas at Brookline High School and used by 10 other schools in the Boston area. The first text in the program includes Helen Keller's account of learning how to name objects, an account of the wild child of Aveyron, selections from Jorge Louis Borges, Plato and other writers, and class experiments with language. Later texts explore animal communication and cultural anthropology.

There is an undergraduate major in semiotics offered at Brown University. The curriculum centers on the study of film, which Robert Scholes, the director of the program, calls ''the semiotic medium par excellance.'' Readings include Saussure, Levi-Strauss and such philosophers and critics as Jacques Derrida, Michel Foucault and Roland Barthes. ''We are trying,'' Mr. Scholes said, ''to encourage a rather rigorous system of thought, based on a body of work that hasn't yet settled down.''

But critics of the semiotic approach contend that the system is far from rigorous and its claims too universal. Moreover, some critics say, semiotics undermines the classical humanistic education - teaching about communication while neglecting the discipline necessary to communicate. In a semiotics course, comic books are as likely to be read as Cervantes and history may be less important than speculative analysis.

In fact, as the study of signs has become more self-conscious, literary texts are read as closed systems of signs or ''tropes,'' rather than accounts of characters caught up in life's drama. Picking up ''advanced'' literary criticism, an uninitiated reader is likely to think he has stumbled on some icon that has arrived undeciphered from another world, full of arcane signs and symbols.

But whatever the controversy over semiotics and its interpretations of human activity, the basic questions of how we shape and comprehend the world remain. And they are given a piquant touch by the sight of that naive naked man waving his hand on the plaque of Pioneer 10.
