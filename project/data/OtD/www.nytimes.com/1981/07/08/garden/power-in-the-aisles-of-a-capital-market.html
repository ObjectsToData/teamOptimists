POWER IN THE AISLES OF A CAPITAL MARKET

By LYNN ROSELLINI
Published: July 8, 1981

WASHINGTON AT the Social Safeway you can find a nice leg of lamb, a fresh head of romaine and George Bush. Looking for the Vice President? Try pet supplies. He was spotted there a few weeks ago.

Looking for the Chief Justice of the United States? Try the meat case. Warren E. Burger stops by occasionally to pick up steaks. Looking for a date? Try the fresh vegetable counter any Friday night between 6 and 9 o'clock. It's packed with single secretaries, bureaucrats and college students exchanging telephone numbers over the lettuce bins.

Sprawled on Wisconsin Avenue above Georgetown, the supermarket, dubbed the ''Social Safeway,'' is a favorite shopping stop of Washington's great and powerful. Outside, their limousines cause traffic jams in the parking lot. Inside, the aisles ring with their greetings.

''Hello, how are you!'' A Congressman from upstate New York pumps the hand of the Ambassador of Finland. At the fruit bins, a senator gently squeezes the peaches. At the checkout counter, the wife of another senator explains to a friend why she missed a recent party. Over by the vegetables, Livingston L. Biddle Jr., chairman of the National Endowment on the Arts and Humanities, chats with a former Congressman about proposed cuts in money for the arts.

''When I first came here, I couldn't believe it,'' said Mike McGinnis, the meat manager, leaning over his neat packages of mixed fryer parts the other day.

''All these celebrities would walk up and down the meat case. All the people you've ever seen on TV.'' Defense Secretary Caspar W. Weinberger drops by occasionally. So does Anatoly F. Dobrynin, the Soviet Ambassador. Amy Carter once stopped in for candy and gum, and Henry and Nancy Kissinger were regular customers before they moved to New York.

Other shoppers include Senator Charles H. Percy, Senator Nancy Landon Kassebaum, former Secretary of State Cyrus R. Vance and the chefs of the British, French, Greek and Russian Embassies. The Iranians were heavy buyers, too, before the revolution.

''I frequently run into people I know,'' said Representative Jonathan B. Bingham of New York, a regular customer in the fastcheckout line. Mr. Biddle, another regular, said of the Safeway: ''It has become like the country store in the cracker barrel days.''

Here is what customers buy each day: 80 cases of Perrier water, 140 pounds of Brie, 50 beef tenderloin roasts at $42 each, several hundred loaves of French bread and lots of drink mixers and health food items.

The buyer from the White House, a regular customer for years, used to buy baby food for President Carter's grandchild. Now he buys artichokes, cheeses and other party foods. The chef of the Vice President's mansion, who used to buy lobsters for Walter F. Mondale, now buys beef fillets for Mr. Bush.

Because many local luminaries do not do their own shopping, the Safeway also caters to a bevy of celebrity servants, including Elizabeth Taylor's housekeeper and Paul Mellon's chauffeur. On any weekday morning, women in white uniforms and men in black chauffeur's caps can be spotted in the aisles, their voices rising in a dozen different languages.

Among them are contingents of elderly women who arrive at the Safeway dressed to the nines. For them, the trip is a social outing. ''We come every Thursday morning,'' said Mrs. Ferdinand E. Walter, a Georgetown resident who stood chatting with her neighbors over filled grocery baskets the other day. Another Georgetown woman, wearing pearls and a bouffant hairdo, agreed. ''None of us would miss it for anything in the world,'' she said.

Not surprisingly, the supermarket has the highest annual sales volume - reported as $22.1 million last year - in the nationwide Safeway chain. But the supermarket, with its odd, multiarched brick facade separated from the street by a vast parking lot, is unusual in other respects as well.

For example, it was designed in conjunction with the Fine Arts Commission and the Georgetown Citizens Association. So discreet is the store that the sign on the building doesn't even way ''Safeway.'' It says just says ''S.''

It is also the only Safeway whose meat manager stocks extra pork on Thursdays, the day the Soviet Embassy shops. The store also hires clerks who speak Chinese, French, Italian and Greek and instructs them not to gape at the famous.

''The store's favorite expression,'' said Dona Turley, a checkout clerk who has waited on Senator Kassebaum, ''is, 'Don't stare at them. They put their underwear on the same way you do.'''

Thus, when Vice President Bush stopped in one Sunday morning a few weeks ago, it caused barely a ripple in the placid composure of people in the store. While two Secret Service agents positioned themselves at the front doors, Mr. Bush and two other agents made their way down the aisles. The Vice President lingered over the pet supplies, picked up a package of plastic forks and checked out.

But perhaps the most unusual customers are the people from the Soviet Embassy. They arrive in a blue van shortly after 9 A.M. every Thursday, and the plainly dressed Russians, most of them women whose husbands work at the embassy, push grocery baskets while their children hold onto the sides.

They rarely smile. If spoken to, they turn away. ''The chef - he talks to me when he's alone,'' Nicholas Triandos, who works in the produce section, said. ''But never when there are other people around. Once, I tried to give a little kid a piece of candy. He wouldn't take it.''

It is only at the checkout counter that the Russians speak. ''No taxes please,'' they say quite distinctly. Diplomatic personnel are not required to pay United States taxes. Then they return to the nearby Soviet compound in the van, which picks up a new load of Soviet shoppers for another trip to the Safeway.

Ambassador Dobrynin went to the Safeway recently and, while an aide pushed the grocery cart, gave orders to a second assistant, who picked items from the shelves and put them in the cart.

In the evening, the scene at the Safeway changes dramatically as women in evening gowns and men in black tie appear in the aisles pushing baskets on their way to or from parties. The produce counter takes on the aura of a singles' bar, with young men and women bending over the vegetables while their eyes roam the crowd.

''I believe it's a better place to meet people than going to a bar,'' said Ron Lipton, a 36-year-old lawyer for the Department of Education who reported dating several women he met at the Safeway.

The favored dress for such activity: three-piece suits for the men, silk dresses for the women. Or casual wear - running shorts and Tshirts, perfectly matched tennis outfits and brand-new Adidas.

The favored technique: ''Pardon me,'' the shopper asks, holding up an eggplant (rutabaga, spinach leaf, etc.), ''but do you know how to cook this?''

But the real question tormenting Safeway shoppers these days has nothing to do with singles. Even the most jaded Georgetown matrons are wondering when they will have a chance to pick out plums next to Elizabeth Taylor. When not appearing on Broadway, the actress lives near the store with her husband, Senator John W. Warner of Virginia.

At the Safeway, where politicians are commonplace, the thought of a visit by Miss Taylor sends shoppers and clerks alike into paroxysms of delight. Dona Turley said she frequently chats with Miss Taylor's housekeeper as she passes through the fast-checkout line.

''She tells us,'' Mrs. Turley said hopefully, ''Liz is interested in seeing the store.''
