STATE U.'S COLLEGES PRESSED TO BROADEN CURRICULUMS IN AN AGE OF

Published: July 1, 1981

TECHNOLOGY By RONALD SMOTHERS

Barely comfortable in their relatively new garb as liberal-arts institutions rather than as teachers colleges, the undergraduate colleges of the State University of New York are feeling pressure to make yet another change in style.

The impelling forces are the drastic numerical decline that is about to take place in the population sector the four-year colleges were created to serve -18-year-old high school graduates - and the growing inclination of such graduates to shun liberal arts in favor of more vocationally oriented programs.

Some of the 13 undergraduate colleges in the world's largest system of higher education are responding by shoring up their traditional liberal-arts programs and increasing promotional and recruiting efforts. Others are moving to broaden their curriculums to include more career-oriented programs such as business, engineering and computer science.

Underlying the debate are not only the fortunes of 11 former teachers colleges and two State University-built four-year institutions but also some fundamental academic issues. These issues include the responsibility of public colleges to be sensitive to changes in student taste and the nature of liberal-arts education in an age of increasing technology.

'Liberal-Arts Era Is Over'

To Dr. James E. Perdue, the State University's vice chancellor for academic programs, policy and planning, the public institution has to work harder to keep ''knowledge the seamless garment it is supposed to be'' and avoid overspecialization.

At the same time, university officials such as Donald D. O'Dowd, the executive vice chancellor, argue that ''the @liberal-arts era is over.''

''Ten years ago someone could go into business with a liberal-arts background and knowing nothing about accounting or computers,'' he said. ''Now employers want to know that you can work with numbers.''

The State University's four-year colleges vary widely in size and character. They range from the 11,000-student College at Buffalo, whose 125-acre campus reflects the urban flavor of the state's second largest city, to the rural College at Geneseo upstate, where the 5,500 students nearly outnumber the village's year-round residents. State System Created in 1948

Others are suburban campuses, such the College at Old Westbury, built on a former Nassau County estate, and the College at Purchase on the fringes of the horse country of Westchester County but within 25 miles of Manhattan. These two schools, both founded in 1965, together with Empire State College in Albany, founded in 1971 as the only nonresidential college in the system, are the only schools built from scratch by the State University.

Dr. Perdue likes to say that the former teachers colleges, which go back as far as the mid-19th century, were ''colonized'' by the state system when it was created in 1948. Under a ''gentlemen's agreement'' not to compete with the private colleges of the state, these colleges were at first barred from becoming full-fledged liberal-arts institutions. In 1960, however, they began this transition -with the small private New England-type liberal-arts college as the model -and their overall enrollment has grown from 20,000 in 1959 to more than 80,000 today. Of the total, about 10 percent are graduate students, most of them in teacher education.

The colleges also differ in admission standards. Geneseo and Purchase require entering freshmen to have high school averages of 87 and combined verbal and mathematics scores of 1,100 out of a possible 1,600 on the Scholastic Aptitude Test, while Buffalo and Brockport, the two largest colleges, accept students with high school averages of only 78 and combined test scores of 950.

In addition, there are Old Westbury and Empire State Colleges, which do not use conventional admissions criteria. Old Westbury was created to serve those among the less affluent, minority groups and women who have not had the traditional college preparation in secondary schools. Most of the 3,300 nonresidential students at Empire State are older students who are employed full time and pursue individualized, nonclassroom, learning programs supervised by faculty members near their homes. Tuition Rates Still a Bargain

Perhaps what the schools have most in common are the characteristics of their students. Most of them are the first generation of their families to attend college, and 98 percent of them are New York residents and recent graduates of public high schools in the state.

A rising problem for the colleges is that high school graduates are starting to become increasingly scarcer. Their numbers are projected to decline 25 percent by 1987 and 42 percent by 1995, according to Dr. Clifton R. Wharton Jr., the university's Chancellor.

But Dr. Wharton, among other officials who dispute the bleak outlook, argues that, with tuition costs alone at competing private colleges rising to around $5,000 and $6,000 a year, the State University's colleges remain a comparative bargain at $1,050. The fact that applications at many of the colleges increased this year, he said, suggests, that many students who would otherwise go to private colleges and universities are giving the state's four-year colleges a more thorough look.
