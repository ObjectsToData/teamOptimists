GIVING A HAND TO THE NONTRADITIONAL

Published: January 10, 1981

To the Editor:

While I have no quarrel with the reports of the Heritage and Rockefeller Foundations recommending that a major focus of future arts and humanities funding be on elementary and secondary schools, I do have a problem with the recommendation to limit funding to the country's ''leading cultural institutions.''

Only a few art galleries and museums around the country fall into the category of ''leading cultural institutions,'' and a funding policy which focused on only a handful of institutions would leave many worthwhile institutions, artists, and projects without funding. To The Times, nontraditional institutions are ''artistic circuses or projects that have nothing to do with the arts and humanities'' (editorial Dec. 15).

Charges of elitism against the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities funding policies were not used to suggest that excellence should not be among the criteria used for making awards. As discussed in the House Ap-propriations Subcommittee Report on Interior (FY 1979), they were made to encourage both endowments to extend their support to nontraditional institutions and artists who lack the reputation of the so-called ''leading cultural institutions'' to receive support from other sources.

In recent years, the problem of elitism has been addressed by both endowments. The National Endowment for the Arts' Expansion Arts Program, which was singled out by The Times, is only one of the programs which fund minority, blue-collar or rural cultures. However, these groups receive a small percentage of total N.E.A. funds. For example, in 1979 the total N.E.A. budget was $149,435,000; only $8,173,679 was awarded through the Expansion Arts program. The average award ranges from $5,000 to $15,000; and the highest award under the Expansion Arts Program is $30,000. Hardly the steal implied by The Times.

The Times's editorial also suggested that minority, blue-collar, and rural groups were awarded N.E.A. funds without having to meet any qualifica-tions. Nothing could be further from the truth. Programs and projects funded under the Expansion Arts Program, Summer Project, Regional Tour, or the Consortia are judged on the following conditions to qualify for funding: artistic merit; experience or competence of artists involved; community involvement and support; organizational ability to carry out proposed projects; constituency served; need; ability to match funds, and accountability. All grants made under this program are challenge grants and are far from the handouts suggested in the editorial.

Federal funding for the arts has come a long way in this country; and I would hate to have the clock turned back by returning to the elitist policies of the past that many of us have worked so hard to change. SHIRLEY CHISHOLM, Washington, Dec. 22, 1980.

The writer, who represents New York's 12th Congressional District, is co-chairman of the Congressional Black Caucus Arts and Humanities Braintrust.
