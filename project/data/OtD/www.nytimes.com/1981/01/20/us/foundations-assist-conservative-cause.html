FOUNDATIONS ASSIST CONSERVATIVE CAUSE

By BERNARD WEINRAUB, Special to the New York Times
Published: January 20, 1981

WASHINGTON, Jan. 19— A number of foundations have spread their money discreetly, supporting academic studies of capitalism, offering money to conservative research organizations and underwriting the salaries of free-market economists and scholars examining military strategy.

And though it is difficult to assess the full impact of the dozen major conservative foundations on Mr. Reagan's election or the nation's shift to the right, the foundations have obviously helped spawn and nurture writers, thinkers and economists whose provocative ideas are now part of the conservative landscape.

''Foundations have been quietly and extraordinarily effective in the propagation of certain ideas,'' said Leslie Lenkowsky, research director of the Smith Richardson Foundation, which helps support The Public Interest Magazine, the Committee on the Present Danger and the Hoover Institution.

''We don't create ideas, we nurture them, a bit like fertilizer,'' said Mr. Lenkowsky. ''If the sprout is there, we make it grow into a mighty oak.''

'Ideals Have Filtered Down'

At the John M. Olin Foundation, which spends about $3 million each year on conservative research organizations and academic programs, Michael Joyce, the executive director, said: ''There is a sense now that the work we've been doing all along, the studies we've been supporting, have reached a kind of fruition with Ronald Reagan's election. The ideals we've been supporting have filtered down into public opinion.''

Richard Larry, president of the Sarah Mellon Scaife Foundation, said: ''Suddenly alternative points of view are highly respectable and debated after years of being ignored.'' The foundation spent $5.3 million last year and has provided grants to such ''libertarian,'' or free-market, economists as Milton Friedman and George Stigler at the University of Chicago. The foundation has also supported Georgetown University's Center for Strategic and International Studies. where about 15 persons had served as Reagan advisers.

''There's this sense now,'' Mr. Larry said, ''that we foundations have finally been discovered and the realization, on the part of some people, that we can read, we can write, we have magazines and we have some very interesting ideas.'' Mr. Larry's 40-year-old organization is one of the older and most established foundations.

The amount of money spent by the foundations is not comparable to the $100 million dispersed last year by the Ford Foundation, which helped foster the spread of liberal ideology in the 1960's and was in the forefront of the bitter New York City school decentralization fight in Brooklyn's Ocean Hill-Brownsville district. Academic Programs Financed

The conservative foundations usually spend $1 million to $5 million a year for academic and other programs that, by and large, according to Mr. Lenkowsky, ''take seriously the current condition and future well-being of the system that gave rise to the foundation in the first place, namely capitalism.

The foundations were developed from a variety of corporate activities. There is Smith Richardson (Vicks Vaporub), which spent about $1.5 million last year and has supported such disparate ventures as Ben Wattenberg's television series, the Andrei D. Sakharov hearings in Washington on Soviet dissidents, studies on national security at Harvard University and books by Jude Wanniski, the economist, and Michael Novak, the theologian.

The John M. Olin Foundation (agricultural chemicals), whose chairman is William E. Simon, a former Treasury Secretary, provides about $3 million a year in grants, mostly to conservative research organizations. Grant recipients of the Sarah Mellon Scaife Foundation (Gulf Oil Company) have included the Center for Law and Economics at the University of Miami, The Public Interest magazine and such academics as Carl Brunner, the economist, at the University of Rochester, as well as Gordon Tullock and James Buchanan at the Center for Public Choice at Virginia Polytechnic Institute in Blacksburg.

Other prominent conservative foundations include the Adolph Coors Foundation (brewing), which spends about $2.5 million a year; the J. Howard Pew Freedom Trust (Sun Oil), about $6 million; the Bechtel Foundation (construction), about $875,000, and the Samuel Roberts Noble Foundation (oil and drilling), about $2 million. Corporate Grants to Academics

The growing link between corporations and conservative academic programs is underlined by the creation two years ago of the Institute for Educational Affairs, which not only dispenses about $4.7 million in grants to academics but also serves as a kind of middleman, or adviser, to smaller companies that want to provide grants. Last year these grants totaled about $2 million.

''We're not especially interested in anyone's ideology,'' said Philip Marcus, executive director of the institute, a former assistant director of the National Endowment for the Humanities. ''We're solely interested in scholars that take seriously traditional values and traditional institutions,'' he said, adding:

''I suppose it is a bit grandiose to say people like us have caused the change of mood that resulted in the recent election. But it certainly is an affirmation of the point of view we've been advancing in the intellectual world.''
