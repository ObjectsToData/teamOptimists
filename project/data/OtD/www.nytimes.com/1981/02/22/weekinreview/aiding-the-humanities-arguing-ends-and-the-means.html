AIDING THE HUMANITIES: ARGUING ENDS AND THE MEANS

By JAMES TRAUB
Published: February 22, 1981

Oral history is enjoying a boom year at the National Endowment for the Humanities. So are professional ethics, Native Americans, regional and ethnic history and structuralism. But oral history is high on the list of recent Endowment awards. Boys Club members are interviewing the aged in Queens, Native American teen-agers are recording their elders in Iowa, inner city youth are putting tape recorders in front of their neighbors, a scholar is talking with waterfowl hunters south of Detroit, and the Southwestern Library Association is promoting programs on the subject in local libraries.

The Reagan Administration's intention, announced as part of the budget-cutting campaign last week, to halve the Endowment's yearly outlay from $165 million to $85 million, puts many such programs in jeopardy. Whether some of them should be eliminated on grounds of pure merit has been a matter of longstanding argument, centering on the Endowment's purpose and role; but the extent of the suggested cuts has caused concern about the health of the humanities in the near future.

Endowment Chairman Joseph Duffey reported that some of the 26 members of the Endowment's National Council, which held its quarterly meeting last week, felt that the cuts were motivated by ''a general suspicion about academe'' and ''an attitude of vindictiveness.'' But Don Moran, a deputy associate director of the Office of Management and Budget, insisted that his agency's recommendations were based on ''relative priorities,'' and that ''if it was a question of taking out food stamps or the Endowment, we felt our choice was fairly clear.''

Whatever the final budgetary figures turn out to be (Congressional hearings on the matter are scheduled for the end of April), the N.E.H. will undoubtedly have to make difficult decisions about priorities of its own. The discussion of possible sacrifices is likely to reflect a debate about the relative merits of promoting scholarly excellence and encouraging widespread dissemination of the humanities to the public.

As part of President Reagan's transition studies, a report written by Michael Joyce, of the conservative Olin Foundation, accused the Endowment of hankering after ''faddishly 'innovative' '' proposals, and suggested that entire divisions that ''exist for specious reasons'' be wiped out. In a 10-page open reply to the report's author, Mr. Duffey disputed the notion that the agency's growing catalogue of public, non-scholarly grants diluted its commitment to excellence. ''Indeed,'' he wrote, ''such projects serve to combat the isolation of scholarly activity.''

Other agency officials say it is their Congressional mandate to ''increase public understanding and appreciation of the humanities.'' Acting deputy chairman Geoffrey Marshall considers this concern ''a natural evolution of the agency,'' which was originally modeled along the basic research lines of the National Science Foundation, and then responded to changing public concerns.

The Endowment's critics argue that some of the work it funds cannot be justified in the name of disseminating the humanities. Oral history projects, funded by a number of N.E.H. divisions, furnish one such example. Jacob Neusner, a member of the Endowment's Council, calls many of the amateur oral history projects ''busy work,'' and some specific cases bear him out. Marvin Hinz, a social worker in Seattle who organized a project involving ''predelinquent'' children in a study of their own Pacific coast fishing town, admits that his principal concern was ''getting the kids off the street,'' even though ''history was what they were giving the money for.'' A comparable program in Lynn, Mass., resulted in an exhibit in which passers-by filled in their favorite song and junk food along a time line. Mr. Duffey himself has become concerned about the quality of oral history projects, and has instituted an additional review panel to weed out shoddy proposals.

Even its critics, however, agree that the Endowment has funded numerous projects of both public and scholarly value. Agency spokesmen proudly point to such achievements as the Harvard Encyclopedia of Ethnic Groups, a groundbreaking four-volume study of bioethics, the widely-acclaimed public television series ''The American Short Story,'' and studies of methods to improve literacy.

To some extent the argument about the Endowment's purposes seems to be motivated by ideology, though both sides deny ideological bias. Michael Joyce, in an interview, cited ''an excessive concern with criminals and the delinquent'' as an instance of the agency's attraction to ''intellectual fashion.'' He pointed to a $145,000 grant to the Maryland Department of Education to increase the use of the humanities in prison, and a $60,000 grant to the Y.W.C.A of the University of Texas ''to develop a humanities program for disabled, truant, drop-out and delinquent youth which will encourage them to examine prevailing cultural values vis-a-vis their own.''

Endowment representatives, besides arguing the specific merits of these awards, contend that objections to ''intellectual trendiness'' misconstrue the way the N.E.H. distributes its largesse. Staff members do not award grants, as is the case at private foundations; rather, they farm out proposals to experts in the field, whose recommendations are then pondered by a second panel of experts convened in Washington. Winning grants are thus supposed to represent a consensus of the intellectual community.

Exactly how the two sides of the debate will be balanced in a diminished budget remains to be seen. Mr. Duffey emphasizes the relative importance of basic research projects as opposed to ''public access'' programs. Trimming with this point of view would curtail grants to libraries and museums, as well as projects like those cited by Mr. Joyce.

Such an emphasis would bring the Endowment more closely in line with the transition team report; but even that report did not recommend a budget cut as a road to excellence. Mr. Duffey called the projected slash a ''major attack on the whole agency.'' What seems certain is that unless N.E.H. officials succeed in persuading Congress to mitigate the cut, the agency will be hard put to fulfill both its scholarly and its public mandate. --------------------------------------------------------------------- James Traub is a writer with a special interest in the humanities and the arts.
