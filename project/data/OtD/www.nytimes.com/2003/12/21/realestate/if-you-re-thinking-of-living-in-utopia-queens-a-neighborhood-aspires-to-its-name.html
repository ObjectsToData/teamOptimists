If You're Thinking of Living In/Utopia, Queens; A Neighborhood Aspires to Its Name

By DIANA SHAMAN
Published: December 21, 2003

RESIDENTS of the eastern Queens community of Utopia say they think their neighborhood is appropriately named. Except for house prices, which have gone up almost a hundredfold, a developer's 1940 brochure promoting the area could be describing the Utopia of today: ''a location of established prestige, unquestionable charm and unmatched convenience,'' with excellent schools.

The brochure noted that not only were the new houses close to Grand Central Parkway and a bus ride from the express subway in Kew Gardens, but also nearby were ''beautiful Cunningham Park,'' ''neighborhood stores of every kind'' and beaches and golf courses ''close enough to drive to after work in the evening.''

The developer, the Gross-Morton Park Corporation of Jamaica, which billed itself as ''America's largest home builders,'' built most of the houses that now occupy Utopia's 24 blocks of neatly kept single-family dwellings on tree-lined streets. The neighborhood is bordered by Utopia Parkway on the west, 188th Street on the east, Union Turnpike on the south and 73rd Avenue on the north.

The original colonial and Cape Cod-style houses had eight different floor plans. When Gross-Morton opened a sales office in December 1940, it also had model homes, furnished by the Gertz department store in Jamaica, of each of its eight designs.

All of the houses had garages; some had fireplaces, terraces and decks; and some had slate roofs. The dwellings ranged in price from $6,490 for a two-bedroom ''bungalow'' to $8,690 for a three-bedroom and den ''two-story home.'' The average lot size was 40 feet wide by 100 feet deep. The company built the same kinds of houses in neighboring Fresh Meadows.

Prices in Utopia now start at $475,000 for a two-bedroom Cape Cod that has not been updated, said Janice Costi, the co-owner with Sally Goller of Goller & Costi Realty on Union Turnpike and 190th Street. The real estate agency has all the original Gross-Morton floor plans in its files. The favorite with today's buyers is a three-bedroom colonial once known as the D Special, Ms. Costi said. Three-bedroom colonials now start at $500,000, Ms. Costi added.

Among her recent sales was an updated and expanded Cape Cod at 75-52 181st Street, with four bedrooms and two bathrooms that sold for $569,000. The asking price was $588,000 and the house was on the market for two weeks. The least expensive homes in the neighborhood are its only attached houses -- a row of brick town houses lining Utopia Parkway that were not part of the original development. One is currently on the market at $340,000.

THE Department of City Planning has Utopia marked on its map of city neighborhoods, but many residents of Hillcrest, Fresh Meadows and Jamaica Estates, which border Utopia, lump the community in with Fresh Meadows, as do local real estate agents. ''We feel like stepchildren,'' said Tami Hirsch, president of the Civic Association of Utopia Estates, which represents about 1,200 homeowners in Utopia. ''Even our library, which we fought very hard for, is called the Hillcrest Library.''

The name Utopia goes back to the turn of the last century when the Utopia Land Company was formed to build a cooperative community in Queens for Jewish families living on the Lower East Side looking for a better life in what was then still the country, said Jeff Gottlieb, president of the Central Queens Historical Association. The company purchased 50 acres of farmland ''east of 164th Street between the communities of Jamaica and Flushing,'' according to a historic plaque at the Utopia Playground on 73rd Avenue.

But the developers went bankrupt before any work took place. Thereafter, Utopia survived in name only. When Gross-Morton and others began developing the area 35 years later, much of the land was still farmland, Mr. Gottlieb said.

Now, after a rapid rise in real estate values over the last five years, several of Utopia's original houses are being substantially expanded by owners who want more space and feel comfortable about investing in the neighborhood. Examples can be found on almost every block. Some are adding an additional floor and others are extending their houses at the back or on the side.

Kristen James, owner of Union Turnpike Real Estate on Union Turnpike and 185th Street, said she welcomed the trend. ''When people put money into their houses, it increases overall values,'' she said.

But Mrs. Hirsch, the civic association president, said she worried that many of the expanded houses overwhelm their lots and change the ambience of the neighborhood. The association zealously pursues possible violations with the Buildings Department, but the agency is not always responsive, she said.

Diane Cohen, district manager of the local Community Board 8, said that while there are instances of illegal construction, often the changes are entirely legal.

UTOPIA is experiencing not only physical changes, but also a change in demographics as the Irish, Italian, Polish and German families who settled the area are replaced by many Russians and Asians.
