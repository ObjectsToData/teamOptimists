Russian Youth Movement Linked to Jailed Tycoon Has Wide Civic Goals

By SOPHIA KISHKOVSKY
Published: December 28, 2003

Outside, arctic darkness envelops the mountains, but inside, a video of a bikini-clad Beyoncé blasts on a big screen in this northern Russian mining town, and members of the New Civilization youth movement are discussing their latest project.

They are working up an MTV-style public service announcement on the benefits of the Internet. Previously they made an antismoking commercial.

But their goals do not stop there. Ilya Scherbakov, the local coordinator for New Civilization, a nationwide organization that claims more than 100,000 current members and 1.5 million since its inception in 1996, says they want to transform Russia.

''Before, the Pioneers and Komsomol united young people,'' he said, referring to the Soviet youth groups that instilled Communist ideology. ''Now it's up to us.''

A poster in the meeting room depicts New Civilization's founder, Mikhail B. Khodorkovsky, a jailed oligarch. His financing of youth organizations has been praised by some as civic minded; others see a self-serving effort to build a political base for a corrupt oligarchy.

New Civilization's biggest program is a game in which children create model states; it culminates in Newlandia, a series of summer camps across Russia where children write constitutions, run governments, start businesses and even turn into oligarchs.

Stanislav Belkovsky, a political scientist said to have influenced President Vladimir V. Putin's battle against some of Russia's billionaires, has derided New Civilization as a ''training ground for the storm troops of big business.''

The only youth organization of comparable influence today is Moving Together, a pro-Putin group that visits orphans and old people, holds rallies in support of the president and wages campaigns against postmodernist writers.

Mr. Scherbakov, New Civilization's coordinator in Kirovsk, is quick to distance himself from Mr. Khodorkovsky's money. The Kirovsk group's annual budget of $50,000 is financed, he said, by the Apatit company, not by Yukos Oil, which Mr. Khodorkovsky headed until his arrest in October. Apatit's complicated ownership structure, however, ultimately leads back to Mr. Khodorkovsky.

Many New Civilization clubs are based at former Pioneer headquarters.

''No one is saying that the Pioneer organization was bad, but what did it teach?'' asked Irina Komarova, a spokesperson for New Civilization. ''It taught about life in socialism. We teach about life in capitalism.''

New Civilization, whose members wear scoutlike uniforms in the gold and green colors of Yukos, says more than half of its clubs are in regions not connected to Yukos business. The organization has been financed by Yukos and is run by the Open Russia Foundation. That group has received money from Mr. Khodorkovsky and other core shareholders of Yukos and is modeled on George Soros's Open Society Institute, which is withdrawing from Russia.

Under the Soviet system, factories were required to maintain kindergartens, medical facilities, youth programs and other social services for employees. Mr. Khodorkovsky has both complained about the remnants of that state-imposed burden and taken up a new mantle.

''We feel that the most important thing for there to be a civil society created in Russia is education,'' Mr. Khodorkovsky said in October in Washington, at a Carnegie Endowment for International Peace briefing.

Open Russia recently announced that it had spent $14 million on programs this year. At the Carnegie Endowment briefing, Mr. Khodorkovsky placed the annual budget of programs administered by Open Russia at more than $100 million. It is run separately from Mr. Khodorkovsky's British-based philanthropic organization of the same name.

Three of the most expensive programs managed by Open Russia are based on Yukos money: New Civilization; the Federation of Internet Education, run by a Yukos executive and intertwined with state education officials; and the Russian State Humanities University.

Yukos has promised the university $100 million over the next decade to turn it into ''the Russian Harvard,'' said Irina Y. Yasina, Open Russia's director.

Addressing Russian educators in November, Steven M. Theede, the new American executive heading Yukos's day-to-day operations, praised the Newlandia concept and the Internet federation, calling them ''just examples of the initiatives Yukos will continue supporting.''

As Mr. Khodorkovsky sits in prison awaiting trial, Open Russia and Yukos civil society programs continue functioning, although the organization has complained that the tax authorities are harassing its beneficiaries.

Open Russia's activities include a school for orphans of Russian border guards killed in action, seminars for provincial journalists and local government officials, financing for regional universities, village libraries, Russia's top literary prize, a leading newspaper and Internet training for religious groups. A new civic education program is financed jointly with the United States Agency for International Development.

Vladimir A. Ryzhkov, a legislator from Siberia, praised the Internet programs seeded by New Civilization in remote regions.

''If something happens to Yukos financing, I don't know where the money will come from,'' he said. ''The state doesn't have money. Regions don't have money.''
