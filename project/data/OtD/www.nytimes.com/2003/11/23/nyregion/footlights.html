FOOTLIGHTS

By ROBERTA HERSHENSON
Published: November 23, 2003

Hailing the Chief

Purchase College showcased its talent at the inauguration of its fifth president, Thomas J. Schwarz, on Nov. 12 at the college's Performing Arts Center. Herald trumpeters played a fanfare composed for the occasion by Graham Ashton, a celebrated trumpet player as well as the presiding officer of the faculty. Four student dancers in tails and white ties performed a Paul Taylor work, accompanied by percussionists from the music conservatory. Jacques Trussel, chairman of vocal music, sang an operatic ''America the Beautiful,'' and Jon Faddis, the college's first artist-in-residence, played a jazzy, bluesy version of ''The Star-Spangled Banner,'' flourishing his famous high notes.

Student actors from the Conservatory of Theater Arts and Film performed a skit designed to thank donors for their scholarship money (85 percent of Purchase College students receive financial aid). The School of Art and Design was represented by a ceremonial wooden mace, fashioned by the faculty members Eric Wildrick and Dennis Fitzgerald, as well as by a digital print by Murray Zimiles, an art professor, which was presented to a guest speaker.

In his address, Mr. Schwarz described the arts as ''a weapon in the war against repressive dictators and governments,'' and called for more governmental and corporate spending for the arts. Dictatorships suppress the arts by force, he said, but ''democratic, free nations should not suppress the arts by starvation.''

Medal Under Secrecy

The best-selling children's author Jean Fritz had a secret when she and Jean Craighead George, another noted children's author, were honored at the Hudson Valley Writers' Center's fund-raising dinner on Nov. 7. On Nov. 14, Ms. Fritz, of Dobbs Ferry, was to be awarded a National Humanities Medal by President Bush in the Oval Office. She was honored for her ''brilliantly imagined'' books that have introduced ''millions of young people to the wonder of American history.''

''We didn't know about the medal until just this past weekend,'' a spokeswoman for the center said. ''She was sworn to secrecy until after the ceremony.''

New Beginnings

When ''Harmony,'' the Broadway-bound musical by Barry Manilow and Bruce Sussman, was suddenly shut down on Nov. 13 as it prepared for a Dec. 2 opening in Philadelphia, Peter Pucci of Mount Kisco was among those affected.

Mr. Pucci, the show's choreographer, had been working on dance numbers since the summer. ''I've been on automatic all week,'' he said by phone. ''I still wake up and think about the things I have to do. The hardest part is to have it yanked from you so abruptly.''

Money for the show ran out after several promises from investors fell through, according to Mark Schwartz, ''Harmony's'' lead producer. Mr. Pucci said other producers were interested, but many legalities had to be resolved before a new deal could be struck. ''We were riding high,'' he said. ''We thought we had a really great show. It's out of our hands now.''

He said he is focusing on his solo show, ''To Begin Again,'' at the Joyce Theater in Manhattan on Jan. 9, 16 and 17. It features music by Sloan Wainwright of Katonah.

Show and Screen

''Blast,'' ''Broom,'' ''The Dial'' ''The Hound and a Horn'' - those were the names of a few of what are called ''the little magazines'' that ushered many groundbreaking novelists and poets into American literature in the first half of the 20th century. The long list of major writers first published in these magazines, many of which were founded and run by women, include Sherwood Anderson, Gertrude Stein, James Joyce, T.S. Eliot and Virginia Woolf. Cubism, Imagism, Futurism and Surrealism appeared in their pages as Modernism took root in American arts and letters.

''I've often been asked, in effect, why hasn't more been said about the large role of women in bringing Modernism to this country?'' wrote Barbara Probst Solomon in the introduction to ''America - Meet Modernism! Women of the Little Magazine Movement,'' the book she edited that was published recently by Great Marsh Press. Ms. Solomon also teaches in the graduate writing program at Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers, which sponsored the book, and curated an exhibition about the Little Magazine Movement, which can be seen at the college through November and in late spring at the Cervantes Institute in Manhattan.

The exhibit includes a display of the magazines, and vintage posters and 40 photographs by Berenice Abbott, Man Ray, Alfred Stieglitz and others. Five short films will be shown continuously during the show, including ''A Marriage: Georgia O'Keeffe and Alfred Stieglitz,'' featuring Jane Alexander and Christopher Plummer (1991), and ''Paris Was a Woman,'' about Gertrude Stein and her circle between World War I and II (1997). Information: (914)395-2470. ROBERTA HERSHENSON
