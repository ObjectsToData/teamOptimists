NEWS SUMMARY


Published: February 4, 2003

INTERNATIONAL A3-10

U.S. Bombers Put on Alert As Warning to North Korea

Defense Secretary Donald H. Rumsfeld has put 24 long-range bombers on alert for possible deployment within range of North Korea, both to deter ''opportunism'' at a moment when Washington is focused on Iraq and to give President Bush military options if diplomacy fails to halt North Korea's effort to produce nuclear weapons. A1

U.S. and Britain Press France

The United States and Britain opened a week of intensive pressure on France and other nations to support quick passage of a second United Nations Security Council resolution that would authorize military force against Iraq. But they seemed to be making little progress. A11

The chief United Nations weapons inspectors will travel to Baghdad for two days of meetings starting Feb. 8 even though they have received no assurances that Iraq will resolve several disputed issues before they go. A11

Prime Minister Abdullah Gul of Turkey said he would ask Parliament this week to consider measures that could clear the way for American combat troops to use the country as a base for an invasion of Iraq. A12

Defeated, Schröder May Act

A day after Chancellor Gerhard Schröder suffered a humiliating rebuke in state elections, politicians and economists said the defeat might liberate his government, at long last, to undertake a wholesale reform of Germany's hidebound economy. A3

Israeli Forces Kill 2 Arabs

Israeli forces killed two Palestinians in the Gaza Strip, and an Israeli military court ordered a 27-year prison term for a Palestinian militant accused of using Al Qaeda training to plan attacks against Israelis. A10

Seoul Calls Off Investigation

Worried about aggravating tense relations during the nuclear crisis, South Korean prosecutors called off an investigation into whether South Korea secretly paid North Korea to agree to their summit meeting two and a half years ago. A8

NATIONAL A14-27

Liftoff Damage Is Focus Of Space Shuttle Inquiry

NASA investigators said that a piece of insulation that broke off from the shuttle's external fuel tank 80 seconds after liftoff was now a prime suspect in the series of failures that destroyed the shuttle Columbia 16 days later. A1

As early as 1997, a senior NASA engineer warned that hardened foam peeling away from the external fuel tank on the Columbia shuttle had caused ''significant damage'' to the ceramic tiles protecting the vehicle from re-entry temperatures. A1

Ronald D. Dittemore, manager of the space shuttle program, went to great lengths to emphasize his personal responsibility for the decisions made and the paths of action chosen since the Columbia's takeoff as he presided over his third press conference since the Columbia broke apart over Texas on Saturday morning. A1

After a closed-door meeting with the administrator of NASA, several lawmakers said they would move quickly to secure money for long-delayed safety improvements. A19

Bush Budget Sent to Congress

President Bush sent Congress a $2.23 trillion budget with record deficits that would speed up billions of dollars in income tax cuts, provide huge increases in defense spending and offer a modest increase for NASA. A1

Democrats Cite Budget Faults

Within hours of its release House and Senate Democrats voiced their disapproval of President Bush's 2004 budget. John M. Spratt Jr. of South Carolina called it, ''the most fiscally damaging budget in U.S. history.'' A26

The budget President Bush proposed did not include any money to help New York out of a fiscal crisis that New York lawmakers say stems in large part from the Sept. 11 attack. A25

Rock Legend Charged in Killing

Phil Spector, the eccentric rock-and-roll legend, was arrested and charged with murder in the death of a woman at his mansion in California. Mr. Spector was released after posting a $1 million bond. A14

NEW YORK/REGION B1-8

Doctors Continue Strike Protesting Rate Increases

Thousands of New Jersey physicians stayed away from their offices and hospitals to protest what they consider to be ruinous increases in malpractice insurance rates. Organizers of the effort urged physicians to keep refusing most nonemergency care until state officials met their demands for limits on jury awards. A1

Ashcroft Decision Reverberates

Attorney General John Ashcroft's rejection of a plea agreement for a suspect who offered to cooperate to avoid a possible death sentence is likely to hamper prosecutors handling federal cases, lawyers say. The possibility of similar death-penalty decisions from Washington may hurt federal investigations because defendants would be reluctant to trust the prosecutors. B3

Adjusting to Added Digits

Verizon warned that if callers in New York City did not dial an area code after Feb. 1, they would hear a message telling them to hang up and dial again. Yet some callers reported hearing that message only sporadically. B6
