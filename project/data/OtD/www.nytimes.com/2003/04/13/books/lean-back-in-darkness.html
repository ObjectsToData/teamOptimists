Lean Back in Darkness

By Martha Bayles
Published: April 13, 2003

GOOD MORNING,

MR. ZIP ZIP ZIP

Movies, Memory, and World War II.

By Richard Schickel.

329 pp. Chicago: Ivan R. Dee. $27.50.

''THAT'S the kind of hairpin I am'': Richard Schickel borrows this line from an old James Cagney movie to justify the baggy shape of his memoir. And rightly so -- ''Good Morning, Mr. Zip Zip Zip'' hangs together in spite of what Schickel admits are ''some odd juxtapositions'' among its three main ingredients, ''conventional reminiscence, critical considerations of movies almost everyone else has forgotten, a certain contempt for the false pieties and hypocrisies of our old public culture.''

To begin with reminiscence, Schickel's account of a boyhood spent in the small town of Wauwatosa, Wis., is a welcome antidote not only to the rose-colored kitsch found in today's ''feel-good'' entertainment but also to the lurid backyard evil found in the ''cutting-edge'' alternatives. Delicately he splits the difference, recalling the occasional murder and sex scandal but adding, ''We were implicitly urged to find no instruction in anything that seriously disturbed our placidity.''

For Schickel the chief escape from placidity was the movies. His discussions of wartime films are refreshingly down to earth, and loyal to the memory of a time when popular culture was actually seen by parents as ''an educational opportunity'' and taking the kids to certain movies, like ''Sergeant York'' (1941), starring Gary Cooper as the World War I pacifist turned hero, was ''almost . . . a moral obligation.''

A film critic for Time and the author of several film biographies, Schickel revisited the movies mentioned here, and for the most part his retro reviews are a pleasure to read. In light of the recent United States invasion of Iraq, it may be wrong to suggest, as he does, that World War II movies are forgotten. What is forgotten, unfortunately, is the faculty Schickel brings to bear: a well-seasoned and tempered sensibility that brings a depth of comparative knowledge to the task of sizing up a movie. This used to be called taste, and amid all the chatter about movies today, it is dismayingly rare.

Among Schickel's retro reviews only one judgment rings false: that of ''wartime's last picture show,'' ''The Best Years of Our Lives'' (1946). Noting that this William Wyler film about three battle-weary soldiers returning to a small city in the heartland was ''instantly beloved -- a huge, multi-Oscar hit,'' Schickel nevertheless calls it ''undeniably lying and sentimental.'' This verdict is surprising given that Schickel acknowledges both the constraints under which Hollywood operated during the war and the need to deal tactfully with certain harsh realities. For example, when revisiting ''A Guy Named Joe'' (1943), starring Spencer Tracy as a fighter pilot who after crashing becomes an angel guiding the missions of others, Schickel admits that despite the film's ''desperate scramble to offer some sort of consolation,'' its ''naturalistic writing and playing'' keep it ''within a good-natured, even plausible realm.'' Couldn't the same be said of ''The Best Years of Our Lives,'' especially since the consolation it offers is not angelic guidance but the love and understanding of three good women?

The strength of Schickel's book is the way it blends evenhanded reminiscence and retrospective movie criticism. The weakness arises with his stated ''contempt for the false pieties and hypocrisies of our old public culture.'' It is one thing to admit that making movies in wartime is a balancing act in which depictions of horrific combat are likely to be muted in favor of inspirational messages. It is quite another flatly to condemn the results as ''lies.''

On the positive side, Schickel offers an insightful critique of the ''greatest generation'' myth. With no disrespect intended toward the 16 million Americans who served in World War II, he worries that ''the ways we are now remembering the war'' are shaped more by Hollywood's faded fantasies than by historical reality. This critique deserves to be taken seriously, not least because it is accompanied by a sometimes brilliant analysis of what was most false about the old movies.

Schickel is especially good on what he calls ''the popular-front sappiness'' of Communist scriptwriters like Dalton Trumbo. Quoting at length a speech from ''Tender Comrade'' (1943) in which a freshly widowed defense worker (played by Ginger Rogers) tells her newborn baby that his father did not leave him any ''country clubs or long shining cars,'' only ''the best world a boy could ever grow up in,'' Schickel asks dryly: ''What can one say about this kind of writing? That no one in human history ever talked like that?'' His accusation that wartime Hollywood produced lies is most persuasive with regard to its balder distortions, chiefly its avoidance of the Holocaust. It is appalling that ''the Office of War Information did not think publicizing the tragedy of the European Jews was effective propaganda.'' Yet, as Schickel acknowledges, this shameful neglect has not led to permanent amnesia on the topic of the Holocaust, either in the movies or in public life.

Less persuasive is Schickel's tendency to lapse into what sounds like borrowed academic jargon: ''This book has been much preoccupied with the kinds of lies we were told during the war, lies that coalesced around a 'metamyth' of democratically parceled-out sacrifice.'' Why would a distinguished critic want to sound like a professor of cultural studies? Is it because Schickel's great gift and achievement, what in another, snootier field might be called connoisseurship, has never found a comfortable niche in American life?

He gives an interesting account of his formation as a critic. From a strong-minded grandfather he ''gathered that it was all right to advance your ideas forcefully,'' even though this entails a certain ''self-centeredness'' and ''sense of entitlement.'' This is not elitism, because as Schickel wisely notes, criticism can also be anti-elitist: ''There was something about the act of criticism -- its assertiveness, its ability to subvert the sometimes pompous, often expensive, object under review -- that appealed to me.''

Yet too often the position of film as popular art means that the review of a thoughtful critic is weighed equally with the tossed-off opinion of Joe Skateboard buzzing through the multiplex. Schickel knows this, and is at his best when veiling his sophistication with a down-home sense of what movies mean to most people. Hence his fond and well-justified claim that many World War II movies handled difficult themes with honesty and skill, even art. And hence his endearing admission to being just another movie addict: ''A lot of the time, at the movies, I am as complicit in their genial and saving lies as I was when I was a boy.''
