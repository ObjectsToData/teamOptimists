When Books Break the Bank; College Students Venture Beyond the Campus Store

By TAMAR LEWIN
Published: September 16, 2003

When Jill Thompson, a junior at Barnard College, went to the bookstore recently and found that her biology text cost $120, she balked, put the book down and went to her computer to look for a cheaper alternative.

''With the tuition we pay, it's a lot to come up with more than $100 for a biology book,'' said Ms. Thompson, a psychology major from New Hampshire. Searchingdogears.net, she found a used copy of the required text for $69.

In the past two decades, the price of textbooks has soared. The price of educational books and supplies has risen 238 percent, while the price of consumer goods over all has increased only 51 percent, according to the Consumer Price Index. At four-year private colleges, the College Board found, students spent an average of $807 on books last year. Some students, particularly science and math majors, spent that much in one semester.

At the same time, more books are being sold in shrink-wrapped bundles with supplements that a student may not need but must pay for, such as a study guide, dictionary or CD-ROM.

But more and more, students are fighting back, finding ways to reduce the costs. They are sharing books, using library copies or going online to find cheap used copies. Indeed, about 20 percent no longer buy all their required texts, according to the National Association of College Stores. And that percentage is growing fast enough to worry both textbook publishers and college bookstores.

''When we first looked at it five years ago, it was about 15 percent, but it's trending up about a percent a year,'' said Laura Nakoneczny, a spokeswoman for the association. The group ran an unsuccessful program last year in which professors worked with bookstores at a dozen colleges to try to increase the percentage of students who buy assigned texts.

Publishers say the prices of textbooks simply reflect the high costs of producing college texts. Most are niche products, written for a relatively small audience. To stay up to date -- and, critics say, to force students to buy new books -- publishers frequently bring out new editions, further limiting sales of used books.

But some legislators are not buying the publishers' argument.

Last year, in response to complaints from students at Pasadena City College, Carol Liu, chairwoman of the California State Assembly's Higher Education Committee, held hearings on the issue, but none of the invited textbook publishers showed up. Ms. Liu's staff is exploring the possibility of using the collective buying power of California's state colleges and universities to negotiate with publishers for lower prices.

Students like Ms. Thompson, meanwhile, are finding their own ways around high book costs.

''When I was a scared freshman, I bought every single book,'' said Ms. Thompson, 20. ''By last year, I was more relaxed. There was one religion class where I didn't buy a single book. I relied on the copies on reserve at the library, and, ironically, that was the class where I got the best grade, maybe because having to do the reading at the library forced me into better time management.''

Many freshmen seem to end up buying everything they are assigned -- even when they have serious sticker shock at the college bookstore.

''I was so surprised,'' said Thomas Blair, a Columbia freshman emerging from the bookstore with a heavy bag. ''The calculus book cost $144. The chemistry book, with the study guide and the answer book, cost $178. I called my dad and said I was going to run out of money a lot sooner than I thought.''

Part of the problem is that a growing proportion of texts, like Mr. Blair's chemistry book, now come bundled with study guides, CD-ROM's, instructions providing access to a special Web site, answer books or other supplements that drive the price up and make it less likely that students will find cheaper used versions.

''We began seeing bundles about three years ago, and this year we're seeing a lot more,'' said Ken Bowers, who runs the bookstore at the University of California at Santa Barbara. ''The professors decide which pieces to include, and the bundles arrive, shrink-wrapped together. It's sold on the premise that it's a value to the student. But it also makes it possible to charge more, and has the effect of making it more difficult for students to get used books.''

Each new bundle gets an International Standard Book Number, the identification number assigned to every book. Professors place their orders using that number, and bookstores buy back and resell only copies with the numbers the professors request. So when professors order a new bundle, most bookstores do not sell used versions -- and only rarely do stores sell the component pieces separately.

At the bookstore at City College of New York, the ninth edition of ''Chemistry, the Central Science'' comes in a $139.25 bundle with no fewer than five supplements: a ''math review toolkit,'' a CD-ROM, a student lecture notebook and booklet of solutions to a set of exercises, and a ''peer-led team learning'' book.
