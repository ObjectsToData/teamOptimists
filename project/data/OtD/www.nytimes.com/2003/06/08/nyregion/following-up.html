FOLLOWING UP

By Joseph P. Fried
Published: June 8, 2003

Ousted Administrator Keeps Teaching Ideas

He was at the center of a campus storm on Long Island, and now, he says, he is enjoying the calm of his professorial pursuits in New England.

In 1997, Peter Diamandopoulos was dismissed as president of Adelphi University after his policies, personality and pay bitterly split the campus in Garden City.

Blunt and strong-willed, seen by supporters as forceful and open to wider academic visions and by critics as autocratic and unapproachable, he had sought during his 12-year tenure to transform Adelphi from a commuter college into a school on the Ivy League model.

A Harvard-trained philosopher, he successfully pressed for the creation of an honors college and a core curriculum emphasizing the classics of Western civilization. He spent millions on modernizing the campus. But he also oversaw sharply rising tuition, a plunge in enrollment to fewer than 6,000 from more than 10,000, and cutbacks in academic budgets that some on the faculty called ruinous.

When The Chronicle of Higher Education reported in 1995 that Dr. Diamandopoulos's pay and a deferred bonus the previous year totaled $523,000, making him the second-highest-paid university president in the country, criticism of him intensified. The disclosure that the university had bought a $1.2 million Manhattan apartment for his use further fueled the ire.

But all that is history in which ''I am not emotionally involved anymore,'' Dr. Diamandopoulos, 75, said last week. His focus now, he said, is on his work at Boston University, where he is a special assistant to the president and a professor of philosophy and humanities.

Teaching is the bulk of his work, ''and I enjoy it immensely,'' he said.

''The exciting thing about my students,'' he said, is that while many plan careers in business and the law, ''the liberal arts still have a vitality that attracts them.''

He is also impressed, he said, by their recognition of the ''extremely complex'' nature of ethical issues.

Dr. Diamandopoulos said he was not bitter over his ouster from Adelphi. ''In the view of many, I may have been misguided and elitist,'' but his own view, he said, is that he ''gave them the opportunity to see that ideas matter.''

The New York State Board of Regents called his Adelphi pay ''far in excess of the value of services performed,'' but Dr. Diamandopoulos maintained that the salary was warranted because ''I worked 80, 90, 100 hours, seven days a week.''

He declined to give his Boston University salary, saying only, ''In general, it is appropriate for a senior full professor.''

Wrapping Up the Case Of a Young 'Soprano'

In July 2001, four teenagers were charged with stealing $40 from two other teenagers on a Manhattan street. Not normally the stuff of big news. But when one of the arrested youths turned out to be Robert Iler, the actor who plays the mob boss Tony Soprano's son on ''The Sopranos,'' the news media descended.

In April 2002, Mr. Iler, above, then 17, pleaded guilty to misdemeanor larceny and got three years' probation. Another suspect, Alban Selimaj, took the same plea deal at the same time. But with Mr. Iler out of the case, the media horde pulled out.

So to wrap up the plot: a third suspect, identified only as Sean M. because he was 15 at the time, later admitted his participation and received 18 months' probation. The fourth, Michael Cournede, 19 at the time, pleaded guilty to robbery and attempted robbery for that crime and an earlier one and is serving 42 months in prison.
