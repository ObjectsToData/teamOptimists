AROUND TOWN

TAKEOVER IN BROOKLYN

The BAMcinématek is known for series on auteurs or masterpieces that its curators want you to know about. So how did “Perfect,” that slice of John Travolta-Jamie Lee Curtis cheese, make it into the second annual Takeover, the Brooklyn Academy of Music’s all-night-long house party?

For “Up All Night in 1985,” one of four film programs that will be shown from 9 p.m. to 4 a.m. on Saturday, the curators wanted gems that were suitable for viewing in the wee hours of the morning but weren’t big hits from that year, explained Lisa Mallory, vice president of marketing and communications. Hence, no to “Back to the Future,” yes to “The Last Dragon,” “Fright Night” and especially “Perfect,” with its never-to-be-repeated combination of a hip Rolling Stone reporter and pornographic aerobics classes. The curators argued “this film needs a cult following,” Ms. Mallory said with a laugh.

Mr. Travolta also has a role in “The Reel World: Brooklyn.” That bill, with “Saturday Night Fever,” “She’s Gotta Have It” and “The Warriors,” was meant to remind Takeover participants of the borough’s “not-so-distant past,” Ms. Mallory said.

On a third screen, “The Kingdom,” a five-hour slice of Lars von Trier’s mini-series for Danish television about the mysterious goings-on at a hospital, is for viewers willing to sit awhile and forgo the other Takeover offerings, like concerts (with St. Vincent and Nico Muhly, among others), D.J.’s, dancing (above) and the Rec Room, with Wii and Guitar Hero.

As if there weren’t enough choices for the short-attention-span crowd, there’s a final film bill, “Bring Back the Draught,” featuring “The Saddest Music in the World,” “Strange Brew,” “The Fatal Glass of Beer” and “Beerfest.” The choices celebrate the decision to allow beer in the theaters (just for the Takeover, though), reversing a ban that was a source of complaints last year.

Besides, with the memory of summer’s “Pineapple Express” still fresh, the curators figured that programming stoner movies would have been too easy. It’s still the BAMcinématek, after all. 30 Lafayette Avenue, at Ashland Place, Fort Greene, 718-636-4100, takeoverbam.org; $25; $20 in advance. STEPHANIE GOODMAN

Museums and Sites

Saturday at 7 p.m., a free outdoor screening of the 1982 film “E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial,”; sponsored by the mansion, the New York City Department of Parks and the Picture House, a nonprofit organization. Filmgoers should take a chair or blanket, insect repellant and a flashlight; the first 100 visitors will receive a free chair from the Parks Department. Tour hours: Wednesdays, Saturdays and Sundays, noon to 4 p.m.; grounds, daily, 8:30 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. (718) 885-1461, bartowpellmansionmuseum.org. Admission: $5; students, $3; free for those 6 and younger.

Wednesdays through Sundays, 1 to 5 p.m., Tours on weekdays at 2:30 p.m. and on weekends at 2 and 3:30 p.m. (718) 351-1611, historicrichmondtown.org. $5; $4 for 65+; $3.50 for students and children 5 to 17; free for members and children under 5.

Hours: Thursdays through Mondays, noon to 5 p.m. $8; $5 for students and 65+; free for members and children under 12.

Through Nov. 2, admission is free for those with a paid receipt from a visit to the Museum of the City of New York.

Through Oct. 31, “Take Me Out to the Ball Game: 100 Years of Music, Musicians and the National Pastime,” an exhibition of slides, photographs, sheet music and recordings that focus on baseball’s influence on composers like Irving Berlin, Charles Ives, William Schuman and George M.
