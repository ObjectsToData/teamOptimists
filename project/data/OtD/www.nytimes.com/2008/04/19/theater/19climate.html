Nine Writers See if the Fate of the Earth Can Be Any More Dramatic

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
By CAMPBELL ROBERTSON
Published: April 19, 2008

A group of playwrights have come together to write one-act plays that will prevent the destruction of the world as we know it.

Skip to next paragraph

A brochure for "Climate of Concern."

Ha, ha! Whoo, that’s rich.

O.K., seriously, what if they’re really good playwrights?

Don DeLillo, Sarah Ruhl, José Rivera, Lisa Kron and Jon Robin Baitz are among the writers on the bill of the second annual New York University Humanities Festival, taking place Saturday through Wednesday. The festival this year, called “Climate of Concern,” is devoted to “themes of global warming.” (Earth Day falls in the middle of the festival, on Tuesday.)

Nine short plays will be shown over three nights (some of the actors involved, including Kathleen Chalfant, are pretty big hitters as well), and the organizer, Lawrence Weschler, a writer and the director of the New York Institute for the Humanities at N.Y.U., is not lacking in his ambitions.

“Scientists and policy wonks have had their chance and have not made this real for people,” Mr. Weschler said of global warming. “That is the job of artists.”

Six of the plays appeared last year at the Chicago Humanities Festival, which Mr. Weschler also organized. Three Chicago theaters — the Steppenwolf Theater Company, the Goodman Theater and the Next Theater Company — each picked one playwright and another Chicago theater; the theaters in the second group also each chose a playwright. The festival commissioned a play from each of the six selected writers with this pitch: Write a one-act play to “wake people up” about climate change.

Mr. Weschler’s intention all along has been to keep adding plays until there would be 20, 25 or even more on this theme. So when he began organizing the N.Y.U. festival, he recruited three more playwrights: Ms. Kron, Mr. Baitz and John Jesurun.

The results are more surprising than you might think.

Most of the playwrights seem to agree on the basic premise, that global warming is an urgent crisis. Several wrote about the impotence of the political system in offering a solution, while others created dystopian “ ‘Twilight Zone’ episodes,” as Mr. Weschler called them, about life in an ecologically decimated future.

But Mr. Jesurun said his play was more about the confusion felt by reasonable people in a debate dominated on both sides by ferociously opinionated political forces. And at least two of the plays, Mr. DeLillo’s and Mr. Baitz’s, seemed to question the exercise itself. Mr. DeLillo’s play, a Beckettian tale about a pilgrim and a scholar, raises doubts about the value of language in the face of catastrophe. Mr. Baitz’s play is a little less understated: it features a writer named Lawrence who is trying to convince a playwright named Robbie Baitz to write a one-act play for a festival about climate change. The Lawrence, uh, character argues that artists can wake people up to an ecological crisis in which scientists and policy wonks have failed. The Baitz character’s response can be summed up in a phrase: Isn’t it pretty to think so?

“I don’t really buy the basic premise, but I find it a romantic and comforting one,” the actual Mr. Baitz said in a telephone interview. “Up to a point I guess artists have their place in the cosmos and in the Tower of Babel that we live in, but I actually think scientists are doing a pretty good job of scaring people to death.”

At best, Mr. Baitz said that art, and especially popular culture, can have a cumulative effect. Taken together, he said, Larry Kramer’s “Normal Heart,” along with Tony Kushner’s “Angels in America,” along with MTV reality shows that have often featured at least one gay man or woman, have contributed to a general cultural change that “helps the little gay kid in Minnetonka.”

As far as climate change, the 2004 global-warming disaster movie “The Day After Tomorrow” would probably be more effective than theater, he said. But that’s not really art.

“The artists can in an elegant way do their part, but the elegant way is smaller than the eye of a needle,” Mr. Baitz said.

Ms. Ruhl, whose play is partly about trying to explain snow to children of the future, disagreed.

“If you look at the great catastrophes and you look what artists were talking about five minutes before, they all had blinders on,” she said, mentioning the Holocaust in particular. She also brought up W. B. Yeats and Vaclav Havel as examples of playwrights who were politically engaged. “I don’t think of artists as being impotent in that way,” she said.

But the biggest advocate for the involvement of artists in the climate-change conversation is, funnily enough, Roger Payne, a biologist. In 1967 Dr. Payne and Scott McVay, who are appearing at a symposium as part of the festival, discovered that humpback whales produced a repeated pattern of sounds that could be described as a song, a discovery that popularized the plight of whales and put the spotlight on commercial whaling.

The discovery, Dr. Payne said in an interview, was both lucky and deliberate. He had studied the auditory faculties of bats and owls, but decided to focus his work on an animal that might draw attention to what he saw, even then, as an environmental crisis. He had never seen a whale, he said, but decided that if he found “some groovy thing” about whales that it would grab the public’s interest.

Having found something about whales that was far groovier than he could have imagined, Dr. Payne set out to find composers, writers and musicians who could propel the finding into popular culture. Judy Collins heard of the discovery, incorporated the songs into her 1970 album, “Whales and Nightingales,” and — voilà! — popular consciousness.

Dr. Payne, who has become an outspoken environmental advocate (and who married an actress, Lisa Harrow), thinks the same sequence of events could happen with artists and climate change.

“Never in the history of the world has it been factually as important to make very big changes in what humanity has done, and yet it’s just these scientists saying all this confusing stuff,” he said. “The real problem, as a friend of mine called it, is ‘getting the listening.’”

The plays in “Climate of Concern,” a free series, take place at 6:30 over three nights: Monday at the Atlas Room, New York University; Tuesday at the Segal Center, City University of New York; and Wednesday at the Rosenthal Pavilion of the Kimmel Center. For full addresses and other information: nyih.as.nyu.edu.
