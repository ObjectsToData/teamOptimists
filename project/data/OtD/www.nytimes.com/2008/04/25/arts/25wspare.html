AROUND TOWN

CHRIS ROCK When Senator Barack Obama made a speech about race last month, the consensus from commentators was that it was rare to hear such a frank meditation on the topic. Well, candor about race may not be a common practice for politicians on the stump, but it’s nothing new to comedy clubs, where black comedians have often held the spotlight by refracting the lens to illuminate issues of race in America. No one is better at this than Chris Rock, above.

Following in the tradition of Richard Pryor and Eddie Murphy, Mr. Rock’s jokes — if they can be called that — would be heartbreaking if they weren’t delivered in a voice ready to start a fight and paired with comedic timing ready to dispel one. Mr. Rock brings his own meditation on race, and class, to Madison Square Garden for five nights starting on Wednesday. The appearance, with Mario Joyner, is a return for Mr. Rock, who opened his appropriately named “No Apologies” tour with a sold-out show at the Garden on New Year’s Eve. Since then he has crisscrossed the country at a pace rivaling that of a political campaign, and this year’s election hasn’t been far from his mind. In January he gave Mr. Obama and Senator Hillary Rodham Clinton a good ribbing, although he reserved his most blistering words for Mrs. Clinton. Challenging her campaign message, that she has more experience, he said, “You get on a plane tomorrow, you want the pilot’s wife flying you?”

It comes as no surprise then that Mr. Rock is a supporter of Mr. Obama’s, and his backing isn’t limited to a mere link on his Web site; he has appeared at a fund-raiser for the senator, and his message was among those left on New Yorkers’ voice mail on Mr. Obama’s behalf on Feb. 5. But his routine isn’t partisan. His insights on race, class and gender relations may not be delivered against a backdrop of American flags, but that makes them no less worthy of consideration. (Wednesday through May 4 at 8 p.m., Madison Square Garden, 212-465-6741, thegarden.com; $49.50 to $79.50.) MONICA DRAKE

Museums and Sites

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Friday, 9 p.m. to 1 a.m., “One Step Beyond,” dance party and screening of “The Search for Life: Are We Alone?” $20. Rose Earth and Space Center. Through May 26, “Water: H2O = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter.” In the Imax theater, “Dinosaurs Alive!,” a film about museum paleontologists in search of dinosaur remains. Timed tickets to “Water: H20 = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax films, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200; amnh.org. Timed tickets: $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center for Earth and Space admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. Hours: daily, 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. The Rose Center is open until 8:45 p.m. for “Starry Nights: Fridays Under the Sphere,” with jazz, a cash bar and tapas on the first Friday of every month.

MOUNT VERNON HOTEL MUSEUM AND GARDEN, Sunday, noon to 4 p.m., “Silhouettes and Tea,” storytelling, silhouette cutting and refreshments in the garden. $30 per silhouette. 421 East 61st Street, Manhattan. Museum hours: Tuesdays through Sundays, 11 a.m. to 4 p.m. (212) 838-6878; mvhm.org. $12, adults; $10, members; $6 for children under 12.

NEW-YORK HISTORICAL SOCIETY, 170 Central Park West, at 77th Street. Through Aug. 17, “Woven Splendor From Timbuktu to Tibet: Exotic Rugs and Textiles From New York Collectors,” an exhibition of more than 70 items including rugs from members of the Hajji Baba Club, a rug-collecting group dating from the 1930s. Also through Aug. 17, “Allure of the East: Orientalism in New York, 1850-1930,” an exhibition of paintings, drawings, photographs and other items. Through Aug. 10, “French Founding Father: Lafayette’s Return to Washington’s America,” a multimedia exhibition with a video display and items associated with the Marquis de Lafayette. Tuesdays through Saturdays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; to 8 p.m. on Fridays; Sundays, 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. (212) 873-3400; nyhistory.org. $10; $7 for educators and 65+; $6 for students; free from 6 to 8 p.m. on Fridays; free at all times for members and children under 12.

NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY, Humanities and Social Sciences Research Library. Through June 14, “John Milton at 400: ‘A Life Beyond Life,’ ” with portraits, documents that include a first edition of “Paradise Lost” and recordings featuring music inspired by Milton’s poetry, from classical to heavy metal. Mondays, and Thursdays through Saturdays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Tuesdays and Wednesdays, 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m.; Sundays, 1 to 5 p.m. (212) 592-7730; nypl.org. Free.

NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY FOR THE PERFORMING ARTS, 111 Amsterdam Avenue, at 65th Street, Lincoln Center. Through June 14, “Writing to Character: Songwriters and the Tony Awards,” an exhibition on Tony Award-winning musicals and the collaboration in putting the shows together, with photographs, scores and other items. Through June 28, “New York Story: Jerome Robbins and His World,” an exhibition of documents, photographs, costumes and other items. Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Fridays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Mondays and Thursdays, noon to 8 p.m.; Saturdays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. (212) 870-1630. Free. nypl.org.

THE JEWISH MUSEUM, “Abstraction and the Holocaust,” a lecture by the author Mark Godfrey, in conjunction with the exhibition, “Action/Abstraction: Pollock, de Kooning, and American Art, 1940-1976.” Thursday at 6:30 p.m. $12 for adults; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students. (212) 423-3337. thejewishmuseum.org.

NEW YORK TRANSIT MUSEUM, Boerum Place and Schermerhorn Street, Brooklyn Heights. Through Dec. 7, “Tools of the Trade,” an exhibition of 50 items that have been used to build and maintain the city’s transportation system since the 1880s; included are an anemometer (wind gauge), a pneumatic drill and signal locks. Tuesdays through Fridays, 10 a.m. to 4 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays, noon to 5 p.m. (718) 694-1600. $5; $3 for those 3 to 17 and 62+; free for members and children under 3 every day, and for 62+ on Wednesdays. At the Gallery Annex, Grand Central Terminal, next to the stationmaster’s office. Through July 6, “A Railroad Reborn: Metro-North at 25,” an exhibition of photographs and artifacts in celebration of the railway’s 25th anniversary. Mondays through Fridays, 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. (212) 878-0106. Free.
