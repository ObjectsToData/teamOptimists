Published: May 15, 2008

Women and Fashion

Museum at F.I.T., Seventh Avenue at 27th Street; (212) 217-4558.

“Arbiters of Style: Women at the Forefront of Fashion,” an exhibition that will explore the influence of female designers, fashion industry professionals and tastemakers on style and design over the last 250 years, will open on May 21 and continue through Nov. 8.

The display of gowns, dresses, accessories and photographs will explore the influence of designers like Gabrielle (Coco) Chanel, Elsa Schiaparelli and Vivienne Westwood; retail executives like Hattie Carnegie and Rose Marie Bravo; and trendsetters like Empress Josephine, Lauren Bacall and Diana Vreeland.

The museum is open Tuesday through Friday, noon to 8 p.m.; Saturday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. Free. Information: fitnyc.edu/museum.

Irises in Bloom

The Presby Memorial Iris Gardens, 474 Upper Mountain Avenue (Laurel Place); Upper Montclair, N.J.; (973) 783-5974.

The bloom season at Presby will take place from May 16 through June 8, when the gardens will be open daily from 10 a.m. to 8 p.m. Among the special events will be the annual Family Garden Party on May 24, from 11 a.m. to 3 p.m., when there will be entertainment. In addition, guided tours will be offered on Memorial Day weekend, May 31 and June 1, at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. Tickets for the tours are $10. Information: presbyirisgardens.org.

Tribal and Textile Arts

69th Regiment Armory, 26th Street at Lexington Avenue; (212) 532-1516.

The 14th annual New York International Tribal & Textile Arts Show will open with a preview on May 14, from 6 to 9 p.m. Tickets, $60.

The show and sale of antiques, artifacts, textiles and carpets offered by 75 dealers will be open May 15 and 16, 11 a.m. to 8 p.m.; May 17, 11 a.m. to 7 p.m.; May 18, 11 a.m. to 5 p.m. Admission, good for the run of the show, $15. Information: www.caskeylees.com.

Images, New York City

The New York Public Library Humanities and Social Sciences Library, Fifth Avenue at 42d Street; (212) 930-0830.

“Eminent Domain: Contemporary Photography and the City,” an exhibition of works by five contemporary New York-based photographers, will be at the library through Aug. 29. The subject matter of the photographers, Thomas Holton, Bettina Johae, Reiner Leist, Zoe Leonard and Ethan Levitas, includes the Lower East Side; Madison Square Garden; one family in Chinatown; people on the elevated subway lines; and the geographical edges of the five boroughs.

Open Tuesday and Wednesday, 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m.; Monday, and Thursday through Saturday, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m. (closed Memorial Day weekend, Saturday through Monday). Free. Information: nypl.org.

Darwin and the Plant World

New York Botanical Garden, 200th Street and Southern Boulevard, the Bronx; (718) 817-8700.

“Darwin’s Garden: An Evolutionary Adventure” examines Charles Darwin as a botanist and explores how his work with plants shaped and validated his theories on evolution.

An exhibition in the Enid A. Haupt Conservatory re-creates the plants in Darwin’s gardens at Down House, his home in England, and the surrounding meadows and orchards. There, his observations of primroses, orchids, insect-eating plants and climbing plants inspired new ideas about natural selection. After the publication of his “Origin of Species” in 1859, Darwin continued to conduct plant experiments at Down House. He published six more books, with insights into cross-fertilization, natural selection and plant response to sunlight, touch and gravity. The Haupt exhibition will close on June 15.

The Mertz Library looks at Darwin’s lifelong study of plants through his writings, illustrated books, manuscripts and other historical documents (through July 20), and an interactive exhibition is at the Everett Children’s Adventure Garden, with a re-creation of Darwin’s laboratory, a display of plants central to his theories and a replica of his ship, the Beagle (through June 29).

Admission to “Darwin’s Garden” in the Haupt Conservatory is by ticket, at specified times. Advance purchase is recommended, at $20; students and those 62 and older, $18; children 2 to 12, $7. Open Tuesday through Sunday and on Memorial Day, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. (Closing at 3 p.m. on May 21 and June 5.) More information: nybg.org.

Japanese Graphic Arts

Brooklyn Museum, 200 Eastern Parkway at Washington Avenue; (718) 638-5000.

A collection of etchings, lithographs, watercolors, pastels and other graphic works will be on display in the Luce Visible Storage Study Center at the museum, in “Japonisme in American Graphic Art, 1880-1920.” The exhibition, which will run through Aug. 3, will examine the influence of Japanese graphic art on such American artists as James McNeill Whistler, Mary Cassatt, Robert Blum, Winslow Homer and Arthur Wesley Dow. Open Wednesday through Friday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m. (except for the first Saturday of every month, 11 a.m. to 11 p.m.). Admission is by contribution of $8; students and those 62 and older, $4; children under 12, free. Information: brooklynmuseum.org.

Shaker Design

Bard Graduate Center for Studies in the Decorative Arts, 18 West 86th Street; (212) 501-3000.

“Out of This World: Shaker Design Past, Present and Future,” an exhibition on the design ethic of Shaker communities since 1820, and their influence on such contemporary designers as George Nakashima and Roy McMakin, continues through June 15.

The exhibition looks at items made by Shaker craftsmen for use in their community as well as those made to sell to the outside world. Among the 150 objects on display are furniture, textiles, seed packages, boxes, ceramics and so-called gift drawings, which were spiritual in nature. There are also photographs, musical manuscripts and recordings.

Open Tuesday through Sunday, 11 a.m. to 5 p.m. (Thursdays until 8 p.m.) Admission is $3; those 65 and older, $2; children under 12, free. Information: bard.edu/bgc.

Swirl and Flourish

Cooper-Hewitt National Design Museum, 2 East 91st Street; (212) 849-8400.

An exhibition that examines Rococo design from its beginnings in 18th-century France through its many revivals runs through July 6. “Rococo: The Continuing Curve, 1730-2008” is a wide-ranging display of some 370 silver-and-jeweled decorative objects, furniture, ceramics, wall coverings, prints, drawings and textiles. The designers include Louis Comfort Tiffany, René Lalique, Toulouse-Lautrec, Alvar Aalto, André Dubreil, Dale Chihuly, Jack Lenor Larsen, Nicolette Brunklaus and Marcel Wanders.

“Campana Brothers Select: Works From the Permanent Collection” is the fifth in a series of exhibitions where designers act as guest curators. Fernando and Humberto Campana have chosen more than 30 objects dating from the 16th to the 20th centuries for their exhibition, including furniture, wallpapers, jewelry and book illustrations (through Sept. 28).

The museum is open Monday to Thursday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Friday, to 9 p.m.; Saturday, to 6 p.m.; Sunday, noon to 6 p.m. Admission is $15; students and those 62 and older, $10; children under 12, free; cooperhewitt.org.

Art at Parsons

Sheila C. Johnson Design Center of Parsons The New School, Fifth Avenue at 13th Street; (212) 229-8919.

“Soft Parade: Selections From the New School Art Collection” features sculptures, paintings, photographs, prints and textiles by David Hammons, Joseph Beuys, Jim Dine, Buckminster Fuller, Felix Gonzalez-Torres, Robert Indiana, Mariko Mori and Kara Walker (through June 20). Open Monday to Friday, 10 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Saturday, to 5 p.m.; Sunday, noon to 6 p.m. Admission is free. More information: newschool.edu/events.

Children’s Furniture

Historic Richmond Town, 441 Clarke Avenue off Arthur Kill Road, Richmond, Staten Island; (718) 351-1611.

“Bringing Up Baby: Children’s Furniture and Family Life,” an exhibition of high chairs, cradles and baby carriages made from the mid-19th to early-20th century, will be on display through March 29, 2009. The 30 pieces of furniture will be exhibited along with historic photographs, clothing, toys and quilts. There will be several pieces of furniture for adults and children to try, to imagine what it felt like for 19th-century children to use.

Fathers will get free admission and special programs will be offered on Father’s Day (June 15) .

Open Wednesday through Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m. Admission is $5; those over 62, $4; those 5 to 17, $3.50; members of the Staten Island Historical Society and children under 5, free. Information: historicrichmondtown.org.

Design in Philadelphia

At the Philadelphia Museum of Art, Benjamin Franklin Parkway at 26th Street, and the Perelman Building, Fairmount and Pennsylvania Avenues; (215) 763-8100.

“Fashioning Kimono,” an exhibition of some 90 kimonos exploring changes in the design of this Japanese garment from the early to mid-20th century, runs through July 20. It will look at the incorporation of Art Nouveau and Art Deco patterns from Western culture and changes to men’s and women’s kimonos as Japanese society turned increasingly toward Western-style dress.

“Colonial Philadelphia Porcelain: The Art of Bonnin and Morris” is at the museum through June 1. The 19 surviving objects made at the Bonnin and Morris company, the American China Manufactory, include woven fruit baskets, pickle stands and sauce boats. Visitors can also see some of American China’s competition and inspiration, in “Turned and Thrown: English Pottery 1660-1820.” It features drinking vessels, elaborate bowls and several services for tea, coffee and chocolate (through July 27).

Hours: Tuesday through Sunday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. (main building, Friday evenings until 8:45 p.m.). Admission: $14; those 62 and older, $12; students, $10; age 12 and younger, free. (Sundays, pay what you wish.) philamuseum.org.

One Architect’s Influence

National Building Museum, 401 F Street NW (near Fourth Street), Washington; (202) 272-2448.

“Eero Saarinen: Shaping the Future,” a retrospective of the architect’s work from the 1930s through the 1960s, continues through Aug. 23. The exhibition, using scale architectural models, photographs, drawings and furniture, will explore how Saarinen influenced design in the United States with projects like the stainless steel St. Louis Gateway Arch, which stands 630 feet above the Mississippi River, and the birdlike T.W.A. Terminal at John F. Kennedy International Airport in New York. Open Monday through Saturday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Sunday, 11 a.m. to 5 p.m. Admission is by donation of $5. More information: nbm.org.

Textile Arts

Textile Museum, 2320 S Street NW, Washington; (202) 667-0441.

“The Finishing Touch: Accessories From the Bolivian Highlands,” a collection of belts, bags and garments, will be on display through Sept. 18. The elaborately decorated items were woven and knitted from hand-spun yarns in the early part of the 20th century. Hours: Monday through Saturday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., and Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m. Suggested donation: $5. Information: textilemuseum.org.
