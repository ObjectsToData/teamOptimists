JAZZ

SONNY ROLLINS AND GARY GIDDINS IN CONVERSATION The tenor saxophonist Sonny Rollins is one of the few indisputable titans in the history of jazz. The writer Gary Giddins is one of its most acute and insightful critics. So the prospect of a conversation between them carries weight for a certain segment of the population. Such an event isn’t unprecedented — as recently as 2005 Mr. Rollins appeared under the auspices of the New Yorker Festival with Stanley Crouch, another critic of serious repute — but it does command fresh attention.

Mr. Giddins has been one of Mr. Rollins’s fiercest champions over the years. He has also described this tenor saxophonist as “jazz’s most provocatively enigmatic man.” His assessments of Mr. Rollins, above, have not only shaped public perceptions but have also had a more material impact: a dozen years ago, after the publication of a Village Voice essay arguing the selective merits of Mr. Rollins’s Milestone Records catalog, the label credited Mr. Giddins with the concept behind “Silver City: A Celebration of 25 Years on Milestone.” (Fans of Mr. Rollins know that two-disc compilation to be essential.)

Few jazz artists have a more complicated relationship with the spotlight than Mr. Rollins, and even fewer exercise his self-lacerating brand of humility. But in conversation he can be entertaining as well as thoughtful, and rewardingly self-aware. And there’s plenty to talk about, including his recent Carnegie Hall concert and his latest studio release, “Sonny, Please” (Doxy).

Mr. Giddins has recently marveled at that album’s closing track, an original calypso called “Park Palace Parade.” More specifically at Mr. Rollins’s solo on the tune, which he sees as intriguingly uncharacteristic. Which begs a line of inquiry, perhaps? (Wednesday at 6:30 p.m., Proshansky Auditorium, the Graduate Center at the City University of New York, 365 Fifth Avenue, at 34th Street, web.gc.cuny.edu/humanities; free, no reservations.) NATE CHINEN

Full reviews of recent jazz concerts: nytimes.com/music.

ADORATION OF THE MELODY: A TRIBUTE TO RUBY BRAFF (Tuesday) Five years after his death, the trumpeter and cornetist Ruby Braff receives affectionate homage from some of the musicians who knew him well: the trumpeter Warren Vaché, and the guitarists Bucky Pizzarelli and Howard Alden. (Nicki Parrott will play bass.) At 8 p.m., St. Paul’s Chapel, Columbia University, 116th Street and Amsterdam Avenue, Morningside Heights, (212) 851-1633, www.jazz.columbia.edu; $10; $7 for students and 62+. (Nate Chinen)

DAVE ALLEN (Sunday) Mr. Allen, a guitarist with a coolly modernistic sensibility, enlists a strong quartet with Tony Malaby on tenor saxophone, Chris Lightcap on bass and Mark Ferber on drums. At 9:30 p.m., 55 Bar, 55 Christopher Street, West Village, (212) 929-9883, 55bar.com; cover, $10. (Chinen)

A BROOKLYN MUSIC FESTIVAL (Friday through Sunday) The drummer Ben Perowsky presides over two weekends of programming featuring bands in which he plays. The lineup will include piano trios led by Jamie Saft (Friday at 9) and Uri Caine (Sunday at 9), as well as the singer-songwriter-accordionist Rachelle Garniez (Saturday at 9). The Moodswing Orchestra, led by Mr. Perowsky, will perform a 10:30 set on Friday and Sunday. At 9 and 10:30 p.m., Tea Lounge, 837 Union Street, near Sixth Avenue, Park Slope, (718) 789-2762, tealoungeny.com; suggested donation, $5. (Chinen)

★ DAVE BRUBECK/RAMSEY LEWIS (Thursday) Mr. Brubeck and Mr. Lewis have been popular paragons of jazz piano for more than 50 years, and each is recognized as a National Endowment for the Arts Jazz Master. Leading their respective bands, they perform a mix of original and standard material. (Through March 29.) At 8 p.m., Frederick P. Rose Hall, Jazz at Lincoln Center, 60th Street and Broadway, (212) 721-6500, jalc.org; $30 to $120. (Chinen)

BUFFALO COLLISION (Monday) Free improvisation is the working method for this collective made up of the alto saxophonist Tim Berne, the cellist Hank Roberts, the pianist Ethan Iverson and the drummer David King. As the name implies, results should be concussive at times, and maybe a bit hairy (in a good way). At 8:30 p.m., Roulette, 20 Greene Street, at Grand Street, SoHo, (212) 219-8242, roulette.org; $15. (Chinen)

★ TEDDY CHARLES QUINTET (Tuesday through Thursday) Fifty years ago, Teddy Charles was one of jazz’s top vibraphonists, leading his own sessions for Atlantic; then he largely withdrew from music, directing most of his energy toward sailing. Now, a few weeks shy of his 80th birthday, he plays a rare weeklong run with a band overseen by the saxophonist Chris Byars. (Through March 30.) At 9 and 11 p.m., Village Vanguard, 178 Seventh Avenue South, at 11th Street, West Village, (212) 255-4037, villagevanguard.com; cover, $20, with a $10 minimum. (Chinen)

RAVI COLTRANE QUARTET (Tuesday through Thursday) As he has since before the release of his most recent album, “In Flux” (Savoy), Ravi Coltrane positions his tenor and soprano saxophone playing against the cohesive stirrings of a good working band: the pianist Luis Perdomo, the bassist Drew Gress and the drummer E. J. Strickland. (Through March 29.) At 8:30 and 11 p.m., Birdland, 315 West 44th Street, Clinton, (212) 581-3080, birdlandjazz.com; cover, $40 and $30, with a $10 minimum.

(Chinen)

DAPP THEORY (Friday) Previewing material from the new album “Layers of Chance” (ObliqSound), the keyboardist Andy Milne fashions a contemporary fusion explicitly indebted to hip-hop, with help from John Moon on vocals, Loren Stillman on saxophones, Chris Tordini on bass and Kenny Grohowski on drums. At 9 and 10:30 p.m., Jazz Gallery, 290 Hudson Street, at Spring Street, South Village, (212) 242-1063, jazzgallery.org; cover, $15, $10 for members. (Chinen)

ENDANGERED SPECIES: THE MUSIC OF WAYNE SHORTER (Wednesday) This tribute to one of jazz’s greatest living composers is led by David Weiss, a skilled arranger for large ensembles. In this one, he marshals talent like his fellow trumpeters Ingrid Jensen and Jeremy Pelt. At 8:30 and 10:30 p.m., Iridium, 1650 Broadway, at 51st Street, (212) 582-2121, iridiumjazzclub.com; cover, $30, with a $10 minimum. (Chinen)

★ DREW GRESS’S 7 BLACK BUTTERFLIES (Wednesday) On his impressive new album, “The Irrational Numbers” (Premonition), the bassist Drew Gress pursues a form of disciplined abstraction with this band, featuring the trumpeter Ralph Alessi, the alto saxophonist Tim Berne, the keyboardist Craig Taborn and the drummer Tom Rainey. At 7:30 and 9:30 p.m., Jazz Standard, 116 East 27th Street, Manhattan, (212) 576-2232, jazzstandard.net; cover, $20. (Chinen)

CHRISTIAN HOWES QUARTET (Saturday) The violinist Christian Howes has a new DVD, “Live in Madrid” (Arkadia), that captures his visceral, high-energy style. Here he works in a band with the pianist Jason Lindner, the guitarist Joel Newton and the drummer Joel Rosenblatt. At 9 and 10:30 p.m., Cornelia Street Café, 29 Cornelia Street, West Village, (212) 989-9319, corneliastreetcafe.com; cover, $10, with a one-drink minimum. (Chinen)

JASON KAO HWANG/EDGE (Tuesday) Jason Kao Hwang, a probing violinist and composer, celebrates the release of “Stories Before Within” (Innova), the latest salvo by his four-piece band Edge, which includes the cornetist Taylor Ho Bynum, the bassist Ken Filiano and the drummer Andrew Drury. At 8:30 p.m., Roulette, 20 Greene Street, at Grand Street, SoHo, (212) 219-8242, roulette.org; $15.

(Chinen)

RUSS JOHNSON: OUT TO LUNCH (Saturday) Mr. Johnson, a forward-minded trumpeter, kicks off Merkin Concert Hall’s “Chamber Jazz” series with his re-creation of “Out to Lunch,” the classic Blue Note album by the multireedist Eric Dolphy. The band features Roy Nathanson on reeds, Myra Melford on piano, Brad Jones on bass and George Schuller on drums; the concert will also feature some recently discovered Dolphy compositions. At 8 p.m., Merkin Concert Hall, 129 West 67th Street, Manhattan, (212) 501-3330, kaufman-center.org; $30 in advance, $35 day of show. (Chinen)

RANDY JOHNSTON QUARTET (Friday and Saturday) A deeply proficient post-bop guitarist, Randy Johnston benefits here from the presence of a front-line partner, the alto saxophonist Vincent Herring. They both enjoy the support of Harvie S on bass and Tony Reedus on drums. At 8 and 10 p.m., Kitano Hotel, 66 Park Avenue, at 38th Street, (212) 885-7119, kitano.com; cover, $25, with a $15 minimum. (Chinen)

JOËLLE LÉANDRE (Friday) Ms. Léandre, a bassist of high repute in European new-music circles, presents the premiere of a work commissioned by the Brecht Forum, with an ensemble that includes J. D. Parran on woodwinds and Mat Maneri on viola. At 9 p.m., Brecht Forum, 451 West Street, near Bank Street, West Village, (212) 242-4201, brechtforum.org; $10. (Chinen)

LOCKE PLAYS MANCINI (Tuesday through Thursday) The vibraphonist Joe Locke brings appropriate crispness and flair to the music of Henry Mancini, with a band that includes the pianist Geoffrey Keezer, the bassist George Mraz, the drummer Clarence Penn and the singer (not the drummer) Kenny Washington. (Through March 30). At 7:30 and 9:30 p.m., Dizzy’s Club Coca-Cola, Frederick P. Rose Hall, Jazz at Lincoln Center, 60th Street and Broadway, (212) 258-9595, jalc.org; cover, $30, with a minimum of $10 at tables, $5 at the bar. (Chinen)

★ LIONEL LOUEKE TRIO (Tuesday) To celebrate the release of “Karibu,” his imaginative and vivid Blue Note debut, the West African jazz guitarist Lionel Loueke performs a single set with his fine working trio, which includes Massimo Biolcati on bass and Ferenc Nemeth on drums. At 7:30 p.m., Joe’s Pub, at the Public Theater, 425 Lafayette Street, at Astor Place, East Village, (212) 539-8778, joespub.com; cover, $15, with a two-drink minimum. (Chinen)

LAGE LUND TRIO (Tuesday) Lage Lund, a Norwegian guitarist with a sleek and introspective style, enlists an assertive rhythm team here: Orlando Le Fleming on bass and Rodney Green on drums. At 9 and 11 p.m., Cachaça, 35 West Eighth Street, West Village, (212) 388-9099, cachacajazz.com; cover, $10, with a $10 minimum. (Chinen)

PETE MALINVERNI (Friday and Saturday) Mr. Malinverni, a thoughtful pianist, regroups most of the musicians heard on his new album, “Invisible Cities” (Reservoir): the trumpeter Tim Hagans, the tenor saxophonist Rich Perry and the drummer Tom Melito. At 8 and 10 p.m. and midnight, Sweet Rhythm, 88 Seventh Avenue South, at Bleecker Street, West Village, (212) 255-3626, sweetrhythmny.com; cover, $20, with a $10 minimum. (Chinen)

CHARLES MCPHERSON (Thursday) Mr. McPherson, an alto saxophonist and one of bebop’s premier torchbearers, leads a band of estimable talent: Randy Brecker on trumpet, Ronnie Mathews on piano, Ray Drummond on bass and Billy Drummond on drums. (Through March 30.) At 7:30 and 9:30 p.m., Jazz Standard, 116 East 27th Street, Manhattan, (212) 576-2232, jazzstandard.net; cover, $25. (Chinen)

NEGRONI’S TRIO (Wednesday) The Puerto Rican pianist José Negroni and the drummer Nomar Negroni, his son, recently released a spirited album called “Father & Son” on the Venezuelan label Cacao. As he does on the album, the bassist Marco Panascia joins them here in interpreting a mix of Latin standards and Negroni originals. At 9:30 and 11 p.m., Smalls, 183 West 10th Street, West Village, smallsjazzclub.com; cover, $20. (Chinen)

★ ARTURO O’FARRILL AND THE AFRO-LATIN JAZZ ORCHESTRA (Thursday) In anticipation of the release of their new album, “Song for Chico” (Zoho), the pianist Arturo O’Farrill and his precision-honed big band perform a program of exuberant pan-Latin grooves. (Also March 29.) At 8 p.m., Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street, (212) 864-5400,symphonyspace.org; $30 and $25 in advance ($35 and $30 on the day of show); $27 and $23 for students and children (Chinen)

JEREMY PELT QUINTET (Friday and Saturday) Jeremy Pelt, a trumpeter with a big tone, a bracing technique and a bold improvisational style, leads a hard-charging band with J. D. Allen on tenor saxophone, Danny Grissett on piano, Dwayne Burno on bass and Billy Drummond on drums. At 8, 10 and 11:30 p.m., Smoke, 2751 Broadway, at 106th Street, (212) 864-6662, smokejazz.com; cover, $28. (Chinen)

BOB REYNOLDS QUARTET (Monday) Bob Reynolds, a resourceful tenor saxophonist and an unassuming yet effective composer, leads a post-bop group featuring the keyboardist Aaron Goldberg. At 10 p.m., 55 Bar, 55 Christopher Street, West Village, (212) 929-9883, 55bar.com; cover, $10. (Chinen)

SPYRO GYRA (Tuesday through Thursday) The saxophonist Jay Beckenstein continues to ride this smooth-jazz work horse, which has a typically upbeat new album called “Good to Go-Go” (Heads Up). (Through March 30.) At 8 and 10:30 p.m., Blue Note, 131 West Third Street, West Village, (212) 475-8592, bluenote.net; cover, $35 at tables, $20 at the bar, with a $5 minimum.

(Chinen)

GRANT STEWART QUINTET (Saturday and Tuesday) The tenor saxophonist Grant Stewart has a solid new album, “Young at Heart” (Sharp Nine), that reaffirms his grasp of the bebop idiom. He plays on Saturday with a bassist and drummer; on Tuesday he leads his regular group with the guitarist Joe Cohn. Saturday at 9 and 11:15 p.m., Bar Next Door, 129 Macdougal Street, West Village, (212) 529-5945, lalanternacaffe.com; cover, $10, with a one-drink minimum. Tuesday at 10:30 p.m. and midnight, Smalls, 183 West 10th Street, West Village, smallsjazzclub.com; cover, $20. (Chinen)

TAIN AND THE EBONIX (Friday through Sunday) Jeff (Tain) Watts, one of the most dazzling drummers in jazz, has a gutsy recent album called “Folk’s Songs” (Dark Key), with this band, featuring Christian McBride on bass, David Kikoski on piano and Marcus Strickland on saxophones. At 7:30 and 9:30 p.m., Jazz Standard, 116 East 27th Street, Manhattan, (212) 576-2232, jazzstandard.net; cover, $30, $25 on Sunday.

(Chinen)

VISION/RUCMA SERIES 2008 (Friday through Sunday) This avant-garde concert series continues each weekend through May. Friday’s program features a quartet led by the tenor saxophonist J. D. Allen, with Matana Roberts on alto saxophone, Eric Revis on bass and Rudy Royston on drums; Saturday and Sunday respectively feature a band called Knucklebean and a trio led by the vocalist Jean Carla Rodea. Friday and Saturday at 10:30 p.m., Sunday at 8 p.m., Living Theater, 21 Clinton Street, near Stanton Street, Lower East Side, (212) 696-6681, livingtheatre.org; $10, students $7. (Chinen)

DAN WEISS TRIO (Sunday and Tuesday) Dan Weiss, a drummer well versed in Indian classical music along with rock and modern jazz, leads a trio with his frequent collaborators, the pianist Jacob Sacks and the bassist Thomas Morgan. (Their set on Sunday, part of the Freestyle Jazz series, will be preceded by the duo of Tyshawn Sorey on percussion and Ben Gerstein on trombone.) Sunday at 9 p.m., Jimmy’s 43 East, 43 East Seventh Street, East Village, (212) 982-3006, freestylejazz.com; cover, $10. Tuesday at 10 p.m., 55 Bar, 55 Christopher Street, West Village, (212) 929-9883, 55bar.com; cover, $10. (Chinen)

DAVID WEISS AND THE POINT OF DEPARTURE QUINTET (Tuesday) An assertive trumpeter and accomplished arranger, David Weiss presents his razor-sharp ensemble with J. D. Allen on tenor saxophone, Nir Felder on guitar, Ameen Saleem on bass and Jamire Williams on drums. At 7:30 and 9:30 p.m., Jazz Standard, 116 East 27th Street, Manhattan, (212) 576-2232, jazzstandard.net; cover, $20. (Chinen)

BEN WOLFE QUARTET (Friday and Saturday) The bassist Ben Wolfe spearheads a group committed to a brand of jazz that’s straight-ahead but not straightforward, thanks partly to the effort of Jimmy Greene on tenor saxophone, Luis Perdomo on piano and Greg Hutchinson on drums. At 10 and 11:30 p.m., Smalls, 183 West 10th Street, West Village, (212) 675-7369, smallsjazzclub.com; cover, $20. (Chinen)

This article has been revised to reflect the following correction:

Correction: March 26, 2008
A jazz entry in the Listings page on Friday, about the Randy Johnston quartet at the Kitano Hotel, misspelled the given name of the bass player and included an extraneous punctuation for the initial that follows it. He is Harvie S, not “Harvey S.”
