AROUND TOWN

NY UKEFEST 2008 When Stephen DiLauro decided to pick up the ukulele again several years ago, he should have seen it coming. After all, this was the instrument that in the early 1900s, fresh from Hawaii, had managed to win over millions of American devotees who bought recordings by Tin Pan Alley musicians like Ukulele Ike. This is the instrument that a generation later was resurrected by Arthur Godfrey, who peddled a plastic version on television.

So when Mr. DiLauro, a guitarist, created a ukulele-playing persona called Uke Jackson in 2000, it should have been no surprise that the ukulele was on the cusp of a comeback driven by fringe musicians and that he would surrender his name to the instrument.

“It just sort of took over,” he said. It has a way of doing that.

Since then he has embarked on a flurry of ukulele-related activity, creating a radio show, joining an ensemble, writing a play titled “Sex! Drugs! & Ukuleles!” and, somewhere in between, starting a New York City festival, above, whose third annual installment begins on Thursday. Offbeat doesn’t begin to describe the roster of performers, who in addition to Mr. Jackson’s New York Ukulele Ensemble include groups like Les Chauds Lapins, who recreate French pop from the 1920s on hybrid banjo-ukuleles, and Bedroom Community, a Brooklyn band that describes its music as “ukulectronica.” The performances are supplemented by workshops, open mikes and what organizers are calling “Planet Earth’s largest display of ukuleles.” And Samuel Adams is throwing in free beer for good measure.

The festival drew about 3,000 visitors last year, and Mr. Jackson is optimistic that this year’s festival will prove a similar success. The festival is a departure from a world in which, he said, “everyone has their own iPod and is listening to the soundtrack of their own movie as they walk down the street.” For four days expert musicians and curious onlookers alike can participate in a communal experience. “I wanted to bring something light to New York,” he said. (Thursday through Sunday, Theater for the New City, 155 First Avenue, at Ninth Street, 212-254-1109, www.nyukefest.com.) MONICA DRAKE

Museums and Sites

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Friday, 9 p.m. to 1 a.m., “One Step Beyond,” a monthly party series with techno, electronic and hip-hop music and dancing, and screenings of the space show “The Search for Life: Are We Alone?”; $20. Through May 26, “Water: H2O = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter.” “Beyond,” large- format photographs of Earth’s planetary neighbors, through April 6; and “The Unknown Audubon: Mammals of North America,” artworks depicting animals, through August. In the Anne and Bernard Spitzer Hall of Human Origins there are more than 200 casts of prehuman and human fossils and artifacts that illustrate stages in physical and behavioral evolution. Also on view: “Vital Variety: A Visual Celebration of Invertebrate Biodiversity,” an exhibition of 24 large-format color photographs by the entomologist Piotr Naskrecki in the gallery next to the Imax theater. The Arthur Ross Hall of Meteorites displays the 34-ton Ahnighito meteorite, part of the Cape York meteorite. In the Imax theater, “Dinosaurs Alive!,” a film about museum paleontologists in search of dinosaur remains. Timed tickets to “Water: H20 = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax films, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200; amnh.org. Timed tickets: $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. Hours: daily, 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. The Rose Center is open until 8:45 p.m. for “Starry Nights: Fridays Under the Sphere,” with jazz, a cash bar and tapas, on the first Friday of every month.

MUSEUM OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, Fifth Avenue at 103rd Street. Friday at 2 p.m., “Discover East Harlem,” a walking tour; $9, $5 for members, Students and 62+. Saturday at 2 p.m., in celebration of Women’s History Month, an interactive performance based on the life of the writer Zora Neale Hurston, featuring the actress Elizabeth Van Dyke. Thursday at 6:30 p.m., a discussion about the cultural history of the Chelsea Hotel, with Ed Hamilton, author of “Legends of the Chelsea Hotel: Living With the Artists and Outlaws of New York’s Rebel Mecca”; Stanley Bard, the hotel’s former manager and one of its owners; Andrew Berman, the executive director of the Greenwich Village Society of Historic Preservation; and Sherill Tippins, author of “Dream Palace: The Extraordinary Life of the Chelsea Hotel,” to be published in 2009 by Houghton Mifflin; $9, $5 for members, students and 62+, reservations: (212) 534-1672, Ext. 3395. Through April 13, “Street Dance: The New York Photographs of Rudy Burckhardt,” a display of 90 works. Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. (212) 534-1672; mcny.org. Suggested admission: $9; $5 for students and 62+; free for children under 12 and for everyone on Sundays, 10 a.m. to noon; $20 for families.

NEW-YORK HISTORICAL SOCIETY, 170 Central Park West, at 77th Street. Monday at 7:30 p.m., “Fantasia for Sondheim,” with songs from “West Side Story,” “Into the Woods” and “A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum,” performed by Elaine Stritch and others; $75 to $500, with proceeds to benefit the American Musicals Project, which promotes the use of musical theater in the study of history and English; (212) 873-3400, Ext. 305. Through April 13, “Here Is New York: Remembering 9/11/01,” an exhibition of photographs, artifacts and oral histories documenting the terrorist attack. Through Aug. 10, “French Founding Father: Lafayette’s Return to Washington’s America,” a multimedia exhibition with a video display and items associated with the Marquis de Lafayette. Tuesdays through Saturdays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; to 8 p.m. on Fridays; Sundays, 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. (212) 873-3400; nyhistory.org. $10; $7 for educators and 65+; $6 for students; free from 6 to 8 p.m. on Fridays; free at all times for children under 12.

NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY, Humanities and Social Sciences Research Library. Through June 14, “John Milton at 400: ‘A Life Beyond Life,’ ” with portraits, documents that include a first edition of “Paradise Lost,” and recordings featuring music inspired by Milton’s poetry, from classical to heavy metal. Mondays, and Thursdays through Saturdays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Tuesdays and Wednesdays, 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m.; Sundays, 1 to 5 p.m. (212) 592-7730; nypl.org. Free.

NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY FOR THE PERFORMING ARTS, 111 Amsterdam Avenue, at 65th Street, Lincoln Center. Tuesday through June 28, “New York Story: Jerome Robbins and His World,” a exhibition of documents, photographs, costumes and other items. Saturday at 3 p.m., a lecture by Lynn Garafola, curator of the exhibition and a dance professor at Barnard College. Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Fridays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Monday, noon to 8 p.m.; Thursdays, noon to 8 p.m.; Saturdays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. (212) 870-1630. Free. nypl.org.

Gardens

NEW YORK BOTANICAL GARDEN, Bronx River Parkway (Exit 7W) and Fordham Road, Bedford Park, the Bronx. Includes 50 gardens and plant collections. Through April 6, the Orchid Show, with thousands of varieties on display, as well as lectures and gardening demonstrations. Open Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. Tram tours daily, every 20 minutes. (718) 817-8700, nybg.org. During the Orchid Show an All-Garden pass is $20; $18 for students and 62+; $7 for children 2 to 12; and free for children under 2. Grounds admission: $6 (Bronx residents, $5); $3 for 62+ and students; $1 for children 2 to 12; and free for under 2. Parking, $12.

Events

AMERICAN SPIRITUALS, at the Church of St. Paul the Apostle, Columbus Avenue at 60th Street. Saturday at 8 p.m. and Sunday at 3 p.m., a performance of Nicolas Flagellos’s “Passion of Martin Luther King” and other spirituals, by the New York City Master Chorale and soloists. nycmasterchorale.org. $15 to $35.

BRONX ARTISTS’S STUDIO TOUR, Sunday at 2 p.m., meeting at the entrance to the Nos. 5 and 6 trains at 138th Street and the Grand Concourse. Sponsored by Bronx Art Tours. (212) 772-3483. $20, including a reception.

CATHEDRAL CHURCH OF ST. JOHN THE DIVINE, 1047 Amsterdam Avenue, at 112th Street, Morningside Heights. Tours of this historic cathedral and its recent restoration, Tuesdays through Saturdays at 11 a.m. and 1 p.m. and Sundays at 2 p.m.; $5, $4 for students. Saturdays at noon and 2 p.m., the tour includes a climb on the spiral stone staircase to the roof for a panoramic view; $15, $10 for students; participants should be 12 and older, and flashlights are recommended. This Sunday at 2 p.m., “Sisters and Saints: Spotlight on Women,” a tour that highlights the contributions of women to the history and spiritual life of the cathedral; $10, $8 for students. Reservations: (212) 932-7347.

CITY UNIVERSITY OF NEW YORK GRADUATE CENTER, 365 Fifth Avenue, at 34th Street. Friday, 9:30 a.m. to 3:30 p.m., “Beyond Boundaries: Music and Israel @ 60,” a symposium on contemporary Israeli music with morning discussions and afternoon performances. Monday at 6:30 p.m., a discussion on the career of the Spanish composer Carlos Suriñach and his influence on modern dance, with dance professionals and scholars. www.gc.cuny.edu. Both are free.

ENDICOTT BATTERIES EXPLORATION, Sunday at 10 a.m. in Fort Totten Park, 212th Street and Cross Island Parkway, Queens. Led by the Urban Park Rangers, a visit to concrete bunkers dating to before the Civil War that once contained artillery guns and map rooms. nyc.gov/parks/rangers. Free.

SHORT-SHORT STORIES FROM AROUND THE WORLD, Friday, 12:30 to 2 p.m., at St. Peter’s Church, Lexington Avenue at 54th Street. Short works by Chekhov, Hemingway, Marquez and others will be read by Tammy Grimes, Jim Dale, Frances Sternhagen, Brian F. O’Byrne, Betsy Von Furstenberg and Bess Rous. Sponsored by the TIPA (Toward International Peace Through the Arts) Project, which has presented free readings for more than 30 years. (212) 567-0666.

34TH ANNUAL FLOWER SHOW, Macy’s Herald Square. Through Sunday, with special events, including design seminars, cooking demonstrations and floral workshops. Visible during store hours: Friday and Saturday, 10 a.m. to 9:30 p.m.; Sunday, 11 a.m. to 8:30 p.m. (212) 494-4495.
