AROUND TOWN

‘I AM A MAN’

You’ve heard this already, but it bears repeating: 40 years after the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated, Barack Obama became the first African-American to be elected president of the United States. The dream of racial equality that Dr. King so eloquently described at the March on Washington has seemingly come to fruition.

But the narrative is not so straightforward, particularly for black men. They are the subject of examination in “I Am a Man,” a concise exhibition at the Museum of Contemporary African Diasporan Arts in Brooklyn. The show is inspired by the Memphis sanitation workers’ strike that Dr. King supported in the weeks before his killing in 1968. Denied the right to form a union, 1,300 workers took to the streets carrying placards with the wording from which the exhibition borrows its name. Ernest C. Withers’s photographs of the striking workers are projected on one of the gallery walls.

The meat of the show, though, is the works by 12 contemporary artists charged with reflecting on the era of the strike. Many came up with images that are jarring only because they run so counter to stereotypes. In a LeRoy Henderson photograph, a boy in a suit stands in front of a coffin and clings to a flag that has been folded in military fashion. Rah Crawford’s buoyant, optimistic depictions of a grinning man (above, “iMan: Media”) suggest endless possibility. And about a third of the works show black men with children.

“It was very important to them to show another side of black men that is not often shown,” Laurie Cumbo, the museum’s founder and a curator of the exhibition, said of the artists. “Often we mimic what we see, and because we don’t see this, we don’t mimic it.”

But the show is not an aspirational one that papers over issues facing black men. A documentary by Lorenzo Steele Jr. deals with prison life. It suggests that despite all the progress that this week signifies, there are still issues to be sorted out. Some of the artists are participating in a talk on Saturday at 2 p.m. that will no doubt explore what those are. (Through Jan. 18, 80 Hanson Place, at South Portland Street, Fort Greene, Brooklyn, 718-230-0492, mocada.org; $4 suggested donation; free for children 12 and under.) MONICA DRAKE

Museums and Sites

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Every Friday and Saturday at 7:30 and 8:30 p.m., “SonicVision,” an animated alternative- music show, is presented in the Rose Center; tickets, $15; $12 for members. Saturday at 1 p.m., storytelling by Herb Rice, an artist and lecturer and member of the Coast Salish First Nation, of the Northwest Coast. Through Aug. 16, “Climate Change: The Threat to Life and a New Energy Future,” an interactive display. Through May 25, “The Butterfly Conservatory, Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter.” Through Jan. 4, “The Horse” examines the ways humans and horses are linked. Through Jan. 5, “Lizards & Snakes: Alive!,” a display of more than 60 live reptiles from five continents. In the Imax theater, playing every hour on the half-hour from 10:30 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., “Sea Monsters: A Prehistoric Adventure,” about reptiles found beneath the water 80 million years ago. Timed tickets to “Climate Change,” “The Butterfly Conservatory,” “The Horse” and “Lizards & Snakes: Alive!,” as well as tickets to Imax films, include museum admission: $24; $18 for students and 60+; $14 for those 2 to 12. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center for Earth and Space admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $32; $24.50 for students and 60+; $20 for those 2 to 12. Hours: daily, 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200; amnh.org.

MUSEUM OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, Fifth Avenue at 103rd Street. Through Feb. 16, “Eudora Welty in New York: Photographs of the Early 1930s,” featuring photographs she took in her home state of Mississippi and in New York City. Monday at 6:30 p.m., a discussion on racial perceptions and profiling with Moustafa Bayoumi, author of “How Does it Feel to be a Problem? Being Young and Arab in America”; Debbie Almontaser, the founding principal of the Khalil Gibran International Academy; and Shadia Marji, a community activist. Leila Buck, a Lebanese-American performer, is the moderator. Through Dec. 31, “Catholics in New York, 1808-1946,” an exhibition of 400 secular and religious items. Thursday at 6:30 p.m., “African-American Catholics in New York City,” a discussion with scholars and clergy members; $9, $5 for members, 62+ and students; reservations required: (212) 534-1672, Ext. 3395. Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; mcny.org. Suggested admission: $9; $5 for students and 62+; free for children under 12 and for everyone on Sundays from 10 a.m. to noon; $20 for families.

NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY, Humanities and Social Sciences Research Library. Through Jan. 25, “Afghanistan, or The Perils of Freedom: Photographs by Stephen Dupont,” a display by the Australian photojournalist that documents daily life. Through Feb. 15, “Yaddo: Making American Cultures,” an exhibition on the artists’ retreat, with photographs, papers and other objects. Mondays, and Thursdays through Saturdays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Tuesdays and Wednesdays, 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m.; Sundays, 1 to 5 p.m. (212) 592-7730; nypl.org. Free.

Gardens

Through Nov. 16, “Kiku: The Art of the Japanese Chrysanthemum,” with an array of varieties, as well as Japanese maples, pines, bamboo and herbaceous plants. Related events include tours (Saturday and Sunday at 12:30, 2:30 and 3:30 p.m.); art workshops (Saturday, 1 to 4 p.m. and Sunday, 2 to 4 p.m.); and dance performances (Saturday at 3:30 p.m. and Sunday at 1:30 and 3 p.m.). Open Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. (later on selected days during the Holiday Train Show, which begins Nov. 23.) Tram tours daily, every 20 minutes. (718) 817-8700, nybg.org. An all-garden pass is $20; $18 for students and 62+; $7 for children 2 to 12; and free for members and children under 2. Grounds admission only: $6 (Bronx residents, $5); $3 for 62+ and students; $1 for children 2 to 12; and free for under 2 and for everyone all day on Wednesdays and from 10 a.m. to noon on Saturdays. Parking, $12.

QUEENS BOTANICAL GARDEN, 43-50 Main Street, Flushing. Saturday at 1:30 p.m., “Medicine Cabinet in Your Kitchen,” a workshop on remedies for colds and ailments using spices and herbs; $15, $10 for members. Tuesdays through Sundays, 8 a.m. to 4:30 p.m.; Parking at the garden, $5 ($3 for members). (718) 886-3800, queensbotanical.org. Donations accepted.

WAVE HILL, Independence Avenue and West 249th Street, Riverdale, the Bronx. Saturday and Sunday at 1 and 2:30 p.m., an art project on how to make a cornhusk doll. Sunday at 2 p.m., a jazz concert by the Ben Monder Trio; $24, $15 for members, $21 for students and 65+. Tuesdays through Sundays, 9 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., (718) 549-3200, wavehill.org. $6; $3 for students and 65+; $2 for children 6 and older; free for members and under 6; Tuesdays, free all day through December; Saturdays, free 9 a.m. to noon, year round. Onsite parking, $8; free offsite parking with free shuttle service to the garden.
