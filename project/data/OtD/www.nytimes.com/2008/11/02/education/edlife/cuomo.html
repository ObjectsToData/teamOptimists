In Class

Lessons on an Election

Angela Jimenez for The New York Times

THE HORSE’S MOUTH At his public affairs class at Baruch College, the former governor Mario M. Cuomo holds forth on the election.

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
By CLYDE HABERMAN
Published: October 30, 2008

ON the same night that Gov. Sarah Palin accepted the Republican nomination for vice president, a public affairs class at Baruch College in Manhattan vigorously debated her qualifications to stand, as the cliché has it, a heartbeat from the Oval Office.

Some of the 23 students in the class scoffed at a theme echoed by many Republicans, namely that Ms. Palin’s brief tenure as Alaska’s governor gave her enough executive experience for national office. C’mon, the students said, who is she? She’s governor of Alaska. It’s a state with fewer than 700,000 people. Brooklyn alone has three times the population.

But the lecturer, Mario M. Cuomo, waved a cautionary finger. A state’s size is not the only thing to consider, he said. A governor is a governor. “The fact that what you do affects more people,” he said, “doesn’t change the nature of what you do.” As a three-time governor of New York, a state with a population of 19 million, Mr. Cuomo, definitely affected many people in his day.

The students digested his words. Some seemed surprised, perhaps expecting him, as a prominent Democrat, to take a potshot at the relatively untested Alaskan. But he went with his policy-focused head, not his partisan heart, and gave the students something to ponder about the nature of authority and leadership.

Which is exactly what he wanted to do this semester when he agreed to teach a course on the presidential election with a veteran professor of public affairs at Baruch, Douglas A. Muzzio. For two and a half hours each Wednesday evening, the two have merged the scholarly imperatives of the classroom and the gritty realities of the arena, through the eyes of a politician who bears the scars of many a battle — some won, some lost.

The title of the course is “The 2008 Presidential Election: Where We Are as a Nation, Where We Want to Be, How We Get There.” What it really should be called, Professor Muzzio told the class, is “two guys from Queens talking politics.” Were Shakespeare around, he might have called it “The Two Gentlemen of Corona.”

Around the country this fall, many colleges have devised ways to incorporate the election into their curriculums. Hofstra University in Hempstead, N.Y., offered no fewer than seven campaign-related courses, including one focused on the presidential debates — no surprise, given that Hofstra was playing host to the final encounter between Senators Barack Obama and John McCain.

At another debate site, Belmont University in Nashville, professors are weaving campaign-themed assignments into their usual material. One humanities symposium examined how language and argument figure in political discourse.

At American University in Washington, Dotty Lynch, a former senior political editor at CBS News, has supervised her 21 students in putting together their own Web-based polling operation.

But the marquee attraction of the fall is Mr. Cuomo at Baruch, a division of the City University of New York.

The undergraduates are clearly delighted to have time with the man introduced to them by Professor Muzzio as “the most gifted rhetorician of his generation,” a soft-spoken politician whose thoughts pour forth in long paragraphs.

“He can explain things from personal experience, and you can’t match that with an ordinary professor,” said Ben Guttmann, a 19-year-old who was just starting grade school when Mr. Cuomo left office in 1994. But the student was quite familiar with the governor’s rousing keynote address at the 1984 Democratic National Convention, the moment that led many Democrats to think of him as a future presidential candidate.

This doesn’t mean these young people are star-struck. On occasion, they have thrown out polite challenges, as one woman did after Mr. Cuomo, 76, argued that affirmative action programs can put the concepts of individual freedom and of social equality at odds.

The syllabus is ambitious, encompassing culture battles, constitutional law, war and foreign policy. Religious and racial perceptions are no small matter given that Senator Obama is running. This semester, too, the financial crisis hit with a bang, validating Mr. Cuomo’s prediction at the get-go that the economy would be the big issue.

The course has roots in a dialogue that the former governor had in 2007 with Newt Gingrich, the former House speaker. From opposite ends of the political spectrum, the two men joined for a debate at Cooper Union in New York, the purpose being to discuss serious issues seriously and at length, liberated from the tortured constraints of televised debates that reward not wisdom but 90 seconds of glibness. Afterward, the audience was asked what it thought of the format. “The place went bonkers,” Mr. Cuomo said.

He hankered to explore issues in depth. Then along came an offer to teach this class; Florence H. Frucher, a Baruch adjunct professor who worked in the Cuomo administration, is assisting.

In the 1960s, well before he entered elective politics, Mr. Cuomo taught law at his alma mater, St. John’s University in Queens. He finds his return to the classroom a challenge. “It’s very hard,” he said. “I take the stuff so seriously. You have to prepare, you have to know what you’re talking about and you have to study. You have to study just to teach.”

In Professor Muzzio’s view, a good teacher, like a good politician, needs pacing, humor and a feel for the audience. “That only comes with doing it,” he said.

The students — shock, shock — have not always been so well prepared. “I know you came to listen to the governor,” Professor Muzzio said in exasperation one evening, the words coming out staccato and his arms sweeping the room in operatic gestures. “I don’t care! You’ve got to do the readings.”

In his talks, Mr. Cuomo has reinforced the inherent contradictions of politics. Important as issues are, he said, “people don’t give you a win on the issues.” Victory or defeat often hinges on gaffes and voters’ gut feelings. Mr. Obama “is the best package of political gifts I’ve ever seen,” he said. Yet for all those gifts, “does he have the answers?” Ms. Palin opposes abortion rights. Yet is it clear what she might do if in office? “We know what her own views are,” he said, “but not necessarily what her policy is.”

Truth itself is elusive. One evening, he shook his head when Professor Muzzio told the class that “lying is an essential component of politics.”

“I think it’s very much overdone, this notion that you have to deceive,” Mr. Cuomo said. But trouble lies in wait for those who speak their minds plainly. On another evening, he cited Walter F. Mondale’s disastrous race in 1984 against Ronald Reagan. During the debates, Mr. Mondale raised the prospect of new taxes no matter who won the election. Those remarks helped do him in. Mr. Reagan won re-election — and then pursued tax increases.

What’s the lesson? “If you’re 100 percent right, 100 percent valorous, 100 percent candid, you lose,” Mr. Cuomo said.

Heads in notebooks, the students took it all down. This was an insight into political reality they wanted to remember.

Clyde Haberman writes the NYC column for The Times. Dabrali Jimenez contributed reporting for this article.
