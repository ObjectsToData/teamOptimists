Older, and Wiser, Students

Jill Connelly for The New York Times

STILL LEARNING Lee Crystal, 61, right, a lawyer, in class at the University of Southern California with Young Miller, a fellow student.

By CHRISTINE LARSON
Published: October 22, 2008

LIKE many retired couples, Wood and Lillian Nordenholz of Lewisville, N.C., enjoy traveling, gardening and socializing. But only after they have finished their homework. For the past three years, the Nordenholzes have been pursuing master’s degrees through the University of North Carolina at Greensboro.

ON CAMPUS Wood and Lillian Nordenholz, both 71, are master’s students at the University of North Carolina at Greensboro.

“So far, our grades are about equal,” said Mr. Nordenholz, 71, a retired telecommunications executive. But it’s intellectual curiosity, far more than grades, that motivates the couple.

Since enrolling in a part-time master of arts in liberal studies program, they have taken seminars about Nobel Prize-winning scientists, American cinema and sustainable agriculture (the latter included an optional field trip to Tuscany). “We don’t have to prove anything,” said Mrs. Nordenholz, a retired Spanish teacher. “It’s the journey rather than the destination.”

It’s a journey shared by more and more older adults. The number of graduate students older than 50 grew by 38 percent between 2001 and 2005, to about 173,000 from 125,000, according to the National Center for Education Statistics. That’s more than twice the rate of growth for graduate students overall.

Many of these students are drawn to graduate-level liberal studies programs like the one at the University of North Carolina. Typically administered by continuing education departments, these programs offer a twist on the traditional liberal arts curriculum, emphasizing in-depth study across disciplines, including history, literature, politics, science and the arts.

Although no national statistics exist showing how many students over 50 are enrolled in such programs, administrators at Johns Hopkins University, Dartmouth College and the University of Oklahoma estimate that at least 10 percent of their graduate liberal arts students are over 55 or retired. At Harvard University Extension School, about 16 percent of 484 candidates for the master of liberal arts degree are over 50.

“Many older students have for a very long time been in a world of careers” that are very specialized, said Robert L. Manuel, dean of Georgetown University’s school of continuing studies, which offers master’s and doctoral degrees in liberal studies. Graduate programs help them “make meaning of their experiences and of the world,” he said.

Dr. Suzanne Koven, 51, enjoyed her work as a primary care doctor in Boston but wondered if she could weave her longtime love of writing into her professional life. She took an essay course through Harvard’s extension school, then applied for its master of liberal arts program.

“I know realistically and also by preference that I wouldn’t want to practice medicine until I’m 90,” she said. She graduated last spring, after writing a thesis that was part personal memoir, part reflection on memoir as a literary genre. She said she hoped to combine medicine and writing, “with the balance shifting more toward writing over time.”

George Sparling, 61, a lawyer in Mechanicsville, Md., found a new career direction by pursuing an advanced degree — although that wasn’t his goal when he enrolled at Georgetown in 2002. “My intention,” he said, “was to take whatever class interested me for no particular reason.”

He studied British theater and the Peloponnesian War before discovering a passion for American political thought and an interest in teaching. When Georgetown introduced a part-time doctoral program in liberal studies in 2005, Mr. Sparling signed up. He hopes to finish in December 2009 and is teaching a continuing education course on American elections.

Other advanced degree candidates are simply seeking more structure and discipline than what they find in noncredit courses.

“I’m essentially on the lazy side,” said Linda Graham, 66, of Baltimore, a retired insurance underwriter. She took courses at Osher Lifelong Learning Institute at Johns Hopkins for four years, then enrolled in the university’s master of liberal arts program in September. “If someone says I have to write a paper and demonstrate that I’ve retained something, I learn more,” she said.

Liberal studies programs often appeal to people whose professional education left a few holes. Bonnie Turman, 60, of Livermore, Calif., earned a Ph.D. in mathematics in 1980. Still, she said, “I always felt a little culturally illiterate. My reading of the great works was hit or miss.”

After retiring from Cisco Systems in 2005, she started working on a master of liberal arts degree at Stanford University, where she took seminars on Dickens, evolutionary thought, linguistics, the geology of California and other topics.

During the year and a half she spent working on her master’s thesis on Nietzsche, she would write in the mornings, then take her grandson, Duncan, to music class. “It was just wonderful to work on the thesis and deal with harsh existential questions, and then go play with this little kid who was so joyful,” she said.

Although students in graduate liberal studies programs typically take just one seminar a term, the work load can be heavy.

In addition to a thesis or similar master’s project, most programs require a series of core courses in humanities, as well as several electives. A few, like those at the University of North Carolina at Greensboro and the University of Oklahoma (all campuses), can be done entirely online, but most require a combination of classroom attendance and online participation. Dr. Koven, from Boston, describes her classes as more difficult than her undergraduate courses at Yale. For one class, she read 30 plays in 13 weeks. “It was pure joy, but it wasn’t a joke,” she said.

The challenges start as early as the application process, which usually requires transcripts, letters of recommendation and essays. “It felt strange to send away for my college transcript,” said Mr. Nordenholz, who graduated from Brown University in 1958.

Admission can be competitive: Stanford usually receives 60 to 112 applications every year for roughly 20 spots in its graduate liberal studies program.

While graduate liberal studies programs generally welcome adult students of any age, some cater to midcareer workers and emphasize professional advancement. “Most of our students have a job and are looking at an advanced degree” to give themselves additional credentials for moving up the corporate ladder, said James Pappas, dean of the college of liberal studies at the University of Oklahoma.

That professional emphasis disappointed Beverly Rorem, 72, who signed up for the master’s program last year, but dropped out because she felt there were too many management-related requirements.

“I wanted to study humanities and writing, and I felt like I had to go through too much other junk that didn’t interest me,” said Ms. Rorem, a retired legal secretary. “If I were 25 years old, I might have waded through it, but it just wasn’t what I wanted.”

For older students, getting what they want is particularly important, as they are likely to pay for their degree themselves. Tuition varies widely. A master’s degree from Oklahoma’s program costs about $8,600 for residents and $22,100 for nonresidents; Stanford’s program costs $26,000 to $35,000, depending how long it takes to complete.

In some states, including Arkansas, Connecticut, Delaware and North Carolina, public universities waive tuition for older students, and some universities offer financial aid. But most older graduate students pay their own way.

“It’s expensive, and it won’t help my job at all,” said Lee Crystal, 61, a Los Angeles lawyer who is completing the master’s in liberal studies program at the University of Southern California. His degree will cost about $34,000. In his view, that’s a luxury, but also an investment. “Maybe I’m doing this instead of buying a Corvette,” he said. “In a few years, the Corvette would just be a used car. But this will last.”

Information on programs at 120 colleges and universities is available at aglsp.org, the Web site of the Association of Graduate Liberal Studies Programs. Click on “current members.”
