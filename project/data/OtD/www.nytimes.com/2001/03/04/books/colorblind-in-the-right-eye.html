Colorblind in the Right Eye

By Scott L. Malcomson
Published: March 4, 2001

GUESS WHO'S COMING

TO DINNER NOW?

Multicultural Conservatism

in America.

By Angela D. Dillard.

245 pp. New York:

New York University Press. $26.95.

One does not associate scholars with perfect timing, news-wise, but Angela D. Dillard's ''Guess Who's Coming to Dinner Now?'' could not be more of the moment. A rainbow arcs above a Republican White House. The desperate mummery of the Philadelphia convention has issued in something more honorable: a looks-like-America cabinet. George W. Bush said that the varied social identities of his early appointments were meant to send a message, namely ''that people who work hard and make the right decisions in life can achieve anything they want in America.'' In other words, the importance of the dark skins and female bodies of those early appointments was that they showed how unimportant dark skins and female bodies are in America. The blackness of Colin Powell is important in that it shows the unimportance of blackness. To judge from Angela D. Dillard's book, this is the essence of multicultural conservatism.

''Given the extent to which the conservative tradition in America was shaped by racialist and outright racist doctrines,'' Dillard notes, ''by heterosexual-patriarchal notions of gender and family and by xenophobic influences, the existence of African-American, Latino, women and homosexual conservatives is that much more fascinating.'' As her language indicates, Dillard, who teaches history and politics at the Gallatin School at New York University, writes within the style current among younger, left-liberal humanities scholars -- a timorous style that often sacrifices verve to caution, advancing gingerly toward rather legalistic formulations about human reality. Her choice of the word ''fascinating'' is also telling. Dillard makes it clear that she disagrees with conservatism, but she tries, successfully, to suspend judgment in presenting conservative arguments.

''Guess Who's Coming to Dinner Now?'' is more valuable for its information than for its arguments. Dillard economically traces the roles of racial activism, feminism and gay liberation as foils and inspirations for neoconservatism, the New Right, the Moral Majority and the Christian Coalition. She meanwhile keeps an eye on the relations between these fired-up groups and the Republican Party, which they have sought to make their mainstream vehicle. Against this backdrop, Dillard analyzes the famous (Clarence Thomas, Linda Chavez), the obscure (Elizabeth Wright's black conservative newsletter, Issues and Views) and the in-between (the memoirs of the gay conservative Marvin Liebman).

Her main inquiries concern blacks and gays, the groups that represent the greatest threat to conservative colorblindness and family values. Blacks, in the conservative view Dillard presents, are the definitive race, and so the greatest obstacle to colorblindness and race-free individualism. Homosexuals, similarly, are the definitive nonheterosexuals -- yes, there is a circularity at work here -- and so the chief obstacle to a pleasing stability of male and female social roles. As Dillard persuasively shows, antigay feeling (and a related antifeminism) has been the cement, along common-enemy lines, for a cross-racial conservatism. And a certain distancing from blacks has helped to unite nonblack conservative groups. Blacks have not, like gays, quite served as a common enemy. But placing one's group at a distance from blacks, especially the black urban poor, has provided a common bond.

Dillard also takes some quiet glee in pointing out that the special legitimacy of prominent conservatives who are not straight white men derives from the ''identities'' they supposedly don't want or consider trivial. Clarence Thomas is black, Linda Chavez is female and at least nominally Hispanic, Andrew Sullivan is gay. Their opinions on issues relating to the groups with which they are identified gain credibility for autobiographical reasons. Dillard devotes one of her most absorbing chapters to conservative autiobiography.

I wish she had pushed at this point a little harder, because it hints at a neglected aspect of American history. Every one of the groups Dillard discusses has a long tradition of conservative separatism. African-Americans have sought various forms of protective isolation, informed by cultural conservatism, since the Revolution. The separate black church, though a complex and frequently riven institution, can be considered conservative, as can many of the Hispanic, Chinese, Korean and other ''ethnic'' churches. There have been many moments in the history of feminism when a separate, distinct female nature and heritage were considered worth conserving. And was that a gay ghetto we just passed, or a gay paradise? Leaving aside the question of straight white men, conservative identity politics is not an oxymoron or a subject for light irony. It is a longstanding fact of American life. And I would note in passing that multiculturalism, in the form of cultural determinism, has become fashionable in American foreign-affairs circles as well, following the early leads of Samuel Huntington, Francis Fukuyama and Robert Kaplan, among others. Here again, multiculturalism is not a new idea nor can it be considered left or liberal.
