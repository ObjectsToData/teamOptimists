WEDDINGS: VOWS; Katharine Emmet and James Peterson

By LOIS SMITH BRADY
Published: March 4, 2001

SOME New Yorkers are easy to classify. You see a six-foot-plus beauty running into the Condé Nast building and just know she's a model who never needs a ladder or a date. Katharine Temple Emmet, on the other hand, is difficult to classify. She speaks her mind, bluntly sometimes and whimsically at other times.

At 54, she's been divorced once and seems both gritty and graceful, an elegant survivor. She wears cashmere sweater sets to the symphony, but also loves folk-rock concerts in beat-up West Village bars.

An interior designer who now runs the New York office of Mlinaric Henry & Zervudachi, a design firm based in London, Ms. Emmet is social and antisocial at once. She has lots of friends, but for years spent most weekends avoiding people at her remote hilltop home in Pawlet, Vt.

One summer Friday in 1990, she boarded a Greyhound bus to Vermont, piled her clothes and papers onto the seat next to her to ensure privacy and began reading a Gabriel García Márquez novel. As the bus traveled north and the sun began to set, she discovered her overhead light was broken. So, she simply read on in the near-darkness until a passenger a few seats away, James Charles Peterson, interrupted her.

''He said: 'Look, my light is working. Would you like to move up here?' '' she remembered. ''I was just grateful for the light, so I moved and buried my head in my book, enthusiastically.''

Mr. Peterson, a former J. P. Morgan banker turned schoolteacher, was traveling to his weekend house in Landgrove, Vt. Known for being contemplative and talkative, he was reading a book on world mythology by Joseph Campbell.

The two rode in silence, until Ms. Emmet asked about the Campbell book. ''That was the icebreaker,'' she remembered. ''After that, we talked about all sorts of things. He had plenty of opinions and plenty of curiosities.''

Mr. Peterson, who is 55 and has been divorced once, now teaches humanities at the Choir Academy of Harlem. He said he tries to inspire his students to ''have a life of the mind'' and to appreciate poetry ''written by old guys with white beards.''

That Sunday, they ran into each other on the bus back to New York and soon became ''bus friends'' as Ms. Emmet said, riding up to Vermont together on Fridays and back to the city on Sundays, talking about literature, design, divorce and aging through numerous traffic jams and snowstorms.

After a few years they began dating, having dinner together in tiny French bistros in the West Village, where Ms. Emmet then lived, and swimming before work in a public pool on Carmine Street. Echoing the moment they met, he continued to make sure she always had enough reading light, heat, conversation and good food.

''We swam in an ocean mile race this summer together,'' said Ms. Emmet, who now lives with Mr. Peterson in Brooklyn. ''He just swam next to me and herded me very gently like a very loving sheepdog seeing that I didn't go astray out into the ocean.''

Ms. Emmet, who has one daughter from her first marriage, added, ''after the loss of one marriage -- the family-making marriage, which is the saddest to lose -- I am finally aware of how fragile a relationship can be.''

On Feb. 17, they got married in downtown Manhattan at St. Paul's Chapel on Broadway and Fulton Street, which is surrounded by a graveyard so old the stones look as if they're melting like Popsicles in the sun. Before the ceremony began, guests listened to a concert of Handel performed by members of the American Virtuosi, a chamber orchestra that played 18th-century instruments. The music created a thoughtful, poetic mood, not unlike a quiet bus ride through Vermont.

''Falling in love and getting engaged and marrying in your 50's is somewhat akin to rereading the great literature you read at 18,'' Mr. Peterson said. ''You have so much more life experience, and you can reflect on it and think about what it all means.''
