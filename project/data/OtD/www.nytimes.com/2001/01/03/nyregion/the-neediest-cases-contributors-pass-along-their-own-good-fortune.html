THE NEEDIEST CASES; Contributors Pass Along Their Own Good Fortune

By AARON DONOVAN
Published: January 3, 2001

The New York Times Neediest Cases Fund has received nearly $870,000 more in contributions than it had by this point last year. If donations continue to flow in at the rate they did last year, the fund will have been given more money than in any other year in its 89-year history.

There are still two months to go before the annual appeal ends, on the last day of February.

During the current appeal, the fund, which supports seven of New York's oldest and most respected charities, has received $4,884,782.59 from readers of The Times and the trusts, foundations and estates that they manage. At an equivalent point last year, the fund had received $4,015,217.81. The highest final total was $6,701,122.57, given during the 1998-99 appeal.

Many readers send short notes along with their donations.

''Up until last week I was reading the letters, but last week the volume became so great that we hired an intern to do it,'' said Kyung Park, the New York Times accountant who keeps track of gifts to the fund. ''A lot of people felt they are very blessed, economically, and a lot of them say they wish they could give more.''

The fund has grown in part because of many large gifts from readers. So far in the current appeal, the fund has received 572 gifts of $1,000 or more, many from trusts and foundations, but many from individuals.

''I earn more money than I need to to buy food and pay my mortgage,'' said a 34-year-old investment banker who lives on the Upper East Side and gave $1,500 on Nov. 24. He spoke on the condition that his name not be used. ''I consider it an obligation to give money to people who can't provide their children with a meal, or don't have any place to live, or who are sick and can't pay their medical expenses.''

Another Upper East Sider, William W. Donnell, gave $1,000 on Nov. 26, and included a note: ''Thank you for your continued efforts in reaching out to the least fortunate.''

On Dec. 2, Julia W. Stern, a humanities professor at Western Connecticut State University, gave $1,000 in memory of her mother, Alexandra Werner. Mrs. Werner died in 1997 at the age of 97, after more than 50 years of giving -- and encouraging others to give -- to the Neediest Cases Fund.

''When I was 5, she started me in on the Neediest Cases,'' recalled Mrs. Stern, who grew up in the South Bronx but now lives in Newtown, Conn. ''She would tell me at Christmas time that rather than asking for things, I should be giving.''

And while officials at the fund and at the seven New York charities that it supports are grateful for the large contributions, they said they are also pleased when someone makes a smaller donation.

''We're mightily pleased at the record pace of contributions this year, but what's even more satisfying is that the number of donors has gone up by 11 percent,'' said Jack Rosenthal, president of The New York Times Company Foundation, which administers the Neediest Cases Fund.

Wan Tisnower, who came to the United States in 1969 from Thailand, gave $300 on Oct. 18, along with a note that said, ''Please use this fund to help the neediest person.''

Dr. Tisnower, who runs a day care center in Philadelphia, worked in a Chicago plastic factory for $1.60 an hour when she first came to the United States. She said she was inspired to give after reading an article in January 1999 about a Mexican immigrant in Queens who had to give back a borrowed sewing machine. The Neediest Cases Fund paid for a new sewing machine for the woman, who was then able to continue running her small business.

''I read it and I was impressed,'' Dr. Tisnower said. ''I was an immigrant myself, and I know that it's hard to come here for the first time.''
