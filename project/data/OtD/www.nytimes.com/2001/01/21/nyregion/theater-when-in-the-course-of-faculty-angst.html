THEATER; When in the Course of Faculty Angst

By ALVIN KLEIN
Published: January 21, 2001

LATE, far too late, in the second act of ''Human Events'' at the George Street Playhouse here, Porter Platt, an associate professor of humanities, comes to his senses.

He's not cut out for his work, Porter concludes, invoking a classical quotation about how ''most of the evil in the world comes from people being in the wrong slot.'' The insight comes from Plato's Republic, he thinks, but he's not sure.

Because A. R. Gurney apparently reached the same conclusion, abandoning the politics of academia for the express school of playwriting, he is just the man to give us an insider's take on an important theme -- and to sound a universal warning. Beware of pretentious people in power positions. On the hallowed grounds of higher education, the implications -- betrayal of colleagues and exploitation of students -- are horrifying. We're talking damaged minds, corrupted values, crushed spirits, stifled creativity.

But Mr. Gurney is not writing an expose. He is not making a moral statement. He is not about to change the world that has been so good to him. He has not created a satire, though there are shadowy indications that he would like to. He has simply written a very cluttered, far-fetched play.

This one actually takes place ''in and around a large technological institution along the Charles River in Cambridge, Mass.,'' according to the program. Clearly, the setting is not unlike the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where Mr. Gurney was a longtime faculty member.

There are glib one-liners; rampant literary references, from the casual dropped-name school to the more seriously considered. There are archetypical characters and colorful incidents, nearly all of the latter offstage. There is standard-issue talk of tenure, of publishing, of electives. And there is a laundry list of details, so carelessly mentioned and preposterous, it is best not to think about them.

But there are great songs. No, ''Human Events'' -- it is no accident that the phrase comes from the Declaration of Independence -- is not a musical, but people walk out whistling and humming and singing lyrics about getting by with the help of their friends. And there is one background number called ''Our House.'' Mostly, it's the last ineffable melody -- ''Here Comes the Sun'' -- and its sunny message that lingers. It is intended to transmit rays of hope. The other songs serve the purpose of paradox, not of the most ingenious kind, but useful enough.

It helps that the songs on tape are the original versions -- by the Beatles and by Crosby, Stills, Nash and Young. And for spaced-out sexiness, you can't beat ''Light My Fire'' by the Doors.

But it is hard to decipher just what ''Human Events'' is really about, at this stage. Mr. Gurney is smart enough to know the formula for success, but he is too good to continue being formulaic. There are issues in this play that need to be explored, not tossed off in the manner of cocktail party banter, and not witty banter at that.

In a contrived plot, it takes Porter an entire first act to open his eyes to the guile that the audience is on to in 10 minutes or less. But at least he has the reflective grace to get out and save his soul. His nemesis, Christopher Simpson, will go on basking in fraudulence and deception. ''Human Events'' pits these two men against each other in the most unpersuasive ways. If Mr. Gurney means to show us how dim faculty members are, he has succeeded with little effort.

Simpson is shrewd, an opportunist whose ''bald careerism'' and ''ham posturing'' has Porter fooled all too quickly. And it is Porter who is his ennabler, so that everybody is taken in. Well, almost everybody. Not Seymour Blum, the only character with the humanity to know the Humanities -- and the play's single sustaining performance by Tim Jerome.

But Porter is on the slow track, falling for Simpson's transparent manipulations and dubious charms as if he had a schoolboy crush on the guy. By the second act, Porter struggles to explain the inexplicable. Is it the anti-semitism within himself that has caused him to embrace, so to speak, another ''goy''? That's just one of the playwright's vain atempts at analysis -- and a bushel of easy jokes that get laughs. More to the core of truth is the perception of the sex-starved Anita, doomed to the fate of an adjunct -- for a time -- who pegs Porter as a closeted homosexual.

It takes two transcendent actors to divert an audience from a playwright's illogic. As Porter, Jack Gilpin comes off as a cipher, confused even in his violence and his epiphany. Simpson's magic in the classroom is hard to believe; anyway, it's only hearsay. Patrick Fitzgerald's mannerisms in the role veer from affected to ludicrous. It is fatuous to give Mr. Gurney credit for having a male spin on ''All About Eve'' in mind, though it almost appears that he might be grasping for that. There are no stars here, displaced or aspiring.

What ''Human Events'' is all about is up to a dramaturge to elicit. In the meantime, it has the best soundtrack in New Jersey.

HUMAN EVENTS

George Street Playhouse, 9 Livingston Avenue, New Brunswick.
