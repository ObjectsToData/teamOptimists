2 in G.O.P. Ask Bush to Keep Clinton's Chief of Humanities

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY
Published: February 9, 2001

Mississippi's two Republican senators have asked President Bush to retain William R. Ferris as chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities even though he was named to the post by President Bill Clinton in 1997.

The unusual gesture of support toward an appointee of a Democratic president by Republicans, including the Senate majority leader, Trent Lott, was prompted in part by Mr. Ferris's efforts to remove the endowment from the ideological wars between the left and the right that have enveloped it in the past.

In addition, Mr. Clinton had originally appointed Mr. Ferris, a Mississippian, at the urging of Mr. Lott and his Republican colleague from Mississippi, Senator Thad Cochran.

''I hope it works out,'' Mr. Cochran said today. ''Bill Ferris has done a great job, has brought new ideas to the endowment and is popular among the state humanities leaders.''

Retaining Mr. Ferris, Senator Cochran said, ''would reflect credit on the Bush administration.''

Asked in an interview whether he was a Republican or a Democrat, Mr. Ferris answered, ''I'm an educator.'' He is the former director of the Center for the Study of Southern Culture at the University of Mississippi.

A spokeswoman for the White House would say only, ''In keeping with our practice during the transition, we won't speculate on any announcements or appointments out of respect for those being considered.''

The Mississippians' request that Mr. Ferris be retained would apply for the rest of his term, which expires in September, but Senator Cochran said he would also support reappointment to another four-year term.

William Ivey, the chairman of the humanities' sister agency, the National Endowment for the Arts, will also evidently retain his post, at least for now. The Office of Management and Budget, which is part of the White House, recently sent out a memorandum that lists the arts and humanities endowments among 40 mostly smaller agencies whose heads will not automatically be changed because of the arrival of the new administration.

The humanities endowment is the principal federal agency engaged in intellectual scholarship, providing grants in history, philosophy and related disciplines, and some previous chairmen have been sharply ideological.

The first humanities chairman in the Reagan administration was William J. Bennett, the conservative academic and writer. He was followed by Lynne Cheney, the wife of Vice President Dick Cheney. She named conservatives to the panels that recommend grant recipients and is reported to have opposed some proposals on political grounds.

At the other end of the political spectrum, the humanities chairman in President Jimmy Carter's administration was Joseph Duffey, a former chairman of the liberal Americans for Democratic Action.

Support for Mr. Ferris also came today from Catherine Rudder, executive director of the American Political Science Association and president of the National Humanities Alliance.

''He's worked assiduously to remove those divisions and difficulties that ideological pressures create, and instead has tried to focus on the importance of the humanities to the nation,'' Dr. Rudder said.

Mr. Ferris also got support the old-fashioned way.

He courted Representative Dick Armey of Texas, the Republican majority leader in the House, by telling him of the endowment's support of The Handbook of Texas, a five-volume encyclopedia of the state. Similar encyclopedias are under way for Georgia, South Carolina and West Virginia, and eventually every state will have one, each done with state humanities councils.

Mr. Ferris also won the support of Senator Jesse Helms, the North Carolina Republican who had been one of Congress's severest critics of the endowment, after William Friday, a former president of the University of North Carolina, introduced Mr. Ferris to Mr. Helms.

''Members of Congress say, 'What are you doing for my people back home?' and we tell them,'' Mr. Ferris said.

Mr. Ferris has approached his job with a youthful enthusiasm that belies his 59 years, and his tenure has had one substantial misstep, perhaps caused by that very enthusiasm.

In 1999, he selected President Clinton to deliver the endowment's annual Jefferson Lecture on the Humanities, a scholarly honor that is going this year to Arthur Miller.

The White House bowed to protests and announced that President Clinton had declined the invitation.
