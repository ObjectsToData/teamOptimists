BULLETIN BOARD

By KATHLEEN CARROLL, KAREN W. ARENSON, ANEMONA HARTOCOLLIS and ALLISON FAAS
Published: February 7, 2001

Scholarships Honoring Amadou Diallo

Two years ago this week, Amadou Diallo was killed by four police officers in the Bronx who mistook him for a rape suspect. Now his father, SAIKOU DIALLO, has set up a foundation to finance four $2,500 scholarships: two at Bronx Community College and two at the John Jay College of Criminal Justice. The Amadou Diallo Educational, Humanitarian and Charity Foundation has asked that the scholarships be awarded this semester to students in various majors who are of African or African-American descent.

Cornell Names Its Performing Arts Center

Cornell University's performing arts center will be named after RICHARD J. SCHWARTZ, chairman of the New York State Council on the Arts, and his wife, SHEILA W. SCHWARTZ, in honor of a gift of undisclosed size that they made to the university. The couple became involved with theater at Cornell through their daughter Jenny, who directed several plays there while she was a theater major in the early 1990's. A businessman and 1960 Cornell graduate, Mr. Schwartz is also a Cornell trustee and has also endowed the directorship of the university's art museum, a professorship in the social sciences and an auditorium. The 12-year-old performing arts center houses three theaters, dance studios, film-editing rooms, and lighting and sound laboratories.

Teacher Nominated for Award

JAN SHAKOFSKY, a humanities teacher at Benjamin Cardozo High School in Bayside, Queens, has been nominated for a national award that champions free speech, the 2001 PEN/Newman's Own First Amendment Award, given by the PEN American Center, an association of literary writers and editors. Ms. Shakofsky was nominated by DONNA LIEBERMAN, interim director of the New York Civil Liberties Union, for her successful fight against the Board of Education's use of filtering technology to block out wide swaths of the Internet, including sites about breast cancer, child labor and a passage from ''The Grapes of Wrath.'' ''Ms. Shakofsky steadfastly advocated for her students' academic freedom despite a hostile climate,'' the nomination said. The award is to be presented on April 23.

Charter School in New Harlem Home

After operating in buildings scattered throughout Harlem, the John A. Reisenbach Charter School has moved into a new home in a converted warehouse at 257 West 117th Street. The warehouse has been partly renovated with $2 million in grants. DANIEL F. OSCAR, president of the school's trustees, said the school's four months of temporary housing reflected the hardship created by the state's failure to provide money for facilities as part of each charter school's allocation.

Business-Aid Center Expanding

In 1994, City College and Columbia University, with a federal urban development grant, joined to form the Harlem Partnership Center on Lenox Avenue in Harlem to offer management assistance to local businesses. On Friday, the center is moving as well as opening an offshoot, a retail location called the Harlem Business and Technology Assistance Center, at 2388 Adam Clayton Powell Boulevard (between 139th and 140th Streets). In addition to business management assistance, the center will offer advice on technology and sell computers, software and Internet connections through a partnership with the Lucille Maud Corporation, a technology concern.

Teaching History Through Songs

Songmasters, a group of music and education industry professionals, has raised $19.2 million for a program to teach classroom content with historical songs re-recorded by today's popular artists. The idea, said JENNIFER COHEN, founder and managing partner of Songmasters, is that students learning about the Dust Bowl might listen to a rap version of a Woody Guthrie song recorded by Queen Latifah and follow up with a dialogue about poverty. ''We are using the primary language of the students,'' she said. The Ford Foundation donated a grant of $200,000. Other partners include Turner Learning, the American Civil Liberties Union and the Anti-Defamation League. KATHLEEN CARROLL, KAREN W. ARENSON, ANEMONA HARTOCOLLIS and ALLISON FAAS
