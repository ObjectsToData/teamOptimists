All the President's Eggheads

By Alexander Star
Published: September 30, 2001

THE TRUTH OF POWER

Intellectual Affairs in the Clinton White House.

By Benjamin R. Barber.

Illustrated. 320 pp. New York:

W. W. Norton & Company. $26.95.

When Bill Clinton was elected president in 1992, the Democratic Party's leading intellectuals were elated. This indisputably brilliant man, blessed with a prodigious appetite for arguments and facts, obviously appreciated their company. For the first time since the Kennedy administration, the trains between Boston and Washington would be filled with a migrating professoriate of eager men and women, each of them expecting to dispense advice, polish words, make policy. With the White House their stronghold, they would take up new positions in the war of ideas that they had been losing to the untenured and the neoconservative for too long.

As it happened, Clinton's romance with the intellectuals, or at least the liberal ones, went sour. Soon enough, it became clear that the president would rather have his administration look like America (or, even worse, Arkansas) than the Harvard government department. Those who did enter the administration often exited on bad terms. Robert Reich stepped down as labor secretary in 1997, and spent the next several years charging that Clinton had betrayed his commitment to ''putting people first.'' Peter Edelman of Georgetown resigned from the Department of Health and Human Services after the enactment of the Welfare Reform Act. At the National Endowment for the Humanities, the historian Sheldon Hackney did not escape obscurity. It was the misfortune of Lani Guinier that she did.

In ''The Truth of Power,'' Benjamin R. Barber, a political theorist and the author of ''Jihad vs. McWorld,'' witnesses the collision of intellectuals who ''rarely have the truth'' and a president who rarely had ''much power.'' It is, as he freely acknowledges, ''the memoir of an outsider.'' Barber's relationship with the Clinton administration was fleeting, tenuous and peripheral. He participated in no significant decisions; he received no furtive phone calls from the Oval Office or public tongue lashings on the campaign trail. But Barber was privy to several wide-ranging conversations among Clinton and his more scholarly accomplices. Despite a penchant for airy rhetoric and self-important asides, he offers a revealing look at the court philosophers of the Clinton era -- the intellectuals who came to dinner.

Following the Republican takeover of Congress in 1994, Clinton was determined to restore his ''relevance'' by any means necessary. Enter William Galston, a senior domestic policy adviser and a well-regarded political philosopher who had once studied with Allan Bloom and Leo Strauss. In January 1995, Galston organized a meeting of leading intellectuals at Camp David; Alan Brinkley, Theda Skocpol, Stephen Carter and Alan Wolfe, among other academic luminaries, were transported in ''oversized S.U.V.'s'' with ''darkened windows'' to a cabin in the woods, where they were asked to help the president prepare for his upcoming State of the Union address. The resulting discussion did not reshape the world, or even the speech, but a tradition was established.

What was the point of these colloquies? Perhaps nothing more than to provide the president with ''intellectual calisthenics'' or his administration with ''intellectual window dressing.'' But whatever their purpose, the talk was lofty and earnest. At the first meeting, the New Dealers and the New Democrats continued their ancient debate. Skocpol insisted that ''class issues'' were still legitimate, and the sociologist Paul Starr demanded confrontation with the Gingrich revolutionaries. In response, the centrist thinkers affiliated with the Democratic Leadership Council repeated their more anodyne calls for a politics of ''community, opportunity and responsibility.'' Robert Putnam, a Clinton favorite, lamented the decline of trust and cooperation in a society of solitary bowlers. Barber's own brief was to advance the institutions of ''civil society'': churches, unions, parent-teacher associations or neighborhood watches. As Barber and Skocpol prepared to debate the relative merits of encouraging local participation or taking federal action, the president interrupted with the telltale words: ''I can do both.''

At the 1996 session, Barber rather grandly urged his host to ''rename politics as the business of ordinary citizens.'' But he was soon disappointed. He came to see the failure of his bid to lead the N.E.H. as a setback not only for himself but for democracy. And he fretted that his own favorite terms, such as ''civil society,'' had become ''White House pap''; they served as ''general-purpose clichés rather than as engines of specific policy.'' Barber worried that when Clinton decreed the ''end of the era of big government,'' he strengthened ''the libertarian prejudices against government for which Americans already had rather too much sympathy.''

Barber's description of these seminars is swift, wry and not especially generous to allies or adversaries. He is not the most civil advocate of civil society: with his ''tone of 50's moral righteousness,'' Alan Ehrenhalt sounds ''like Dr. Laura''; Amitai Etzioni cannot distinguish ''the triumphs of the communitarian movement'' from ''the triumphs of Amitai Etzioni.'' The president himself induces both awe and anxiety, but especially the former. He is a ''pool of luminosity.'' The most strongly held convictions ''withered in the hot sun of his presence.'' When Jane Mansbridge, a Harvard political theorist, attempts to explain her decision to abandon the Democrats for the left-wing New Party, she loses her nerve and stammers, ''Sorry, Mr. President.'' Only the philosopher Richard Rorty appears unmoved by the proximity to power: he ''simply rehearsed his philosophical position as if he were addressing a graduate seminar in political theory.'' Barber wonders if Rorty is onto something that everyone else missed: ''Since in the end we probably had as little to show for our compromises as he had to show for his diffidence (or indifference), his was perhaps the more prudent strategy.''

Despite these misgivings, Barber does not judge the president or himself too harshly. Clinton was ''an emblem of tolerance, openness and good will'' to millions of Americans. And when he didn't take his mandarins' advice, it was possibly for the best. After all, he knew that he was ultimately accountable to the will of the people and not to the whims of professors. Throughout his tumultuous years in office, Clinton listened to intellectuals with one ear and to the polls with the other. For Barber, if not for most of his peers, this was the most that could be wished for.

Alexander Star is the editor of Lingua Franca.
