BULLETIN BOARD

By ABBY GOODNOUGH, KAREN W. ARENSON, KATHLEEN CARROLL and ALLISON FASS
Published: April 11, 2001

Recruiting in Caribbean

The Board of Education is sending recruiters to the Caribbean this month in hopes of luring teachers from Barbados, Trinidad and Jamaica to New York City schools. The board wants to increase the number of Caribbean teachers in Districts 17 and 18 in Brooklyn, which encompass Flatbush and Crown Heights and have many students from Jamaica, Trinidad and Haiti.

New Humanities Leader

The New York Institute for the Humanities at New York University, which has been looking to reclaim some of its old intellectual momentum, has named LAWRENCE WESCHLER, a staff writer for The New Yorker and the author of 10 books, as its new director. The institute was founded 25 years ago by a group of intellectuals, including Susan Sontag and Joseph Brodsky, and was intended to be a forum for discussion that linked the university with the city's intellectual, cultural and artistic life. It has about 120 members. Mr. Weschler replaces LEONARD BARKAN, who began leading the institute in 1997.

International Affairs

The New School, the adult-education division of the New School University, has created a graduate program in international affairs and named MICHAEL A. COHEN, a former World Bank official and expert on urban policy in developing countries, as director. Dr. Cohen said the program was intended to train practitioners rather than theoreticians. In the fall, he will teach a course on financial bailouts, looking at the experiences of Argentina, Brazil, Russia, Turkey and Indonesia.

Immigrant Scholarships

To encourage more immigrant children to think of attending small liberal arts colleges, Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, N.Y., will begin offering 20 full-tuition scholarships a year to immigrants and children of immigrants. LEON BOTSTEIN, the president of Bard, said that immigrants tend to prefer large universities like the ones in their native countries and to focus on fields like science, technology and business because they believe careers in those fields will yield the greatest payoff.

Harlem Robot Is Tested

Cross a moving bridge, pick up a large ball and place it on top of an eight-foot structure and drag the structure back across the bridge without dropping the ball.

That was the challenge faced by robots created by 330 schools across the country at the National First Robotics Competition in Orlando, Fla., last week, and a robot built by 30 students at Rice High School in Harlem, a Roman Catholic school better known for basketball than academics, came in 11th out of 84 schools in its division. In March, the Rice team, helped by engineers from Consolidated Edison, won the New York City regional competition.

Technology Grants Offered

New Visions for Public Schools, a nonprofit education group, is seeking proposals for $1.1 million worth of grants to help New York City schools enhance their use of computers and the Internet. Grants of up to $20,000 will be awarded to 24 public schools over a two-year period, starting in July. New York Life Foundation is awarding the grant money, and New Visions will help choose the winners.

Theater Group Moves

After 14 years at the Bayard Rustin High School for the Humanities on West 18th Street, the Irondale Ensemble Project, an experimental theater group, has moved to the Brooklyn High School of the Arts at 345 Dean Street in Brooklyn. TERRY GREISS, Irondale's executive director, as well as an actor in the group, said the company would help students and teachers at the new location ''in conceptualizing and implementing their musical theater program.'' Irondale will also continue producing its own professional plays. ABBY GOODNOUGH, KAREN W. ARENSON, KATHLEEN CARROLL and ALLISON FASS
