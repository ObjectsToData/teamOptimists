WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: February 18, 1996

RAILS ON PAPER

An exhibition of railway poster art, now on view and titled "All Aboard! Advertising the Railroad," is the first of three train exhibitions to open at the Hudson River Museum in Yonkers. "Next Stop Westchester," a history of the railroad in Westchester, will begin on March 1, and "Model Trains Through Time" opens on March 15. All three will remain through Sept. 1.

Posters for the exhibition were obtained from several private and public collections, including the Albany Institute of History and Art, Fred N. Arone-The Depot Attic in Dobbs Ferry and Henry S. Hacker.

Railroad posters peaked in the 1920's and 30's, featuring trains like the New York Central's 20th Century Limited and the Pennsylvania Railroad's Broadway Limited, frequently pictured against majestic scenery or historic sites. Their popularity declined after World War II, when travel by plane and automobile accelerated rapidly. Because most of the posters were discarded, those that survive are rare.

The exhibitions were sponsored by Citibank with support from the National Endowment for the Humanities, New York Council for the Humanities, New York State Council on the Arts and the Samuel Freedman Charitable Trust. Assistance was provided by Metro-North, the Yonkers Model Railroad Club, Fred N. Arone-The Depot Attic and Polk's Model Craft Hobbies-Artisto-Craft of Jersey City. The museum is at 511 Warburton Avenue. The phone number for more information is 963-4550. SAILING LESSONS

For 15 years Ferry Sloops, the nonprofit environmental organization that operates in the Hudson River aboard the 42-foot gaff-topsail sloop Sojourner Truth, has offered free sailing lessons. This year's programs begin in the next two weeks in the Museum Junior High School cafeteria on Warburton Avenue in Yonkers.

Two series will be held. Beginners will meet for the first time on Tuesday from 7 to 9:30 P.M. for nine weekly sessions; advanced classes will start on Feb. 27 and run for eight weeks. Registration in advance is not required.

In addition to teaching basic sailing theory, day and night navigation, chart reading, small boat handling, knots, anchoring and other essentials, the instructors will discuss Hudson River history and environmental information.

"Some people who started as beginners come back for the advanced course the next year," said Doug Maass, one of the instructors. After that, anyone who wishes to become a member of Ferry Sloops, at $25 a year, can become a trainee sailor on the boat from April through mid-November.

It takes two training seasons to qualify for a rating as crew, up to five seasons for mate, and up to six seasons for captain. For more information, call 631-7541 or 963-6305.

'THE KENTUCKY CYCLE'

"The Kentucky Cycle," a 1992 Pultizer Prize-winning series of nine one-act plays, will be read during play-reading parties organized by the Theater Arts Guild of Peekskill. Because of its length, the cycle was originally performed on two successive evenings or at matinee and evening performances on the same day. Following that format, the readings will take place on Wednesday and Thursday at 7:30 P.M. at the Paramount East on Brown Street, next to the Paramount Center for the Arts in Peekskill.

The cycle tracks the history of the United States from the perspective of a single family living on a single piece of land in Kentucky from pre-Revolutionary times to the 20th century. It covers early frontier days, dealings that left American Indians bereft of their land, the Civil War, Reconstruction and exploitation by coal-mining companies.

The playwright, Robert Schenkkan, researched the history of several families in Kentucky and shaped them into a composite family. The play was first produced in Seattle and at the Mark Taper Forum in Los Angeles in 1992.

The next play to be read, on March 14, is "Harley Holmes," a farce about Sherlock Holmes' less successful brother. It was written by Joe Carlisle.

It is not necessary to arrive at the starting time in order to participate in the event, and those who prefer not to read but to listen and take part in the discussion afterward are free to do so. For more information, call 736-6729.

DIASPORA LECTURE

Victor Perera, author of "The Cross and the Pear Tree: A Sephardic Journey," published last year by Alfred A. Knopf, will give a talk at Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers on Tuesday at 1:30 P.M. in Titsworth Lecture Hall.

Sponsored by the college's office of multicultural affairs and other campus organizations, Mr. Perera will focus on his own family history, through which he traced the Sephardic Jewish diaspora from the time the Jews were expelled from Spain in 1492 to the present. He has spent several years researching his family's travels to and from Latin America, the United States and Israel.

Titsworth Hall is at 1 Mead Way. Call 395-2411 for more information.

AFROCENTRIC MUSIC

A program of Afrocentric music, poetry and stories will be presented next Saturday at 2 P.M. in the Warner Library, 121 North Broadway in Tarrytown. Appropriate for all ages, from children to elderly adults, it will be performed by Nicola James, mezzo-soprano, and Rufus James, actor and percussionist, free of charge.

Their repertory includes early American slave songs, the poems of Langston Hughes and Paul Lawrence Dunbar, calypso, rhythm and blues and music popularized by Billy Taylor, Bessie Smith, Eubie Blake and Scott Joplin. Call 631-7734 for more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
