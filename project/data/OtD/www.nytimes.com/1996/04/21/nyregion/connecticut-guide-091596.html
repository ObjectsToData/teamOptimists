CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: April 21, 1996

AUTHORS, AUTHORS

Some eminent authors will participate in the 21st annual meeting of the Connecticut Humanities Council next Saturday in the Hamden Library at 2901 Dixwell Avenue. Biography and autobiography in Connecticut are the themes.

Justin Kaplan, author of works on Mark Twain, Lincoln Steffens and Walt Whitman, and one of several Pulitzer Prize-winners among the speakers, will deliver the keynote address at 9:45 A.M.

Biographies of Abraham Lincoln, Harriet Beecher Stowe and Eugene O'Neill will be discussed in seminars by their authors: Michael Burlingame, Joan Hedrick and Arthur and Barbara Gelb, respectively, while autobiographical discussions will be led by William Styron and Arthur Miller. Richard Buel will lead a session on his account of a woman and her family during the Revolutionary War, and Christopher Collier's subject is Roger Sherman and Yankee politics.

Registration costs $35. The number for more information is 860-685-2260.

BRINGING CULTURE

The English Lady who in real life is Susan Caroselli, visiting assistant professor of religion and the arts at the Yale Divinity School, will give her First Annual Final Farewell Performance in Marquand Chapel, 409 Prospect Street, on the New Haven campus, on Friday at 8 P.M.

Presented by the Yale Institute of Sacred Music, the English Lady's mission is "to bring culture to deprived areas of North America" in a manner that one institute staff member described as a cross between Anna Russell and Beatrice Lillie.

Admission is free and the lecture-demonstration will be followed by a reception. More information may be obtained by calling 432-5180.

JUDAIC TREASURES

The actor and singer Theodore Bikel will speak about "Jewish Creativity Through the Ages" tomorrow at 5 P.M. in the Lincoln Theater of the University of Hartford. On the following Monday, at 8 P.M., Marvin Kalb, the journalist and professor of press and public policy at Harvard, will lecture on "The Shadow of the Holocaust on the Free Press." Admission to both programs is free, and registration is available by calling 768-4228.

The two talks have been scheduled in connection with an exhibition of more than 200 items, titled "From the Ends of the Earth: Judaic Treasures of the Library of Congress," opening on Wednesday through June 15 in the university's Joseloff Gallery. Gallery hours are Tuesday through Friday from 11 to 4, weekends from noon to 4. Admission is $5, or $3 for students and older adults. The address is 200 Bloomfield Avenue, West Hartford (768-4090).

ACCESSIBLE 'HAMLET'

Litchfield Performing Arts will present a single performance of "Hamlet" by the National Shakespeare Company on Friday at 8 P.M. in the Thomaston Opera House. During its 33-year history of touring the the United States, the company has earned a reputation for making Shakespeare thoroughly accessible. Each year 1,000 New York actors are auditioned to fill slots for the new season. Tickets are $20. The number for reservations and more information is 567-4162.

COASTAL MAINE

The Maine coast has long been an inspiration to artists, and it is the subject of an exhibition titled "Maine Waters," at the Cooley Gallery in Old Lyme through May 4.

There are no Winslow Homer or Frederic Church works in this show, which concentrates instead lesser-known but highly regarded painters. A sunset over the sea, measuring only 7 1/2 by 11 1/2 inches, by William Hart; a windswept sailing scene by Edward Dufner; Monhegan Island painted in Charles Ebert's impressionist style, and Frederick Judd Waugh's surf crashing against a rocky shore are a sampling.

The gallery, at 25 Lyme Street, is open Monday through Saturday from 10 to 5. The number is 434-8807 for more information.

CHANGES AT THE C.I.A.

Changes taking place within the Central Intelligence Agency will be discussed by Carolyn Ekedahl, chief of public communication for the agency, at a World Affairs Council lunch on Tuesday at noon. It will be held at the Hartford Club, 46 Prospect Street in downtown Hartford, and the fee will be $30, or $8 for the talk without lunch.

Ms. Ekedahl will preview some of the restructuring that is expected to take place as a result of a study by the House Permanent Select Commission on Intelligence, scheduled to be made public soon.

An officer of the C.I.A. since 1962, Ms. Ekedahl is an author of "The Wars of Eduard Shevardnadze," to be published this year, and "Moscow and the Third World Under Gorbachev," published in 1990. The number for reservations and more information is 594-4100.

ON NIXON

"Nixon: The Man and the Movie" will be the subject of Sander Vanocur's lecture tomorrow at 8 P.M. in the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale. The veteran journalist covered the former President during good times and bad, working as a correspondent for NBC and later for ABC News over almost 30 years.

The Humanities Center is at 53 Wall Street, New Haven, and admission is free.

'BOSTON TECHNICIAN'

Laurene Buckley, director of the New Britain Museum of American Art, will give a talk on Joseph Rodefer DeCamp, a turn-of-the-century artist whom she has dubbed "The Boston Technician," on Wednesday at 7 P.M. in the Florence Griswold Museum, 96 Lyme Street in Old Lyme.

Ms. Buckley came to know DeCamp, a major painter of his time, while writing an essay for a catalogue of his work before his death in 1923.

Admission will be $6, and registrations are suggested by calling 434-5542. ELEANOR CHARLES
