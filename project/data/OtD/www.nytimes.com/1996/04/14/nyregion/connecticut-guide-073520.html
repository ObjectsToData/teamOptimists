CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: April 14, 1996

ORGANS GALORE

Dozens of Connecticut organists will perform today in a nationwide celebration of the American Guild of Organists Centennial. More than 500 organists will give simultaneous recitals in 50 states and in Scotland and South America.

Bach's "Toccata and Fugue in D Minor" will open a program at 4 P.M. in St. John's Episcopal Church, 679 Farmington Avenue, in West Hartford, followed by a variety of works chosen and performed by 10 Connecticut organists. At 6 P.M. in Stamford, 10 members of the Fairfield West Chapter of the Guild will present their recital at St. John's Episcopal Church.

In New Haven, area organists will take turns performing at Center Church on the green, opening the 6 P.M. recital with the same Bach Toccata, considered to be the most famous piece of organ music ever composed.

Recitals will also take place at the United Congregational Church in Bridgeport, St. Lucy's Roman Catholic Church in Waterbury, the Congregational Church in Storrs, and Harkness Chapel at Connecticut College in New London, all at 6 P.M.

Admission is free, but offerings to help support the Guild's "Pipe Dreams" program on Connecticut Public Radio are welcome. For more information call 860-233-1622.

ENGLISH GARDENS

Daphne Foulsham, chairwoman of the council of the National Gardens Scheme Charitable Trust in Surrey, England, will lecture twice in Fairfield County this week while on a tour of the East Coast.

Ms. Foulsham will speak on Wednesday at 10 A.M. at the Garden Education Center on Bible Street in Greenwich, and on Thursday at 3 P.M. in the Pequot Library, 720 Pequot Avenue, in Southport.

She will later appear at Colonial Williamsburg, the Arnold Arboretum in Massachusetts and in Washington, D.C., and Pennsylvania. Proceeds from ticket sales will support the National Gardens Scheme's annual publication, the Yellow Book, which lists 3,500 private gardens in England and Wales that welcome the public throughout the summer.

Ms. Foulsham's slide presentation will review the history of the National Gardens Scheme since its founding in 1927. The numbers are 869-9242 for $25 tickets to the Greenwich event, or 259-6389 for Southport tickets at $35.

RUN-BIKE-RUN

An off-road Duathlon will depart next Sunday at 9 A.M. from Veterans Park in Wallingford. The run-bike-run course will traverse trails and dirt roads, and the event is open to anyone 16 years of age or older.

The course, nicknamed the Chameleon Mudslide, begins with a 2.5-mile run through fields and hilly back roads, followed by a 9-mile bike ride consisting of three 3-mile loops over a single hilly track and a stream crossing. The last mile is a run around flat open fields.

Registration, from 7:30 to 8:30 A.M., costs $30 for individuals and $40 for teams. Participants who register before Tuesday will pay $25 and $35 respectively. The top three male and female winners will receive gift certificates of $50 to $100.

Veterans Park is off Interstate 91, Exit 14 northbound or southbound, on East Center Street. The number for more information is 234-2171.

ON PHOTOGRAPHY

Contemporary photography is the subject of a symposium at the Yale Art Gallery in New Haven on Saturday, opening at 10 A.M. with a talk by Ellen Handy, of the International Center of Photography in New York. Her topic will be "Approaching the Millenium: Photography's Diversity Today and in the Future."

Lectures will be presented by the photographers Laurie Novak and David Levinthal, by the critic Fred Ritchin, by Tod Papageorge of the Yale School of Art, and by Richard S. Field, curator of prints, drawings and photographs at the Yale Art Gallery. The speakers will hold a panel discussion at 3 P.M.

Reservations, at $40, may be made by calling 432-9568.

FILM FESTIVAL

A three-day film festival opens on Friday at 7 P.M. and continues through Sunday afternoon in New Haven at the Little Theater, 1 Lincoln Street, at the Whitney Humanities Center on the Yale campus and the Creative Arts Workshop on Audubon Street. A total of 28 films will be shown, 9 of them feature length, produced in the United States, Canada and France.

Friday's program ranges from a one-minute piece called "Breaktime" to an hour-and-a-half feature by Tom Brozovich titled "Love Gets Tougher the Longer You Chew It."

Two family films: a feature about a teen-ager's handling of her parents' adultery, called "Sacred Hearts," and "Coolbreeze and Buzz," a short film focusing on the relationship between a young girl and her estranged father are on the Saturday morning bill.

Love and lust are the themes for Saturday afternoon and evening, and experimental films fill the Sunday agenda. Admission is $5 or $7, depending on the program. For a complete schedule, the number is 865-2773.

ALTERNATIVE LAWNS

"Bidding Goodbye to the Great American Lawn" is the title of a slide-talk by Diane Balmori, a landscape designer and teacher at Yale, to be given on Tuesday at 7:30 P.M. in the Notre Dame Church Hall on Morehouse Road in Easton.

"This is not about eliminating lawns," Ms. Balmori said, "but altering the ways in which one has a lawn, by mixing in certain things like clover, which eliminates the need for nitrogen -- the biggest problem in water pollution."

She will discuss the many alternatives to the traditional greensward, including a wildflower meadow, and how to treat an existing lawn. "Having a town green makes a lot of sense," Ms. Balmori continued, "but all yards need not be all lawn." By reducing the size of a lawn and exercising other options homeowners may reduce the cost and time spent on upkeep as well as provide themselves with an attractive new landscape.

"The lawn is one of the most expensive ground covers per square foot," she said, "given the normal practice of fertilizing and the use of pesticides and watering. Watering is particularly expensive financially and environmentally. The biggest problem all nations will have over the next 25 years is water."

Admission is free. For more information, the number is 261-0134.

FURRY CREATURES

Paul Rego, a wildlife biologist at the Connecticut Department of Environmental Protection, will discuss "The History and Status of Fur Bearing Animals in Connecticut" today at 3 P.M. in the Life Sciences Building on the UConn campus in Storrs.

One of his subjects is the black bear, which disappeared from the state in the 1870's after farmland took over its forests. As farms were later abandoned, the forests grew back and the bear reappeared. The free slide-talk will also track the ups and downs of the state's coyotes, fishers and many other mammals.

While on campus, visitors may look in on an exhibition of nature photographs by Terry Dickinson and a display of Russian objects at the State Museum of Natural History. Hours on Monday, and Thursday through Saturday, are from noon to 4 P.M., Sunday from 1 to 4 P.M.

In connection with the display an ethnic dinner and a Russian travelogue presented by Lawrence Langer, professor of history at UConn, are scheduled at the museum on Friday at 6 P.M. for a fee of $30. Call 486-4460 by Tuesday for reservations or more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
