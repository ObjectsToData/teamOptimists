Films Face the Squirm Factor

By MICHEL MARRIOTT
Published: January 7, 1996

OH, how America loves the movies. But does it love them three hours' worth?

Film patrons are finding their devotion severely tested by a spate of exceedingly lengthy offerings. In just the last couple of months, for instance, audiences have had to sit still for "Casino," "Heat" and "Nixon." While these cinematic labors share short titles, they also pack long running times, hovering at three hours, twice the once-time-honored Hollywood standard of 90 minutes.

As the credits rolled on Martin Scorsese's "Casino," a 50-something theater patron hobbled to the exit. Disappearing down the escalator at the Ziegfeld Theater in midtown Manhattan, he could be heard repeating, "Too long . . . too long."

Long films, while sometimes wildly entertaining, are nonetheless complicating the lives of many of the very peple who love them, often making for longer waits in line and inconvenient show times and generally heightening the risks that the movie might be a waste of time and money. For movie makers, that's no small concern in the wake of an off year like 1995, when box-office receipts were flat.

Some moviegoers say long movies complicate dating, leaving little time afterward for dinner. And consequences could be dire if a date thinks he or she has just been subjected to three hours of tedium, warned Robin Gorman Newman, the founder of the School of Flirting at Stand-Up New York, a comedy club on the Upper West Side.

"Maybe they should plan a little break together to get some popcorn and a soda," said Ms. Newman, 35, the author of "How to Meet a Mensch in New York" (City & Company, 1994). "That way they can get a moment to confer on whether to stay or leave the theater together." Of course, time was when an intermission came with the price of admission.

Couples with young children lament that they have particular problems with the longer movies, namely prohibitive baby-sitting costs.

Lonnie Soury, 43, the father of two children, ages 3 and 7, said he and his wife did not see a movie for a year because juggling time, money, children and baby sitters was too stressful for what should be a simple night out.

"In a conversation my wife and I had, 'Casino' came up," recalled Mr. Soury, a public-relations executive in Manhattan. "I said: 'How long is it? Three hours. Forget it! We can't do this.' "

Cathy Che, 28, who was mulling over movie options at a theater in Greenwich Village, said: "I certainly have to prepare myself differently for a long movie. I go to the bathroom first."

For staunchest movie fans, those for whom video just won't do, three-hour movies are presenting a different sort of problem: they can't get enough of them. Also concerned are theater managers who have to deal with the fact that long movies cannot be shown as many times a day as shorter ones, and therefore the seats cannot be filled as often.

For example, "Heat," which is 2 hours 41 minutes long, plays 4 times a day at the Murray Hill Cinemas, at 34th Street and Third Avenue. The computer-animated megahit "Toy Story," which is 2 hours 21 minutes long, is shown 6 times a day at the same theater complex, offering more moviegoers convenient starting times.

Roberto Liy, a manager at the 4,000-seat Sony Lincoln Square theaters at Broadway and 68th Street, said longer running times may cut down on the amount of popcorn and soft drinks moviegoers buy, again because there are fewer shows.

Stuart Applebaum, 46, an executive at Bantam Doubleday Dell in Manhattan and an ardent moviegoer, said, "I don't have a problem with movies being long, but I have a problem with the theaters' inability to schedule them the way I want." Mr. Applebaum goes to no less than 30 movies a year with friends.

For Mr. Applebaum, moviegoing is both escape and social ritual. Part of that ritual, he said, is getting enough popcorn before the film starts to last through the entire screening. He said he had learned the hard way that a three-hour movie requires at least a tub of popcorn.

He also has learned to insist on an aisle seat for his 6-foot-2-inch frame. "The longer the movie, the more the leg cramp-up issue becomes a factor," he explained.

"Two of the burning issues of the moment," he added, "are 'Are the movies too long?' and 'Will the Government go back to working as it should again?' "

So why are more movies getting longer?

Morris Dickstein, the director for the Center of the Humanities at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, explained that a film's length can be as much of a statement as the film itself. "Film makers have always used length to show that they are making a major work of art," he said.

But today's longer-running films may be better understood, he said, as a response to changes in the audience. Television and its popular mini-series -- essentially 4-to-10-hour movies shown over days or weeks -- have helped to condition viewers to richly detailed and complex stories with lots of characters, often adapted from a book, Mr. Dickstein said. And movie audiences have come to expect the same intricate storytelling, he said.

Long movies usually do not come without staggering economic risks. Mel Gibson took the risk last summer and won at the box office with "Braveheart," a 2-hour-48-minute sweep of medieval Scottish history, with him as director, star and producer. Although the film cost $70 million, it has earned $175 million in worldwide release. "It was a creative decision, really," Mr. Gibson said of the length of the film. "Those sorts of decisions can't be based solely on economics. You have to make the film you want to see and hope others will want to see."
