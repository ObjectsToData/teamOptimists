Courses at Your Convenience

By MARIA NEWMAN
Published: November 3, 1996

A student in Tisha Bender's Reading for Writing class at the New School for Social Research in New York City wanted to contribute to an ongoing class discussion.

''Now that I have cleaned up from dinner, cleaned out the potty, put away the laundry and congratulated my friend on giving birth, I will hope to tap into the 'collegiate' side of my brain,'' wrote Stacy Moskowitz, the student.

Like all her classmates -- and the professor -- this student was not in a classroom. She was at home in Hillsdale, N.J., tapping into the class through cyberspace.

The class is among the fast-growing number of continuing education courses that colleges and universities around the country have begun offering to anyone with access to a computer, a modem and an Internet address.

The trend is an outgrowth of colleges making more frequent use of computer technology in undergraduate degree-granting programs or to earn master's degrees in business administration or in engineering at schools like Harvard and Duke. Some colleges are offering classes that are a combination of on-line dialogues, videotaped lectures and in-class work that, of course, require being on campus.

But now students who are not enrolled full-time in such universities, and those who live far away from a particular university, can take their pick of a plethora of courses without ever setting foot on those campuses, if they have access to the Internet or the World Wide Web. No one knows how many on-line classes are being offered, but a search of the Web indicates that new ones are being added almost daily.

The selection of courses is mind-boggling. They range from ''How to Open a Beer Bar or Brew Pub'' (New School), to ''Faces of Ancient Egypt'' (University of Chicago), and ''Shamans and Healing: the Anthropology of Traditional Medicine'' (University of California at Los Angeles Extension).

With on-line classes, if a student prefers to work in the wee hours of the night, he or she can access a professor's lectures and even a class discussion at any time.

Students usually enroll on line, pay their tuition on line via credit card -- fees for on-line courses are generally comparable to that of on-campus classes -- and then receive a password that will give them access to a world of learning.

Lectures are presented on line, and professors may pose questions to begin the on-line discussions. In a physical classroom, a lecture can last an hour and a half. In the virtual classroom, it can last for weeks.

Sometimes students meet with their professors in ''chat rooms'' with catchy names like the Patsy Room at the Blue Moon Cafe, a place in the virtual class ''Parent-Child Relationships'' taught by Charles Smith, a professor at Kansas State University.

Students can retreat to the privacy of E-mail to consult with their professors, and the required writing assignments or exams are graded via E-mail.

Some professors grouse about not having one-on-one contact: Students cannot hear the inflection in a professor's voice when he or she is trying to make a point.

''Sarcasm, for example, is very difficult to convey in this medium,'' said Andrew Shaindlin, who was the director of Bruin, an on-line program of courses offered by the alumni office of Brown University. But Mr. Shaindlin, who pioneered on-line alumni education at Brown, said that an advantage to on-line education is that students and professors have a written record of what everyone in the class says during discussions.

''In the written format, you can still interpret what people are saying, but you can cut and paste exactly what they wrote when you respond to them,'' Mr. Shaindlin said.

The new technology has spawned a new language in academia, with professors tossing around phrases like ''asynchronous learning network'' -- a term used to describe a conversation between professors and their students that doesn't take place all at once, but rather as students log on and retrieve or send messages. They talk about ''lurkers'' -- those students who enroll in class and observe all the conversations, but who choose not to contribute.

Universities are conducting seminars to discuss the implications of these new tools on learning, like a workshop this month at Virginia Tech, in Blacksburg, Va., with panel discussions entitled ''E-literacy: Renegotiating Pedagogy in Electronic Environments,'' or ''The Computer Is Down -- and So Am I,'' and finally, ''Muse Meets Mouse.''

The explosion of on-line learning has also led many scholars to worry over the future of traditional classroom learning. Kathy McGuire, the director of Distance Learning at the U.C.L.A. Extension, a continuing education program affiliated with the University of California in Los Angeles, said scholars should be looking at the new technology as a way to reach older students, who every year make up a larger proportion of college populations nationwide.

''They're not going to stop learning, but they're not going to want to drive all over the place to do it,'' she said.

A peek at some on-line classes shows that serious discussions are taking place in a format that requires students to carefully articulate their thoughts as they write them into cyberspace for classmates to see. Some of the discussions are also amusing.
