When Danbury Shackled the Still River

By ALBERTA EISEMAN
Published: August 25, 1996

A VISITOR driving through Danbury on the way to an exhibition entitled ''A River Runs Through It'' looks in vain throughout the city for a body of water.

The reason is spelled out in the subtitle in bold letters at the entrance to the exhibition: ''The Urbanization of Danbury's Still River.''

The show is on view at the Danbury Railway Museum, formerly Union Station, on White Street.

The Still River -- which does indeed meander through the city -- is now almost invisible -- held within high concrete banks and often driven underground.

The show explores how this came to pass, how the relationship between river and city developed across the centuries.

When the project was conceived more than a year ago, the intention was to commemorate the floods of 1955, the most devastating natural disaster in Danbury's history.

After some months of research, however, the project team realized that the floods were but one chapter in a continuing story and that the Still River -- a 19-mile-long segment of the Housatonic River system -- has always had a major influence on life in Danbury.

The Danbury Preservation Trust and the history department at Western Connecticut State University, sponsors of the exhibition, then concluded that a more comprehensive treatment was required.

''We saw the project as an opportunity to present the flood not as a unique happening, but as an episode in the ongoing process of interaction between human beings and the natural environment,'' said Dr. Herbert F. Janick, a professor of history at Western Connecticut State University and a director of the exhibition along with Dr. Paulette L. Pepin of the University of New Haven.

The exhibition -- a self-contained, freestanding module set within the former station's handsome waiting room -- was created by the designer Debra Strucky-Kinnane of Danbury and received support from the Connecticut Humanities Council.

Using photographs and maps, postcards, newspapers, videotapes, interactive games, models and artifacts of all kinds, the show is devised as a circular tour.

Exhibits illustrate the initial harmony between people and river, then rampant abuse by both industry and municipality as well as the river overflowing its banks and the community's decision to banish it. Finally, there appears a new appreciation of clean waterways.

Idyllic though they are, the photographs of 19th-century residents fishing, or relaxing on the banks, don't pack the punch of later exhibits of industrial pollution.

For most of a century, from the 1870's on, Danbury despoiled its river.

The hat factories that made the city famous dumped their untreated waste into it; the growing city built on flood plains, extending buildings, sewers and water lines into the riverbed.

Newspaper articles speak of rising mortality rates, of entire families made sick by pollution, and of cattle with feet poisoned by chemicals in the river. ''Our cattle just would not drink river water,'' reminisced Charles Bardo, a retired businessman and farmer who visited the show during a stint as a volunteer for the new railroad museum.

To illustrate the floods of October 1955, the project team -- made up of educators, students and members of the preservation trust -- reproduced the office of Stephen A. Collins, then editor of The Danbury News-Times. His old Royal typewriter -- or one very much like it -- holds a page that begins ''Water rushes across Main Street into White Street as this is written,'' and behind his desk, three windows look out over vistas of city streets turned into white-capped waters.

A videotape set up in the editor's office skillfully blends dramatic photographs with interviews in which people recall that rain-drenched day.

Losses suffered by businesses and individuals were so great that a visitor can almost sympathize with the community's reaction -- to imprison the river in a concrete ditch. It was a decision that led to massive urban renewal, but one that many now consider unwise.

The show ends on a rosier note as it relates how environmental protection efforts began to reverse river pollution in Danbury, as such efforts did across the nation.

Exhibits of fish, birds and mammals that have returned to the area, along with photographs of hikers and kayakers enjoying the outdoors, paint a healthier future for the river. So do profiles of people who have dedicated themselves to restoring the quality of wetlands and water and the development of recreational resources.

''The problems will negatively impact my life and that of my children and grandchildren,'' wrote Joseph P. McCarthy, who led water quality investigations during the 1970's and came back as a volunteer to help with the exhibition.

''I don't want my grandchildren to ask me one day, 'Why didn't you do anything, Grandpa?' ''

''A River Runs Through It'' remains on view at the Danbury Railway Museum (Union Station), White Street, Danbury, through Sept. 29. No admission charge. Hours are Wednesday through Sunday, 1 to 4 P.M.
