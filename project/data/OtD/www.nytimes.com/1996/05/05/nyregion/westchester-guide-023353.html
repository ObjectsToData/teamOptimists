WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: May 5, 1996

'BUSBOY'S' STORY

When "Member of the Club: Reflections on Life in a Racially Polarized World" was published last year by HarperCollins, it again thrust its author, Lawrence Otis Graham, into the spotlight. A Princeton- and Harvard-educated lawyer, Mr. Graham researched the book by posing as a busboy at the all-white Greenwich Country Club.

He was interviewed on dozens of television shows, and he became the subject of many newspaper and magazine articles. The story is now being adapted for a motion picture with Denzel Washington.

On Thursday at 7:45 P.M., Mr. Graham will talk about racism and bigotry at Esy's Kafe, 146 Mamaroneck Avenue in White Plains, sponsored by the Westchester Coalition for Legal Abortion.

Admission will be $15 and includes dessert, coffee and a donation to the organization. Call 946-5363 for more information.

WALKING TOURS IN RYE

Sunday afternoon guided walking tours of the Boston Post Road Historic District in Rye begin today at 2:45 P.M., starting and ending at the Jay Heritage Center, 210 Boston Post Road. All one need do is show up with the $5 fee, or $10 a family.

Led by Anthony Bellow, an architect and museum educator, the tours will focus on three pre-Civil War estates sitting side by side along the Post Road.

The Peter A. Jay House and Lounsberry were completed in 1838 in the Greek Revival style, and Whitby, now the Rye Golf Club, is a Gothic Revival mansion built in 1854. Lounsberry is the only one of the three that is privately owned.

The Jay estate is owned by the Jay Heritage Center. When restoration on the house and carriage house are completed, they will serve as an educational resource for the history, architecture and ecosystem of the historic district and Long Island Sound. Also on the property are the family cemetery, a 1906 carriage house and the Marshlands Conservancy, which runs down to Long Island Sound. The house was built by the son of John Jay, first Chief Justice of the United States, and it will be open for tours after each walk.

Visitors are also welcome on Wednesdays and Sundays from 2 to 5 P.M. For more information, call 698-9275.

ANIMAL BLESSINGS

The bagpiping of Kevin O'Hagan will lead a procession of pets and their owners from the courtyard into the Japanese Stroll Garden of the Hammond Museum in North Salem today at 3 P.M. for the museum's 31st yearly Interfaith Blessing of the Animals and Land.

Members of the clergy who will preside over the ceremonies include a minister, a rabbi, a priest, a Zen monk and an American Indian shaman.

Pets of all kinds are welcome, from worms to barnyard animals, so long as they are leashed or properly confined in cages or containers. Following a general blessing, pet owners will be invited to have their pets or plants individually blessed. For directions or more information, call 669-5033.

'ARCADIA' IN PEEKSKILL

Tom Stoppard's "Arcadia," winner of the best play award for 1995 from the New York Drama Critics, will be presented by the Theater Arts Guild of Peekskill from next Saturday through May 18 at the Paramount East on Brown Street in Peekskill.

Called Mr. Stoppard's richest comedy, the play shifts back and forth from the early 1800's to the present, exploring the phenomenon of recollection -- that is, events as they really happened and those same events as interpreted later.

Performances next Saturday and from May 16 through 18 will be at 8 P.M. A matinee next Sunday starts at 3 P.M. Tickets are $10. Seats can be reserved by calling 736-6729.

ALFRESCO PAINTING

It is that time of year again -- when the Garrison Art Center sends 85 artists to various spots along the Hudson River to paint from early morning to mid-afternoon. The event will take place next Saturday, featuring professional artists from New York, Connecticut and New Jersey.

When they return to the center at Garrison Landing, they will hang their paintings, still wet, in a tent, where they may be viewed during a wine and cheese reception from 3:30 to 4:45 P.M. At 4:45 sharp an auction of all the works will begin. Half the purchase price of each painting is a tax-deductible contribution to the center.

In addition to exhibiting in Saturday's auction, each artist may show a previously completed work for a separate silent auction, open through May 19 in the center galleries. For directions or more information, call 424-3960.

GARDENING FESTIVAL

A four-hour Gardening and Environmental Festival begins today at noon at the Y.M.-Y.W.H.A. of Mid-Westchester in Scarsdale. Admission is free.

Seminars for adults will focus on cooking with herbs, balancing the backyard ecosystem, cyber gardening with CD-ROM software, organic vegetable gardening, flower arranging and an opportunity to test a soil sample.

Children will have their own workshops on making planters, botanical art and storytelling, and everyone will be entertained by Annie and the Natural Wonder Band in two performances, at 1 and 2 P.M.

Booths will offer annuals and perennials for sale, gardening tools and books, products that do not degrade the environment, arts and crafts and information from garden clubs and environmental organizations.

The Y is at 999 Wilmot Road. The number for more information is 472-3300.

HOPPER AND HIS WIFE

Gail Levin, an authority on the painter Edward Hopper, will discuss the artist's pathological relationship with his wife in a lecture on Wednesday titled "Edward Hopper and His Silenced Collaborator."

The program will take place at 4:30 P.M. in Room 102 of the F. W. Olin Humanities Building at Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson. Admission is free, and the public is welcome.

Ms. Levin's biography, "Edward Hopper: An Intimate Portrait," published by Alfred A. Knopf, described a twisted and mutually dependent relationship between Hopper and his wife, Josephine Nivison. Potentially an artist of some stature herself, the book says she allowed her aspirations to be subjugated to her husband's need to repress, dominate and abuse her, thus making her a collaborator in his artistic achievements and her own psychological destruction.

Ms. Levin's books have been published in 15 countries, and she has received grants from the Smithsonian Institution, the Rockefeller Foundation, the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, the National Endowment for the Humanities and Yale University. She is a professor of art history at Baruch College and the Graduate School of the City University of New York.

For more information, call 758-6822.

LECTURE SERIES

Two noteworthy lectures will be given at Temple Israel in New Rochelle this week. Terry Anderson, former hostage of Shiite terrorists in Lebanon, former foreign correspondent for the Associated Press and now an author and syndicated newspaper columnist, will discuss "The Islamic Mind as It Confronts the Jewish State and the Western World" on Tuesday at 8 P.M.

On Wednesday at 8 P.M. Lisa Keller, assistant professor of history at Purchase College, a graduate of Vassar College with a doctorate from Cambridge University, will speak about "Recovering the Lost Voice of Jewish Women."

Her appearance in a series of talks by Purchase College faculty members will be followed by a lecture on May 15 titled "Hannah Arendt and the Jewish World," to be delivered by Morris Kaplan, assistant professor of philosophy and a former trial lawyer. His book on sexual justice, related to gay and lesbian issues and written while he was a Rockefeller Foundation Fellow at the Stanford Humanities Center in 1993-94, is expected to be published this fall.

Temple Israel is at 1000 North Avenue. Admission to all three lectures is free. Call 235-1800 for more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
