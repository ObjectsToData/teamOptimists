As Arts Prizes Multiply, So Do Doubts on Value

By JUDITH MILLER
Published: December 2, 1996

Pick a night, any night. Somewhere in Manhattan, someone is giving an award for artistic achievement, often more than one. New York, according to the city's Office of Cultural Affairs, is now host to more prize ceremonies than any other American city.

But do they matter?

Yes and no. Or sometimes. But recipients of such honors, those who bestow them and people who cover or work in the fields being honored say many prizes no longer matter nearly as much as they once did, thanks in part to their sheer proliferation.

''There is now a veritable awards industry, complete with tchotchkes, and it's spreading,'' said Robert Marx, director of the New York Public Library for the Performing Arts at Lincoln Center, who called prize obsession a very ''American thing.''

''I come from a strict German Jewish family whose motto was 'Don't praise the children,' '' Mr. Marx said. ''But obviously, my industry disagrees.''

Joseph Horowitz, executive director of the Brooklyn Philharmonic and author of ''The Ivory Trade,'' about the Van Cliburn International Piano Competition, said, ''There are simply too many music competitions for them to have the impact they once did.''

Isabelle Stevenson, the president of the American Theater Wing, which awards the Tonys (and next year will add a new one for orchestration), agreed. ''It has all gotten a little out of hand,'' she said.

The prestige of the prizes varies wildly; so does the money. Some prestigious awards offer winners significant sums; the Nobel Prize in Literature, for instance, comes with a check for $1.12 million.

But some relatively obscure prizes offer big payoffs, too. The Lillian Gish Prize for Distinction in the Arts, awarded to Ingmar Bergman this year, gives the winner $250,000. In poetry, the International Prize and the Tanning Prize, provide $150,000 and $100,000, respectively. ''That's more than tipping money,'' said Roger Straus, a founder of Farrar, Straus & Giroux. ''But Bergman hardly needed another prize or the money, and neither of the poetry prizes sells books.''

That may be true for books published by large houses, said Bill Wadsworth, director of the Academy of American Poets, established in 1934 to promote poetry, mainly by granting prizes and fellowships. ''But winning a Whitman insures that a poet will be published.''

The Walt Whitman Award, established in 1975, comes with $5,000 and hardcover publication of a poet's first book by the Louisiana State University Press. But since the academy also buys 6,000 copies for its members, and the average print run for a poet's first book is 3,000 copies, a Whitman, or a James Laughlin Award ($5,000), and the sale of the 6,000 copies guarantee a best seller in the tiny poetry market.

Even in prize-mad poetry, however, word of mouth counts more than awards. ''A Pulitzer initially gives a book a little burst by putting books in a few more readers' hands, but if those readers don't like the book, the prize won't sell it,'' said Paul Ingram, manager of Prairie Lights Books, an independent store in Iowa City.

Because of its proximity to the University of Iowa's poetry workshop, the store has a relatively large audience for sophisticated fare. An Iowa City poet, Mary Szybist, was one of this year's eight grant winners in the second annual Rona Jaffe Foundation Writers' Awards, which identify women of exceptional talent at the early stages of their careers.

Awards do matter in children's books, however, because purchasers are less familiar with the books and look at prize seals for guidance and validation. Among the 100 annual awards for children's books, the Newbery Medal and the Caldecott Award for illustrated books, are more powerful in the marketplace than even a Nobel Prize or a Pulitzer: a Newbery usually guarantees sales of 100,000 copies in hardcover in the first year and more enduring status as a children's classic. This may account for the fact that both the National Book Foundation and the Cable Ace televison awards added prizes this year for material for children.

The scramble to call public attention to a book -- or a film, a play or a musician -- partly explains why publishers and their counterparts in the performing arts often spend thousands annually to enter competitions. A Pulitzer Prize nomination costs $50 a book; an entry for a National Book Award nomination, $100, and for the Global Business Book Awards, $500.

''It adds up, particularly for smaller houses,'' said Carol Schneider, director of publicity at Random House, which spends several thousands of dollars a year on prize nominations.

But a small arts producer can benefit disproportionately from winning. Sam Hamill, the founder of the Copper Canyon Press, a poetry publisher in Washington State, said Hayden Carruth's collection ''Scrambled Eggs and Whisky'' sold 6,000 copies the day after it won this year's National Book Award. Mr. Hamill is now doubling the book's first printing.

Winning prizes, of course, brings psychic satisfaction, particularly if it translates into future commercial value. Prizes give artists stature, which often means larger advances for their next works. Prize-winning playwrights or actors are usually paid more.
