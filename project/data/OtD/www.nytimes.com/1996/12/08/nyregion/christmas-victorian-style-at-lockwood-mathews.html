Christmas, Victorian Style, At Lockwood-Mathews

By BESS LIEBENSON
Published: December 8, 1996

THE Lockwood-Mathews Mansion Museum in Norwalk has created a winter exhibition around a Victorian American Christmas celebration.

''A Victorian Holiday at the Mansion'' will remain on view through Dec. 20 when the museum closes for the season to reopen in March.

The museum's traditional holiday program ''Five Decades of Christmas Trees'' has been expanded and integrated into a grander presentation in a storytelling style. The museum's mansion rooms offer views of the opulence of America's Gilded Age. Vignettes from the 1850's through the early 1900's include taking tea in the dining room, decorating mantels and trees in an entrance hall as well as drawing, music, library and card rooms with mannequins dressed in festive holiday attire.

The exhibition was made possible by a grant from the Connecticut Humanities Council. ''It enabled us to expand and develop an elaborate presentation of Christmas through the 19th century,'' said Kathy Maher, the museum's curator.

The heart of the exhibition is the development of the tree as a symbol of Christmas. ''The celebration of Christmas as we know it today is derived from a 19th-century desire to recapture a jovial past of previous centuries -- which in fact did not exist,'' Ms. Maher said. ''The idea of bringing Christmas into the house and decorating with Christmas trees, gifts and elaborate festivities was created from an imaginary fact and exclusive to the Victorian era. Prior to the 19th century, the Christmas holiday was not revered in the way it is today. It was celebrated as a religious holy day as opposed to a holiday, restricted to church services and a dinner.''

In 1850, Godey's Lady's Book illustrated an image of Queen Victoria, Prince Albert and their children gathered around a tabletop Christmas tree, an image that popularized the celebration of Christmas using an evergreen as a focal point. Early trees were embellished with natural elements -- everything from cookies to fruit to tiny presents. ''And on Christmas, the family ravaged the tree to eat the cookies and fruit and gather the presents,'' Ms. Maher said.

In the dining room of the mansion gauzy gilded swags embellished with fruit and birds frame an elaborately carved walnut and mirrored mantel. Festively costumed mother and child mannequins admire a tabletop tree of the 1850's with candles wired to branches. A second tree, from the 1860's, reflecting patriotic fervor during the Civil War, is covered with American flags and patriotic rosettes.

But a focal point is a table set for tea -- lavished with silver tea service, epergnes of flowers, crystal and silver jelly jars, ruby glass compotes dripping with fruit and silver platters, pedestals and baskets filled with bonbons, cookies, breads, berries and finger sandwiches.

''By the 1870's, commercialization of decorations became prominent and households combined manufactured with homemade ornaments,'' Ms. Maher said. ''Trees became room height as bigger became better. Presents surrounded the base of the tree, making it unnecessary to pull the tree apart on Christmas morning.''

In the mansion's dining room a bride in gray silk (white was not customary) and an attendant admire the tree and mantel lavished with white flowers and snow-tipped garlands.

By the 1890's, the tree dominated the parlor. In the museum mansion a 13-foot tree with glittering miniature lights and swags of red and gold, is displayed in the music room where the fireplace is layered with doll houses depicting 19th-century architecture.

Finally, a tree decorated with live flowers dominates the library, which will be used as a procession hall leading to a conservatory where Santa will hold court next Sunday.

The Lockwood-Mathews Mansion Museum, 295 West Avenue in Norwalk, is open Tuesday through Friday from 11 A.M. to 3 P.M. and Saturday and Sunday from 1 to 4 P.M.
