Notable Books of the Year 1996


Published: December 8, 1996

This list has been selected from books reviewed since the Christmas Books issue of December 1995. It is meant to suggest some of the high points in this year's fiction, poetry, nonfiction, mysteries and science fiction. The books are arranged alphabetically under genre headings.

FICTION & POETRY

ANNAM. By Christophe Bataille. (New Directions, $15.95.) This austere, spooky short novel concerns an 18th-century French mission to Vietnam, which failed because of inflexibility and ignorance.

ANTS ON THE MELON: A Collection of Poems. By Virginia Hamilton Adair. (Random House, $21.) Short, vigorous lyrics by an accomplished, original American poet who was never so bold as to offer a collection before.

ATTICUS. By Ron Hansen. (HarperCollins, $22.) A rich, disturbing novel whose title character forgives his prodigal son more than most readers would be inclined to forgive.

THE AUTOBIOGRAPHY OF MY MOTHER. By Jamaica Kincaid. (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, $20.) A shocking fable of willed nihilism whose paranoid protagonist defines herself and her world through acts of simple hatred.

BABEL TOWER. By A. S. Byatt. (Random House, $25.95.) In the third entry of a projected tetralogy about her own times, Ms. Byatt incarnates her awareness of fiction's fictiveness in a provincial Englishwoman who lusts for sex and books both.

BATTING AGAINST CASTRO: Stories. By Jim Shepard. (Knopf, $22.) Various in style and subject, imaginative and mysterious in conception; in the title story, Cuba's future leader challenges an American visiting team in 1951.

THE BEAUTY OF MEN. By Andrew Holleran. (Morrow, $24.) A bold, contrary novel whose hero, a gay man nearing 50, finds in his life a claustrophobic vacancy that owes more to a modern atrophy of spirit than to homosexuality.

BEHIND THE SCENES AT THE MUSEUM. By Kate Atkinson. (St. Martin's, $22.95.) This impressive first novel, a multigenerational tale of a spectacularly dysfunctional Yorkshire family, transmutes the native gloom to guilty merriment.

BELLE DU SEIGNEUR. By Albert Cohen. (Viking, $34.95.) A profoundly disturbing novel, published in France in 1968 and promptly canonized, about the doomed love affair of a cosmopolitan cynic and a slave to feminine cliches about passion.

BIRDSONG. By Sebastian Faulks. (Random House, $25.) A novel (the author's fourth) in which the ''hell of the trenches'' of World War I is played out in the literal underworld of mine and countermine, and the past recaptured through the decoding of a sapper's diary in the 1970's.

THE BOOK OF MERCY. By Kathleen Cambor. (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, $22.) Ms. Cambor's absorbing first novel concerns a family (father an alchemist, children a psychiatrist and a priest) first broken but later mostly restored by the centripetal force of mutual forgiveness.

BRAIN FEVER. By Valerie Sayers. (Doubleday, $23.95.) A failed academic breaks down and flees in this deep, ambitious novel, a case study of the workings of insanity, mercy and faith.

BURNING YOUR BOATS: The Collected Short Stories. By Angela Carter. (John Mac-rae/Holt, $30.) A strange, dangerous world, full of surprises and reversals, comes to life in this collection, which includes six previously uncollected tales by this English writer, who died in 1992.

CIVILWARLAND IN BAD DECLINE: Stories and a Novella. By George Saunders. (Random House, $22.) Cool and wicked satires, mostly taking place in theme parks where reality is created by the owners and external perceptions are fenced out.

COYOTE V. ACME. By Ian Frazier. (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, $17.) A collection of short humor pieces full of moments of dazzling nonsense and pure joy.

CROSS CHANNEL. By Julian Barnes. (Knopf, $21.) Short stories, crowded with understated or invisible meanings; the characters are English or Irish, living in France; the Channel is a metaphor for separations of a far more intimate kind.

CROWS OVER A WHEATFIELD. By Paula Sharp. (Hyperion, $22.95.) In this novel, the effects of child abuse reverberate over decades, from a small Wisconsin town to New York City.

DANCING AFTER HOURS: Stories. By Andre Dubus. (Knopf, $23.) Gracious and graceful short fiction, chiefly about love, that addresses matters closer to the province of poetry than of the novel.

DEATH IN THE ANDES. By Mario Vargas Llosa. (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, $24.) In his 11th novel, the Peruvian writer uses the story of a police investigation in the mountains to explore the political, social and religious complexities of his country.

THE DEBT TO PLEASURE. By John Lanchester. (Holt, $20.) A delicious first novel by a British editor and food critic; his hero and narrator, the self-confident, loquacious Tarquin Winot, travels through France, measuring out his life by menus.

DERBY DUGAN'S DEPRESSION FUNNIES. By Tom De Haven. (Metropolitan/Holt, $23.) An antic rags-to-rags novel whose narrator, a hack strip and pulp writer, struggles to earn a living in a world where commerce inevitably conquers art.

DON'T CALL IT NIGHT. By Amos Oz. (Harcourt Brace, $22.) Mr. Oz's 11th novel is a middle-aged-love story of good intentions and very small deeds (but big enough in Israel, where ''ordinary'' is a condition to which many passionately aspire).

THE DREAM OF THE UNIFIED FIELD: Selected Poems 1974-1994. By Jorie Graham. (Ecco, $23.) This collection of philosophically and historically alert poems won a Pulitzer Prize in April.
