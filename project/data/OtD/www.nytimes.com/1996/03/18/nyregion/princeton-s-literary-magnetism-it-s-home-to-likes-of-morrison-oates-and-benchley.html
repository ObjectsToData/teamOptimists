Princeton's Literary Magnetism;It's Home to Likes of Morrison, Oates and Benchley

By JANNY SCOTT
Published: March 18, 1996

If someone were to write a memoir of the literary life that has flourished in this area in recent years, one chapter might cover the curious adventure of the distinguished poet and the Volvo repair man.

The poet, Daniel Halpern, is a transplant to Princeton -- along with Joyce Carol Oates, Toni Morrison, Russell Banks and others. One day, he took his car in for repairs, a volume of Czeslaw Milosz's poems in the back seat.

The mechanic, Arthur Helnke, happened to remark that he, too, was an admirer of Milosz. So the men got to talking -- and ended up putting together a poetry-reading series that took place amid the lathes and drill presses in Art's Garage.

Ms. Oates read there. So did Mr. Halpern and the Irish poet Paul Muldoon. In the audience have been the likes of Victor Brombert, the professor, critic and author of books on Victor Hugo and Balzac, Stendhal and Baudelaire.

"We had some wonderful readings there," said Mr. Halpern, who is also editor in chief of the Ecco Press, which he moved recently from Manhattan to Hopewell, a small town outside Princeton. "Pretty fancy readings."

There is a saying heard around Princeton, that there's a real estate agent and a writer on every block. Writers have never been in short supply here, but these days they may be getting the upper hand.

The Princeton area has become host to a dense concentration of novelists, poets, biographers, critics, historians and others -- clusters of writers that occasionally resemble a kind of literary commune.

Many of the writers, successful and internationally known, turn out regularly for each other's readings, throw parties for each other, go out for Chinese food with each other, publish each other's work.

Ms. Oates and Mr. Halpern are good friends, as are Ms. Oates and Mr. Banks, the novelist, and his wife, Chase Twichell, the poet, who is a friend of Kathryn Watterson, a journalist and author, who is a friend of Richard Preston.

Mr. Preston, whose latest book is "The Hot Zone," goes mountain biking on Sourland Mountain with his friend John McPhee, the writer, who is a close friend of Peter Benchley, best known as the author of "Jaws."

So when The New Yorker published the article by Mr. Preston that became the best-seller "The Hot Zone" and Hollywood producers beseiged the magazine, Mr. Preston turned to an indigenous Princeton source for advice.

He telephoned Mr. Benchley, whom he had never met. "I said, 'I've got myself into a terrible Hollywood situation,' " Mr. Preston recalled. "His reply was, 'Is this an emergency?' I said, 'It is.' He dropped everything."

They met at the Alchemist & Barrister, a restaurant Mr. Benchley frequents (sometimes with John Chancellor, who retired to Princeton -- to write). Hollywood is a parallel universe, Mr. Benchley suggested; keep your distance.

"Later on, he gave me two other extremely useful and valuable pieces of advice," Mr. Preston remembered recently. "Don't move into a large house. And don't be tempted to give a lot of money to Princeton University."

'People Just Assume You're a Writer'

Ms. Watterson moved here from Manhattan with her son in 1986, knowing no one but her real estate agent. Then one day, a neighbor stopped by her mailbox and dropped off an invitation to an event at the Princeton Public Library.

The event was a reception for published authors living in the area who had books on the library's shelves, Ms. Watterson recalled. The place was packed. She cannot remember exactly how many writers showed up; was it 247 or 274?

A few years later, she and her new husband, Ron Sitts, a contractor and builder, were studying tax maps after buying a house. "Isn't this guy a writer?" Mr. Sitts asked. Mr. Banks owned the house next door.

Cornel West, who wrote the 1993 best-seller "Race Matters," lived across the street until he left to become a professor of Afro-American Studies at Harvard. Down the block is Howard Greenfeld, the prolific biographer.

"For people who aren't writers, like Ron, people just assume you're a writer," said Ms. Watterson, who even bumps into writers at the fitness center. "At parties, you hear people say: 'You're not a writer? Oh, great!"

Princeton University has a lot to do with why many of them are here. They came for jobs or to use the university's libraries. Some, like Mr. Preston and his wife, who is an editor, came as students, then stayed.

In recent years, many of the best-known writers have come to teach in the undergraduate creative writing program, which started in 1939, grew during the 1970's and 1980's and this year lists 18 writers on staff.

Ms. Oates joined the program in 1978, intending to stay for one year. James Richardson, the poet, came in 1980. Then came Mr. Banks, then Mr. Muldoon, then Ms. Morrison, who went on to win the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1993.

The temporary faculty members -- who stay from one semester to many years -- currently include, among others, Breyten Breytenbach, the South African poet; Peter Carey, the Australian novelist, and Thulani Davis, the poet and playwright.

"To name the people who are here is to name why one would stay," Ms. Oates said. "If they weren't here, the community would still have great attractions. But these people make it very wonderful."
