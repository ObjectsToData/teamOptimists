GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 31, 1996

MUSEUM BENEFIT

The list of artists whose works will be for sale through April 21 at the Katonah Museum of Art reads like a Who's Who in contemporary art. More than 200 prints and photographs executed in the last five years have been curated by Ellen Handy, associate curator of the International Center for Photography in New York, and Robert Monk, director of the Department of Art at Sotheby's.

The show, titled "New in the 90's: An Exhibition of Prints and Photographs," opens today. It is the first benefit that the museum has held for itself and proceeds from sales ranging from $500 to $10,000 will be used for the museum's education and exhibition programs.

Most of the 70 print makers and 33 photographers are internationally known. The artists include Mel Bochner, Chuck Close, Richard Diebenkorn, Jim Dine, Eric Fischl, Helen Frankenthaler, Lucian Freud, Red Grooms, David Hockney, Jasper Johns, Alex Katz, Sol LeWitt, Roy Lichtenstein, Claes Oldenburg, Robert Rauschenberg, James Rosenquist, Julian Schnabel, Richard Serra and Neil Welliver.

Among the photographers are Sally Apfelbaum, Zeke Berman, William Christenberry, Robert Flynt, Nancy Goldring, Andrea Modica, Abelardo Morell, Sabastiano Salgado, Jerry Uelsmann and Neil Winokur.

In addition, three-dimensional multiples by Cindy Sherman, Arman, and George Segal are on view.

Three events are scheduled in conjunction with the exhibition. The first, on April 8, is a talk by Grace Mirabella, former editor of Vogue and Mirabella magazines, on "The Elements of Style: On Photography and Fashion." The $45 fee includes lunch and a tour of the exhibition.

Children 5 and up will be able to create multiples of family portraits in various printmaking techniques on April 14 at $8 a child. On April 24 the museum will lead a trip to David P. Tunick's Townhouse Gallery in Manhattan to see his collection of Old Master prints.

Visiting hours for the exhibition are 1 to 5 P.M. on Sunday and Tuesday through Friday, 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. on Saturday. The museum is located on Route 22, south of Route 35 in Katonah. For more information, call 232-9555.

DYNASTIES UPFRONT

"The Parade of Dynasties," a pageant of Chinese dance, acrobatics, costumes and music, will be performed today at 2 P.M. at Westchester Community College in Valhalla.

In five segments, the troupe covers the history of the Chinese Empire over 2,000 years, from the pre-Qin Dynasty around 200 B.C., through the Manchu Dynasty that ended in 1911. Because of its size and scope the production is to be held in the college gymnasium, the largest space on campus.

Each segment is costumed, choreographed and performed in a setting that replicates its historical period. The music, recorded in China by the Shanghai Traditional and Minorities Instruments Symphony, is played on authentic instruments and faithfully reflects the musical sounds of each era.

The production is staged as living drama, with the audience at times finding itself invaded by soldiers in battle, or mingling with country peasants or in the presence of the Emperor at court in the Forbidden City.

Reviewers who saw the show in Boston and Los Angeles were independently struck with its reliance on human skill and virtuosity rather than electronics and special effects for its ability to entertain.

The college is located at 75 Grasslands Road. Tickets are $15; people 65 and older, $10. Call 785-6567 for more information. HUDSON CLEANUP

Today is beachcombing day at Croton Point Park in Croton-on- Hudson -- the day that Christopher Letts, the county Parks Department's Hudson River educator, leads a number of hardy, conscientious souls in cleaning up the Hudson River shoreline of driftwood and debris.

Anyone who would like to take part should meet the groups in the parking lot of the park at 2 P.M., wearing old clothes and shoes suitable for the weather.

While the volunteers perform this public service Mr. Letts will regale them with lore about the river and its importance as a commercial fishing and shipping resource.

At the end of the day there will be a marshmallow roast over a driftwood fire.

FOCUSING OUTSIDE

The American backyard, said James A. Abbott, "was born of the 19th century dilemma in finding a middle ground between rural and urban living." Mr. Abbott, curator of collections and director of education at the Boscobel Restoration will lecture today to a members-only preview of a new exhibition at the Scarsdale Historical Society. Titled "Inside Out: Residential Landscapes in America, 1840-1940," the exhibition opens to the public on Wednesday and will remain through June 30 at the Historical Society Museum, 937 Post Road.

Its theme is the translation of 19th-century interior design concepts to the outdoors, and the creation of an outdoor environment that was, and still is, either a miniature of the grand estate or the farm. On loan from various antique dealers are garden ornaments, furniture, fencing, and other accoutrements in styles that changed over the years with prevailing fashions in interior decorating.

Brochures from the 1920's promoting suburban living, Currier & Ives prints of gardens, and photographs of existing Scarsdale and White Plains gardens as they appeared 70 years ago will be on view. There will also be text by Alexander Jackson Downing, a mid-19th century gardening authority who came from a family of nurserymen in Newburgh. In 1841 he published "Landscape Gardening," a book which set the standard and style for backyard gardens in the region at that time.

On April 24 a talk about "Formal Gardens in England and America" will be given by Lorella Brocklesby, adjunct professor of humanities at New York University. The program will take place at the Scarsdale Public Library, Post and Olmsted Roads. Admission will be by a suggested donation of $4.

Exhibition hours are Wednesday through Friday from 1:30 to 4:30 P.M. and Sunday from 2 to 4:30 P.M. Admission is $1.50; students and people 65 and older, $1.25. Admission is free the first Sunday of each month.

For more information, the number to call is 723-1744. ELEANOR CHARLES
