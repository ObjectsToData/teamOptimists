CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: March 31, 1996

FILMS FROM HONG KONG

A comprehensive study of movies made in Hong Kong begins this week at Yale in New Haven. The series, at the Whitney Humanities Center, is free to the public. Co-sponsors include the Yale Council of East Asian Studies, the Hong Kong Economic and Trade office in Manhattan, the Yale-China Association and Yale Film Study Center.

Five films made between 1987 and 1994 will be shown over the next two weeks, and on the final day, April 14, Barbara Scharres, director of the film center at the School of the Art Institute in Chicago, will lecture on "Current Trends in Hong Kong Cinema" at 1 P.M. A panel discussion will conclude the series at 7:30 P.M.

The first film, on Tuesday at 7:30, is "A Chinese Ghost Story" with Leslie Cheung (of "Farewell My Concubine") as a tax collector who seeks shelter from a storm in a deserted temple haunted by a beautiful spirit.

"God of Gamblers," on Wednesday at the same time, is one of Hong Kong's highest grossing films.

On April 11 at 7:30, "Green Snake" is the feature, based on a Chinese folk tale and the Beijing opera about two sister snakes who have practiced for centuries to become human.

On April 14 at 2 P.M., "He's a Woman, She's a Man" will be shown -- a comedy about three people falling in love with each other.

The final film, "The Bodyguard From Beijing," concerns a mainland Chinese bodyguard assigned to protect a fashionable Hong Kong woman who is a sequestered witness in a trial. For more information, the numbers are 432-9392 or 432-3426.

JACKIE ROBINSON

"Jackie Robinson and the Integration of Professional Baseball" is the subject to be discussed on Tuesday at 7:30 in the Gengras Student Union of the University of Hartford. The speaker will be Warren Goldstein, author of "Playing for Keeps: A History of Early Baseball," which won the 1989 award for the best book of sports history from the North American Society for Sport History.

Mr. Goldstein, who received advance degrees in American studies from Yale, is to join the University of Hartford faculty as a professor of American history this fall.

The university is at 200 Bloomfield Avenue in West Hartford, and admission is free. The number is (860) 768-4806 for more information. SHORT SUBJECTS

"All in the Timing," comprising six comic short works, previews on Tuesday at the Long Wharf Theater in New Haven. Written by David Ives, the show is said to be the most-produced theater piece of 1995, having had 600 performances at Primary Stages Off Broadway, as well as productions in Vienna, Berlin, Sydney, Montreal, Paris and London. Individual segments include "Sure Thing," concerning romance the second, third and fourth time around, as well as "Words, Words, Words," in which three chimpanzees are locked in a room with three typewriters.

"The Universal Language," is about a language that no one can speak but which turns out to hold the meaning of life. The remaining segments, called "Degas, C'est Moi," "Foreplay, or the Art of the Fugue" and "Variations on the Death of Trotsky," are equally arcane.

Officially opening on April 10, the show will run through May 5. Performances are at 7 P.M. on Tuesday and at 8 P.M. Wednesday through Friday. Saturday performances are at 4 and 8:30; Sunday at 2 and 7. Weekday matinees at 2 P.M. are scheduled on April 17 and May 1. Tickets at $20 to $24 for previews and $28 to $38 for the regular run may be reserved by calling 787-4282. 'UNCLE BOB'

Austin Pendleton, playwright, actor, director and artistic director of the Circle Repertory Theater in Manhattan, will have the title role in his own comedy, "Uncle Bob," opening at the Hutensky Theater, 233 Pearl Street in downtown Hartford on Friday.

Bob, who has AIDS, lives like a hermit, albeit an abrasively funny one, in the West Village in Manhattan. His nephew Josh takes it upon himself to hitchhike to New York from the Midwest to care for his uncle and perhaps resolve some of his own problems.

The play has been produced by Theater Works under the direction of Steve Campo. Performances are on Wednesday through Saturday at 8, Sunday at 2:30, through May 5. Ticket prices range from $16 to $19 with discounts available for students and people over 65. A surcharge of $3 is applied for some seating. The number is 527-7838 for reservations.

CRAFTS CLASSES The Brookfield Craft Center, in an old grist mill complex on Route 25, will hold an open house today from 1 to 4 P.M., when 15 artisans will demonstrate skills they teach in year-round classes.

Loom weaving will be demonstrated by Barbara Dull; Jody Hyman will focus on spinning; metal jewelry will be made by Anne Marie Cuiffini, and Hattie Newman will work with beads. Wheel-thrown pottery by Kristin Muller and Chris Alexiades, wood turning on the lathe by Angelo Iafrete, cabinet making by Larry Hendricks, and decorative furniture painting by Carol Sue Roberts are also on the agenda.

A free course will be awarded from a list of visitors who register for the drawing. The number for more information is 775-4526. ELEANOR CHARLES
