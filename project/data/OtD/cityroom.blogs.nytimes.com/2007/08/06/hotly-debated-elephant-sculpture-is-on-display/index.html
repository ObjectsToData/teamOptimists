    A Fiberglass Elephant in Chains

    By SEWELL CHAN
    August 6, 2007 5:12 pmAugust 7, 2007 12:56 pm
    A sculpture of a circus elephant by Harry Bliss that will be displayed in Union Square park through Aug. 20. (Photo: Oscar Hidalgo/The New York Times)

    A sculpture depicting a tearful elephant in chains that was designed in New York and became the center of a federal lawsuit in Washington was put on display in Union Square Park today, with the blessing of New York City officials.

    The fiberglass sculpture, “Ella PhantzPeril,” shows a circus elephant with its front right leg in shackles. In 2002, People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals, the animal welfare group, tried to place the sculpture on view as part of the “Party Animals” exhibition — a summer public art show depicting festive donkeys and elephants — organized by city officials in Washington. The city refused, and PETA sued the District of Columbia Commission on the Arts and Humanities in federal court.

    A federal judge, Richard J. Leon, sided with PETA, finding that the commission’s arguments for excluding the elephant “strained credulity.” However, the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit later reversed that decision, ruling that the “Party Animals” exhibition consisted of “government speech,” rather than individual artistic expression.

    In addition to Washington, the sculpture has been shown in Bridgeport, Conn., and Tarrytown, Tex.

    In New York City, the Department of Parks and Recreation granted permission for the sculpture to be displayed at the southwest corner of Union Square Park, off 14th Street, from noon today through Aug. 20, PETA announced.

    The fiberglass elephant, which weighs 150 pounds and stands 6 feet tall and is covered in graffiti-resistant paint, was designed by Harry Bliss, a cartoonist who has designed several cartoons for The New Yorker.

    The Web site of the Ringling Brothers and Barnum & Bailey circus has no circus listed for the New York area until October, when the circus will travel to Bridgeport. The Big Apple Circus has performances scheduled for Damrosch Park, in Lincoln Center, starting in October.

    Elephants are at the heart of a PETA campaign against the use of elephants in circuses. The activist group asserts that cruelty is inherent in the training of circus elephants. “Circuses subject elephants to cruel training and constant confinement,” PETA said in a statement. ” ‘Positive reinforcement’ is little more than a myth propagated by the circus industry. Trainers dominate animals using painful punishment to ensure that they perform tricks on command.”

    Feld Entertainment, which runs the Ringling Brothers circus, vigorously disagrees. The company says in a statement [pdf] on its Web site:

    Ringling Bros. has established itself as a recognized leader in elephant conservation efforts by building the Ringling Bros. and Barnum & Bailey Center for Elephant Conservation, a state-of-the-art facility for the reproduction, research and retirement of the Asian elephant. Scientists and animal care experts from around the world visit and participate in the centers program.

    This 200-acre plant, in central Florida, is home to the largest sustainable population of captive Asian elephants in the Western hemisphere. Twenty elephants have been born to date under Ringling Bros. care, and the staff shares the knowledge gained over 137 years of working with these magnificent animals with the worlds veterinary and research communities. The ability to educate the public and find ways for elephants and humans to live and work together will help ensure the survival of the species, with fewer than 35,000 left in the wild.

    The circus contributed $135,000 to the Smithsonian Institutions National Zoo last year for a reproductive research study about the endangered Asian elephant. In 2005, the circus gave $180,000 to the zoo for research on endotheliotropic herpes viruses, a threat to the the Asian elephant today, and announced an important partnership with the Fort Worth Zoo. The circus says it also supports conservation efforts in Thailand and other parts of Southeast Asia.

    In an interview, Ian Blessing, a PETA spokesman, said the future of Ella the elephant is uncertain. “It will be going into storage after the two weeks here,” he said.
