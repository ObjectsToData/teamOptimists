    Which Came First? (Part Three): Can George, Lionel and Marmaduke Help Us Order the Fenton Photographs?

    By ERROL MORRIS
    October 23, 2007 11:42 pmJune 10, 2009 4:10 pm

    (To see Part One of this article, click here. To see Part Two, click here.)

    “Too bad, it was a cloudy day. You really can’t see any shadows.”

    My friend, the inventor Dennis Purcell, corrected me: “I don’t think it was cloudy. It was a bright, sunny day. Or perhaps cloudy-bright.”

    Dennis explained that most 19th century photographic emulsions are blue-sensitive and hence cannot record the sky – overcast, partially cloudy and sunny skies are all overexposed. The sky is a featureless white, but the “whiteness” of the sky is unrelated to the question of whether there are clouds or whether you can see shadows. It was only much later that panchromatic film was developed. (This accounts for what I would call The Wisconsin Death-Trip Effect, after the book by the same name. Scandinavian immigrants in turn-of-the-century Wisconsin might not be insane, but they look insane because their blue eyes are white in the pre-panchromatic emulsions used to produce the photographs.)

    Valley of the Shadow of Death. By Eric Zimmerman.
    The same image as above, digitally altered to demonstrate the effect of blue sensitive emulsion. Alteration by John Purcell.

    In both ON and OFF the sky may look overcast, but you can see shadows. You have to look at the shadows on the cannonballs and from those shadows it may be possible to calculate the height of the sun in the sky.

    Long before I journeyed to the Crimea, I attempted to look for evidence in the Fenton photographs themselves. I had been shown an extraordinary book by Billy Kluver, “A Day with Picasso.” It sets the stage for what follows[1]. A University of California Berkeley-trained engineer, Kluver had collaborated with a number of artists, including John Cage, Robert Rauschenberg and Andy Warhol. Over a period of some 20 years, Kluver had assembled a collection of photographs of Picasso, Modigliani and Moise Kisling, but it was not until fairly late in the game that Kluver surmised that the photographs were taken with one camera on four rolls of film, 24 pictures, six pictures on a roll – and that they were taken over a short period, possibly a single day.

    “When I came across the tenth photograph (No. 24 in the sequence in which they were taken) reproduced in the catalogue of the Modigliani exhibition at the Musee d’Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris in spring of 1981, with its very distinct shadows on the buildings in the background, it occurred to me that if the buildings could be identified, it might be possible to use the shadows to determine the exact day and time the photographs were taken.” (From Kluver, “A Day with Picasso”)

    The first step for Kluver was sun-angle. Then, various markers. The awning of the Cafe de la Rotonde. The foliage on trees. Kisling’s dog, Kouski. The dress worn by Picasso’s mistress, Paquerette. Using these, he was able to order the 24 photographs, to determine that the photographs were taken on August 12, 1916, and to identify the photographer: Jean Cocteau.

    Max Jacob standing by the tram tracks on the Boulevard du Montparnasse. Ortiz and Picasso are standing with their backs to the camera. The rays of the sun are almost perpendicular to the buildings on the Montparnasse. 2:15 p.m.
    Picasso and his mistress, Paquerette, at the Cafe de la Rotonde. 2:20 p.m. The bar in the background is lit up by the sun, indicating that the sun is still nearly perpendicular to the buildings on Montparnasse.

    The Fenton photographs offer few opportunities for this sort of forensic examination. The lack of features that make the photographs a powerful metaphor for the desolation of war also make them a difficult source of information about shadows and sun-angle. Where Kluver had buildings, light-posts and various people to cast different sorts of shadows at different times of day, as well as the correspondence of many of the people in the photographs, I had a couple of letters and an almost featureless landscape. A kind of existentialist’s dream. Something like a surrealist painting without the driftwood.

    The cannonballs become the first and only place to look for shadows. Nothing else in the picture casts one. There are no trees; no landmarks of any kind, save for the dirt road and foot path that bisect the frame and disappear into the distance. There is also nothing to tell us about the sky – it is washed out, featureless and dirty white. Did a cloud pass across the sun, changing well-defined shadows to diffuse, murky lines? Was there bright sunlight? The sky – or lack of sky – is compatible with both.

    Have a look at this sun-map for Sebastopol, April 1855. The software is routinely used by gaffers (lighting technicians) in the motion picture business. I set the latitude (44° 37’N) and longitude (33° 31’ E) for Sebastopol and the date, April 23, 1855. (The software only goes back 100 years or so, but April 23, 1855, mercifully is much the same as April 23, 1955 – at least as far as sun-angle in Sebastopol is concerned.)

    Courtesy of sunPATH from www.wide-screen.com

    The sun-map tells us on April 23, 1855, the sun rose around 6 a.m. and set a little before 8 p.m. It also tells us that the sun reached its zenith around 1 p.m. and was 60 degrees above the horizon from the south. The sun at around 4 p.m. was about 40 degrees above the horizon.

    The sun-map, of course, can take us just so far. We have to know additional things. Were the photographs taken in the morning or afternoon? Which direction was the camera facing – east, west, north or south? Happily, disagreements about the direction Fenton was facing when he took the picture were resolved by my trip to the Crimea. I discovered that Fenton was facing north toward the shelling – toward Sebastopol. Is this so surprising? Isn’t this the first law of artillery? Face the direction from where the fire is coming. That way you don’t get hit in the back with a cannonball.

    If we are facing north in the afternoon, from the sun diagram we would expect the cannonballs to be front-lit, then side-lit as the sun moved from east to west, that is from right to left. (Of course, everything changes if we are facing south. Then we would expect the shadows to be moving in the opposite direction. Direction and time-of-day are critical in determining the sequence – to understanding the shadows.)

    I began to have an uneasy feeling about my data. Hovering close by was my arch nemesis, self-deception. Had I convinced myself that the cannonballs were back-lit? Or side-lit? Or front-lit? Were the shadows that distinct? The sun-map showed that if the photographs were taken about an hour apart, then the sun moved not much more than a 10-degree arc – up and down or left or right. Ten degrees is not that much. Could it be accurately measured in the photographs – that subtle shifting of shadows from right to left or up and down? And how long did it take Fenton to take the two photographs? An hour? More than an hour? Perhaps. But perhaps much less time elapsed. Furthermore, armed with my 24-pound cannonball in the Crimea, as it became more and more hazy, the shadows on the cannonballs became less and less distinct.

    I began to doubt myself. I had entered the shadow-land of the shadows themselves[2]. Before I had determined the direction of Fenton’s camera, I had thought that the cannonballs in ON looked more top-lit than the cannonballs in OFF. But then when I determined that we were looking north or north-northwest, not south or south-southeast, the cannonballs looked different. I mean, they actually looked different. What was I measuring? The shift in the shadows could be very small, too small for accurate measurement. Furthermore, what did I know – what did I really know — about interpreting shadows?

    Type in “shadow master” or “shadow expert” on Google, and a number of bizarre references pop up. Connie Zweig, author of “Romancing the Shadow: A Guide to Soul Work for a Vital, Authentic Life” (with Steve Wolf), “an in-depth look at our hidden, wounded selves and how they form in early childhood…often wrecking [sic] havoc on our lives.” Ms. Zweig was followed by how-to-do-it books on eye-shadow application, video games, comic-book heroes. Eventually, Ralph Bouwmeester, an engineer and self-styled sun and shadow expert, popped up.

    I got excited. The very idea of “a shadow expert” conjures something out of film noir. Armed with a set of sine and cosine tables, isn’t it possible to undermine the world?

    R. BOUWMEESTER & ASSOCIATES
    Sun & Shadow Position Specialists
    with Modeling Applications in
    Accident and Crime Scene Reconstruction,
    Urban Development, Site Planning and Building Design

    I sent Mr. Bouwmeester copies of ON and OFF and shortly afterward we got down to business.

    RALPH BOUWMEESTER: The bottom line is that the checks and balances seem to point in the same direction, and I am coming up with a couple of decent times for the two pictures. Not only that, I’m sure you’re aware that there’s a stark difference in the objects that are visible in the two pictures.

    ERROL MORRIS: O.K.

    RALPH BOUWMEESTER: Balls that appear in [OFF] all of a sudden are gone in [ON], as if they have been picked up from the hill and moved over to the road. But the nature of the cannonballs on the road – just the way they look – appears to be different from the cannonballs on the hill in the foreground.

    ERROL MORRIS: I’m not sure I understand.

    RALPH BOUWMEESTER: I don’t know how you would ever doctor these. But, if these were digital photos, I’d be inclined to say that it was a poor job of cutting and pasting. The perspective doesn’t seem to be 100 percent right from what I can see.

    ERROL MORRIS: How so?

    RALPH BOUWMEESTER: What I’m trying to say is there are cannonballs that seem to be exactly the same size, some are in the foreground and some are set back further down the road. When you measure them, they’re exactly the same size, which would happen if you were to clone them without resizing them. There are many little inconsistencies like this that make me uncomfortable. I’m not completely familiar with the various sizes, various kinds of cannonballs used in the Crimea. My understanding is that there were differences. But just looking at the photographs, I get the feeling that we are looking at cannonballs of roughly the same size. A lot of them[3].

    [Bouwmeester was not proposing uncertainty; he was suggesting a conspiracy. The cannonballs are all the same size? Because they have been cloned? How would this affect the measurement of sun-angle? A spherical cannonball is a spherical cannonball is a spherical cannonball. Would it make any difference – for calculating sun-angle – to know that one cannonball is a 24-pounder, another a 32-pounder? Why the interest in the various diameters of cannonballs? To prove that the photographs were doctored? This was an even more damning claim than Sontag’s. Not only did Fenton oversee the scattering of the balls, he possibly oversaw their cloning, using some Victorian version of Photoshop. – E.M.]

    This brings up something that greatly interests me: why people are attracted to conspiracy theories – even conspiracies of one – and to claims of posing, fakery and alteration. We look at data. It’s complex, hard to understand, but instead of trying to determine the meaning of the data, we leap to a simplistic conclusion: the data is not real data. It has been doctored. A group of people (or even one person) conspires to trick us, to give us false information, and we’re too smart to be fooled by such a conspiracy.

    Isn’t Sontag’s theory something like that? She resolves a mystery simply by declaring it: a trick, a plan to deceive[4]. The claim that a photograph is posed is a claim that the photographer intended to deceive the viewer. It’s not that photographers never set out to fool or trick us, or that there are no conspiracies, it’s just that trickery (or conspiracy) is the last explanation I consider, not the first I jump to.

    Today, possibly because of Photoshop and other photography-doctoring software, people have become suspicious of photographs. This is a good thing. It’s better to be on the tip of our toes about the possibility of error rather than oblivious to it. But not every photograph has been tampered with. Bouwmeester wasn’t analyzing shadows; he was looking for things hidden in the shadows.

    We returned briefly to Bouwmeester’s original hunch: that the cannonballs had been removed from hillside and placed on the road.

    ERROL MORRIS: Have you done a count, by any chance?

    RALPH BOUWMEESTER: I was thinking about it, but I thought the heck with that; I’m not going to count them.

    Dennis Purcell offered to help me count the cannonballs but was working overtime on a new invention for Tenebraex Corporation, where he is senior scientist. He suggested that I talk to John Russ and Chris Russ, the father-son creators of Fovea Pro – a forensic photography program for processing and measuring images.

    John Russ was busy, but Chris had time to look at the photographs and to do a forensic analysis.

    CHRIS RUSS: Are you trying to go a step farther and say how much time went by? Because it should be pretty simple to illustrate whether OFF comes before ON or ON comes before OFF.

    ERROL MORRIS: Maybe it is simple, but I haven’t come up with an answer. My first thought was: let’s try to compute sun angles from the shadows.

    CHRIS RUSS: Well, you need to find the same object casting shadows with those images, which is not necessarily the easiest thing.

    ERROL MORRIS: Except that there are cannonballs.

    CHRIS RUSS: Are they still there now?

    ERROL MORRIS: No. They’re all gone.

    CHRIS RUSS: Did you go put new ones down?

    ERROL MORRIS: I did. But only one.

    CHRIS RUSS: And you know that Fenton was looking northward?

    ERROL MORRIS: Yeah. North by northwest, like the movie.

    CHRIS RUSS: O.K., knowing that you’re pointing north-northwest in the afternoon, shadows will be longer on the right side later.

    ERROL MORRIS: Yes.

    CHRIS RUSS: Well, I’m going to try to match the overall contrast between the two pictures, if I can. The top layer of [OFF] is considerably brighter in places. So it’s got a much higher contrast. So I could try to flatten that and see what I can do. That’s about 151[5].

    [Here is where the problem began to look intractable. If the contrast levels in the two photographs is different, what does that mean? The hill on the left is darker in ON. Lighter in OFF. A cloud? Haze? A different time of day? Or something more elusive? An artifact of how the emulsion was put on the glass-plate, or how it was exposed, developed or printed? With so many, many variables, how can one ever determine whether the shadows reflect real shadows or some artifact of the photographic process. An epistemic shadow. I was trying to figure out the next step. One possibility: collecting photographs (from around the world because the Fenton prints had been widely dispersed), comparing them, trying to match the various versions of ON and the various versions of OFF. It seemed painstaking and absurd, but would this be more absurd than what I had already done? – E.M.]

    CHRIS RUSS: O.K, this is going to take a little bit of work to match the contrast between two images. I would say that [ON] has a much more diffuse light than [OFF]. The shadow trick works pretty well on a no-cloud-in-the-sky day where you’ve got nice, sharp shadows. The problem with one of these pictures is that it was taken at a time where there’s cloud cover.

    ERROL MORRIS: How do we know that?

    CHRIS RUSS: There are specular highlights on a great number of objects from [OFF]. [ON] is more diffusely illuminated. I’m going to have to balance these pictures to match each other. Let me play for a few minutes, I’ll be right back with you.

    A couple of hours later.

    CHRIS RUSS: I have balanced the two images out and I’ve got some observations. Looking at the two images, now that I’ve balanced the contrast and dealt with a few interesting printing artifacts, the shadows of the balls are very, very hard to measure. So instead, I’m looking at the valley as a sort of a bowl shape that is lit in different places depending on the time of day. You with me so far?

    ERROL MORRIS: I think so.

    CHRIS RUSS: And that’s consistent with the other way of measuring where the sun is – looking at the balls (because they’re spheres) and determining where the light is on them. In OFF the light is further down toward the right. In ON the balls seems to be lit over pretty much over the top half of the ball. Closer to the middle of the day. [The sun is at its zenith around 1 p.m. – E.M.] Assuming afternoon for both, and assuming a camera direction of north-northwest, then ON was taken first. I’ll try to balance the gray scale between the images using the road itself. There are specular highlights in OFF. It’s very, very specular, making me think there are no clouds at that point.

    ERROL MORRIS: Specular highlights? What do you mean?

    CHRIS RUSS: O.K., the difference between a bright metal surface and a polished plastic surface is the plastic seems flat and the metal has a bright spot on it relative to where the light source is. That bright light source is a specular highlight. That happens with material if you’ve got something that’s polished versus something that isn’t. It also happens with the same subject if you change the light from being a single point source, which you have on a sunny day, to a diffuse light source, which you have on a cloudy day. So one of the things that’s causing problems for us is that one of these pictures was taken – and you can tell that by the contrast of the picture – one was taken with clouds and the other was taken with no clouds. So that’s one of the things that’s going on. Considering that, I felt that because the balls were metal, they might not be as good a measure as the countryside itself. Therefore, it’s important to know the angle of the hills on the sides of the road and the road itself. That’s the part I started looking at. Because first I felt, “Oh well, let me compare the relative shadow from left to right on the two different sides of the road.” When I first did that, I got the impression that ON happened earlier in the day than OFF, with the sun going further over because the embankment on the right side got brighter. Now, this is all completely backwards if Fenton’s pointing southward instead of northward. The thing is, how close together in time do you think they’re taken?

    ERROL MORRIS: I don’t really know. I’ve talked to a number of people about how long it took to take the two photographs. Fenton says that he stayed about an hour and a half and got two pictures, but I believe the interval between the two photographs was determined not by the time it took to prepare the two plates, but by the time needed to place the cannonballs on to or to take the cannonballs off of the road. My guess is that Fenton wouldn’t have lingered there for very long.

    CHRIS RUSS: There is one other thing different between these two images that makes it so hard to tell anything from the lighting. In ON, there’s a hill off in the distance – just straight ahead – that is roughly the same intensity of the embankment on the right. In OFF the hill’s considerably brighter. And I’m trying to figure out what that means. Because we could be chasing our tails in a circular argument just because of what the clouds do.

    ERROL MORRIS: Yes.

    CHRIS RUSS: Now, the other observation I have is the number of balls that seem to be missing from the left side and present on the road are not consistent. A lot more show up in the road than disappear from the side. And most of the ones in the major gully, which are where the vast majority of the balls are, don’t change, although a couple have moved slightly.

    ERROL MORRIS: Another odd thing, of course, is if you look, there’s that dirt path that you see coming down on the left, and you’ll notice again just to camera left of that path, there’s a rock which is present in ON, but absent in OFF. I’ve named the rock “Esmeralda.”

    CHRIS RUSS: Why add a rock?

    ERROL MORRIS: Or subtract a rock?

    CHRIS RUSS: Yes, I saw that rock. I was using the rock that’s further up that path. It’s present in both, a fairly large rock, given the distance, as my primary method of balancing brightness. Because I felt that the side to the camera of that rock was least likely to be affected by the lighting. But why would someone add the rock?

    ERROL MORRIS: Where is the logic here? I don’t know. Maybe someone rolled the rock down from further up the hill. Maybe Fenton thought there were too many rocks in the photograph. Maybe he thought there were not enough. Maybe Esmeralda improved or loused up his composition.

    [At this juncture Chris had given up on the detail of how each cannonball had been lit by the sun and was now concerned with the global pattern of lighting of the landscape. – E.M.]

    CHRIS RUSS: That hilltop off in the distance is very interesting. It would have had strategic value, or I should say at least tactical value. I’m going to do some toggling to see if I can get a shot of it. I don’t think he gathered up cannonballs from a remote site and put them in the road. I think he just moved them. I don’t think he added to the scene; he just moved them. I’ve been thinking about this lighting a little bit more. If we assume that we’re looking uphill instead of down, then I think it is consistent with OFF being earlier. Because now you’ve got light further down the road, which is on the other side, and if we’re looking northwest, then that works. It’s all about the topology of the land. Let’s look at OFF. If I’m looking somewhat uphill, then we’ve got light up here, but also down there in the valley. And in ON, there’s just light down there because the sun has gone further down. It’s only lit down there because the side up here isn’t pointing toward the sun anymore. So OFF would be earlier in the day than ON. It’s a function of what can be hit by the sun and how far toward northwest you’re at. I don’t know. I’ve flipped back and forth at least three times in the last hour. So we really need to get an idea of direction.

    ERROL MORRIS: I’m convinced we are looking north. We’re looking down into the valley, although the hills that you see to the right and to the left of the road trend up from the road.

    CHRIS RUSS: There are objects in the scene that have been moved, and you might argue that someone walked through there.

    Detail of “OFF.” Fenton, Roger. Valley of The Shadow of Death. Harry Ransom Humanities Research Center, The University of Texas at Austin.
    Detail of “ON.” Fenton, Roger. Valley of The Shadow of Death. Harry Ransom Humanities Research Center, The University of Texas at Austin.

    ERROL MORRIS: Someone had to have walked through there. Something happened between the two photographs. The cannonballs didn’t just roll off or on to the road. Someone moved them – even if it wasn’t Fenton.

    CHRIS RUSS: I would like to find an area where there is a footprint in one but no footprint in the other. The erasing of a footprint is a whole lot different than moving a rock. But this ground is too hard for that, which makes me wonder why I’ve got these blown out highlights in OFF on the left side of the image. There are some very, very bright points on the road.

    [All of a sudden there was an exclamation from Chris Russ. It was a Eureka moment for him, but I was no longer sure what he was talking about. Had we both retreated into a pixel-based pixilated fantasy-land? – E.M.]

    CHRIS RUSS: Bingo! Bingo! You know that hill in the distance? Let’s say we’re close to sunset in the second shot. It would be lit up. Earlier in the day, it wouldn’t be. O.K., there’s this other hill… There’s another hill, interestingly enough, over toward the left. Do you mind if I take the phone and beat it against my head for a couple of minutes?

    ERROL MORRIS: Please don’t injure yourself on my account over this.

    CHRIS RUSS: Oh, don’t worry. This is a very intriguing puzzle. One trick that’s going to help you: after you scan the images at high resolution, change your Photoshop mode to 16 bits. That won’t do anything right away, but then change the image size. And what that’s going to do is: every new pixel is going to be made up of original pixels from before, and will produce much better averages and give you tonality. And then as you start stretching and compressing contrast, you might start to see something. I reduced my image size down to about 2,000 wide from the 5,000 we attempted before, and it let me do some things. Got rid of some, some but not all of the speckle. But it also let me see some definition, some tone on the back of rocks. A useful trick. I am hoping to find a footprint.

    [Find a footprint! The mark of Fenton? Of his assistant, Sparling? Or some nameless British soldier? The unknown soldier with a mark in one of the iconic pictures of the Crimean War? A phantom? A clue? And just what was Chris’s Eureka moment? Later, he told me what was going through his head: He went from (1) looking at the cannonballs as reflective spheres that should show the direction of the light; to (2) looking for some kind of addition to the scene that might indicate the passage of time, e.g., a footprint,; and then, finally, to (3) looking at the valley as a bowl in which he could study an overall change in the lighting, The Eureka moment was, presumably, the moment he abandoned looking at lighting on the cannonballs and started looking at the overall pattern of the light on the landscape. – E.M.]

    CHRIS RUSS: I’ve got a proposal for you. Let’s say the person on the hill was chucking balls down toward the gully from the left. Later, they walked down and picked some up and put them in the road. That would account for why a number of rocks have been moved toward the right.

    ERROL MORRIS: The cannon balls could have been coming from camera right to left. Now, they could have overshot the road and then rolled down that hill —

    CHRIS RUSS: But where did the balls come from? There are some that are in OFF that are not in ON and vice versa.

    ERROL MORRIS: This could lead to madness, no?

    CHRIS RUSS: The cloud cover makes arguments about shadows ineffective. If the time of day was close together, you could get information here. But what if instead of 30 minutes, it was three hours? You know, it would certainly make sense that if they were the same caliber ball, then you could chuck them back. Say, for the sake of argument, Susan Sontag is right. Fenton posed one of the photographs, but she’s wrong about the order. They are in the reverse order to the one claimed by Sontag, Keller and Haworth-Booth, and established by Dennis Purcell. Consider this hypothetical -story. Fenton came across the balls on the road and scattered them on the hillside, thereby posing the picture that he took of the road without the balls on it [OFF]. (It doesn’t matter why he did it – just that he intended to do it. It wasn’t inadvertent.) But if OFF is posed, it’s posed because of the absence of something. The cannonballs should have, could have been there, but Fenton altered the landscape by removing them. Couldn’t you argue that every photograph is posed because every photograph excludes something? In every photograph something is absent. Someone has made a decision about what time-slice to expose on the emulsion, what space-slice to expose on the emulsion. Fenton could have had an elephant in the Valley of the Shadow of Death, plus all the workers you might imagine heaving cannonballs about. The elephant could have been walking through the frame of Fenton’s camera, and Fenton just waited until the elephant left the frame. Or told Sparling, his assistant: “Get rid of the elephant.” He posed the photograph, but how would you know? It’s posed not by the presence of the elephant but by its absence. Isn’t something always excluded, elephant or otherwise?

    How to proceed? Dennis has solved the problem. There is no way – at least as far as I can see – to wiggle out of his argument. And so, it turns out that Keller, Haworth-Booth and Sontag are right. It is OFF before ON. I tried hard to prove that Keller and Sontag were wrong – to prove that ON came before OFF. I failed. I can’t deny it. But I did prove that they were right for the wrong reasons. It is not their assessment of Fenton’s character or lack of character that establishes the order of the pictures. Nor is it sun-angle and shadow. Rather it is the motion of ancillary rocks – rocks that had been kicked, nudged, displaced between the taking of one picture and the other. Rocks that no one cared about. “Those little guys that got kicked aside.” Their displacement was recorded on those wet collodion plates not because someone wanted to record it. It happened inadvertently. Ancillary rocks, ancillary evidence – essential information.

    The one thing that we know about the rocks — Marmaduke, George, Lionel, Oswald and Fred — is that they were not posed. No one noticed them, let alone posed them. But together they helped unlock the secret of how to order the Fenton photographs.

    Photographs preserve information. They record data. They present evidence. Not because of our intentions but often in spite of them. Sontag, Keller and Haworth-Booth could be right about the order of the photographs, but nothing that they have said shows that one photograph – either ON or OFF – or both photographs were posed. We know nothing about any such thing. Chris Russ had noticed that the rocks were moving towards the road, but he didn’t take the next step. He didn’t see how gravity provided an independent path to reality – a path separate from sun-angle. Dennis steadfastly defends Chris Russ. He makes a comparison between a police detective and a prosecutor. It was Chris’s job to uncover the evidence, Dennis’s job to make the case before a jury.

    At the very end of my discussion with Roy Flukinger, he turned to the mystery of photographs – the mystery of recording light and shadow without telling us what they mean.

    ROY FLUKINGER: It’s one of the fascinating things about photo history. It always gives us more questions than answers. Historical photographs may give you the possibility of new facts, and may give you the chance to ask new questions. The thing I like about old photographs is that they offer me a different sort of visual, and hopefully, therefore, emotional experience to what I’m looking at, that words can’t do, or that words can only do part of.

    ERROL MORRIS: Something beyond language?

    ROY FLUKINGER: Definitely. There’s a whole other level that reaches a person through the eyes. And I just find that fascinating.

    ERROL MORRIS: How would you describe that “other level”?

    ROY FLUKINGER: Photography has a certain immediacy – not only in the taking, but also at the end of the equation, the presenting of the image to a viewer. There’s a strong chance for distortion of fact or for inadequate – no, not inadequate, shall we say, limited amount of experience. But there’s always something that’s possible to be revealed and shared in the process. If you sit down and really lay out these portfolios of prints on a table, and look at them for an afternoon – we’ve got a big wall easel here so we can lay out a bunch of them – and you move them around and look at them, you really get a sense of what it must have been like to be there – even a level of goose-bump experience. That’s the sort of power that photography can have. If you’re looking at the original prints the way the photographer printed them, and wanted to make them known, that possibility is enhanced, even beyond just what’s reproduced in a book.

    ERROL MORRIS: The feeling of being there?

    ROY FLUKINGER: At least a better sense of being there, a better sense of the emotional experience that went into the creation of the image. Fenton was there – freezing his butt off and getting in the mud and everything else. That’s something you experience when you look at the roughness of the prints and the detail of the imagery – that is, when you stop and look at what’s there in the photographs.

A safe crammed with documents, photographs in a hatbox, a packet of letters tied with a faded yellow-ribbon. I spoke with Dennis Purcell recently and asked, “Do you think these essays will put this issue – the issue of which came first – finally to rest.” Dennis replied, “No. I don’t think so. There could be some guy who reads your essays, writes in, and says: ‘You know, there aren’t just two photographs. I found another. There are actually three.’ ”
