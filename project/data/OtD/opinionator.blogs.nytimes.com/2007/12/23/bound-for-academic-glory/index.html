    Bound For Academic Glory?

    By STANLEY FISH
    December 23, 2007 5:19 pmFebruary 10, 2008 9:29 pm

    The preliminary report of the New York State Commission on Higher Education (established earlier this year by Governor Spitzer in an Executive Order ) has now been issued, and in many ways it is a remarkable and commendably ambitious document. It is also very badly written, although it must be acknowledged that the nature of these reports part cheerleading, part politicking, part policy making pretty much ensures that the prose will be less than elegant.

    In this case the bad writing takes two forms. First, there are the sentences made up of empty abstractions linked together in an awkward and strained syntax: The goal is to magnetize lost talent and ensure that students thrive and progress, in order to create new generations of innovators who will enable New York State to continue as one of the worlds idea capitals.

    And there are the sentences that actually say something, but in a prose so clotted and bureaucratic that it takes several readings to figure out what it is: … the Commission recommends campuses focus on the major transfer pathways, such that by the start of the 2010-11 academic year, articulation agreements are in place that specify how every comparable lower-division course can be transferred for credit, as prerequisite for more advanced courses in parallel programs and counted toward baccalaureate degree requirements at receiving institutions within the system.

    But once you get past the style (I can not resist noting one use of lagged as a transitive verb), there is more than a little substance and it is presented with an unusual candor: There is troubling evidence that the State has slipped in stature over the past twenty years. Responsibility for this slippage (not quite the right word; the New York higher education system has never had much stature in the first place) is distributed among several constituencies, including the legislature (Too little revenue has forced campuses to compromise academic quality), the central administration (Over-regulation pervades every aspect of SUNYs operation ) and the Board of Regents (…the Commission has concluded that further devolution of responsibility for day to day affairs to the …individual campuses… will likely enhance performance and achievement).

    The conclusion is straightforward and simple: “too little revenue, too little investment, and too much regulation.

    This is by no means a unique situation. In 2002 , Mark Yudoff, then president of the University of Minnesota, wrote an essay titled Is the Public Research University Dead? His main point was that the historic compact between state governments and public universities well support you with revenues in return for low tuition and broad access has been broken: …over the past 25 years , that agreement has withered, leaving public research institutions in a purgatory of insufficient resources and declining competitiveness.

    “Declining competitiveness means declining ability to compete with private institutions that certainly have their problems but are in general more in control of their funding and less hemmed in by burdensome regulations. As many have observed, the gap in resources between the publics and the privates is steadily increasing, and we seem to be moving to a two-tier system of higher education.

    This is not true of the public institutions to whose status New York and other states aspire UC Berkeley, UCLA, the University of Michigan, the University of Virginia. These state universities, and a few others, enjoy the luxury of decades and even centuries of support. Generations of faithful alumni have established special funds that sustain programs that might otherwise be cut. More often than not a good number of the loyal alumni populate the state legislature and are devoted to their alma maters athletic teams, a devotion many ordinary citizens share.

    None of this is true of the New York system, which is both huge (64 institutions) and young (dating from the mid-’60s). That youth does not insulate it from infra-structure problems. Many 40-year-old buildings that have not been properly maintained are in various states of decay (I saw this problem at the University of Illinois at Chicago, also of mid-’60s vintage), and the cost of what is euphemistically called deferred maintenance is estimated by the report to be in excess of $5 billlion, and growing every day.

    Not a pretty picture. Declining academic quality, a deterioration of the states share of national academic research and development dollars; a low percentage (relative to a national average) of full-time faculty members, inadequate (and again below average) stipends for graduate students, no low cost student loans, crumbling facilities, crippling oversight, political interference (implied, not stated). In short, a big, wealthy, powerful state with a public higher education system that is middling at best and less prestigious than the systems of smaller, poorer states (Wisconsin, Iowa, Georgia, Arizona, Minnesota, Kansas, Indiana, to name a few).

    And the remedy? Money — a lot of it.

    In addition to the $5 billion for deferred maintenance, the Commission calls for a $3 billion research fund, 4,000 additional graduate students supported by competitive stipends, and 2,000 new faculty, recruited from out of state and including 250 academic superstars. The bill will be astronomical, and even higher than the Commission thinks. A significant number of the new hires will be scientists who will require a start up package (laboratory space and special equipment). The report relies on 5-year-old statistics that put the price tag at $300,000 for an assistant professor in biology professor to nearly a million dollars for a senior chemistry professor. But those figures are low even by 2002 standards: $800,000 might get you a really promising assistant professor and a senior researcher will cost anywhere from $2 million to infinity.

    Two questions: Could it work, and will it be attempted?

    It might work if it is sustained, a word the Commissions report quite properly emphasizes. Any number of institutions, public and private, have attempted to raise their status and move into the category of world-class institutions, a status the Commission believes can be attained by the universities at Buffalo, Stony Brook, Albany and Binghamton (which would be the main beneficiaries of the reports proposals).

    But in almost every case, after a few years of enhanced revenues the legislatures attention turns elsewhere, and those who have been lured by the promise of massive transformation depart for the next state on the make. If, this time, the governor and the legislature were to sign on to the long-range plan of support outlined in the report, everyone in the academy would be paying attention and many would be thinking, Well, something good may be going on there.

    But where would you get 2,000 new faculty members? Given that the scholars you would be going after are productive and well-regarded (if they werent, there would be no point in wooing them), what you can count on is 2,000 bidding wars. And as for 250 academic superstars (not a term the report uses), there just arent 250 academic superstars, unless you engage in a form of grade inflation. But if the system managed to attract even 50 of this rare breed, it would make the right kind of news.

    So, yes, it could possibly work, although probably not to the extent of having Buffalo, Stonybrook, Albany and Binghamton join Berkeley and Michigan at the top of the heap. Will New York try it? That is a political question, and more than a local one. The decline in state support for higher education noted by Yudoff is in part a legacy of the 60’s. I remember as a fledgling member of the Berkeley faculty in 1962 being given special treatment by merchants. The university, known as Cal, was a source of community pride. Three years later , after the Free Speech movement and a wave of student protests across the country, I had to be careful not to identify myself as a university employee. The middle-class reaction to left politics on campus had already set in, and in the decades that followed it has become an orthodoxy.

    The Commission says nothing about this public relations problem, but appeals instead to state pride. Every idea entertained, the report says, was tested with a simple question: How does this compare with other states? More often than not, the answer was distressing, and the Commission is counting on New Yorkers to ask, Are we satisfied with a system of higher education that doesnt match up to what those other guys have? Of course, the answer may be yes, and if it is, the report of an official commission, bold and comprehensive though it may be, will end up being little more than a footnote in history.

    But even if, mirabile dictu, the Commissions proposals are implemented, the benefits will not be evenly distributed. In his 2002 essay, Yudoff observed that a university might succeed in competing in the academic market, but still lose hold of what makes it what it is: … to remain a great learning institution, it will have to continue to nurture learning for its own sake… and foster other less quantifiable and profitable but still valuable features of the university. Theres nothing like that in the report. All the emphasis is on science, partnerships with industry and advances in technology. If there is to be a brave new world in New York higher education, it doesnt look as if the humanities and the arts will be a significant part of it.

    Stanley Fish, New York State, public universities

    Linda Kaboolian January 4, 2008 · 12:16 pm

    The story of support and quality is much more complicated that presented in Fish’s commentary. I write as a proud recipient of public education: kindergarten through PhD. and a faculty member for 20 years at an ivy league university. Explaining the distress of public higher education requires a more nuanced political and institutional analysis than Fish provides. When I graduated from SUNY-Albany, nearly 10 years after the Free Speech Movement, tuition was waived for Regents Scholars and nominal for everyone else. CUNY tuition was still free. Given the rapidity of legislative action, there was more than enough time for negative reaction to the student movements of the 60′s to effect funding.

    The real declines in support began later. Like all other public services since the Reagan revolution, public universities, especially the young, non-land grant systems such as SUNY, are suffering from divestment of tax revenues and general demeaning of all public institutions. This is particularly hurtful when, at the same time private institutions are currying tax-deductible donations from their affluent alums and watching their endowments swell in the exuberant investment markets. Private institutions are capable of using these resources to bid up the price on star faculty and provide amenities that add to the impression that they are worth mortgaging the family home to attend. Public universities, late comers to the donations game, are on a steep learning curve — one that I have had the opportunity to witness as I advise my own alma mater on its new fund raising initiatives.

    The truly excellent public research universities, such as Michigan, are suffering not only from diminished state support but also from the loss of the federal research dollars, which helped to support infrastructure as well operations.

    The support and quality of institutions of public higher education will only improve when their constituencies see research and education as crucial to the economic future of their state and themselves. Until then, detractors should be reminded that nearly every K-12 school teacher and a vast percentage of doctors and engineers, are now educated in public institutions — so even if they never attend, they are benefitiing from their investment.
