    Did My Brother Invent E-Mail With Tom Van Vleck? (Part Five)

    By ERROL MORRIS
    June 23, 2011 9:00 pmJune 29, 2011 1:19 pm

    Errol Morris on photography.

    This is part five of a five-part series. Parts 1 | 2 | 3 | 4

    5.

    A VERY SMART SOCIAL SCIENTIST

    The strongest impacts of an emergent technology are always unanticipated. You can’t know what people are going to do until they get their hands on it and start using it on a daily basis, using it to make a buck and using it for criminal purpose and all the different things that people do.

    William Gibson, interviewed in The Paris Review, Art of Fiction #211

    Courtesy of the Department of Special Collections, University of Wisconsin-Madison.

    Robert Fano was born in Turin in 1917. He is Jewish and came to the United States in 1939 because of Mussolinis anti-Jewish legislation passed the previous year. [33] [34] He is now 93 years old and has been a professor at MIT for over 60 years. I called him not knowing what to expect, but he is as sharp as a tack. [35] (I am reminded of Bertrand Russells response when he was asked by an interviewer about reports that he might be senile. He was in his mid-90s at the time. Russells response was, If Im senile, I guess I would be the last to know.)

    ERROL MORRIS: How are you?

    ROBERT FANO: Surviving.

    ERROL MORRIS: Quite clearly.

    Our conversation turned inevitably to the origins of time-sharing.

    Interactive Feature

    Try writing some code and then send an e-mail from 1965.

    ROBERT FANO: I’m trying to think briefly how to put it. There were two ideas. One was Lickliders vision of man-computer symbiosis. The other was John McCarthys vision of a computer utility analogous to an electric-power utility that allowed individuals to access a computer at an affordable cost. Now, that started activities at MIT in developing a time-sharing system. Fernando J. Corbat, best known as Corby, led the implementation of CTSS, Compatible Time-sharing System. I was involved just on the periphery of all this. And when Licklider went to Washington in 1962 as head of the computer section at ARPA, he obviously wanted to fund a big project on the subject. He visited various universities, but it was MIT that had the CTSS available. I believed that computer science was going to be an important new discipline and such a project would provide a great opportunity to get started. Unfortunately, at that time there was no senior faculty interested and knowledgeable about computers. At a certain point, I decided, if nobody was ready to get the ball rolling, I was going to do it, although my experience with computers was close to nil.

    ERROL MORRIS: Nil?!

    ROBERT FANO: Yes. Now, that led to Project MAC. Do you want to know the origin of the name? I created that name overnight. The first staff member applying for a parking permit was asked, “What are you going to work on?” And he said, “I don’t know, there’s no name for it.” So that evening I decided on Project MAC. I could not use the word “laboratory.” That’s why “project” was used. When I wanted to change the name to “laboratory,” there was opposition from the higher-ups, because they didn’t believe that computers were here to stay.

    ERROL MORRIS: My brother was getting a degree in electrical engineering because there was no degree in computer science.

    ROBERT FANO: I know. I started it.

    ERROL MORRIS: You started it?

    MIT MuseumRobert Fano

    ROBERT FANO: That’s right. When I started Project MAC, I wanted at the same time to build up computer science as part of the electrical engineering department. It was a double mission. First, thats where the money was. Second, thats where the future was. And eventually, the higher-ups understood that computer science was going to stay. And over the years, the curriculum in computer science kept developing. So much so that in 1971, after I stopped being director of Project MAC, I was asked to be the first associate department head for computer science in the electrical engineering department. A little later, a new department head changed the name of the department from electrical engineering to electrical engineering and computer science. And shortly thereafter, Project Mac became LCS, the Laboratory for Computer Science.

    MIT Museum

    ERROL MORRIS: This whole idea of time-sharing, why did it become so important? Was there a certain moment where it became evident that computers were being used in the wrong way?

    ROBERT FANO: The person that really invented time-sharing, technically speaking, was McCarthy. Because of his frustration with batch-processing. McCarthy was trying to find a way out of this frustration. And he thought of the idea of time-sharing. Now, he also thought of a new terminology that gave a role to time-sharing. He coined the name computer utility. It was something parallel to an electric utility. It gave me an image of the future. That’s why I thought it was very important, the name.

    ERROL MORRIS: It gave you an image of the future? What kind of image?

    ROBERT FANO: The image of people benefiting from personal access to computers, access that was convenient and affordable. To have that, time-sharing was essential.

    ERROL MORRIS: And computer terminals.

    ROBERT FANO: Yes, the computer was connected through telephone lines to terminals. We had terminals all over the MIT campus. People could also use CTSS from other locations through the teletype network. CTSS was capable of serving about 20 people at a time without their being aware of one another. But they could also communicate with each other. A whole different view of computers was generated.

    ERROL MORRIS: How so?

    ROBERT FANO: Before CTSS, people wrote programs for themselves. The idea of writing programs for somebody else to use was totally alien. With CTSS, programs and data stored could be stored in the common memory segment and they were available to the whole community. And that really took off. At a certain point, I started seeing the whole thing as a system that included the knowledge of the community. It was a completely new view. It was a remarkable event. In retrospect, I wish I had gotten a very smart social psychologist on the premises to look at and interpret what was happening to the community, because it was just unbelievable.

    ERROL MORRIS: Because it was a social phenomenon?

    ROBERT FANO: Yes. There was a community of people using the computer. They got to know each other through it. You could send an e-mail to somebody through the system. It was a completely new phenomenon.

    ERROL MORRIS: But was this envisioned at the outset or was it because once you got all those terminals hooked up and you got all of those people using the machine, that all of these other things suddenly just ?

    ROBERT FANO: The technical aspects were carefully planned, and the social aspect just happened. CTSS resulted in a community of people working together, they were part of a new entity. Today you see groups of people forming on the Web. That was the first event of that kind.

    ERROL MORRIS: Partnerships?

    ROBERT FANO: Yes, it was a marvelous phenomenon. Very exciting. This is why I should have had a smart psychologist on the premises to note what was happening and make sense out of it. But, as a non-psychologist, I knew that they were very happy. It was a social thing. It belongs to the users. And all sorts of phenomena happened on the side, some of them kind of strange. First of all, hackers appeared.

    ERROL MORRIS: Really?

    ROBERT FANO: Oh yes, and I had to deal with them. Computer resources were divided among the users, computer time and storage space were allocated to each user. Some users wanted more resources, so they stole them, by illegally changing counters. Just all sorts of human phenomena took place.

    ERROL MORRIS: Also, weren’t there all kinds of privacy issues?

    ROBERT FANO: Well, first of all, there were people that didn’t believe in privacy. The artificial intelligence community believed that one should be able to look at all the affairs of the others, but we did not. Corbat specifically did not, and had arranged to provide privacy through the use of passwords, just like today. The trouble was that the hackers found ways of getting around them, just like today. We haven’t solved that problem yet. It started then.

    ERROL MORRIS: Did you know who the hackers were? Were they identifiable?

    ROBERT FANO: In one case somebody found a way of inserting a message into the system, requesting a reply from management. And it was signed, “I did it. Ill tell you how I did it.” He was obviously proud of his accomplishment. My reply was, Come immediately to my office. I had to read him the law. He was a freshman, a very smart guy, and hes still around the lab. But I had to deal with problems like these.

    ERROL MORRIS: Was he the first hacker?

    ROBERT FANO: No, no, he was not the first. There were others before him. The word “hacker” originated with MIT undergraduates long before that. Undergraduates were well known for doing things like putting a car on the MIT dome, by taking it apart and then remounting it there. Such activities were known as “hacks and the people involved were called hackers. Getting into a computer system was originally regarded as a hack.

    MIT Hacks

    ERROL MORRIS: Hacking, word processing, e-mail … Also, the Internet.

    ROBERT FANO: Yes. Project MAC was an early step toward the Internet. It had a central memory where things were stored, telephone lines all over the United States, usable e-mail and many other characteristics.

    ERROL MORRIS: The ’60s must have been a very exciting time?

    ROBERT FANO: Oh, yes. Lots of things happened. It had a great social impact. Something new was created. Now, of course, the whole situation changed with the advent of personal computers. In a time-sharing system the computing capacity was provided by a large central computer installation. Information was also stored there. Users just had terminals capable of communicating with that central computer, primarily through text messages. Personal computers moved both the computation capacity and the stored information to the periphery, with telephone lines and related equipment linking the personal computers. That’s how the Internet started out. You’ve heard of cloud computing?

    ERROL MORRIS: Yes. Its the current thing.

    ROBERT FANO: Its equivalent to recentralizing the computing capacity like it had been with time-sharing systems. The personal computers are tools for accessing that, theyre the terminals.

    ERROL MORRIS: Are we returning to the world before Project MAC?

    ROBERT FANO: Chips have changed the world and put the power in a lot of different places. And now the chips are so cheap that you can have thousands of them working together. What is missing still is the technique to really utilize them effectively.

    ERROL MORRIS: Well, I’m delighted to talk to you.

    ROBERT FANO: People ask me, “What are you doing today?” And the answer is, “Not much, but it still takes all my time.”

    *******

    The issue of who did what, when is a complicated one. Inevitably, there are cart-before-the-horse arguments. Which was invented first, the axle or the wheel? Dont you need an axle to make a wheel function properly? And yet, electronic mail was clearly part of CTSS and Project MAC.

    ERROL MORRIS: Did you realize at the time how important this invention would be?

    TOM VAN VLECK: We invented lots of stuff, little and big, including something called MAIL in 1965. But the day we got MAIL to work was not as memorable as the day, say, when we got Multics to boot, or the first Multics picnic, or lots of other good days.

    ERROL MORRIS: You also said its not important to you who was first. But you know that you and Noel invented e-mail. There is this other guy, right, Ray Tomlinson, who in the early 70s sent e-mail between computers connected to the ARPANET, correct?

    TOM VAN VLECK: Right.

    ERROL MORRIS: Tell me about Tomlinson. Have you ever talked to him?

    TOM VAN VLECK: I have never talked to him. We have exchanged e-mail. We have mutual friends. Hes a good guy. People had said, “Geez! We want to be able to do this.” And he actually sat down and did it. Just like Noel and I did. There were people saying, “There should be a way to send mail between computers over this network we are building.

    ERROL MORRIS: If I understand this correctly and I probably dont the difference between what he did and what you and Noel did is that you were working on one mainframe. Whereas he was communicating between two computers. Is that a bad way of describing it?

    TOM VAN VLECK: No, its a good start. When Noel and I were doing it, there really was only one computer we could use. There was only one time-sharing computer, and so thats what we did.

    ERROL MORRIS: Right.

    TOM VAN VLECK: So there have been discussions about when is it e-mail. Some people say what Noel and I did wasnt really e-mail.

    ERROL MORRIS: Why not?

    TOM VAN VLECK: Because it didnt go between computers.

    ERROL MORRIS: Ah.

    TOM VAN VLECK: But my thought is, I walk into my office. I sit down at my terminal. I type a message and say, Send. And someone else in some other room sits down at his terminal and reads it. Who cares how many computers there are? Who cares whether the computers are owned by the same agency or different agencies? That was another argument. It isnt e-mail unless it is between separate administrative domains. You can argue the semantics of this back and forth as much as you want and finally, I dont think it matters. Ray is a great guy. Hes done lots of good things. And the code that he wrote was one more necessary step.

    ERROL MORRIS: But is it substantially different from what you and my brother did?

    TOM VAN VLECK: It was the same thing. He was faced with an idea and a blank sheet of paper, and he wrote a program that put that into practice. He had to deal with different kinds of computers. He had to deal with the networking stuff. He had to invent the rules and protocols that made it work. It was no easier or harder. He didnt use any piece of our code. He reinvented it from scratch. So it was just the same thing. That is what programming is.

    ERROL MORRIS: Was he aware of the work that you and Noel had done?

    TOM VAN VLECK: Oh, sure he was. Everybody said, Well, gee, Ray did this great thing. He sent electronic mail from one computer to another. But how did they know it was electronic mail? The TENEX [the operating system for the DEC PDP-10] machines that he used all had internal electronic mail just like CTSS had.

    ERROL MORRIS: Really.

    TOM VAN VLECK: Sure. TENEX had its own, internal mail command. What he did was to figure out how to break out of one box and get it to another box, using the new ARPANET.

    ERROL MORRIS: But of course, when you were doing it, there was no ARPANET. There was just the one mainframe.

    TOM VAN VLECK: Thats right. There was no place else to send the mail.

    *******

    ERROL MORRIS: You have become the historian of Multics.
    But when did you get interested in doing this?

    TOM VAN VLECK: It started gradually. When CTSS was shut down [in 1965], they were going to have this ceremony. And so they asked me to put together a list of who had contributed to CTSS. And so I made up this list and then I said, You know, gee, it might be fun to keep a list like that about Multics. And so I made one up at the time that listed names and what they did. I left the Multics project in 1981. It was kicking around. I brought it with me when I went. And when the World Wide Web came along I thought, This is the tool. And so I just started writing down what I remembered. And there was a way to put it online and there was a way to put pictures in. And so I just kept doing it. Thats all. And so the Multics Web site was first put up in 1994, when the Web was just barely beginning. And Ive just added a piece here and a piece there and asked other people to throw in their pieces and their recollections.

    ERROL MORRIS: Im really indebted to you for all of this.

    TOM VAN VLECK: Well, its a way of remembering a lot of good times and good people. The people are the most important thing for me.

    ERROL MORRIS: And Corbat?

    TOM VAN VLECK: We did a lot of this without his permission, as it were. From his point of view, e-mail was just a little thing. It was a thing done by two of his people and was kind of a waste of time

    ERROL MORRIS: Really?!

    TOM VAN VLECK: But he let it happen. He let it happen because he was a good boss and knew when not to argue. And the fact that it has spread out and become useful, Im sure hes glad. But it wasnt the main ring in the circus. You know what I mean? He was changing the world. Now, e-mail has had a lot of play but it probably would have happened, even if Noel and I had been told, No. Dont do that. Get back to work. E-mail still would have happened because it was in the air. It was an idea whose time had come.

    EPILOGUE

    … one of the things you can learn from history is that men have learned to live with machinery at least as well as, and probably a good deal better than, they have learned to live with one another.

    E. E. Morison, from Computers and the World of the
    Future

    Gaudi’s Model of the Sagrada Familia, 1898

    Van Vleck asked me in an e-mail if I had ever visited the Sagrada Familia in Barcelona. He wrote, It is a cathedral that has been under construction for over 100 years and is still only half done. Gaudi, the church’s architect, used ideas and approaches that were years ahead of others. But he died before it was finished. Working on CTSS and Multics was like working on a cathedral. We laid our bricks and knew that others might later replace them with better bricks. We believed in the cause even if we didn’t completely understand the implications. But was there a master architect in the 60s? Did things happen that could not have been anticipated? That were not part of any plan? There was time-sharing, but as Van Vleck emphasized: The idea of time sharing was to make one big computer look like a lot of different little computers that were completely unconnected to each other. But it turned out that what people really liked about time-sharing was the ability to share data.

    In 1961, there was a conference at MIT to celebrate its 100th anniversary. [36] (It was later collected in a book, Computers and the World of Tomorrow.) An impressive array of participants, including Robert Fano, Norbert Wiener, Claude Shannon and J. C. R. Licklider a veritable Whos Who of the emerging science of computers and computation spoke on time-sharing, information management, and artificial intelligence. I was particularly moved by the words of Elting E. Morison, a humanities professor at MIT, who spoke about men, machines and the uncertainties of existence. Can computers help us to control a world that is seemingly out of our control?

    Morison was writing about the persistent human temptation to make life more explicable by making it more calculable, to put experience into some logical scheme that, by its order and niceness, will make what happens seem more understandable, analysis more bearable, decision simpler. Morison is posing a problem. Yes, computers can make life more calculable, but can they make it more explicable? It was my mothers lament at my brothers funeral. She said she was able to help her son my brother with almost everything. But she was not able to help him out of the woods that terrible morning.

    *******

    [33] Italy passed its racial laws, the Manifesto of Race, in July, 1938, excluding Jews from the civil service, the armed forces, restricting Jewish ownership of certain companies and property; and prohibiting intermarriage.

    ERROL MORRIS: What were the circumstances that forced your family out of Italy?

    ROBERT FANO: Well, we were a Jewish family. And Mussolini started making laws against us. And it was pretty clear there was going to be a war. So we left. And the only one who was not inclined to leave was my father [a professor of mathematics at the University of Turin]. But we got him out, too. I could not get him into the United States. Because he told me before I left, Dont force me to come to the United States. I will not go to a country that is likely to be at war with Italy.

    ERROL MORRIS: Oh.

    ROBERT FANO: And he was not the only person of that generation that felt that way. Many did. These were people that were attached to the country. My grandfather volunteered under Garibaldi to fight against the Austrians. That was the way, and many people felt that way. You know, there was no anti-Semitism in Italy before the War. But wen I came to the United States, I was amazed by the amount of anti-Semitism. The fact that the country was divided. There were the Jewish and non-Jewish doctors, and the hospitals were separate. Thats why there was the Jewish Hospital in Boston [Beth-Israel].

    ERROL MORRIS: Anti-Semitism in Boston, not in Italy.

    ROBERT FANO: Yes. I didnt expect it. That Jews were not welcome was totally alien to me. In Italy the Jewish population was totally mixed with the Italian population. Thats why the laws against Jews were not sustained by the Italian people. And they helped a lot of Jewish people to survive.

    [34] From the Department of Special Collections, Memorial Library, University of WisconsinMadison: As the introduction announces, the first book of Fascism is a manual accessible to all to make known our Revolution, the Party, the Regime, and Mussolini’s State. A short and concise history of Fascism is followed by questions and answers about the Duce, the Fascist revolution, the party, the army, the corporate state, and the defense of Italian purity. The chapter on the defense of the race explicitly excludes Jews from being considered of pure ancestral Italian blood and spells out government measures taken against them. Jews are accused, not without justification, of having led international efforts against Fascism.
