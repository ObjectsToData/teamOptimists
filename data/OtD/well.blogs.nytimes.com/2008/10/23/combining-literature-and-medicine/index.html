    Combining Literature and Medicine

    By TARA PARKER-POPE
    October 23, 2008 3:28 pmOctober 23, 2008 3:29 pm
    Essays and poems are part of Dr. Richard Panush’s rounds at Saint Barnabas Medical Center. (Aaron Houston for The New York Times)

    As a health and medical writer, I have been fascinated to learn about the role writing, and reading, can play in medicine and better patient care.

    That’s the issue explored by Dr. Pauline Chen in this week’s “Doctor and Patient” column, where she writes about how literary training can be an essential part of medical training.

    Dr. Chen writes: “There is now emerging evidence that exposure to literature and writing during residency training can influence how young doctors approach their clinical work.

    “‘We’re teaching the humanities to our residents, and it’s making them better doctors,’ said Dr. Richard Panush, a rheumatologist and chairman of the department of medicine at Saint Barnabas Medical Center.

    “The idea of combining literature and medicine — or narrative medicine as it is sometimes called — has been part of medical education for over 40 years. Studies have repeatedly shown that such literary training can strengthen and support the compassionate instincts of doctors.”

    Click here to read the full column, “Stories in the Service of Making a Better Doctor.” And please join Dr. Chen in the discussion by posting your comments below.

    Elizabeth Barrett November 1, 2008 · 5:37 pm

    People, especially practicing physicians, forget that medicine is an art at much as a science. This presents a major problem when trying to get doctors to diagnose and treat according to “statistics,” rather than using their intuition and understanding in addition to the statistics. My experience is that what may “cure”/help one person can literally kill another. By making room for that (both mentally and physically) when approaching patients, we will greatly reduce dissatisfaction — and lawsuits..

    Fred Schert, MD November 3, 2008 · 12:29 am

    Medical school was one of the saddest times of my life. I had come from places where doing your best was praised but found in medical school that you were expected to do the work and the was the end of the matter. No pats on the back until well on in residency and even those were few. The joy came from mastering the material and learning bit by bit to help make people’s lives better, if it was possible to make them better, and learning to know the difference.

    I have found that most of my friends were in classes the year before or behind my own. They posed less competitive threat to me. I got up the courage to call my first year room mate the other day. We seemed distant but made plans to get together if either was visiting the general area of the other. Our school tends to have a geographically diverse group of alumni.

    Our medical school dean of admissions gave our class periodic lectures our freshman year explaining his MD-psychiatrist-Methodist Minister’s take on the process of medical education. Basically, as I remember his lectures, med school was meant to induce a depression that would be treated by learning to actually do the physical and mental things that doctors must do to help patients. Without inducing the depression, we would not be able to make the mental/emotional transition necessary to take patients’ lives into our hands.

    He cautioned us not to marry someone during our sophomore year that we hadn’t known for a long time, for our depression would likely cause us to make a different choice than what we would have otherwise made.

    His favorite statement was that to whom much is given, much is expected. I still think of his words today.

    I remember feeling so guilty that if I didn’t remember every fact given us to learn that I would likely hurt someone, not an unreasonable emotion. I have learned that you remember the facts you need to know by doing the work of caring for patients. We were told that medicine is a ruthless mistress that will never be satisfied. I believe that grieving over the loss of innocence of “just wanting to help people” is what motivates me to keep learning 35 years after graduation.

    There is an inseparable link between the arts and the medical sciences. Before one can postulate (diagnose), one must observe. To know what you are observing you must know a body of scientific facts. But observation is learned in the arts, especially when observing human behavior. Perfecting the art of observing human behavior, I think, makes medicine more art that science.

    Well Arts Institute November 3, 2008 · 6:18 pm

    To respond to R Carter…”does anyone know where to find good stories…”

    The Well Arts Institute in Portland Oregon (non profit) has been using storytelling and theater to help people heal from chronic illness and life threatening challenges for several years.

    We begin by choosing a focus (cancer, post traumatic stress, MS, juvenile diabetes, ect..). We work with local organizations/institutions to find people living with the illness willing to share their story. We team these people with professional writers, whom over an 8-week creative writing process, craft their personal story. We then partner these “writers” with professional actors. These actors help develop the story for the stage. The culminating event is a theater production with the community as an audience. The writers get to hear and watch their stories be told. For the writer, it is cathartic. For the audience it is deeply moving. And now I understand, that we need to reach out to the medical profession as well.

    http://www.wellarts.org (for more info)

    Richard Smith November 6, 2008 · 4:15 am

    There seems to be widespread agreement that increasing specialisation and use of technology has caused something precious to be lost from medicine–we might call it the “soul.” Narrative medicine is one way to try and recover that soul.

    As a small contribution I am editing an online, open access journal called Cases Journal (http://www.casesjournal.com/home/), where we are willing to publish all case reports that are “authentic, ethical and complete enough.” They don’t have to be “original and important,” the usual criteria for publication in a journal.

    I tried in an editorial to explain why we are doing this: “Health care is in some ways nothing more than an accumulation of case reports just as a population is a collection of people. And just as every person is important and different so is every case – even when “it’s just another sore throat”. Indeed, part of the idea is to get away from seeing a morning surgery in general practice or an afternoon’s list of men undergoing vasectomy as drudgery or factory work. Clinicians have the privilege of dealing with individuals in all their complexity and magnificence. Every person, every “case” can teach us something. We challenge contributors to draw out the lessons – but they don’t have to succeed in order to be published because readers may see what they can’t and there will be value in the mass that may not be immediately apparent in the single case.
    Perhaps some of our contributors will attempt to write case histories like those of Sigmund Freud. To catch the full mystery, excitement, and interest of a case may demand literary skills, and we know that many clinicians and even more patients have such skills. Jean Cocteau asked his doctor why he continued to attend him day and night at his home and the answer was that the doctors learnt from Cocteau because of his ability to describe his symptoms [9]. Cocteau produced a quote that is directly relevant to this endeavour: “Take a commonplace, clean it and polish it, light it so that it produces the same effect of youth and freshness and originality and spontaneity as it did originally, and you have done a poet’s job. The rest is literature”.

    Joaquin Paone, MD November 16, 2008 · 7:05 pm

    Humanizing medicine has been a critical part of the “art” of medical practice. Increasing dependence on laboratory diagnosis without a thorough history and physical examination is becoming the dominant diagnostic methodology. Why? Because medical teachers want to uncover every potential patient malady within the first hour.How many students and residents are taught how to perform these skills, percuss a chest, examine the mouth & oropharynx for example. The arts of conversation and real physical contact with the patient are rapidly disappearing. Can today’s residents & graduates interpret a Gram stain? Do they know what that is? Medical practice needs to be “touchy-feely”for the dual benefits to both the patient and the doctor.
    I recently was taken to a very good local emergency room for urgent evaluation of seizures related to medication reaction. No neurologic exam was done by the attending physician. Instead I was whisked off to CT to look at my head. Only the nurse asked me about medications I was taking and subsequently made the diagnosis.If I as a doc get that kind of evaluation it’s one thing because I know the drill. What about the ignorant poor and uneducated unprotected from lazy & incompetent physicians? I honestly believe I could make a good living attending patients & protecting them from their care-givers & hospitals..a sort of “rent-a-doc” deal. Maybe I’ll just do it for free.

    Pip Hardy December 8, 2008 · 11:04 am

    I have been fascinated – and delighted – to read Dr Chen’s article and subsequent responses.

    It is not only the creation and reading of ‘literature’ that can help make better doctors (and other health professionals).

    We have been working with patients, family carers, clinicians and healthcare managers to help them present their stories of healthcare as short (two or three minutes) ‘digital stories’. Small groups of storytellers spend several days in a carefully faciliated workshop that combines elements of creative writing, art therapy, and just enough technology to enable them to create their own mini-movies.

    They begin by drafting a personal story, informed by all the elements of good stories identified by Aristotle some 2500 years ago. This story becomes the script they record. The voiceover is then combined with pictures, ideally from the family photo album, often supplemented by additional images, occasionally augmented by a small snippet of video and topped off with some carefully chosen music. The resulting tapestry is an extremely effective (and affective) way to convey what really matters to those receiving and those delivering healthcare.

    All stories, once approved (witn informed consent) by storytellers, are released via a publicly accessible website, where they can be used, for free, for the purposes of healthcare education and quality improvement, provided that they are not changed or sold.

    Over the past five years, nearly 300 stories have been recorded. Storytellers tell us that they value the opportunity to be creative, to find meaning in their stories and, as Dr Charon says, to step outside their experience, walk around it and see what sense can be made of it. Healthcare educators, as well as clinicians and managers value the stories for their brevity, the authenticity and their versatility – they are a remarkable teaching resource.

    Despite protestations from various groups, it is our observation that ‘what unites us is greater than what divides us’. Sharing our stories is, after all, what makes us human. One well-told story has the potential to teach us as much about humanity and compassion as a whole semester of ‘the humanities’.

    As for who should teach and who should tell these stories, I happen to have a first degree in English Literature, a second in lifelong learning and qualifications in counselling and group work. I am not a clinician and have limited experience of being a patient. But I do have stories to tell…. however it was not until someone encouraged me to tell a two-minute personal story, with pictures, that I found a voice of my own. It has been a great priviledge to offer that possibility to many people whose stories would otherwise go unheard.

    In our experience, it is not necessary to be a ‘great’ writer. Some of the most powerful stories we have heard are from people who have protested that they can’t write, or have no story to tell.

    It is only necessary to have the courage to tell a personal story, from the heart, to be willing to work on that story to find its essence, and then to share it with others so that all may share in what one of our storytellers called ‘our own, all-too-common humanity.’

    The stories can be seen at http://www.patientvoices.org.uk
    Please watch them, listen to them, learn about your patients, your colleagues, your families, yourself.

    Pip Hardy
    Cambridge, England

    Lionel Jacobson December 9, 2008 · 12:47 pm

    Here in Cardiff, Wales I teach with a number of colleagues on an optional Medicine, Literature and Culture course to a group of 8 self-selected intersted 3rd year medical students. My colleagues and I are all attached to the Department of Primary Care and Public Health (Family Medicine) at Cardiff University.

    None of us has particular expertise in literature but we use our seminars to provoke thought, reflection and improved consideration of our own professional roles, and those the students will have some years hence.

    The seminars are fun, stimulating and educationally helpful even without the presence of ‘experts’ in Literature because we want to stmiulate discussion and not arcane thought on Literary style.

    We have had positive feedback on the course on each of the years it has been run, and it has been going now since the year 2000. I thought that I would give a positive feedback report on the article and that this is demonstrating that such courses, education and discussion are helpful in helping to understand patients and doctors from a holistic standpoint.
