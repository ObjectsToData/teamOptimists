    The Last Professor

    By STANLEY FISH
    January 18, 2009 10:00 pmJanuary 23, 2009 5:03 pm

    In previous columns and in a recent book I have argued that higher education, properly understood, is distinguished by the absence of a direct and designed relationship between its activities and measurable effects in the world.

    This is a very old idea that has received periodic re-formulations. Here is a statement by the philosopher Michael Oakeshott that may stand as a representative example: “There is an important difference between learning which is concerned with the degree of understanding necessary to practice a skill, and learning which is expressly focused upon an enterprise of understanding and explaining.”

    Understanding and explaining what? The answer is understanding and explaining anything as long as the exercise is not performed with the purpose of intervening in the social and political crises of the moment, as long, that is, as the activity is not regarded as instrumental valued for its contribution to something more important than itself.

    This view of higher education as an enterprise characterized by a determined inutility has often been challenged, and the debates between its proponents and those who argue for a more engaged university experience are lively and apparently perennial. The question such debates avoid is whether the Oakeshottian ideal (celebrated before him by Aristotle, Kant and Max Weber, among others) can really flourish in todays educational landscape. It may be fun to argue its merits (as I have done), but that argument may be merely academic in the pejorative sense of the word if it has no support in the real world from which it rhetorically distances itself. In todays climate, does it have a chance?

    In a new book, “The Last Professors: The Corporate University and the Fate of the Humanities,” Frank Donoghue (as it happens, a former student of mine) asks that question and answers No.

    Donoghue begins by challenging the oft-repeated declaration that liberal arts education in general and the humanities in particular face a crisis, a word that suggests an interruption of a normal state of affairs and the possibility of restoring the natural order of things.

    Such a vision of restored stability,” says Donoghue, “is a delusion because the conditions to which many seek a return healthy humanities departments populated by tenure-track professors who discuss books with adoring students in a cloistered setting have largely vanished. Except in a few private wealthy universities (functioning almost as museums), the splendid and supported irrelevance of humanist inquiry for its own sake is already a thing of the past. In two or three generations, Donoghue predicts, humanists . . . will become an insignificant percentage of the countrys university instructional workforce.

    How has this happened? According to Donoghue, its been happening for a long time, at least since 1891, when Andrew Carnegie congratulated the graduates of the Pierce College of Business for being fully occupied in obtaining a knowledge of shorthand and typewriting rather than wasting time upon dead languages.

    Industrialist Richard Teller Crane was even more pointed in his 1911 dismissal of what humanists call the life of the mind. No one who has a taste for literature has the right to be happy because the only men entitled to happiness . . . are those who are useful.

    The opposition between this view and the view held by the heirs of Matthew Arnolds conviction that poetry will save us could not be more stark. But Donoghue counsels us not to think that the two visions are locked in a struggle whose outcome is uncertain. One vision, rooted in an ethic of productivity and efficiency, has, he tells us, already won the day; and the proof is that in the very colleges and universities where the life of the mind is routinely celebrated, the material conditions of the workplace are configured by the business model that scorns it.

    The best evidence for this is the shrinking number of tenured and tenure-track faculty and the corresponding rise of adjuncts, part-timers more akin to itinerant workers than to embedded professionals.

    Humanities professors like to think that this is a temporary imbalance and talk about ways of redressing it, but Donoghue insists that this development, planned by no one but now well under way, cannot be reversed. Universities under increasing financial pressure, he explains, do not hire the most experienced teachers, but rather the cheapest teachers. Tenured and tenure-track teachers now make up only 35 percent of the pedagogical workforce and this number is steadily falling.

    Once adjuncts are hired to deal with an expanding student body (and the student body is always expanding), budgetary planners find it difficult to dispense with the savings they have come to rely on; and as a result, an adjunct workforce, however imperceptible its origins . . . has now mushroomed into a significant fact of academic life.

    What is happening in traditional universities where the ethos of the liberal arts is still given lip service is the forthright policy of for-profit universities, which make no pretense of valuing what used to be called the higher learning.” John Sperling, founder of the group that gave us Phoenix University, is refreshingly blunt: Coming here is not a rite of passage. We are not trying to develop value systems or go in for that expand their minds” nonsense.

    The for-profit university is the logical end of a shift from a model of education centered in an individual professor who delivers insight and inspiration to a model that begins and ends with the imperative to deliver the information and skills necessary to gain employment.

    In this latter model , the mode of delivery a disc, a computer screen, a video hook-up doesnt matter so long as delivery occurs. Insofar as there are real-life faculty in the picture, their credentials and publications (if they have any) are beside the point, for they are just delivery people.

    Sperling understands the difficulty of achieving accreditation for his institution as a proxy for cultural battles between defenders of 800 years of educational (and largely religious) traditions, and innovation that was based on the ideas of the marketplace transparency, efficiency, productivity and accountability.

    Those ideas have now triumphed (Carnegie and Crane are victorious), and this means, Donoghue concludes, that all fields deemed impractical, such as philosophy, art history, and literature, will henceforth face a constant danger of being deemed unnecessary.” And as a corollary professors will come to be seen by everyone (not just those outside the academy) as unaffordable anomalies.

    In his preface, Donoghue tells us that he will offer nothing in the way of uplifting solutions to the problems [he] describes. In the end, however, he cant resist recommending something and he advises humanists to acquire a thorough familiarity with how the university works, for only by studying the institutional histories of scholarly research, of tenure, of academic status, and . . . of the ever-changing college curriculum, can we prepare ourselves for the future.

    But and this is to his credit he doesnt hold out the slightest hope that this future we may come to understand will have a place in it for us.

    People sometimes believe that they were born too late or too early. After reading Donoghues book, I feel that I have timed it just right, for it seems that I have had a career that would not have been available to me had I entered the world 50 years later. Just lucky, I guess.

    Stanley Fish, Humanities, universities

    Chris L January 19, 2009 · 10:49 am

    I find something in the tone here disturbing. Isn’t one of the values of cultivating insight the ability to see the merits of all sides to a problem? If Fish were young again, and required to make his way in the world at large, I believe he would have a more nuanced view of the constant interaction of education and utility.

    That said, I think there are a few misconceptions in what he’s floating here. First, I think we need to acknowledge that there has been a tremendous expansion in the size of the student population, predominantly by a ’70′s – 90′s outreach to the working class, whom we’ve convinced that a college education is needed to “succeed in life.”. By so doing, those promoting the humanist goals of higher education — the advocates of “education for all” — have misread the needs of this sector of the population. Emphasizing the desire for and benefit of a college education, these advocates have devalued the development of skill sets that rely on different sorts of abilities: spatial reasoning, understanding of mechanics, ability to address practical problems, and most of all the simple ability and value of using one’s hands to create or correct what one sees as needed. The use of these skills has traditionally been the route to the middle class for millions of Americans, and what we have now, in the abandonment — even denigration — of technical and vocational schools, is a class of people trapped as wage-slaves in an ever-expanding service industry. The exceptions to that find the only sector of our society that values their abilities is the military. Draw your own conclusions as to what that says about our values.

    A second, and hopefully briefer, point I’d like to make is that at some point all academic inquiry attenuates. The very real phenomenon of thousands of doctoral theses going unread and unvalued suggests a certain exhaustion of purpose as well as a painfully blinkered assessment as to the societal utiiity of academic product. RJ’s point about the over-supply of young Ph.D.’s is exactly to the point here. (Whatever happened to valuing a teacher with a master’s degree?)

    Yes, by all means let’s have more and better teachers. But they shouldn’t all be forced to run around making abstruse arguements in obscure academic journals at the expense of actually focusing on educating people. And by God, let’s take a long and hard look at what they should be teaching, what of that teaching their students are actually going to remember a week after the exam, and whether the overall effect of their curriculum promotes a fruitful and proudly useful interaction with the real challenges of the future.

    Surely no one is surprised by this shift in the focus of universities from higher learning to skill acquirement. This has been going on for decades, ever since posts for university presidents & upper administration ceased to be filled from within the academy and schools instead hired people from the corporate world who bent academe to fit a business model. Universities have become businesses: it’s all about results, not ideas (unless they lead to patents). And believe me, it’s not just non-tenured adjunct faculty who are asked to perform to this model. Tenured and tenure-track faculty are now asked to spend countless hours performing endless “assessment” activities to determine whether students are actually acquiring the skills and knowledge they need to succeed in the marketplace. Whether students have a deeper understanding or solid context for such knowledge is immaterial.

    And the demise of the academy and its life of the mind is NOT restricted to the humanities! I teach plant biology, a topic considered irrelevant by undergraduates who major in biology merely as a stepping stone to medical school. They care little (if at all) about biology as a discipline. All that matters is the almighty GPA. This despite the fact that the majority of them will never go to med school. Where will our researchers come from? You know, the people who actually discover cures for diseases or solutions for ecological problems, toiling away in obscurity not for the money (there is comparatively little) but to satisfy their curiosity and make a contribution to the betterment of society.

    Sad, indeed.

    Brenna January 19, 2009 · 10:57 am

    An interesting post, and the book that inspires it certainly sounds like a worthwhile read. As a humanities adjunct, however, I would argue that the humanist allergy for usefulness/ work-force training and refusal to state the purpose of a liberal arts education has contributed to the problems we face today. The fact of the matter is, humanists have not done a very good job of peddling their wares to a skeptical, pragmatic public. The liberal arts “curriculum” at most universities is all over the map, and while there ARE certain ways of thinking that connect this hodge-podge course catalog, this is rarely made clear to students. Meanwhile, these same students are facing staggering student loan debt, and often they are already working full-time (see Marc Bousquet’s How the University Works for a discussion of this). They thus have a pressing need to be immediately employable upon graduation, and they know what employers are interested in (hint: not their coursework on Foucault). The fact that humanities are in a more permanent state of decline at institutions more likely to educate poorer students (community colleges and state schools) supports this interpretation.

    However, we should be careful not to blame students (who are reacting to very real realities of the world beyond the ivory tower) or adjuncts (who are very often just as committed to the liberal arts as tt profs are–perhaps more as they are willing to teach for pennies) for this. It is the professors with power–those who already have tenure–who are responsible for articulating to the public at large why their disciplines matter. Business degrees aren’t going anywhere, but the liberal arts have a utility of their own, both for the individual and for the larger culture, even if the rewards are less immediate. It’s the willingness of tenure-fattened cynics like Prof. Fish (for whom there is nothing at stake, because he was already “born at the right time”) to celebrate the profound purposelessness (and thus the disinterested superiority) of their disciplines that endangers the humanities.
