    Getting into College and Paying for it

    Calculating the Potential Return on Your Major

    By JACQUES STEINBERG
    May 24, 2011 12:31 pmMay 24, 2011 12:31 pm

    As the cost of college climbs ever higher each year, amid a national economic forecast that remains cloudy, questions about the value of a four-year degree are being raised with increased urgency.

    The latest piece of evidence to become part of that debate is a report released Tuesday by the Georgetown University Center on Education and the Workforce. Its seductive title: “What’s it Worth? The Economic Value of College Majors.”

    The good news? “While there is a lot of variation in earnings over a lifetime,” the center said in a press release, “the authors find that all undergraduate majors are ‘worth it,’ even taking into account the cost of college and lost earnings.”

    And yet, for those applicants to college seeking the most bang for their buck — at least as defined as maximizing their lifetime earnings — the authors used census data to conclude that the top majors as ranked by highest median earnings included petroleum engineer ($120,000), pharmacy/pharmaceutical sciences ($105,000) and mathematics and computer sciences ($98,000). The lowest? Counseling/psychology ($29,000), early childhood education ($36,000) and theology/religious vocations ($38,000.)

    Often in discussions like these, the “value” of a liberal arts education becomes difficult to peg. While not always utilitarian or pragmatic, a degree in English or the humanities can enrich one’s life, in incalculable ways, for decades. But do such degrees “pay”?

    “Liberal arts and humanities majors end up in the middle of the pack in terms of earnings and employment,” the center says in its release. “They are the third most popular major group, and earn median incomes of $47,000. Moreover, about 40 percent of people with these majors obtain a graduate degree, reaping a return of almost 50 percent.”

    Those who wish to dive further into the report, which numbers more than 200 pages, can do so here.

    But as a less intimidating first stop, I suggest that readers detour instead to the Web site of The Chronicle of Higher Education, where the editors have created an interactive graphic in which users can easily seek out the range of career earnings for those who have studied particular majors.

    But regardless of where you seek out more information, I hope you will take a moment to register a comment on The Choice using the box below. I am particularly interested in how strong a factor you believe one’s future earnings should be in choosing a course of study in college.

    Allison May 28, 2011 · 7:57 am

    Kat,

    It turned out one way in your family, but, really, there are many pathways. For example, my brother, an anthropology major at a 2nd-tier college that he barely got into, turned out to be a very successful business manager who retired, very comfortably, in his early 40s. I, the always-A student, went to a top-tier university, majored in biological science, went on to an MA and PhD, also from top-tier university, and am tenured faculty at an Ivy. So I’m certainly more comfortable than some, but the truth is I make only a tiny fraction of what he did (and nowhere near 6 figures, despite what some people seem to think professors earn). The other side of it is that part of the reason my brother retired so early is that he disliked his work, whereas I love my work and will only retire when I feel making room for younger people is the right thing to do.

    I’m encouraging my own children to try to think hard about what really matters to them: what level of material comfort do they want/need? what elements of a field or life’s work do they enjoy? what elements might they dislike? what talents do they bring to bear that could influence their chances of success? if their passion is a highly competitive field, can they also develop and prepare for a plan B? Given the number of job offers my oldest (who just graduated from a liberal arts college with a theatre major) entertained, I’m encouraged that this is a good strategy!

    Jennifer May 28, 2011 · 8:46 pm

    I enjoyed my major (one of the lowest-paying ones) and in fact got a PhD in it (which, like most humanities PhDs, has also not proven to be the royal road to the well-paid interior). As someone else said, without very good connections or good luck, with a liberal arts degree it’s difficult to make your way up a non-academic professional ladder. (That’s why so many liberal arts people end up going for a graduate degree — what else can you do with the BA, after all?)

    I don’t regret my major, and the work I have now is related to it, so it hasn’t been a financial washout. However, I believe that it’s unfair to tell young people “just do what you love” and “it’ll enrich your life” without also preparing them honestly for the job market. It’s unfortunate that the four-year degree is supposed to do both, yet only does one or the other. Would it be possible to structure higher education so that students major in two vocations — one to expand the mind and one to prepare for gainful, meaningful employment? In the liberal arts, the two are rarely the same.

    Stanton Green May 31, 2011 · 11:47 am

    Liberal arts Majors do not necessarily map onto careers. In my 35 years in higher ed – the past 18 as dean of liberal arts – i have found that there is at times a self fulfilling prophecy for liberal arts majors to earn less because they often aim or are aimed at lower paying jobs. By way of example, most people with Anthropology degrees are advised to look for anthropology type jobs – museums for example – when well paying corporate positions might well suit them. The skills and inclinations of a person who enjoys working with people and understanding social and business organization can be well applied to corporate work – for example humsn resource – but liberal arts majors are rarely advised about this. I recently assembled 4 high powered and well paid business persons to tell their stories and they all began with their liberal arts education.

    About 15 years ago I was asked to join a meeting of all of my univrrsity’s deans to meet with university president and the President of a major bank to discuss the success of our students at his bank. He noted that our students did well as interns and entry level managers and then noted what was surprising to some but not to me : liberal arts students were his best employees – and the best of these had been – read my lips – theater majors. I beamed next to my business dean colleague and have since taken his observation to the ‘bank’ of parents and students wanting to know what to major in.

    Americanist May 31, 2011 · 12:24 pm

    A careful read of the data suggests that this is actually good news about the value of a liberal arts education, since many u/g majors that may at first strike anxious parents as more “practical” (many branches of the sciences, agriculture, public policy, everything in the social sciences except economics, pre-med, pre-law, etc.) comes out in the same ballpark as the much-maligned English major. In addition to the methodological flaws already pointed out by others, the report errs in making no distinction between higher- and lesser-quality institutions. While a degree’s prestige factor can certainly be overrated, there are indisputable differences in the quality of education delivered at different kinds of colleges and universities. (Witness the recent flap over the revelation that some college courses only require 10 pages of writing per semester: many places continue to require 40-50, with rewrites, and by and large they turn out better writers.) Finally, the breadth of the age range–25-64–makes the data all but useless. Aren’t education pundits always talking about how we have to prepare students for the careers of the future, not of the past? Why assume that someone’s choice of major 40+ years ago is a reliable predictor for the coming generation? Where are all those petroleum engineers going to be in 50 years when we run the world on hydrogen fuel cells and solar energy? Makes you wonder whether the authors took any statistical reasoning courses in college.

    Andrew June 7, 2011 · 11:56 am

    Investment banks generally don’t look to hire English majors, but I’ve seen it from the inside in rare cases. As someone who gave up a top undergraduate business degree for a Liberal Arts education, I have never regretted the decision. Still, I know I was one of the lucky ones that was able to turn a general degree into an offer at a top-tier investment bank. It’s a have your cake and eat it too type of decision that I would not recommend.

    On the flip side, I’ve seen first hand what a Wharton undergraduate degree provides. Those that wanted to go into Finance and dedicated their lives to the field back when they were applying to college have succeeded financially more than any other singular group I’ve witnessed as a Manhattan resident.

    In summary, it depends on your risk tolerance and career goals. If you know you want to lock in a high paying career its always the smart choice to dedicate yourself to a high-paying major as you will be that much more valuable to an employer. If you want to roll the dice, go ahead. You may hit it big on Jeopardy with your well-rounded knowledge but it’s a much more difficult path to financial security.

    For what it’s worth, I just graduated from Harvard with a science degree. I was not passionate about it, but I chose it because I listened to the kind of advice that is thrown at humanities majors on this thread: that “following your passion” is self-indulgent, that majoring in Comp Lit signals that you couldn’t hack math, that I was making a more “adult” choice.

    Maybe if I had studied the humanities, the angst confronting me at graduation would have been of a different sort. Maybe the grass is always greener.     rbyrnes June 6, 2013 · 11:26 pm

    According to the stastics cited in this article and so many others like it, my liberal arts (English) degrees place my ‘earning potential’ in the middle of the pack (~$47k). While entry level earnings are fairly clearly determined by major, that line blurs and virtually disappears over time once you’ve established yourself, broadened your skillset, and have access to–and take full advantage of–the same growth opportunities available to your colleagues (of course, company policies for growth differ). I started out quite close to the bottom at a major US manufacturer. Today I earn more than 3X what this article suggests my ‘potential’ is worth, and many of the good people who now report to me have more desireable and more sought after degrees. Yes, by all means share this article with your kids—but please NEVER forget to mention that the degree does not make you a leader; it is not an indicator of your commitment to hard work; it does not create strong business ethics; and does not guarantee an abiltity to deliver under pressure. Those are the truly desireable traits that are most often recognized and rewarded in the business community.
