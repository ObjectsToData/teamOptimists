Showcase: The Puerto Rican Diaspora

By DAVID GONZALEZ
In order to view this feature, you must download the latest version of flash player here.

A photograph in Frank Espada’s book “The Puerto Rican Diaspora” shows a faintly smiling man sporting a button that declares, “We Try Harder.” That detail easily applies to the subjects in the impressive portfolio Mr. Espada amassed over decades photographing Puerto Ricans throughout the United States.

Traveling from Puerto Rico to New York and from Hartford to Hawaii, he documented in loving portraits his countrymen and women engaged in ballet and theater, drug rehab and field work. Their faces are open, direct and trusting, presenting a nuanced take on complicated lives that are often led out of the spotlight.

Mr. Espada could relate, because he had to try harder himself. For much of his life, he put his artistic dreams on hold while working in a series of day jobs to pay the bills and raise three children. Thirty years ago, at 49, he was finally able to dedicate himself totally to photography after winning a federal grant that allowed him to travel and shoot the images that form the core of his Diaspora series.

Frank Espada Manny Diaz

And like a better-known Frank, he did it his way.

“I had been photographing for more than 30 years before I got the chance to apply my knowledge and skills to make an honest statement about our experiences in our diaspora,” he said. “I was not pre-programmed by some know-nothing editor to bring back more proof as to the miserable lives we were living. It was to be as loving a document as I could produce.”

His insistence on staying true to his principles paid off this year when the Library of Congress acquired an 83-print portfolio of his Diaspora images. Beverly Brannan, the library’s curator of photography, said the acquisition was unusual. Most of its postwar holdings on Puerto Ricans consist of photos taken for newspapers or magazines.

“The library holds few photographic surveys of entire countries,” Ms. Brannan said. “Most visual documentation comes from a variety of sources. This survey provides a coherent statement about people of Puerto Rican background at a time in American history.”

Mr. Espada’s work stands in stark contrast to much of the photojournalism produced over the years in Puerto Rican communities, where despair was never far from the camera. Never one to mute his opinions, he called some of those now-celebrated photographers “rip-off artists” who built their reputations on the backs of the dispossessed.

“I have nothing but contempt for the lot of them,” he said. “They were documenting our young people doing destructive acts. They went in there and photographed it, and these young kids felt like big shots because they were being photographed. I’ll never forget that, because it did great damage.”

While photographers like Bruce Davidson became famous for images of poverty on East 100th Street, Mr. Espada’s chronicle of that same community is like an insider’s dispatch from a parallel universe. His book features the annual Three Kings Day Parade, where later-day shepherds walked in the snow past the stone railroad viaduct, presided over by the devilishly smiling Jack Agüeros, a noted poet and activist. Elsewhere in the city, he found Puerto Ricans like Miriam Colón, the actress, converting an old firehouse into a theater, or graduates being awarded their diplomas at Boricua College. At the Ballet Hispanico, he zeroed in on graceful, tiny dancers.

Frank Espada Ballerina at Ballet Hispanico, NY, 1980

This is not to say he sugar-coated the reality of Puerto Rican life. He captured the dehumanizing backdrop of urban decay in Manhattan Valley and Hartford, and the tough lives of migrant workers in New Jersey. But even in his portraits of recovering drug addicts, there is tenderness, hope and natural dignity.

“I had plenty of opportunities to photograph people hurting themselves,” he said. “But I have never done that. That’s not what I’m about here. Even when you see the section about people in rehab, it’s an ‘up’ section.”

Pablo Delano, a photographer and son of Jack Delano — who photographed Puerto Rico for the Farm Security Administration — said Mr. Espada’s work complemented his father’s. In fact, he is hoping to mount a joint exhibition.

“Frank’s work is profoundly humanistic,” said Mr. Delano, who teaches at Trinity College in Hartford. “Stereotypes about Puerto Rico and Puerto Ricans are so deeply embedded in mainstream U.S. culture that even some Puerto Ricans have started to believe them. Frank’s images dispel all that. He does it in a dignified way, yet without becoming sentimental or romantic.”

Mr. Espada’s path had some serious detours. Born in Puerto Rico in 1930, he moved to New York when he was 9. Early on, he witnessed a jarring moment that would, in time, lead him to refuse to accept the way things were.

“My activism goes back a long way,” he said. “I’m old enough to remember my father crying — the only time he did — when he went out looking for a job, and it was ‘no Puerto Ricans wanted.’ People really don’t realize how close we were to all that.”

A cousin turned him on to photography when he was 16. After serving in the military, he studied photography under the G.I. Bill. He also became friendly with a small group of like-minded shooters who have remained close friends. He studied for a year and a half with W. Eugene Smith, whose influence as a documentary photographer he still cites to this day.

Frank Espada Rosa Santiago, Naguabo, PR, 1981

“My eye hasn’t changed too much since then,” he said. “I wanted to use photography the way Smith did. But it took a while.”

Married and with three children to raise, he ended up working for an electrical contractor, a job he despised. He later worked for agencies that helped community groups and others. All the while, he was busy as an activist, organizing low-income tenants and welfare recipients in East New York, as well as working nationally alongside African-American civil rights leaders like Bayard Rustin.

By the 1970s, he was in Washington, at an organization that had him traveling the country to organize drug-treatment and prevention programs in Puerto Rican communities. The job helped him forge contacts that he would later rely upon when he embarked on his Diaspora project after receiving a modest grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities in 1979.

After crossing the country for about two years, taking pictures and recording interviews, he continued to raise money and awareness by helping local groups apply for arts grants to bring his exhibit to their cities. In 15 years, he held 45 exhibits across the country. By then, he had also moved to San Francisco, where he began to teach at several colleges.

He stopped teaching in 2005, the same time a close friend gave him the money to self-publish the book.

He still looks at the people he photographed as his personal heroes, people who are trying in one way or another to improve themselves and their communities. At the same time, he is upset that progress has eluded many Puerto Ricans in this country. In fact, he said, things might actually be worse with the recent economic downturn. Now, as then, the circumstances of his countrymen anger him.

“I did a lot of stuff in anger,” he said of his photography. “It was an expression of my frustration at not being able to do more and see these people struggling all the time and not making any significant progress.”

But there was an equally strong emotion tempering his mood.

“Without love, I couldn’t have done this,” he said. “I was angry at the system and what happened to us and the suffering I saw. But as far as I’m concerned, these are beautiful people.”
