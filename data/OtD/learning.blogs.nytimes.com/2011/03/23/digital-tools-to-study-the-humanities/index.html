      A.B. Coyle March 23, 2011 · 3:19 pm

      I have a Fair Use question arising from my use of NYT articles and opinions as required reading in the undergraduate business law class (BUS 250) I teach each semester at The University of Mississippi. There are approximately 70 students in each class.

      A hot button topic that never fails to inspire classroom discussions is Eminent Domain. Several articles and opinions that appeared in the NYT shortly after the US Supreme Court’s decision in KELO v. CITY OF NEW LONDON, 545 U.S. 469 (2005), are particularly useful as teaching, thinking, arguing, and intellectual horizon-lifting tools.

      I made PDF copies of the NYT articles and opinions; the copies are stored on my personal computer (not university property). As the time approaches to teach Eminent Domain, I send my students an email message with the PDF documents attached for the students to open and read. I do not instruct or require students to print hard copies of the items, but I am confident that many–perhaps all–do.

      Query: does my practice of requiring students to open and read NYT articles and opinions that I have stored as PDF documents violate Fair Use of the NYT’s materials?

      If yes, is there a method by which I might seek permission from the NYT to use the paper’s materials as I described above?
