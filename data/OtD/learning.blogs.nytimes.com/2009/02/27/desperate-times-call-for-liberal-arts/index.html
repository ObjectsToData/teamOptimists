    Desperate Times Call for … Liberal Arts?

    By AMANDA CHRISTY BROWN and KRISTIN MCGINN MAHONEY
    February 27, 2009 12:00 amDecember 9, 2011 9:09 pm

    Note: This lesson was originally published on an older version of The Learning Network; the link to the related Times article will take you to a page on the old site.


    Teaching ideas based on New York Times content.

    • See all lesson plans

    Overview of Lesson Plan: In this lesson, students examine the value of humanities education during a time of economic turmoil by reflecting on their learning experiences and discussing the role the humanities play in their lives in school and beyond. Click here for a companion lesson for Grades 3-5.

    Authors:

    Amanda Christy Brown, The Learning Network

    Kristin McGinn Mahoney , The Learning Network

    Suggested Time Allowance: Three to five class periods

    Activities/Procedures:

    Click here for a companion lesson for Grades 3-5.
    1. Warm-Up/Do Now:
    Note to Teacher: Though the article defines the humanities as including the arts, for simplicity’s sake, you may wish to limit the following activities to include English/language arts, languages, history/social studies and civics. You may also wish to include any other humanities courses offered at your school, like religious studies or philosophy.
    In their journals, students respond to the following prompt: “Imagine that budget cuts at our school forced us to stop teaching English, foreign languages, history, art and music. What would happen?” Tell students they may choose to respond to the cancellation of one or all of the subjects listed.
    Once students have finished writing, invite them to share. Tell students that these subjects are all grouped together as the humanities, and that their value is often questioned during periods of economic trouble. Then ask the following questions to encourage deeper reflection:
    What subjects would be left in the curriculum if the humanities were cut?
    What value do these subjects have that the humanities don’t?
    How would the school experience be different without the humanities?
    Why might the value of humanities be questioned during downturns in the economy?
    What would be lost if languages, history and the arts ceased to be taught at our school? (Encourage students to think of specific experiences they would miss out on if this came to be.)
    How might their loss affect you — both individually and as a group — and your future?

    2. Article Questions:
    As a class, read and discuss the article “In Tough Times, the Humanities Must Justify Their Worth,” focusing on the following questions:
    a. What have universities traditionally believed the purpose of a liberal arts education to be?
    b. Why have questions about the importance of the humanities taken on a new urgency?
    c. What are some signs of trouble for the humanities?
    d. In what ways do the disciplines grouped together as humanities (language, literature, the arts, history, cultural studies, philosophy and religion) help people explore “what it means to be a human being”?
    e. What practical and economic value do the humanities have?
    f. Why are some in the field uneasy about “marketing” the humanities in this way?
    g. Why does Anthony T. Kronman, a law professor, believe that studying the humanities is more urgent today than ever before?
    h. Do you agree?
    i. Why does the author refer to President Obama in the article?
    j. What would happen if humanities education became a luxury that only the very rich could afford?
    3. Activity:
    Ask students to use the handout My Life of Learning to make a timeline of their learning in the humanities from their earliest memories forward, writing down significant moments that have to do with their learning in this domain. Tell them to write positive memories above the line and negative memories below the line, as in the model on the handout. Students should feel free to skip around in time as memories come to them. Tell students that they will be sharing these in class, so they should not write down anything they won’t be comfortable sharing later with their classmates. Before sending them off to work independently, model your own timeline on the board for students.
    Once they have completed their timelines, ask them to choose one experience and reflect on it more fully in writing, using it as a microcosm to show what they learned and how it applies to the real world and their lives. Ask them to examine (in their journals) what they learned from it, how it applies to today and how it influences their thinking and understanding about the world or themselves.
    Then, have students post their timelines around the room and examine one another’s, looking for commonalities.
    Reconvene as a class and begin a discussion by asking the following questions:
    What are some of the experiences you might have missed out on if the humanities were cut from school?
    What value do these experiences have?
    Besides the experiences themselves, what would you miss out on?
    What are the implications for missing experiences like these?
    How might their loss across the nation affect our country and world?
    What is the value of the humanities?
    What do we learn from them?
    What values does our educational system implicitly and explicitly teach?

    Revisit Professor Kronman’s comments in the article: “But ‘the need for my older view of the humanities is, if anything, more urgent today,’ he added, referring to the widespread indictment of greed, irresponsibility and fraud that led to the financial meltdown. In his view this is the time to re-examine ‘what we care about and what we value,’ a problem the humanities ‘are extremely well-equipped to address.’ ”
    To wrap up, ask: Given the state of the world, why study humanities? Is it a luxury? What is the value of a liberal arts education now? What is the value of the life of the mind?
    4. For Homework or Future Classes:
    Individually, students write a personal statement about their experiences in school and what they have come to value about their education. They might write for an audience of their peers, of incoming students or for a college admissions board. Encourage them to use concrete evidence from their own history as scholars as they detail their beliefs. They might draw inspiration from or bolster their arguments with quotations from the article or from the nearly 400 comments on it.

      elite, civic, critical, sprawling, vocation, prerequisites, endowment, urgency, lamented, hiring freeze, irrelevant, vitality, staunchest, advocates, reassessment, disjunction, chasm, Ivory Tower, baldly, stem cell research, consumable, indictment, fraud, humanist, travesties, heyday, province, inevitable
    • Extension Activities:
      1. Many schools across the country are currently cutting programs in the humanities because of budget shortfalls. Imagine that your school is forced to cut art, music or drama. Write a letter to the school board in which you argue the value of one of these subjects to save it from being cut.
      2. In addition to humanities and arts, other major educational categories are the sciences (including math) and vocations and trades. What is the value of each of these? How do they overlap and inform one another? Stage a debate with peers in which one side defends and the other attacks the premise that, for example, budding carpenters should be required to read Shakespeare, musicians should study math, and so on, as opposed to being permitted or required to specialize at a young age.
      3. Engage in a research project to find out how the education backgrounds of the adults around you have affected their careers and lives. Draw up questions to ask about your subjects’ educational background and how they use and draw upon what they learned in their current lives at work and at home. Then interview parents, other relatives, teachers and school officials. In class, share results and then discuss whether they suggest that indeed the humanities are of value beyond school.
      Interdisciplinary Connections:
      Global studies: Choose a country and research how it educates its children. Pay attention to factors like subjects taught, how students are grouped or tracked, the daily schedule, graduation requirements and any significant differences from the United States. Then present your findings in class. Engage in a discussion of how the educational systems of other countries reflect what they value. What place do the humanities hold in different places?
      Language arts: Choose a novel, play, story or poem that had a significant effect on you and write personal essays exploring its influence on your life. You may wish to cast essays in the style of National Public Radio’s series “You Must Read This.”
      Academic Content Standards:
      Grades 6-12
      Behavioral Studies Standard 3: Understands that interactions among learning, inheritance and physical development affect human behavior.
      Language Arts Standard 1: Demonstrates competence in the general skills and strategies of the writing process.
      Language Arts Standard 7: Demonstrates competence in the general skills and strategies for reading a variety of informational texts.
      Language Arts Standard 8: Demonstrates competence in speaking and listening as tools for learning.
