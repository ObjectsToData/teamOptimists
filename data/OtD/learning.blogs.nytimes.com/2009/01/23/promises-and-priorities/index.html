    Promises and Priorities

    By AMANDA CHRISTY BROWN and KRISTIN MCGINN MAHONEY
    January 23, 2009 12:00 amMarch 10, 2009 3:26 pm

    Note: This lesson was originally published on an older version of The Learning Network; the link to the related Times article will take you to a page on the old site.


    Teaching ideas based on New York Times content.

    • See all lesson plans »

    Overview of Lesson Plan: In this lesson, students complete a “SWOT” (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats) analysis as they consider priorities for President Obama’s first 100 days in office. Then, students look to history to see what this president might learn from past presidents’ early days in office. Finally, students chart the progress of the first 100 days of the Obama presidency by updating events as they occur on a class timeline.
    Click here for a companion lesson for Grades 3-5.

    Author(s):
    Amanda Christy Brown, The New York Times Learning Network
    Kristin McGinn Mahoney , The New York Times Learning Network

    Suggested Time Allowance: 2-4 class periods

    Resources / Materials:
    -computers with Internet access and projection equipment, if available
    -copies of the article On Day One, Obama Sets a New Tone(one per student)
    -copies of the handout SWOT Analysis: Obama’s First 100 Days(one per student)
    -butcher paper or a large poster board

    Activities / Procedures:
    Click here for a companion lesson for Grades 3-5.
    1. WARM-UP/DO NOW:
    Ask students to look at The New York Times Interactive Feature, I Hope So Too, and listen to several of the audio clips. You may choose to project this graphic and explore it as a class or have students look at it on their own or in pairs. If students explore the graphic independently, bring the class together after a short time to share some of the hopes they heard people express.
    Teachers without access to the Internet should ask students to list the many hopes people (in the United States and elsewhere) have for Mr. Obama’s presidency. Record their ideas on the board.
    Then, engage students in a brief class discussion by posing the following questions:
    -Why are there such big hopes for Mr. Obama’s presidency?
    -In what way might these hopes be beneficial to him?
    -In what ways might they be harmful?
    -Of the categories from the interactive feature (or big list of hopes on the board), which do you think President Obama will be able to realistically address/take action on during his first 100 days of office, and why?
    -What should he address first, and why?
    2. ARTICLE QUESTIONS:
    As a class, read and discuss the article On Day One, Obama Sets a New Tone focusing on the following questions:
    a. In what ways were the moves President Obama made on the first day in office “a clean break from business as usual”?
    b. How will Mr. Obama bring greater transparency to Washington, and why is this important?
    c. Why might historians be particularly happy about the availability of presidential records?
    d. Which cabinet members were confirmed on Mr. Obama’s first day in office? Which nominees still await confirmation?
    e. How might the freeze on salaries for White House employees earning above $100,000 be considered symbolic?
    f. Who are lobbyists? How will President Obama’s order change the way they do business in Washington?
    g. In what ways is the administration already violating its own rules about lobbyists, according to the article?
    h. What minor glitches did members of the Obama administration experience on their first day of work?
    i. In what ways do you think President Obama’s first executive orders, and the messages they sent, fulfill his campaign promises?
    j. Based on the events of the first day, what predictions, if any, can you make about the president’s first 100 days in office? Why?
    3. ACTIVITY
    Tell students that today they will be completing a SWOT analysis for President Obama and using it to identify priorities for his first 100 days in office. Let them know that, because they will later be following and charting the president’s actual progress in the first 100 days, it is important to first understand some of the opportunities and challenges he faces.
    Ask students if they have heard of “SWOT.” Explain to them that the acronym stands for “Strengths,” “Weaknesses,” “Opportunities,” and “Threats.” These categories are areas of analysis that businesses and not-for-profit organizations use to determine their internal strengths and weaknesses and their external opportunities and threats for strategic planning purposes. This form of analysis can also be applied to departments or branches of larger institutions, as well as to individuals within these institutions.
    Create a chart with the acronym SWOT on the board. Ask students where they might place the hopes people have for President Obama. Then, look to the article for quotations to add to the chart. For example, the delay in approving nominations might be perceived as an external threat to the new administration’s progress (e.g., “Republicans forced a one-week delay in the vote on Mr. Obama’s nominee for attorney general”). Aim for at least one idea and/or quotation for each column as you model the process.
    Distribute the handout SWOT Analysis: President Obama’s First 100 Days to students. Have them work in groups to complete the chart by jotting down quotations that illustrate each category. Students may use information from the article to complete their charts, but should also draw from New York Times articles and features, as well as the Times Topics page on Barack Obama. (Teachers without Internet access might consider printing several articles beforehand for use by student groups during class.) Suggested resources include:
    Transcript: Barack Obama’s Inaugural Address
    Op-Ed: The Remaking of America
    Interactive Feature: The New Team
    Blog: The Caucus
    Magazine: Is (His) Biography (Our) Destiny?
    Once students have completed their charts using information from the article and other New York Times resources, have groups come together to share their analyses with the rest of the class in a wrap-up discussion. For time and efficiency, you may wish to divide the presentation of categories “S” and “W” from “O” and “T” among groups. You may also wish to list examples under each letter on the board as groups offer them, or have group representatives take turns writing on the board without repeating examples. Encourage all students to take notes to refer to as they consider how President Obama might best spend his first 100 days in office.
    Now, have students reconvene in their groups to discuss what the president might tackle in his first 100 days in office, given his strengths and weaknesses and existing opportunities and threats. Ask them to choose issues that they believe to be most urgent and prioritize them. You may wish to provide the following subject categories (or others) on the board to guide group discussion:
    -national security
    -the Economy
    -foreign policy
    -healthcare
    -education
    -immigration
    -the environment/climate change
    -civil liberties
    -wars in Iraq and Afghanistan
    Encourage students to be realistic in their discussion about what a president might hope to accomplish in a relatively short period of time and to think about some of the small initiatives or changes he might implement before taking on the bigger issues. Have them consider what his priorities should be and why. Ask them to think about what strengths he might capitalize on early and what opportunities might prove most fruitful in his efforts. They should consider, too, which threats or challenges might be considered the greatest obstacles.
    Instruct groups to synthesize their thoughts by completing the following statement about their top priority:
    Given President Obama’s opportunities in __________________________ and his threats in ____________________________, we think it would be realistic for him to (achieve/take action on/complete) _____________________________________ in his first 100 days of office.
    Once group conversations wind down, have students come together and share their statements. Post these in the classroom for students to look back at as they track President Obama’s first 100 days.
    Conclude this activity by asking students to reflect on what they have learned. You may wish to use some or all of the following questions:
    -What common priorities did you outline for President Obama? Why?
    -What omissions did you notice? Were these purposeful?
    -How realistic do you think these plans are?
    -What strengths will enable the president to tackle some of the challenges your plans outline?
    -What might get in the way of his accomplishing some of the goals you have set for him?
    -How realistic is it to expect major change in only 100 days?
    -Is it fair to judge a president on his first 100 days? Why or why not?
    -What about the role of the American people? Do you think people will live up to President Obama’s call for public service and personal responsibility? Why or why not?
    4. FOR HOMEWORK OR FUTURE CLASSES:
    The idea of assessing a president’s first 100 days in office originated with Franklin Delano Roosevelt. For homework, ask each student to choose a president from President Roosevelt to George W. Bush to research using Good Magazine’s The Good Sheet: The First 100 Days, a graphic illustrating highlights of the first 100 days of every president since FDR. Ask them to document their research in their journals, using the following framework:
    -Context: What was going on in the world when this president took office?
    -Causes: What causes did this president focus on in his first 100 days?
    -Concerns: What issues or problems did this president face during his first 100 days?
    Once students have completed their research, ask each of them to write a short memo to President Obama briefing him on what he might learn from the highlights of a former president’s first 100 days.
    In future classes, follow the first one hundred days of the Obama presidency in the New York Times. Create a “living” classroom graphic timeline on butcher paper or a large poster board using daily and weekly headlines to mark the change Mr. Obama brings in his first 100 days in office. You might assign responsibility to individual students or groups for updating the display on a daily or weekly basis. Check the 100 Days Blog for regular updates.
    As time goes on, ask your students to notice not only what happens, but also how the media covers what happens. How, if at all, does the tone of the news coverage change? Be sure to plan some class time at the conclusion of President Obama’s first 100 days for evaluation and analysis, and to look back at student SWOT charts and plans for Obama. How do students’ early expectations compare to reality?

    Extension Activities:
    1. The president’s call for public service involves everyone in his plans for the nation. As a class, plan a service project or projects to complete during Mr. Obama’s first 100 days.
    2. Compare the first 100 days of each of the presidents students researched for homework to his public approval rating at the end of his term(s). How indicative, if at all, were the first 100 days of things to come? Write a short piece on how the first 100 days might “set the tone” for a presidency.
    3. Use Good Magazine’s 100 Days graphic as the starting point for creating a poster on another president’s first 100 days. Once President Obama’s 100 days are up, have student experts on each president present their posters and offer a comparison between their presidents’ early days and Mr. Obama’s.
    4. What are the expectations for first lady? Assess Michelle Obama’s first 100 days in her role in a report card.
    5. What about the vice president? What is his role in the first 100 days of the administration? Follow Vice President Biden’s words and actions, as reported by the media in the next few months, in a parallel timeline.

    Interdisciplinary Connections:
    Fine Arts – While government funding for the arts is overseen by two independent grant-making government agencies, The National Endowment for the Arts and The National Endowment for the Humanities, there is currently no cabinet official dedicated to furthering the arts in America. Should President Obama consider creating a cabinet-level position dedicated the arts? What would be the advantages of such a decision? What duties might a secretary of arts perform? Write an editorial in which you argue either for or against the creation of such a position.
    Language Arts – Elizabeth Alexander, the inaugural poet, wrote what is called an “occasional poem” to deliver on the occasion of Mr. Obama’s inauguration as president of the United States. Her poem shared hopes and dreams and put the election in the context of our nation’s history. Write an occasional poem to deliver to your class on President Obama’s 100th day in office. Consider the full spectrum of what unfolds during his first 100 days in your writing.
    Science – Environmental issues are among those that the new administration will need to tackle early on. What are these issues? What should President Obama’s priorities be when it comes to science? Why? Create a PowerPoint or other visual presentation to outline the issues this administration should address immediately and explain their urgency.

    Civics Standard 9- Understands the importance of Americans sharing and supporting certain values, beliefs, and principles of American constitutional democracy.
    Civics Standard 27- Understands how certain character traits enhance citizens’ ability to fulfill personal and civic responsibilities.
    Civics Standard 29- Understands the importance of political leadership, public service, and a knowledgeable citizenry in American constitutional democracy.
    Language Arts Standard 1- Demonstrates competence in the general skills and strategies of the writing process.
    Language Arts Standard 7 – Demonstrates competence in the general skills and strategies for reading a variety of informational texts.
    Language Arts Standard 8 – Demonstrates competence in speaking and listening as tools for learning.
    United State History Standard 31 – Understands economic, social, and cultural developments in the contemporary United States.
