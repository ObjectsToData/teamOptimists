    Reader Ideas | Teaching the Civil Rights Movement

    By KATHERINE SCHULTEN
    January 27, 2012 3:16 pmFebruary 16, 2012 8:40 am
    Jeremy M. Lange for The New York TimesThe International Civil Rights Center and Museum opened in 2010 inside a former Woolworth building in Greensboro, N.C. The store was the site of a series of luncheonette “sit-ins” against segregation beginning on Feb. 1, 1960. Go to related article »
    Ideas From Readers

    Submit your story about how you’ve taught with The Times.

    • Submit an idea »
    • See more ideas »

    This month, we asked educators, How Do You Teach the Civil Rights Movement?

    Many echoed the findings of the Southern Poverty Law Center by writing that this era of history is little taught in their own schools and districts.

    Just as many educators mentioned the importance of teaching the civil rights movement in the context of African-American history as a whole, since many students bring very little background knowledge to the subject. And, as good teachers will, most wrote that they try to connect this “ancient history” to the world today.

    A comment from John Padula, a Boston middle school teacher, brought together many of the points others raised:

    I teach grades 6, 7 and 8 in the Boston Public Schools. Our city curriculum guides provide one unit in grade 8 that focuses on civil rights. In our case, we focus on the integration of Little Rock High School in 1957.

    When I first taught this, the kids were completely lost. I followed the teaching guide and began with “The Legacies of Segregation.” They were asking great questions like “Why did we treat blacks differently?” and “Why did we even have slavery?” I had to go back and give them a solid background in these areas before we could go forward. That takes time!

    Now, six years later, that unit is one of the most memorable for students, as we begin with slavery in the 1600s and make our way through the Dred Scott and Plessy cases; the Civil War; the 13th, 14th and 15th Amendments; Jim Crow and, ultimately, to the Brown v. Board of Education decision – all before we even begin to look at the integration in Little Rock.

    I’ve had to throw out or severely pare down at least one other unit to fit this in, but I think it’s worth it in the end. These kids leave understanding segregation, Little Rock and, most importantly, how in Boston that “ancient history” directly connects to our own experiences with forced busing in the 1970s (in which I was a participant).

    I am not surprised by the Law Center’s findings. Good, solid history teaching takes time. These days, testing is king, and it’s always easier to make room for more math or E.L.A. test prep at the expense of some “dusty history” class.

    Below we’ve sorted by subject area the best ideas sent in by teachers at all levels — though so many are interdisciplinary that we recommend scanning them all. We’ve edited them lightly for space and Times style, but each links back to the original comment. (We’ve also included a few posts that have come in through our Black History Month resource page.)

    It’s not too late to add your own thoughts. How do you go beyond “I Have a Dream” in making the civil rights era meaningful to your students? What, if anything, will you do for Black History Month, which begins next week?

    History, Social Studies, Civics

    Slavery and Civil Rights Taught Together:
    Because my middle school American History eighth grade never got past 1950, I moved the civil rights movement to right after the Civil War, working on the assumption that when my students knew slavery was abolished, they would assume everything was fine.

    I use a Southern Poverty Law Center documentary. We interview grandparents for their remembrances of the 1950s and ’60s. We go through the 13, 14, 15th Amendments, Plessy and Brown, and look at the struggle for civil rights in modern times with (yes) The New York Times.
    — Chris Kozar

    From Abigail Adams to the ’60s:
    In my classes, civil rights education starts when the colonies become the United States. We begin when Abigail Adams asks her husband in a letter to “remember the ladies” during the time of the Second Continental Congress. We progress from 1776 to the status of women in colonial times. We discuss the status of African slaves, rights given to former male slaves (not women) during Reconstruction, the Great Migration, the women’s suffrage movement, the status of African-American soldiers during World War I, and onward to the explosion in the ’50s and ’60s.

    This year, I was thinking about showing my students the movie, “The Help.” Has anyone else used it in a classroom?
    — Sandra Talarico

    Re-enacting Harpers Ferry:
    I think this lesson works well for the history leading up to the civil rights movement. I had my students re-enact the Harpers Ferry court case where John Brown was tried and convicted of treason against the state of Virginia. I assigned each of them to a role in the courtroom including a bailiff, prosecutor, defender and several jury members. For each of their roles, they had to research the court maneuvers and story from each of the different perspectives. My students really got into the acting and began to understand some of the underlying issues of the Civil War. It was particularly useful for my students (who are primarily African-American and Hispanic) to imagine a perspective different than their own.
    — Natalie

    Comparing Rights Movements With Navajo Students:
    In the last quarter, we focus on social justice more than civil rights to help guide our inquiry projects. My students are 99.9 percent Navajo (Dine), and we are on the Navajo Nation. Almost all of them want to learn more about the American Indian Movement. In my first year, I just assumed that given our student population they would be familiar with it. Not so. I am developing a project that compares the different movements of that era for students to learn more and find out what similarities helped unite their causes and what differences made it difficult to make progress.
    — Sarah Garcia

    ‘Make the Right Choices’:
    M.T.R.C.: That’s how I teach all my classes – and it’s also how I try to live my life. M.T.R.C.? Make the Right Choices. “How people treat people” encompasses each and every lesson and contact I attempt. Whether voting rights with seniors, historical slavery issues with sophomores or American remnants of discrimination with freshmen, I tie these actions of prejudice to M.T.R.C. We connect all this with studies of anti-Semitism, genocide and the Holocaust. Civil rights in America cannot be separated or isolated from the rights of all humans everywhere in the world.
    — Alan Bush

    ‘Collective Amnesia,’ Even Among College Students:
    I am a college professor of African-American literature, and not a teacher of high school history, so my challenges are obviously different. Still, I, too, am dismayed by the collective amnesia I encounter among junior-level students at a major public university. My approach to the civil rights movement is to defy my students’ feeling that it is a closed period in American history. Certainly, the antagonism surrounding our national discussions of race, and the statistics that speak to enduring racial inequality show that the civil rights movement, is not a finite epoch that we can look back on with closure. Moreover, as I point out in the classroom, the civil rights movement was such an important model for other collective challenges to inequality that are obviously ongoing as well, like gay rights activism.
    – Michael Borshuk

    English Language Arts

    A Paper on the ‘N Word’:
    I celebrate Black History Month in my research writing class (grades 9 to 12 inclusive) by having them produce a five-page, M.L.A.-cited, persuasive essay answering this thesis question: “Has the use of the ‘N word’ benefited the African-American community?”

    It is the most energized and profoundly thoughtful learning experience for all of us. Each year, I am swayed from one position to another. Each year, the sources — including a personal interview — become more varied and controversial. It is fraught with verbal bombs, faux pas, and hands slapping foreheads in disgust or awe. We end the unit with a debate before a school audience. It is gratifying as much for the learning as for the “real life” connections.
    — Joan Malerba-Foran

    Bob Dylan as a Lens:
    I teach English at a rural high school in central Montana. My biggest success incorporating the civil rights movement with juniors (American literature) has been focusing on the music and lyrics of Bob Dylan. YouTube has the news footage of his performance (along with Joan Baez, Len Chandler and others) at the March on Washington in 1963. The footage spawns questions ranging from factual — who is Medgar Evers? (sung about in “A Pawn in Their Game”) — to philosophical — does Mr. Dylan’s race “matter”?

    I then give the class the lyrics to “The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll,” and play the song. Students are curious about the story that Dylan was returning home from the march in Washington when he read about the light sentence Hattie Carroll’s killer received. Was it a harsh dose of reality after hearing about the “dream”? We also talk about reports that Dylan still performs the song in concert and how it is relevant nearly 50 years after the events that inspired it.

    Students then spend time listening to and researching Odetta. I incorporated Odetta to make students see at least one African-American artist respond to and support the movement. I understand that there are concerns that Dylan was the “white” voice. But in our overwhelmingly Caucasian community, for students to see that a young man from a town very much like their own chose to support a cause that was perhaps not naturally “his,” is important. It helps eliminate the “this has nothing to do with me” attitude that can be easily brought to literature.
    — Annie Fort

    A Biographical Stamp Project:
    My gifted students in grades 1 to 5 learn about Black History Month and National Women’s Month together. They then decide which person they would like to highlight as they work on a unit called “The Stamp Project.” They discuss their person’s biography, accomplishments and achievements, why they picked the person and his or her impact on the world. They also list where they found their resources. Then, they create a stamp in that person’s honor to display.
    — Ruthann Paul-Suess

    The Arts

    African, and African-American, Music:
    I teach elementary music and used this theme as a basis for a unit of instruction. I spend three to four consecutive lessons in January showing music from Africa, the time of slavery and spirituals and then music of Dr. King’s era. If we have time, we go on to jazz for a week. The students sing, dance and play instruments to music while viewing PowerPoints, excerpts of concerts and books on the subject.
    — Christi Davis

    Interdisciplinary

    Social Justice as a Humanities Theme:
    We are implementing a new curriculum at our high school this year that links English/language arts with U.S. history in a thematic approach for our sophomores. Our theme for the third marking period is “social justice,” and deals with the period of Reconstruction and the civil rights movement. While the history students are learning about the Civil War amendments, the beginning of the Jim Crow South, the Harlem Renaissance and the important people and events from the 1950s and ’60s, they will be reading “To Kill a Mockingbird” in English. Teachers in both disciplines will be referring to the events going on in each others’ classes as a way to bring the time period more alive for them. They will also create a Ken Burns-style documentary about one of the figures from the period, complete with voice-over and images.
    — Dan Boyle

    Studying Forms of Protest in First Grade:
    I teach a first grade unit on this subject to a bilingual class. We have turned the focus to “forms of protest” as we use books and drama to portray Jim Crow laws and the various ways people reacted to the laws to try to change them. We include discussions about racism and current protests and how people can still try to change things when they see something that is not fair.
    — Meesa Jeans

    ‘A Century of African-American Firsts’:
    Here in Philadelphia, we begin African-American history month each year by teaching a lesson on Carter G. Woodson and his original purpose for “Negro History Week.” (Dr. Woodson’s purpose was for students, educators and parents to use February to demonstrate what what was learned about African-Americans all year.) The lessons and activities encourage students to creatively express their knowledge and understanding of African-American history. Students research multiple sources to write essays or poems; compose songs; create artwork or choreograph a dance to demonstrate their understanding of the theme.
    — Dana King

    A College to K-12 Partnership:
    Here in Walla Walla, Wash., we are taking action to go beyond the disheartening “F” that Washington State received in this [Southern Poverty Law Center] report. Whitman College and the Walla Walla Public Schools have joined forces, with training support from the law center, to start the Whitman Teaches the Movement initiative.

    One hundred Whitman students will go into more than 40 2nd-, 5th-, 7th- and 11th-grade classes to teach age-appropriate lessons about historical civil rights activities like the Greensboro lunch counter sit-ins (using the book “Freedom on the Menu”), Jackie Robinson and Pee Wee Reese (using the book “Teammates”), female civil rights activists, and Dr. King’s “Letter From Birmingham Jail.”

    We know that this singular project will not change the state’s standards. We also are aware, though, that we have a responsibility to do what we can to offset the low expectations in this critical area of American history. So, in our way, it’s a start.

    You can watch a newscast about our project on KVEW-TV.
    — Noah Leavitt

    ‘The Day I Made Everyone Move’:
    When I taught sixth grade several years ago, my principal came into my room the morning before M.L.K. weekend and said, “Remember, you have to do something for M.L.K. Day on Tuesday.” Most teachers read the “I Have a Dream” speech, or the “Letter From Birmingham Jail.” I thought: If my kids hear the words “I have a dream” again, they will revolt.

    On Tuesday morning, my sixth graders walked in to the room. A veritable rainbow coalition of races: white, Hispanic, black, Asian. When they took their seats, I told them that I decided to organize the class differently. I told them that I wanted three rows: The black children in the back row, the Hispanic students in the middle and the white and Asian kids in the front.

    Most of the students simply obeyed my instructions. Others began moving, but doing so very slowly and looking at me questioningly. One black girl, Carolyn, said, “Mr. Abadi, are you sure you can do this?” I replied, “Of course I can. It’s just a way for me to keep things organized.” Other kids chimed in and said things like, “It’s not fair…. Why do we have to sit in back?” I responded by saying, “Everyone will be treated the same. If anyone in the back row has a question, I will be happy to go back there and answer it.”

    Over the next 20 minutes or so, I heard many complaints, but most kids took their seats in the manner that I had prescribed. Carolyn spoke up again, not disrespectfully, but very seriously. She said something like, “Mr. Abadi, just separating us is like showing you have something against us.”

    So (and I am making this much shorter than it actually was) I turned to the class and said, “In 1954, the Supreme Court of the United States said exactly what Carolyn just said: Segregation in and of itself is discriminatory. And this is the type of legislation that Martin Luther King fought for during his entire life. Do you understand how important it is for all of you to be sitting in the same school, in the same classroom, and being able to demand things equally?”

    “So, Mr. Abadi, you did this just to teach us a lesson?”

    “Yes.”

    They all hurried back to their desired seating in the classroom.

    Carolyn came up to me at the end of the year and said she would never forget the day I made everyone move. It was a lesson they all remembered.
    — Abraham Abadi

    Resources Recommended by Teachers:

    ‘Eyes on the Prize’:
    I always take a couple of weeks to show the old Pepsi-sponsored, edited version of “Eyes on the Prize,” using the material provided with it along with additional readings (a lot of Dr. King speeches), and requiring a project focusing on a key individual other than Dr. King.
    — Bill Toscano

    Facing History and Ourselves:
    The focus on testing and accountability has made it difficult for classroom teachers to take the time to let students experience our own history, pushing more easily tested material and skills. What gets lost is the reflection on how we have become the nation we are today. In turn, our students begin to lose the ability to empathize and appreciate the brave people who struggled and fought for universal freedoms that we often take for granted.

    In an effort to curb this and teach valuable content with a methodology that will also prepare students for the dreaded standardized tests, like many teachers across the country have partnered with Facing History and Ourselves. While this wonderful organization began with a resource book focused on the Holocaust, it has expanded to include a brilliant compilation of materials for the civil rights movement in America as well. I suggest checking it out and attending a weeklong workshop, which then allows you to use its lending library as well.
    — Michael Schneider

    More About Facing History and Ourselves:
    I’m teaching 11th grade U.S. history here in New York City, and I’ve been relying heavily on the Facing History and Ourselves stuff, which has a central focus on social justice and emotional connection to content. I’ve been trying to use it for all my lessons, but they have some incredible civil rights resources.
    — Brodie Crawford

    Powerful Children’s Literature:
    As a school librarian, I use a lot of powerful children’s literature to talk about the civil rights movement, like Doreen Rappaport’s book, “The School Is Not White!” and books about Ruby Bridges. We also read about slavery, the Civil War, and events and social conditions that led to the civil rights era.

    Children’s literature provides a safe space in which children wonder, ask questions and empathize with the character’s situations. I provide additional support by sharing nonfiction titles with photographs from the civil rights era, like “Through My Eyes” by Ruby Bridges. Our school has a strong focus on social justice and students are accustomed to thinking about history from multiple perspectives. I see every student in the school every week, and I have found that a well-written picture book is far more powerful than reading history in a textbook.
    — Cheryl Gerken

    More Children’s Books:
    The books “Martin’s Big Words” by Doreen Rappaport, “If a Bus Could Talk” by Faith Ringgold, “The Other Side” by Jacqueline Woodson and “As Good as Anybody” by Richard Michelson are some of my favorite read-alouds.
    — Marcy Prager

    ‘Hip-Hop Speaks to Children’:
    I teach sixth graders in an after-school program. My kids love hip-hop, and I was fortunate that the musical anthology I ordered – “Hip-Hop Speaks to Children” – actually has a recording of M.L.K.’s “I Have a Dream” speech. I’m excited to use that recording to teach my kids about civil rights!
    — Lydia

    Rhythm, Rhyme, Results:
    Some great teaching materials for the civil rights movement and black history can be found at the home site of Rhythm, Rhyme, Results, one of the best hip-hop education companies in the United States. They have created excellent fact-based hip-hop songs that include songs, lyrics and custom-made worksheets for classroom or individual use. Perfect for grades 5 to 12. They are offering a free download of their educational rap song, “Civil Rights Movement,” through the end of February
    — Helen Jackson

    ‘Let Freedom Sing’:
    As a middle school music teacher, I found a wonderful resource for teaching the seventh grade unit on how music influenced the civil rights movement and vice-versa. The DVD is called “Let Freedom Sing” and is produced by Time-Life. It is one of the best documentaries I have ever found. Some of it is quite graphic and might need to be edited. Although I rarely allow my students to watch an entire film and prefer snippets to make my point, the kids ask to see more of this one.
    — Jacquelyn

      Michele Hemenway January 27, 2012 · 11:19 pm

      Americans Who Tell the Truth (AWTTT) is a portrait and biography series created by artist, Robert Shetterly, designed to offer “Models of Courageous Citizenship” to teachers and their students.

      Of the 176 portraits, more than 40 are used to help students create a visual timeline of the civil rights movement, which we define in the broadest sense, to include African-American rights, women´s suffrage, gay rights and other social justice issues.

      Beginning with slave uprisings, AWTTT shares the histories of each individual depicted and encourages students to become active in civil rights struggles.

      We begin with a look at the life and words of Frederick Douglass, helping students to understand his pivotal role in negotiating the end of slavery: “Where justice is denied, where poverty is enforced, where ignorance prevails, and where any one class is made to feel that society is an organized conspiracy to oppress, rob and degrade them, neither persons nor property will be safe.”

      AWTTT includes individuals not often studied in current history lessons, including – to name a few, Ida Tarbell, Claudette Colvin, Barbara Johns (specifically important with respect to Brown vs. The Board of Education), Jane Addams and Harry Hay. Each of the portraits and biographies highlights some aspect of the movement: suffrage, voting rights, school equity, etc. For example, we teach the biography of Shirley Chisholm, as many students are not aware that an African-American woman made a bid for the presidency. We include the biography of John Lewis to bring to light the courage of the Freedom Rides. His capacity to forgive the KKK member who beat him during the rides always has a big impact on students.

      In each case, we try to lift the veil that tends to oversimplify these histories. And, through dramatic role-play, using the example of selected figures, AWTTT teaches students how essential — and difficult –are the strategies related to non-violent resistance and civil disobedience. The songs of Pete Seeger, Woodie Guthrie and Paul Robeson help students understand the role of song and its power throughout the Civil Rights movement.

      Importantly, we do not confine Civil Rights to the 20th century; contemporary figures, including Eva Patterson, Van Jones and LeAlan Jones show students that gaining and protecting Civil Rights is an ongoing project.

      The entire story told through portraits will be exhibited at the Muhammad Ali Center in Louisville, KY in the fall of 2012.

      Jean Schmeichel January 30, 2012 · 2:31 pm

      Honoring MLK by Staying In School on His Birthday

      I would like to share with you the rationale for why South Lyon Community Schools does not call off school as our way of honoring Martin Luther King on his birthday. While making this a holiday would be the easy and politically correct thing to do, it is not consistent with King’s message or dream for schools and education. I am not sure King would be honored by this approach.

      Anyone who was privileged to march with King and hear him personally, as I was, came to appreciate the passion and depth of his commitment to equity of opportunity, of learning standards, and equity of results for ALL students. You can also gain this appreciation by listening to King give his speech. King pleaded for black, white and students of all backgrounds to learn side-by-side with common expectations and access to instruction, and he especially focused on the schools.

      Are we honoring King and his message by calling off school and separating students? By doing so we leave them to their own means if they are to have the opportunity to participate in activities that will help them learn about King and his civil rights message? Instead of having students of all backgrounds sitting side by side, we are separating them as they are in the larger society. Once again only the students from families resourced well enough or who value King’s dream will have the opportunity to participate and learn. What about the other students who need this equity of outcome? What about the ALL? To honor King’s dream of ALL students learning the same things in the same classrooms with the same teachers and to give ALL students the opportunity to learn the meaning of his message, SLCS keeps students in school and provides related learning activities for all. This is the way we honor King on his birthday and throughout the year.

      This year we will be reviewing the activities each building does on King’s birthday. We will continue to relate the activities to the building goals and curriculum while we also strengthen our systemic approach to celebrating King’s civil rights message. With ALL students in school, all students will have equity of opportunity, instruction and message outcome. Below are a few quotes from Dr. King for your reflection:

      “The ultimate tragedy is not the oppression and cruelty by the bad people, but the silence over that by the good people.”
      “The function of education is to teach one to think intensively and to think critically. Intelligence plus character—that is the goal of true education.”

      Dr. Jean Schmeichel
      Assistant Superintendent of CITA
      (Curriculum, Instruction, Technology, Assessment)

      I want my students to understand how Jim Crow laws had such an impact on black Americans. We study an excellent article on ‘Racial Etiquette’ from http://www.jimcrowhistory.org.

      Then, I want them to feel the strength that poured from black Americans as they fought these racist practices, so we study 5 poems by Langston Hughes. The tone is strong, the symbolism is clear, but the message is even clearer: “We shall not be denied!”
