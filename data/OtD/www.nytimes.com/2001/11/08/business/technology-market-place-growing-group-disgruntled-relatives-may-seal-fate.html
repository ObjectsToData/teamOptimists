    TECHNOLOGY: Market Place; A growing group of disgruntled relatives may seal the fate of Hewlett-Packard and Compaq.

    By Steve Lohr
    Published: November 8, 2001

    WITH David W. Packard joining the opposition to the proposed merger of Hewlett-Packard with Compaq Computer, executives and directors of both companies were scrambling yesterday to rescue the deal.

    The board of Compaq, based in Houston, met yesterday and issued a signed statement reaffirming its ''strong support'' for the deal, declaring itself ''more convinced than ever that the merger serves the best interests of the shareholders, customers, partners and employees.''

    And Carleton S. Fiorina, chief executive of Hewlett-Packard, made a series of calls on Tuesday and yesterday to major investors, urging them to support the merger, according to a person close to the company.

    Mr. Packard, the eldest son of one founder of the company, issued a statement late on Tuesday siding with Walter B. Hewlett, son of the other founder, who earlier in the day said he and his family's trust would oppose the merger. That places the planned combination under attack from a growing group of disgruntled relatives of the founders of the Silicon Valley company. The fate of the deal, analysts say, may well now hinge on whether other family members join in dissent.

    If the deal collapses, it would be blow for the leaders of both companies, but especially for Ms. Fiorina, who came in two years ago as a young, charismatic outsider with a mandate to reinvigorate Hewlett-Packard.

    Her tenure as chief executive has been a struggle, as she has tried to make the staid company more nimble and shift into faster-growth fields like services at a time when the computer business is in a slump.

    Some members of the Hewlett-Packard board were on a private conference call until 11 p.m. on Tuesday on the West Coast, discussing the new challenges to the deal, a board member said. Those board members, he said, continue to support the deal with Compaq as the right strategic choice for Hewlett-Packard in a difficult time for computer industry. Of Ms. Fiorina, he said, ''She is absolutely not at risk.''

    In his statement, Mr. Packard strongly suggested that the merger was a cruel departure from the values and corporate culture nurtured by the founders, William Hewlett and David Packard.

    ''The announced logic of this merger plan,'' he said, ''depends on massive employee layoffs'' at least 15,000, probably more. While he acknowledged that the founders never guaranteed job security, Mr. Packard added, ''I also know that Bill and Dave never developed a premeditated business strategy that treated H.P. employees as expendable.''

    ''While change is necessary and inevitable,'' Mr. Packard said, ''it does not follow that every innovation is an improvement.''

    Mr. Hewlett and the Hewlett family trusts own about 8 percent of Hewlett-Packard. Mr. Packard is the president and chairman of the Packard Humanities Institute, which holds 1.3 percent.

    In addition, the David and Lucile Packard Foundation owns 10.3 percent of the shares of Hewlett-Packard, and Mr. Packard's sister, Susan Packard Orr, is the chairwoman of that large family foundation.

    In his statement, Mr. Packard said he was not speaking for other family members or for the David and Lucile Packard Foundation. A spokesman for the foundation said yesterday that the foundation would not decide until after a Hewlett-Packard board meeting next month. The move to deliberate until next month came despite a call from the company on Tuesday, urging the foundation to lend its immediate support for the deal, said a person close to Hewlett-Packard.

    The foundation's choice could be crucial. ''The merger comes down to one question: Which way does the David and Lucile Packard Foundation vote,'' said Daniel Niles, an analyst for Lehman Brothers in San Francisco.

    A unified chorus of dissent from the family foundations would be a severe setback for a merger that has been given a chilly reception on Wall Street since it was announced in early September. And the accelerating slide in investment in computer technology and the turmoil in the stock market in the wake of the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks has depressed the shares of both Hewlett-Packard and Compaq further.

    On the last trading day before the deal was announced, the price of Hewlett-Packard shares closed at $23.21 and Compaq shares closed at $12.35. Yesterday, Hewlett-Packard shares closed at $19.18 a share and Compaq closed at $7.99 a share.

    Hewlett-Packard and Compaq hope they can weather the shareholder dissent and stock market turmoil, as they continue to try to convince investors of the long-term wisdom of the deal. Some industry analysts say that while the merger creates problems of integrating two big companies and cultures, hindering the business of both companies in the period of uncertainty, the merger may indeed prove to be a good move.

    ''Wall Street is understandably skeptical,'' said Richard Shaffer, principal of Technologic Partners, a research firm. ''But what is being ignored is that these two companies don't have a lot of choice.

    ''Much of the personal computer industry is in survival mode,'' he added. ''These two companies want to combine, consolidate in the PC business, create a larger services business in the corporate market, so they can emerge as a solid company and still be around when the slump ends.''
