Sarah Lawrence: Still Innovative, Still Cash-Poor

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: May 6, 2001

IN a recent student production of ''Romeo and Juliet'' here, the two lovers had to compete for audience attention with Asian drums, a police siren, a hit parade of oldies and a Shakespearean character (Mercutio) who kept reappearing after he was stabbed to death.

It was a deconstructed version of Shakespeare's tragedy, directed by Kit McKay, a student from Indianapolis, and the only thing that wasn't surprising about it was that the play was presented at Sarah Lawrence College -- one of the most liberal and resolutely experimental colleges in the United States.

Sarah Lawrence has among the lowest student-teacher ratios in the nation -- 6 to 1. Grades are virtually nonexistent, and there are few course requirements.

A recent visitor to the school's 40-acre campus here just over the border from Bronxville gathered much evidence of the sort that prompted the editors of the current Fiske Guide to Colleges to declare, ''The students and faculty at Sarah Lawrence College believe that there aren't any rules, and if they do exist they are made to be broken.''

That spirit has long imbued the 1,000-student college, and has remained through its transformation from a women's college to a co-ed school in 1968 -- a transformation that has been less successful than at other former single-sex schools.

But something else has lingered too -- a serious shortage of money.

Sarah Lawrence has been strapped for cash ever since its founding in 1926 by William Van Duzer Lawrence (he named the school after his wife, Sarah).

Mr. Lawrence, who made a fortune in patent medicines and real estate, died a year later -- before he had substantially endowed the school. Henry Noble MacCracken, then president of Vassar, saw Sarah Lawrence as a potential feeder of students to Vassar and came to its rescue by raising enough money to get the college on its feet by 1928. But money continued to be hard to come by.

Today, the school rests uneasily on an endowment of only $46.4 million. That sounds like a lot, but is small change compared with competitors like Williams ($1.3 billion) and Swarthmore ($963 million).

Sarah Lawrence ranked 410th on a list of college and university endowments for the year 2000 compiled by The Chronicle of Higher Education, behind many less prestigious colleges, and even behind many public universities.

The low endowment means that Sarah Lawrence is ''outgunned by a factor of 2 or 5 or even 10 by other small liberal arts colleges, and may lose students and faculty for that reason,'' said David L. Warren, president of the National Association of Independent Colleges and Universities.

It also means that Sarah Lawrence must charge the highest tuition and fees of any college in New York State -- $26,668 -- and the second highest in the nation, after Landmark College in Putney, Vt., which serves students with learning disabilities and charges $30,000, according to The Chronicle of Higher Education.

Room and board at Sarah Lawrence is an additional $8,460.

If the college is to preserve its cherished student-faculty ratio, keep faculty salaries competitive, maintain its physical plant, provide financial aid to needy students and meet the costs of the latest information-age electronics, the endowment must grow.

That's not easy, especially for a college whose alumni are mainly women. (Since women tend to earn less than men, professional fund-raisers recognize them as poor donors compared with men.)

But Michele Tolela Myers, a specialist in communications who became president of Sarah Lawrence in 1998, is undaunted. During her nine years as president of Denison University in Ohio, she helped increase that school's endowment to $275 million, from $75 million.

''Women give generously when properly motivated,'' she said. ''Men are quite happy to make unrestricted gifts. They don't care if you use the money to repair a leak in the roof. Women, on the other hand, want to make designated gifts. Only a few days ago, we received a gift of $250,000 from a woman who wanted to help us build a greenhouse -- which we need.

''Beyond that, I'm very passionate and I raise money well,'' said Dr. Myers, who speaks with the trace of a French accent. (She was born in Morocco and educated in Paris.) ''I go to people who have benefited from their education here and I tell them that I don't believe in mass education. I don't believe in mass lectures. I believe that the close encounter at Sarah Lawrence between students and faculty is the best way to educate. Sarah Lawrence is one of the last bastions in a world gone crazy.

''People believe that if something doesn't make money it isn't important. Something is not right about that.''

But Dr. Myers recognizes that the college's small endowment poses serious competitive challenges.

It is one reason for the high tuition, she said, along with the low student-faculty ratio, ''which means we must support greater number of faculty members with tuition dollars from fewer students.''

A major concern, she said, ''is the gap between the colleges that are getting wealthier and wealthier, building huge endowments, and colleges like ours that have no such resources.''

''My worry is what happens to the institutions that are less wealthy. They can lose faculty and they can lose students.''

To prevent such losses, Dr. Myers said, she raised faculty salaries by 10 percent two years ago.
