BOOKS OF THE TIMES; Seduction and Reduction On a British Campus

By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: June 5, 2001

THINKS . . .

By David Lodge

342 pages. Viking. $24.95.

Critic and novelist, academician and author of satiric novels about academia, David Lodge has often composed his comic fictions around a lofty literary or philosophical concept. The Arthurian quest romance served as a structural armature for his uproarious sendup of the academic conference circuit in ''Small World'' (1985). Shakespearean comedies yielded a narrative arc for his fish-out-of-water tale, ''Paradise News'' (1992). And the writings of Soren Kierkegaard provided a kind of ironic counterpoint to the hero's self-indulgent midlife crises in ''Therapy'' (1995).

In the case of Mr. Lodge's rather mechanical new novel, ''Thinks . . . ,'' it is the old C. P. Snow debate about Two Cultures -- science on one hand, the humanities on the other -- that is supposed to lend ballast and depth to an otherwise generic story of campus adultery set at a fictional British university.

The two main characters in ''Thinks'' -- Ralph Messenger, a notorious womanizer and the director of a cognitive sciences center; and Helen Reed, a recently widowed novelist -- engage, throughout the novel, in flirtatious banter about their differing views of the world. Despite Mr. Lodge's energetic efforts, this banter more often than not devolves into tired and predictable exchanges about art and technology, religion and behavioral science.

Ralph, who is known as a ''media don'' for his frequent television appearances on subjects like artificial intelligence, thinks of the individual human being as ''a machine that's been programmed by culture not to recognize that it's a machine'' and consciousness as a form of information processing. He does not believe in God, does not believe in the concept of the soul; and he has a Darwinian explanation for virtually all human actions and beliefs. He thinks it would be interesting to try to ''discover the mechanism behind laughter'' by getting a computer ''to crunch millions of jokes instead of numbers.''

Helen, who has written several semi-autobiographical novels and who is now teaching a creative writing class, rejects Ralph's arguments as cynical and reductive. She defends the primacy of the individual and the mysteries of human emotion and argues that ''human consciousness is uniquely capable of imagining that which is not physically present to the senses, capable of imagining things which do not exist, capable of creating imaginary worlds (like novels) and capable of abstract thought.''

Though Mr. Lodge uses his antic sense of humor and novelistic craft to try to enliven Helen and Ralph's debates, there is something canned about their discussions: the reader can feel the author trying to shoehorn into their talks all sorts of research about cognitive science and familiar humanist arguments about the perils of scientific hubris.

It is when Mr. Lodge turns instead to describing the day-to-day dramas and high jinks at the University of Gloucester that the novel actually begins to engage the reader's interest. As he did in earlier academic satires like ''Changing Places,'' ''Small World'' and ''Nice Work,'' he uses his heat-seeking eye for detail and radar-sharp ear for dialogue to humorously chronicle the pretensions and prevarications that flourish in a small college community.

The politically correct one-upmanship that thrives at faculty parties, the rivalries among professors for recognition both on campus and off and the efforts of students to ingratiate themselves with their teachers -- all are expertly and wittily rendered. We hear how a dean's descriptions of the campus strike Helen as having ''a faintly used feel,'' like ''paper that has lost its crispness by being handled too frequently.'' And we hear the pompous gibberish that passes for wisdom at a prestigious university conference.

From the very start of the novel the reader is supposed to wonder whether Ralph will succeed in his campaign to seduce Helen. Will she put aside her grief for her dead husband and her moral scruples about committing adultery to begin an affair with a married man whose ideas strike her as repellent? Is Ralph's wife, Carrie, really a long-suffering victim, or has she been carrying on an affair of her own? And what about Martin, Helen's late husband: is it possible that he, too, once had an affair? And with a young woman who just happens to be one of Helen's new students?

The reason we can't get very interested in these questions is that both Ralph and Helen -- who talk to us in the first-person through their respective journals -- feel like stick figures, spouting their respective views in the Two Cultures debate. Worse, the meticulous balance Mr. Lodge achieved between satire and sympathy in earlier books like ''Therapy'' and ''Paradise News'' is curiously lacking in this novel.

Ralph is a parody of the male chauvinist pig: a randy, puffed-up man who sees women as creatures to be conquered and then tidily disposed of. And while Helen starts off sympathetically enough, she grows increasingly narcissistic as the novel progresses. What's more, she often comes across like an oddly synthetic creation: it's hard to believe that this prim, proper admirer of Henry James would write a novel featuring a sex scene about bondage and masks; it's equally hard to believe that she would fall in love with the odious Ralph.

Indeed, the reader's suspicions that the talented Mr. Lodge has written this book on automatic pilot only grow in the novel's final pages, which feature a host of highly improbable developments including a cancer scare, a campus sex scandal involving child pornography, student protests concerning the Ministry of Defense and the arrival of one of Ralph's former lovers, who has blackmail on her mind. These problems, lobbed at a furious pace at the characters like so many tennis balls from an automatic ball machine, not only feel ridiculously contrived, but they also suggest that Mr. Lodge was trying in vain to provide this otherwise lackadaisical novel with a big finish.
