Commissioner Tells Schools To Use Tests, Not Portfolios

By LYNETTE HOLLOWAY
Published: April 26, 2001

State Education Commissioner Richard P. Mills yesterday once again rejected end-of-year projects as alternatives for the Regents exams required for high school graduation, citing concerns about erratic grading patterns at schools that use such assessments.

The ruling dealt a critical blow to a group of 37 small alternative schools -- most of them in New York City -- and is likely to force them to revamp their unconventional curriculums so that their students can pass challenging statewide exams in five subjects. Their curriculum, the schools say, is the heart of what makes them alternative.

Mr. Mills had made his original decision about 15 months ago, but he commissioned a panel of outside experts to review the projects and portfolios used by the alternative schools after an outcry from parents, teachers and students, who worried that the decision would force teachers to direct their teaching to the standardized tests for English, social studies, science, college-level mathematics and a foreign language.

The report by the six-member expert panel found that the grading systems at the 37 schools did not always conform to the academic requirements used by the state, but urged Mr. Mills to give the schools three more years to bring themselves up to state standards. Yet Mr. Mills said yesterday that the schools had been given more than enough time.

Many of the city's alternative schools rely mostly on portfolio work, which might include a research project, an internship, a literary essay, a science experiment and a mathematical project.

The ruling continued to push the state in the direction of a single standard for public high school graduation, which has become increasingly common nationally. But it is also causing political concern because it means that a high school diploma will become more difficult for many students to earn.

Parents of students from the city's schools have united with parents from suburban districts, including Scarsdale in Westchester County, to protest the proliferation of standardized tests. Scarsdale argues that the tests force it to change a curriculum it sees as more rigorous and creative. Some parents plan to attend a rally next month and urged the Board of Regents to fire Mr. Mills.

''The commissioner is about to destroy our schools,'' said Jane R. Hirschmann, chairwoman of a consortium of parents with students at the alternative schools. ''There is no one way of learning.''

Accompanied by dozens of parents, teachers and students, Ms. Hirschmann held a news conference at Urban Academy on the East Side yesterday. The group announced its support for a bill that calls for the Legislature to set up regional assessment panels under the control of the State Board of Regents instead of the commissioner. But Mr. Mills is carrying out the mandate of the Board of Regents. Nevertheless, it is unlikely the bill will pass, because a spokesman for Assembly Speaker Sheldon Silver said it does not have his support.

The city's schools chancellor, Harold O. Levy, criticized Mr. Mills's decision yesterday. ''At its core, this decision undermines our ability to provide a wide variety of educational options for our children,'' Mr. Levy said in a statement. ''Both as a member of the Regents and now as chancellor, I support alternative education models.''

Mr. Levy had supported Mr. Mills's earlier stand requiring English Regents exams, but in an interview he said he was concerned that requiring Regents exams in other subjects would change the nature of the alternative schools.

In yesterday's ruling, which followed a yearlong study to determine whether to exempt the schools from Regents exams in social studies, science and college-level mathematics, Mr. Mills said he would require the schools to give the English exam to all of their students. Students who enroll starting this fall must begin a program that offers all of the Regents exams, or an approved alternative, like the SAT II in math or the International Baccalaureate.

Vincent P. Brevetti, director of Humanities Preparatory High School in Manhattan, said that the ruling would significantly change the culture of his school of 175 students, many of whom are making a second go at school. He said he planned to hold an emergency staff meeting to figure out a plan of attack.

''Students will be grade-conscious,'' Mr. Brevetti said. ''Kids left back will do portfolios, but those who are new will take the Regents. That will seem odd.''
