Bagman for Democracy

By David M. Oshinsky
Published: July 15, 2001

AMERICA AND THE INTELLECTUAL COLD WARS

IN EUROPE

Shepard Stone Between Philanthropy, Academy, and Diplomacy.

By Volker R. Berghahn.

Illustrated. 373 pp. Princeton, N.J.:

Princeton University Press. $39.50.

When one thinks about the cold war today, images of political confrontation, military crisis, scientific competition and athletic rivalry come to mind. After World War II, however, a few Americans waged a subtler campaign designed to win over European intellectuals who viewed the United States as an inferior culture, unworthy of respect. In his excellent new book, ''America and the Intellectual Cold Wars in Europe,'' Volker R. Berghahn, the Seth Low professor of history at Columbia, examines this campaign through the eyes of Shepard Stone, a Zelig-like American bureaucrat who devoted his life to the cause. In doing so, Berghahn combines biography and cultural history to provide a deft portrait of European-American relations in the modern era.

As a military intelligence officer during World War II, Stone bore witness to Hitler's crushing defeat. A devotee of the ''high culture'' that filled Germany's museums and concert halls, he believed in the ability of small elites -- in business, the arts and the media -- to rebuild West Germany as a democratic, capitalist state. In 1949, after working as an editor at The New York Times, he signed on as public affairs director for the United States High Commission for Germany, where his main task was to encourage a ''free'' and friendly press through hidden subsidies to favored newspapers. Secret payments to editors and politicians were common at this time, with the C.I.A. distributing large sums throughout Western Europe as part of a larger offensive against left-wing propaganda. Stone believed such payments to be ethical because those receiving the money already held clear-cut pro-American views.

Stone's tenure at the commission mirrored the troubled politics of that age. In 1953, the Eisenhower administration, responding to pressure from Red-hunters like Senator Joseph McCarthy, issued a cowardly directive banning the works of so-called ''controversial persons'' from the United States government's overseas libraries. Though Stone's office reluctantly complied, Stone left the commission in 1954 to become director of international affairs at the Ford Foundation.

It seemed a perfect fit. The foundation was swimming in money. Stone already had access to intellectuals and public officials on both sides of the Atlantic, and he could begin new projects without fretting about government interference. Furthermore, he knew that Russia's standing in Western Europe had plummeted, that no amount of Communist propaganda could wipe away the crimes of Stalin, the brutal coup in Czechoslovakia or the scenes of Soviet tanks rumbling through Budapest. At Ford he could focus less on the evils of Communism, which were self-evident, and more on the virtues of the United States.

This new strategy seemed essential. Contempt for American culture ran deep in European intellectual thought, and Berghahn describes it well. Some writers perceived the United States in 19th-century terms, as a wild and dangerous place. Others saw it as a technological wasteland, dominated by mass production and mindless consumption. Still others blamed its ''vulgarity'' on a political leveling process that promoted egalitarianism at the expense of elitism. By the 1950's, Elvis Presley seemed to epitomize American culture to these intellectuals, along with Joe McCarthy and Marilyn Monroe.

Stone had little sympathy for this grumbling. He knew that snobbery and resentment had blinded many European intellectuals to America's growing leadership in the fields of architecture, fine arts, music and literature, and that, in truth, the United States had now surpassed Europe in the sciences, the humanities and, most important, the strength of its democratic civic culture. For Stone, the remedy was clear. He would start a campaign ''to let the Europeans see an America that was different from their customary stereotypes.''

Berghahn spends much energy -- perhaps too much -- on the minute details of this campaign. What comes through, nonetheless, is Stone's relentless pursuit of European-American cooperation. In an era when the United States government looked suspiciously on the funding of cultural projects, the Ford Foundation spent millions to support student exchanges, organize scholarly conferences and send American writers, poets and artists abroad. It provided seed money to the International Institute for Strategic Studies in London and the Institute for European Sociology in Paris. Its grants subsidized colleges at Oxford and Cambridge, and programs at the Free University of Berlin.

The largess didn't end there. A good chunk of money went to the Congress for Cultural Freedom, a cantankerous group of Western intellectuals devoted to democratic governance and the exposure of Stalinist lies. Because so much has already been written about this group, Berghahn finds himself on familiar ground. He contends-correctly, I believe -- that Stone's affinity for the congress was based less on its trademark anti-Communism than on its ability to recruit prominent intellectuals in combating Europe's ''cultural superiority complex.''

Stone also knew what others only suspected: the congress was getting subsidies from the C.I.A. Once again, he faced the issue of secret government funding. Should Ford knowingly support a group receiving such money? Stone answered yes. Like others ''who had witnessed the rise of fascism and Stalinism and who participated in World War II,'' Berghahn writes, Stone endorsed ''the principle of cooperation between government and private organizations.'' Exposure came in 1966, effectively ending Stone's career at Ford. Seeing no better prospects, he became president of the successor to the disbanded C.C.F., the International Association for Cultural Freedom, which limped along until its formal demise in 1979. Stone died in a car crash in 1990, on his way to a conference about European-American affairs.

As a new century dawns, Stone's vision of two continents in harmony remains elusive. Yet in contrast to the 1950's, Berghahn concludes, the Atlantic is again ''a two-lane highway,'' with American mass culture and technology reshaping Europe, and European goods and immigrants reshaping the United States. Shepard Stone would be pleased.
