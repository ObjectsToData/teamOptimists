BOOKS ON HEALTH; Poignant Tales From the Front Lines of Medicine

By John Langone
Published: March 13, 2001

''A Piece of My Mind,'' edited by Roxanne K. Young, AMA Press, $34.

There is an especially touching moment in this collection of essays from The Journal of the American Medical Association that reveals how a simple common interest, an instance of shared humanity, can strengthen an otherwise tense relationship between a physician and a patient.

A 56-year-old man, down and out and demented, shuffles into an examining room and gives evasive and hostile answers to the doctor's medical questions. Frustrated, the doctor is ready to refer the man elsewhere.

Then, the doctor asks the man about his line of work. The man says he played the trombone and recounts gigs with John Coltrane, Miles Davis and Dizzy Gillespie. Interested, the doctor tells the patient that he, too, plays, the saxophone.

''Hey, that's real cool, I got a sax-playin' doc,'' says the patient. Later, the patient is filling out a Mini-Mental State Examination and is failing miserably until he comes to the final item, which directs him to write a sentence. The patient slowly draws the notes of a measure of music on a horizontal line. ''What does it say?'' the doctor asks. The patient smiles proudly. ''Boom, boom, diggiduh bmm-boom,'' he says. The doctor gives him a point.

All physicians have stories about what it takes to care for the sick, just as all patients can recall and relate their feelings about the emotional toll of an illness. But this link between medicine and humanity, between the sometimes cold and calculated world of science and the personal, is not always evident, least of all in the medical journals, with their clinical reports on the causes, treatments and cures of diseases and disorders. The Journal of the American Medical Association has published such clinical reports ever since its founding in 1883, but it has also regularly offered something else: a weekly Piece of My Mind column, with tales that bridge the gap between science and the humanities and look at human experiences with disease, healing, violence, heartbreak and death. Here, we read what it is like to hold a living heart in one's hands (''You can read, as if in Braille, the brittle threads of atherosclerosis''), how a simple touch can convince one that he or she is not a faceless case, how an orderly listens to a patient talk about dying of cancer while his family pretends he has only a virus.

''Often, physician-writers want simply to acknowledge and honor their patients by telling their stories,'' says Roxanne K. Young, the director of medical humanities for the journal. ''They want to thank them for helping them come to grips not only with death, but with life.''
