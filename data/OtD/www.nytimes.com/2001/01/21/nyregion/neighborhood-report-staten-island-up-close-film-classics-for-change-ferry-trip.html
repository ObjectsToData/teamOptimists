NEIGHBORHOOD REPORT: STATEN ISLAND UP CLOSE; Film Classics (For a Change, Ferry Trip Not Needed)

By JIM O'GRADY
Published: January 21, 2001

Last Wednesday, an anti-authoritarian prostitute named Salome explained to a would-be assassin of Mussolini that she was helping him in memory of an anarchist she once knew.

''I swore in my heart to poor Anteo that one day that pig dictator would die a pig's death,'' she declared between drags on an unfiltered cigarette as an audience of about 120 Staten Islanders watched on a 20-foot-wide movie screen.

The viewers were sitting in an old stone chapel at the Snug Harbor Cultural Center that had been converted for the night into a revival house by the Staten Island Film Society. They seemed riveted by Lena Wertmuller's 1973 film, ''Love and Anarchy,'' the first of eight offerings that the newly reborn society plans to screen every other Wednesday night until May.

On screen, the characters' fates were being complicated by love, and the success of the assassination plot was seeming doubtful. But the full house watching the story unfold in flickering 16-millimeter images left little doubt that the society's third season was off to a rousing start.

Jenny Tango, one of the evening's organizers, said the society was born in 1998 out of frustration with the borough's three multiplex theaters, which show classic foreign films as often as Rudolph Valentino, who died in 1926, makes a movie these days.

''All my friends would say we wished there was a place on Staten Island where we could see foreign and independent films without trekking into Manhattan,'' she said. So Ms. Tango, a painter, and her friend Ed Davin, a photographer, formed the society, which charges $40 for a ticket that lets a member and a guest view eight films. People can also pay $10 to see a single film.

Ms. Tango and Mr. Davin drew 110 paying members in their first season and 90 in the next, enough to keep the nonprofit program afloat. But the effort exhausted them, and they halted operations in 2000. They resumed this year with an $1,800 grant from the Coalition of the Arts and Humanities on Staten Island, an arts support group, which helps them pay for programmatic flourishes like the pianist who is scheduled to play at the March 14 showing of the 1928 silent ''Pandora's Box,'' starring Louise Brooks.

Mr. Davin begins each presentation by providing backstage tidbits about the film being shown. ''Lena Wertmuller was very temperamental,'' he observed on Wednesday night. ''She bullied the actors.'' Afterward, Ms. Tango led a dozen members in a discussion of the film. ''I found him to be a fanatic and a madman,'' one member said of the assassin in ''Love and Anarchy.''

A first-time member, Robin Lockmonda, praised the event. ''It's terrific not to have to make that terrible schlep into Manhattan to see a film you can enjoy,'' she said. JIM O'GRADY
