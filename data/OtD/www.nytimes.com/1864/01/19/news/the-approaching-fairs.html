The Approaching Fairs.

Published: January 19, 1864

The great Fairs for the benefit of the Sanitary Commission, to be held next month in Brooklyn and the month after in New-York, are already exciting an almost unprecedented social interest in the two communities. Churches, schools, business corporations, literary and art associations, entertainment clubs, and every walk of private life, high and humble, are almost all manifesting a purpose to cooperate in these grand efforts for our sick and our wounded soldiers.

We have, indeed, heard of individuals -- one or two of them in the most sacred of all callings, -- incredible as it may appear, who have exhibited coldness and even aversion toward these enterprises; but such persons are rare exceptions. It is hard to conceive how such insensibility can be produced in this Christian part of the world. It is so essentially pagan -- so identical with the spirit that ruled in the ancient nations, which had no such idea as brotherhood, no such word as charity -- that we would almost as soon think of hearing of heathen sacrifices in one of our churches, or one of our households. We live under a religion that tells us to succor the distressed, whoever they may be -- to feed even our enemies.

The Sanitary Commission, rightly considered, is one of the noblest Christian manifestations of the age. War has tracked the earth with blood from time immemorial; but not until now has it ever been attended by an angel of charity. The great religious wars of the seventeenth century, and the great political wars of the eighteenth and early part of the nineteenth century, were equally unblessed, as all previous wars had been, by any organized effort to mitigate the awful suffering which comes from the privations of the camp, hardships of the march, and from the shock of battle. Christian civilization had advanced, but war still remained essentially barbarous. An ancient battle-field and a modern battle-field differed little but in the instruments of the slaughter. Cannae and Waterloo were in most respects the same. The ancient TACITUS, who portrayed the plain covered with bodies, gashed and mangled, with broken limbs, and men and horses in one promiscuous carnage, clotted gore and filth -- the whole a dreary waste, the desolation of nature shocking to humanity, -- made a battle scene that in his day was complete. And it vividly describes every great battle that has occurred, down to the epoch of the American rebellion. But now new figures come into the picture -- ministers of mercy who flee to the relief of every stricken soldier with all remedies and comforts; who nurse him with all the tenderness and all the patience of a brothers love if he can live, and who, if he is to die, receive his last message for his loved ones at home, and cheer his spirit as it takes its flight to the other world. What ROBKRT HALL said in the last century is no longer true, that war is a temporary repeal of all the principles of virtue. There are, in fact, principles of virtue which have never flourished more than during this rebellion. Never were there nobler sacrifices than by our soldiers never sweeter humanities than by our soldiers friends.

The Sanitary Commission is peculiarly the emanation of the Christian patriotism of the land; and it has done a work for the war which will be as memorable as the war. The receipts of its central treasury, in cash, from its establishment, two and a half years ago, exclusive of what has gone into the minor treasuries of its many branches, have exceeded a million of dollars. Its plan was most admirably conceived in every detail, and has been carried out with an administrative ability and fidelity seldom equaled in any public enterprise. The amount of good it has accomplished in preventing and relieving suffering is incalculable. The best idea of it may be gained by a single comparative statement of results in respect to the saving of life.

The average annual loss of the British army during the Peninsular war was one hundred and sixty-five out of every thousand, of whom 113 died by disease or accident. In the Crimean war, the British army lost in July, August and September at the rate of two hundred and ninety-three out of every thousand, of whom 96 per cent. were by disease. During the next three months, their loss was at the annual rate of five hundred and eleven out of every thousand, seven-eighths of which loss was by disease. During the three months following, their loss was at the annual rate of nine hundred and twelve out of every thousand, of which 98 per cent. was by disease. On the other hand, our armies, up to the middle of last May, had lost only at the annual rate of fifty-three per thousand, and only 44 per cent. of that loss was by disease and accident. Of course it would not be right to attribute this enormous difference solely to the efforts of the Sanitary Commission. Many minor influences have contributed to it, but there can be no question that it is very largely due to the wise and untiring exertions of this agency.

The peculiar glory of this Commission is, that its splendid work enures alike to patriotism and to humanity. It is not only human life that is saved, but soldierly service for the country. Thousands of veterans are now fighting the battles of the Republic, who, but for the care of this great sanitary agency, in warding off and relieving disease, and in nursing the wounded, would now be in their graves, of be incapable of service by physical disability. Our people are appreciating better than ever the vast public benefit thus realized, for the immense bounties tell of the worth of tried soldiers with a directness not to be mistaken.

The Commission has to rely upon the people alone for the means wherewith to carry on this sublime work. The people have nobly responded to every call made by it heretofore. But its treasury is now nearly exhausted, and there is necessity for special efforts to enable it to meet its duties in the great Spring campaigns which will soon begin, and which, it is hoped, will sweep all that remains of the rebellion to the dust. It is with extreme satisfaction that we mark the earnestness with which New-York and Brooklyn are preparing to do their whole duty is staying up an agency which has so blessed the country. They have adopted methods which need only the continued stimulus of the spirit now prevailing, to secure most magnificent results.
