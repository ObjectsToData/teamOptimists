ITALIAN-AMERICAN PATRIOT'S 250TH BIRTHDAY TO BE CELEBRATED AT FAIRLEIGH DICKENSON

ITALIAN-AMERICAN PATRIOT'S 250TH BIRTHDAY TO BE CELEBRATED AT FAIRLEIGH DICKENSON; MADISON

By S.J. HORNER
Published: April 19, 1981

THE 250th birthday of Philip Mazzei, the Italian-American patriot, will be celebrated next Saturday at Fairleigh Dickinson University here.

The program, called ''Philip Mazzei's Role: Age of the Enlightment'' and financed by the state's Committee for the Humanities, is open to the public. It will offer lectures, discussion, exhibitions and Italian songs and dances.

''Move over, Benjamin Franklin -and Lafayette and Kosciusko, too,'' said Sister Margherita Marchione of Morristown, professor of Italian language and literature at Fairleigh Dickinson and director of the program. ''Make room in the history books for a brilliant contemporary who, like you, was tirelessly devoted to the cause of American independence.''

''I've almost rivaled Mazzei as a world traveler,'' said Sister Margherita in recounting her trips abroad that resulted in her tracking down almost 3,000 documents. The memorabilia, stored at the Salvatori Center for Mazzei Studies at 455 Western Avenue, Morristown, include copies of his correspondence with the first five Presidents, from Washington to Monroe.

Before Mazzei arrived in America at the age of 43, Sister Margherita said, he already had earned ''by his insight and experience the title of 'Citizen of the World.' ''

Born in rural Tuscany in 1730 - Italy was not yet unified - he early relinquished the practice of surgery and enjoyed prosperity as a merchant of cheese, wine and olive oil in London. An invitation from Benjamin Franklin in 1773 brought him to Virginia, then under English rule.

Besides cuttings from Tuscan vines to stimulate Colonial commerce, Mazzei also brought some farmers and tools for his new home, as well as a tailor and a ''consort,'' whom he soon married, to avoid ''the mere suspicion of concubinage.''

Almost immediately, Mazzei became so caught up in the events leading to the American Revolution that he joined Jefferson in writing articles of protest against the British.

Returning to Europe for good in 1785 and assuming myriad diplomatic entanglements there, Mazzei continued to promote the ''goodness and unity'' of the new nation. He wrote four volumes on American history, and referred to himself as a ''citizen of Virginia'' until his death in 1816 at age 86.

''He's so Italian,'' Sister Margherita said, ''in his love for women, for good conversation and food - and in his lifelong advocacy of political and religious freedom for all men.''

Her new book, ''Philip Mazzei: My Life & Wanderings'' (American Institute of Italian Studies, 455 Western Avenue, Morristown; 472 pages, $9.95 paperback, $14.95 hardbound), translates into English a 905-page autobiographical letter that Mazzei wrote when he was 80. It contains 32 pages of illustrations.

The book provides an often-uninhibited account of life two centuries ago, including the tastes of Virginia, the terror of the French Revolution and the tragedy of Louis XIV, ''who, unlike his predecessors, never had any desire for any woman other than his wife (Marie Antoinette), who never had any for him.''

Sister Margherita indicated that ''generous support'' from national and regional organizations and individuals also had enabled her to edit a nine-reel microfilm edition of Mazzei's papers covering the period fropm 1730 to 1816. Proceeds from the book will go toward further studies.

''I can't tell you how happy I am about this program because it especially demonstrates the good deeds and contributions of Italians to this country,'' said Vincent Visceglia of Short Hills, chairman of the board of Summit Associates Inc. of Edison. Summit Associates is a developer of office, commercial and industrial parks.

Mr. Visceglia, who financed the publication of the Mazzei autobiography, came from Bari, Italy, to Jersey City in 1924. He worked days while studying nights at Dickinson High School.

''Besides Mazzei's love for this country,'' he said, ''I'm proud of his mastery of English, which for him was a new language, as it was for me.''

Dr. Peter Sammartino of Rutherford, president emeritus of Fairleigh Dickinson, said he would like to see greater recognition of Italy's part in the ''creation of America.'' Dr. Sammartino, who founded Fairleigh Dickinson in 1942, first suggested the study of Mazzei to Sister Margherita.

''Italians,'' he noted, ''published more books about America than any other group between the 15th and early 19th centuries.'' Formerly a civil engineer, Frank Raciti of Warren now devotes all his time to philately and, especially, to the new 40-cent Mazzei postage stamp. It is significant, he said, as ''the first U.S. air mail to feature a person of foreign birth.''

Discussing Mazzei, Filomena Peloro del Olmo of Bloomfield, Professor of Education at Fairleigh Dickinson's Rutherford campus and who will participate in the performance of Italian songs and dances on Saturday, observed:

''Although Mazzei seems to have had a good life, his real luck was falling into Sister Margherita's lap. Wherever she goes on his behalf, the doors spring open, too.''

The program on Saturday will begin at 9 A.M., and there is no charge for admission. The university is at 285 Madison Avenue.
