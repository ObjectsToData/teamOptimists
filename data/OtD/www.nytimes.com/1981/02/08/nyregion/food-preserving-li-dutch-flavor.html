Food; PRESERVING L.I. DUTCH FLAVOR

By FLORENCE FABRICANT
Published: February 8, 1981

WEST SAYVILLE WEST Sayville overlooks the Great South Bay, that once-teeming repository of succulent shellfish, so it was only natural that Dutch immigrant settlers would cluster here and set out their rakes for harvesting their favorite catch - oysters. From the 1840's until the turn of the century, Dutch settlers arrived directly from the Netherlands or by way of Lodi, N.J., to engage in oystering. But by the 1950's, the bluepoint oyster industry on the Great South Bay was all but defunct, and the Dutch identity was disappearing.

For the last 18 months, the Suffolk Marine Museum here has been conducting a research project devoted to the oyster industry and Dutch community history, and thereby hangs the tale of a cookbook and an attempt to preserve the Dutch heritage.

Peggy Holsten, who is directing the project under a matching grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities, said that the original idea had been to study the oyster industry.

''It had never been thoroughly researched in this area before,'' she said. ''The Suffolk Marine Museum is the only marine museum in the county that is not devoted to whaling. I began holding Wednesday evening oral history sessions, to record the memories of the old oystermen, some of whom are now in their 80's and 90's, and it became evident that most of them were first- and second-generation Dutch. So Dutch community history became part of the project.''

One tangible result of the research is the recent publication of ''A Rare Blend, '' a small paperbound cookbook of Dutch and seafood recipes. The book, edited by Miss Holsten and Mary Libaire, was published to benefit the museum.

''We had not intended to do a cook-b ook at first,'' Miss Holsten said. ''It came about almost by accident. One evening a year ago December, one of the men in the group brought a special homemade Christmas drink called 'boer en jungen' (farmer boys) to the meeting. It is made of raisins, sugar and whisky that have been soaking for three weeks. So we started talking about food.''

A problem arose almost immediately. The wives of the former oystermen were the ones who knew the recipes, but they were very reluctant to participate. According to Miss Holsten, there is a tradition called ''menschenschur,'' which means ''people shy,'' and insists that one avoid the limelight.

''Men did not feel that way,'' Miss Holsten said, ''only the women. A number of them did not even want their names on the recipes in the book and they refused to be photographed. At first, they did not even want to talk to me because they considered me an outsider.'' Miss Holsten is from Massapequa.

''Gradually we convinced them of the importance of what we were trying to do,'' she said. ''They were the only ones who could provide this information for posterity. As it is, most of the Dutch heritage will probably vanish with them. Today there are no longer any of the Dutch shops left in the community. But the Volunteer Fire Department is still called the Flying Dutchmen.''

In addition to the cookbook, the project involved an exhibition, now being prepared for showing in early May, called ''Oysters, Dutchmen and the Bay.'' A book entitled ''Dutchmen of the Bay'' is also being written.

The cookbook contains a number of simple, hearty Dutch recipes for pea soups, stews, warm salads and sweets as well as recipes for oysters and other Long Island shore fish. The Dutch recipes include many versions of doughnuts, said to have been introduced in America by settlers from the Netherlands. The recipes are interspersed with historical and anecdotal morsels for the delectation of the reader as well as the cook.

The book may be obtained from the Suffolk Marine Museum, Montauk Highway, West Sayville, N.Y. 11796. The price is $3 at the museum, $4 postage paid. Here are several recipes from ''A Rare Blend. '' ROODE KOOL (Red Cabbage) 2 pounds red cabbage 3 apples, peeled, cored and thinly sliced 3 tablespoons bacon fat or butter 1 cup cider vinegar 2 tablespoons dark brown sugar 1 teaspoon cinnamon Salt and freshly ground pepper

1. Shred cabbage and place in a heavy three-quart saucepan with a tight-fitting lid. Add apples, bacon fat or butter, vinegar, brown sugar, cinnamon and salt and pepper to taste. Stir to combine ingredients.

2. Cover tightly and cook over medium heat for 10 minutes, until cabbage is just tender. Uncover and stir for a minute or two. Serve. Yield: Four to six servings. OYSTER FRITTERS 2 eggs 11/3 cup flour 2 teaspoons baking powder 1/2 cup milk 1 pint oysters or clams, drained and chopped Salt and freshly ground black pepper Oil for frying Tartar sauce

1. Beat eggs until light. Sift flour and baking powder together and add to eggs alternately with milk. Add oysters or clams. Season with salt and generously with pepper.

2. Heat oil to depth of one-half inch in a heavy skillet. Drop batter by teaspoonsful into oil. Turn when browned on one side, brown second side, drain on absorbent paper and serve. Tartar sauce may be served on the side.

Yield: 24 fritters. OLIE KOOKEN (Dutch Cookies)

Nellie Quaak Daane, who contributed this recipe, described these as a tasty, easy-to-make doughnut served traditionally at birthday celebrations. 4 eggs 1 1/2 cups sugar 4 cups flour 4 teaspoons baking powder 1/2 teaspoons salt 1 scant cup lukewarm milk 2 cups raisins Oil for deep frying Granulated or powdered sugar (optional)

1. Beat eggs and sugar together until thick and light. 2. Sift flour, baking powder and salt together and stir in alternately with milk. Stir in raisins. 3. Heat oil for deep frying to 350 degrees. Drop batter by halfteaspoonsful into hot oil and fry until nicely browned, turning once. If the doughnuts are frying at proper temperature, they will probably turn themselves. Do not crowd them in the pan. It is a good idea to fry one first, as a test, to see if it is done in the center. If not, make the doughnuts smaller or lower the temperature slightly.

4. Drain on absorbent paper. Doughnuts may be dusted with powdered or granulated sugar when cool. Yield: Eight to nine dozen small doughnut balls.
