EDUCATION

By GENE I. MAEROFF
Published: June 23, 1981

DONALD NEVINS, who majored in history at the College of Staten Island, has left behind Caesar and Charlemagne and gone on to work as a broker on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange, where an understanding of the causes of the Peloponnesian War probably never earned anyone an extra buck.

While Mr. Nevins, a 1977 graduate, did not study history with the intention of trading General Motors shares, he says there is no reason why liberal arts majors should not be able to succeed in the marketplace.

Not all corporate recruiters seem to endorse Mr. Nevins's hypothesis. Liberal arts majors, particularly those in the humanities, have grown accustomed in recent years to being rebuffed by business and industry, though a few graduates like Mr. Nevins have been fortunate enough to get jobs in fields that have no outward relationship to their undergraduate studies.

As business has grown more complex, students who majored in technical areas and gained a grasp of such specialties as finance and marketing have won increasing favor in the job market. In turn, openings have diminished proportionally. Students have abandoned such disciplines as history, literature and philosophy, moving instead to career-oriented courses that will provide specific job skills.

The Rev. T.P. O'Malley, president of John Carroll University in University Heights, Ohio, said, ''The almost overwhelming trend in higher education is directed away from the college student's formation as a 'whole' man or woman, and is directed, by means of increasingly early and narrow specialization, towards obtaining immediately lofty employment.'' Father O'Malley devoted his 1980 president's report to ''Liberal Education and the World of Work.''

Now, however, there are signs that both educators and business leaders are reappraising the situation and that some companies may start paying more attention to liberal arts majors.

Such a revision in attitude would reflect a changed outlook on both sides. Educators are realizing that unless they do more to persuade industry of the value of the humanities there will soon be even fewer liberal arts majors; and businessmen are coming to appreciate an education that at its best produces graduates who can write and think clearly and solve problems - even if it takes them longer to become conversant with some vocational techniques.

''In the 60's, corporate people were alienated by what was going on in colleges,'' said David T. McLaughlin, chairman of the Toro Company, a maker of light machinery in Minneapolis. ''But now there seems to be a swing back and the corporations and the colleges are moving closer together to find a common ground on which they can meet, giving new currency to the liberal arts degree.''

Mr. McLaughlin will leave one world for another in August, when he gives up his post at Toro to become president of Dartmouth College in New Hampshire, where he was a liberal arts major 30 years ago.

An indication of the the new attempt to reassess the relationship between business and the liberal arts was a conference last fall at Colby College in Maine, where educators and students met with highranking executives of 17 companies.

Threaded through the discussions was the proposition that liberal arts graduates may have more to offer than many businessmen have tended to acknowledge. Proponents of the liberal arts argue that the narrow skills that may promote a quick start in a person's first job can become outdated, but that a liberal arts education gives greater flexibility for the future.

''The people with an A.B. degree are an undiscovered secret for business,'' said Dwight L. Allison Jr., president and chief executive officer of the Boston Company, a financial concern,, who participated in the Colby gathering. ''They are people with a broad educational background, and are motivated. They can quickly become contributing members of a business organization like ours.''

It remains to be seen whether the sympathies that some top executives are showing for liberal arts majors are adopted further down the corporate ladder by the recruiters, and whether liberal arts majors will be acceptable only if they come from elite colleges.

Strengthening the case for liberal arts majors in business are the recently released findings of a 20-year study by the American Telephone and Telegraph Company, disclosing that employees with liberal arts majors have shown stronger managerial skills and have advanced further than those with other college majors.

''There is no need for liberal arts majors to lack confidence in approaching business careers,'' said a summary of the study, ''Career Patterns: The Liberal Arts Major in Bell System Management.'' The study was published by the Association of American Colleges, an organization representing hundreds of liberal arts colleges.

The greatest drawback found among liberal arts graduates in the Bell System, according to the study, was their mathematical deficiencies, which apparently did not prevent them from being promoted.

Another example of the renewed relationship between business and the liberal arts is the National Humanities Institute operating this summer at Mohawk Valley Community College in Utica, N.Y. Its purpose is to help community colleges develop new courses, and links between existing courses, to give business majors more exposure to the liberal arts.

Two-member teams from 20 colleges, each consisting of a business professor and a liberal arts professor, are meeting for four weeks to shape plans that they can take back to their institutions.

One of the colleges, for instance, is trying to create a course called ''Business Through Novels: Fact or Fiction,'' in which students will read such works as James Clavell's ''King Rat'' and Kurt Vonnegut's ''Player Piano.'' The books will be considered not only for character development and writing style, but also for their portrayals of business and the merits of the management techniques used by the characters.

An effort of a different sort is under way at the University of Iowa, where the Academic Careers Cluster Project is an attempt to identify the skills needed in various career fields and define ways for students in the College of Liberal Arts to acquire them without abandoning their majors.

Nancy L. Harper, the project director and assistant dean of liberal arts at Iowa, said, ''We're trying to make the case that being a music major does not mean that the only thing you can do with your life is teach music.''

Some educators, however, object to what appears to them to be the infusing of the liberal arts with a dash of vocationalism simply to make liberal arts majors more acceptable to business.

Donald D. Schmeltekopf, president of the Community College Humanities Asociation and director of the institute this summer at Mohawk Valley, thinks the objections are misplaced.

''The purists say that the humanities are self-justifying and that to try to think about the practical value of the humanities in this way is a diminution of the humanities,'' said Mr. Schmeltekopf, a philosophy professor at Union College in Cranford, New Jersey, ''But there is nothing wrong with the approach because it is imperative for those of us who teach the humanities to make clear that they have value in a practical sense.''
