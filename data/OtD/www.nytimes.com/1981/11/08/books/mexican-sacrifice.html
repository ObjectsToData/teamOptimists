MEXICAN SACRIFICE

By BENJAMIN DeMOTT; Benjamin DeMott is Mellon Professor of Humanities at Amherst.
Published: November 8, 1981

THE WHITE RAINBOW By Alan Harrington. 379 pp. Boston: Little, Brown & Co. $14.95.

TOMAS ITURBI, a rich, leukemia-battling, Yankee-hating Mexican radicalized at Columbia in the Mark Rudd days, heads up an Aztec fundmentalist cult in remotest Michoacan. The cult's chief article of faith is that a little human sacrifice helps the sun to rise, and as Tomas's disease worsens, he teases himself with the possibility that sacrificing precisely the right person might just bring off his own cure - ''a miracle of transference ... a spontaneous remission by means of ecstasy and shock.'' The death of the first scapegoatcandidate he lures to the cult-grounds, a Boulder, Colo,, anthropologist done in before this story starts, launches a Congressional inquiry in the United States and brings no improvement in Tomas's health. But Tomas is still hunting as the book's curtain rises.

Enter Todd Deming, Harvard's wunderkind in his mid-30's, gifted linguist, man of Viking beauty, specialist in ''Human Sacrifice Around the World,'' authority on the Aztec myth of the Fifth Sun, which sees the re-energizing of Old Sol as dependent on ''the blood of human captives.'' (Tomas's cult is called the Sixth Sun: ''He has it, mystically ... that the Fifth Sun is on the point of being extinguished by industrial wastes.'')

Resolved, over the opposition of his Harvard colleagues, to investigate the Sixth Sun cult, Todd Deming takes off for the border. A villainous go-between named Zeb Brownlee brings him ambiguously hospitable messages from Tomas Iturbi, and Todd finds his way to cult headquarters - where he's promptly stripped of watch and mirror, placed under genteel house arrest, fed exotic mushrooms and berated by Tomas as a despoiler worse than the Spaniards who melted great altars into bars of gold: '' 'You came in false humility,' (Tomas) said, 'bearing foolish gifts. But we know, we're not deceived, behind your courteous smile we can recognize one more plunderer! This is in your blood. You've no way of being otherwise. Scholarship is your gold. As such it can't be force-fed, as with the gluttonous Spaniard in Peru, poured molten and glowing down your throat. But (we) purge, heal of their innocence, invading scholars such as

After some weeks of this, during which he employs the courier Zeb to send home tapes describing the situation, Todd Deming grasps that he's been set up as Visiting Anthropological Scapegoat No. 2; thereafter much of the book recounts the struggle of his wife and a colleague to effect his rescue.

The Mexican landscape in ''The White Rainbow'' is carefully drawn, and, so too are details of the cult's rituals - from the priests' costumes to the antics of a black goat who shadows Deming on his solitary doped-up march to the place of execution. There's a measure of intellectual interest in the theorizing about academicians who perceive themselves as preservers of endangered linguistic species. And Alan Harrington's eye and ear for suggestive incongruity produce several arresting moments - most notably when Todd Deming, on an eerily supervised night ramble, stumbles upon a plane wreck, picks up a ''small black and chrome object,'' hears a click, and, even as he hallucinates, understands he's listening to a radio disk jockey brought in faintly from over the border: ''For Chuck and Bernie, happy anniversary! This is KOMO, San Antonio, the River City, home of the Alamo, our Spurs, and Mr. Cool, the Ice Man, Mr. George Gervin, who scored forty-three all by himself against the Knicks last night. Ah, Sweet San Antone! ...''

On balance, though, ''The White Rainbow'' struck me as disappointing work. Both its principal figures are longer on ideology, methodology and fustian than on personality, and neither achieves, on the page, more than a talking stick-figure presence. The minor personages are stereotypes - jealous, backbiting Harvard professors, alcoholic faculty wives, timid Foreign Service cookiepushers, the formulaic seedy go-between, the inevitable self-involved shrink. (Todd Deming behaves as he does, says the shrink, because he's locked in a fantasy pursuit of his lost twin brother, dead in infancy). The climax is a meretriciously delayed Charlton Heston spectacular featuring a cast of zonked peasants, mile-high bonfires, gongs, drums, bloody revelry, and a rented, mission-of-mercy helicopter packed with soldiers of fortune and Todd Deming's necessarily gorgeous wife.

More troubling than any of this, there's a streak of schoolboy Nietzscheanism in the book that I found hard to take. An occasional word is spoken in ''The White Rainbow'' against cruelty, but the most passionate rhapsodies trumpet the glories of pain - witness Tomas's animadversions on the bleeding hearts who worried about the hostages:

''For (Americans) life free of pain was timidly held precious, the highest good of all. The screams of others horrified them; a scream of one's own was out of the question. Thus, it had been discovered, they quailed en masse before demands for ransom and indemnity. Their obsession that, no matter what, please, no pain must be inflicted on our hostages had caused them to be reduced and humiliated by the bullying old patriarch of Iran. Their pipsqueak President hid among his roses, promising all concerned no pain. Thus, on their knees, they fell from the grace they once had.

''And when these prisoners were released the entire bourgeois nation went mad with joy, waving yellow ribbons about, astonished by the captives' fortitude under relatively moderate abuse, proposing for them awards of valor.''

Alan Harrington's odd, original writing career has more than once veered toward over-reaching. I think for instance of ''The Immortalist,'' a feverish disquisition on our need to launch a Manhattan project to overcome death (bedside reading, it might appear, for Tomas Iturbi). But that career has also produced modest, memorable novels such as ''The Secret Swinger'' - sad, acute, splendidly grainy studies of what used to be called the human heart. I wish he'd give us another of these.
