ANTIQUES; Victorianism, Redefined, at the Met

BY RITA REIF
Published: October 19, 1986

Victorianism - a style of excess in carving, ornament and pattern - was banished from parlors across the land a half-century ago. Now, an exhibition, ''In Pursuit of Beauty: Americans and the Esthetic Movement,'' opening Tuesday at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, makes it clear that the late 19th-century style is returning to favor - redefined, reorganized and radically simplified. The show, which remains through Jan. 11, is the most comprehensive museum presentation to date dealing with late 19th-century taste in this country.

Spawned by British mid-19th-century protests against industrialization, the Esthetic Movement was led by such English critics and artists as William Morris, John Ruskin and James Abbott Whistler. They sought to curb the ugliness that characterized mass-produced furnishings and objects by inspiring an appreciation of taste, beauty and refinement in everything made for use in the home. They were fervent in their presentation and amazingly successful. Their theories had a pervasive effect - not all of it good - on the furnishings and architectural amenities acquired in England and the United States by the emerging middle class during the 1870's and 1880's.

The exhibition presents 200 examples of works awash with pattern - furniture, stained glass, metalwork, textiles, wallpaper, paintings, sculpture, books and a crazy quilt. No flat surface was left untouched by floral and figural motifs borrowed from an eclectic mix of ancient and Asian cultures. The crazy quilt, a skillfully stitched coverlet from 1877 made from scores of variously shaped scraps, is an unbelievably chaotic mix of embroidered floral and geometric images. One of John La Farge's stained-glass windows from a Newport mansion is a masterwork of the medium in this period.

La Farge outlined his painted blossoms in black, updating the flattened imagery produced by leading in medieval stained glass windows, a technique that increases the impact. The installation of the window in a blue-green wall, the color of the stained-glass leaves, is one of many ways that David Harvey, a staff designer, quiets the rich palette of competing colors and busy patterns throughout this exhibition.

Viewed in hindsight, the greatest problem in late 19th-century decorative art is that no one knew where to stop. Patterns blanketed rooms in layer on layer of floral and figural motifs. Botanical images vie with Greek gods, Islamic motifs and Japanese assymetric patterns on the doors of chests or the surfaces of vases. Taken one by one, or in small groups, there is much to admire in the most inventive objects and textiles, especially if viewers prefer furniture and objects embellished with pictorial, as opposed to sculptural, decoration.

Artists who were influenced by the Japanese taste, on the other hand, often achieved works of admirable simplicity. The best of the Herter Brothers furniture reflects the Japanese style, as can be seen in a boxy, ebonized cherry wardrobe that belonged to Lillian Russell when she was the toast of Broadway in the 1880's. Echoes of Japanese prints can be seen in inlaid studies of winding branches and showers of blossoms down its facade. Agnes Pitman and M. Louise McLaughlin, two of the Cincinnati women whose ceramic and metal works brought international acclaim to Rookwood, the producer, interpreted the Japanese mode differently in highly sophisticated crackled glazes and bold floral treatments. The silver producers - Tiffany, Gorham, Whiting - translated the Japanese esthetic most successfully in both the shaping and relief decoration.

Louis Comfort Tiffany left his mark on this period in many works influenced by ancient and Middle Eastern works. The massive candelabrum designed under his direction by Associated Artists for the 1880 Veterans' Room of the Seventh Regiment Armory on Park Avenue is vaguely Medieval. By 1890 Tiffany was looking to the East when he devised a glowing, iridescent mosaic panel of peacocks to decorate the wall above the mantel in the Fifth Avenue mansion of the Henry O. Havemeyer family.

The exhibition is a remarkable undertaking that explains in both its selection and presentation the best of the period, thanks to the clear thinking of Alice Cooney Frelinghausen, project director, who is an assistant curator in the department of American decorative arts, and the curatorial team working under her. Most of the historians in the field were consulted or participated in the two-year effort of organizing the show and writing its massive 500-page catalogue, which includes essays by its curators and such scholars in the field as Marilynn Johnson, David Hanks and Catherine Lynn, Roger Stein, Jonathan Freedman and James D. Kornwolf.

The exhibition is aided by the Meredith Corporation, a diversified media company of Des Moines, Iowa. Additional funding for the catalogue ($45 in hardcover, $35 in paper), which was published by the museum and by Rizzoli, New York, came from the National Endowment for the Humanities.

The revival of interest in all late 19th century styles began to take hold in 1970, when the Metropolitan Museum presented its 19th-century show, said Margot Johnson, a dealer in this material at 18 East 68th Street. ''I fell in love with the eccentricity of many of the pieces,'' she said. ''I started to buy what I could afford. Fine wicker tables and chairs were $50 and $100. The same pieces would cost thousands today.'' She stocks the works of the best cabinetmakers of the period - Herter Brothers, Alexander Roux, Pottier & Stymus. Marcotte, Horner Bros. - and calls it, not Victorian, but late 19th-century American.

Richard McGeehan, a Bedford Hills, N.Y., dealer, sees the strong graphic character of the Esthetic Movement as appealing to late 20th-century taste. He will exhibit a leaf-inlaid ebonized cabinet, attributed to the Herter Brothers, at the Fall Antiques Show next weekend, at $25,000. ''These pieces have skyrocketed in price,'' he said, adding that furniture he sold three or four years ago at $3,500 has doubled in price.
