WASHINGTON TALK: CONGRESS; The Politics of End-of-Session Nominations

By LINDA GREENHOUSE
Published: October 15, 1986

WASHINGTON, Oct. 14— The end of a Congressional session resembles a frantic marketplace. Legislation is the main commodity, with bills being traded, killed and resurrected at a dizzying pace.

But legislation is not the only commodity. There is also a lively commerce in a particular group of human beings.

These are Presidential nominees who need Senate confirmation before the session ends. Nominations, like bills, die at the end of a Congress. With 120 unconfirmed nominations still pending, this is a time for do-or-die bargaining.

One of the most byzantine of these last-minute scenarios began unfolding today as the Senate started debating an obscure ambassadorial nomination on which as many as 18 other nominations may depend in the closing days of the session. Familiar Players

The key characters are familiar players in these recurring dramas. At the center, almost predictably, is Senator Jesse Helms, the Republican from North Carolina who has made something of a specialty of opposing the State Department in what would seem to be routine nominations. This time, he is championing one nominee and opposing another.

The target of his opposition is Edwin G. Corr, the current Ambassador to El Salvador, a career Foreign Service officer who incurred the Senator's wrath in previous posts.

Mr. Corr will keep his current job no matter what the Senate does. At issue is a routine promotion for which he was nominated a year ago, to the senior rank, the highest rank within the Foreign Service. Senator Helms, invoking the tradition that allows any senator to put an indefinite ''hold'' on a nomination, has refused to let the promotion come to a vote.

The nominee Mr. Helms is championing is James L. Malone, whose appointment as ambassador to the Central American nation of Belize was turned down by the Senate Foreign Relations Committee in April. The 9-to-7 vote against Mr. Malone marked the first time in the century that the committee refused to approve an ambassadorial nomination.

The Democrats who opposed Mr. Malone, led by Senator Edward Zorinsky of Nebraska, contended that as an Assistant Secretary of State in the first four years of the Reagan Administration, he had displayed an ''inadequate'' management style and ''questionable'' judgment. But Senator Helms was not willing to give up. He let it be known that unless the committee reconsidered, he would keep indefinite holds on a number of other diplomatic nominations. Chief among these was the nomination of Morton I. Abramowitz to be Assistant Secretary of State for Intelligence and Research.

The Foreign Relations Committee had approved Mr. Abramowitz by a vote of 16 to 1, Senator Helms casting the negative vote. The diplomat was another longtime target of Senator Helms, who accused him of, among other failings, being insufficiently interested in the welfare of the nationalist regime on Taiwan.

Senator Charles McC. Mathias Jr. of Maryland, the only Republican on the Foreign Relations Committee to vote against Mr. Malone, decided to try to break the logjam. He told Senator Helms he would change his vote and allow the Malone nomination to reach the Senate floor if Senator Helms would allow a Senate vote on Mr. Abramowitz.

Senator Helms agreed, and Mr. Abramowitz was confirmed. Democrats on the Foreign Relations Committee, angered at the notion of reopening a matter they had considered closed, boycotted the committee session at which the new Malone vote was scheduled. Last month, shortly after Congress returned from its summer recess, the Republican members of the Foreign Relations Committee held a meeting and voted 9 to 0 to send the Malone nomination to the Senate floor.

Senator Bob Dole, the Republican leader, said on the floor this morning that Senator Helms had agreed to drop his opposition to Ambassador Corr's promotion in return for a vote on the Malone nomination. But Senator Zorinsky was not so easily appeased. He announced that he still opposed Mr. Malone and planned to debate the nomination ''at length.'' That is Senate code language for a filibuster, the most dreaded prospect in the closing days of the session.

With the final chapter in this drama still unwritten, other ambassadorial nominations are also at stake. Seventeen nominees who have been approved by the Foreign Relations Committee are before the Senate, including Charles J. Pilliod Jr. to be ambassador to Mexico and L. Paul Bremer 3d to be ambassador at large for counter-terrorism. Mr. Helms denies having a hold on any of these nominations, but the Democrats dispute this assertion and say that he has made these nominees, as well, hostage to the fate of Mr. Malone. A Form of Sweet Revenge

Nomination politics is being played in other cases. Democrats on the Judiciary Committee last week effectively killed the nomination of William Dwyer, a lawyer from Seattle, to be a Federal district judge. Their action was a form of sweet revenge against Senator Slade Gorton, the Washington Republican who was Mr. Dwyer's sponsor.

Senator Gorton cast the deciding vote two months ago when the Senate, after bitter debate, confirmed Daniel O. Manion as a Federal district judge in Indiana. The Senator said then that in return for supporting Mr. Manion he had exacted a promise from the Reagan Administration to nominate Mr. Dwyer, whose moderate-to-liberal leanings had encountered opposition in the Justice Department. The Democrats' response was conveyed last week when, on a procedural vote, they refused to let the Judiciary Committee take up the nomination at its last meeting of the session.

Sometimes nominations founder in the closing days not because of politics but because it is so hard to focus senators' time and attention. The Labor and Human Resources Committee has been unable to get a quorum to consider several undisputed nominations, including that of Mary Cracraft to be a member of the National Labor Relations Board.

Then there are the nominations that are so overtly political that they leave even the most seasoned politicians shaking their heads. A few weeks ago, the White House sent the Foreign Relations Committee the nomination of Helen Marie Taylor to be a member of the United States delegation to the United Nations General Assembly. The resume attached to the nomination did not mention a key fact: that other Senate committees had failed four times to confirm Mrs. Taylor to previous jobs.

But Senator Claiborne Pell of Rhode Island, the ranking Democrat on the Foreign Relations Committee, recognized the name. He remembered that the Labor and Human Resources Committee, on which he also serves, had refused three times to confirm Mrs. Taylor, a wealthy fund-raiser and contributor to right-wing causes, to the National Endowment for the Humanities. (Her nomination to the Corporation for Public Broadcasting was withdrawn in the face of likely defeat before the Commerce Committee.) There would appear to be no chance that the Foreign Relations Committee will act on the nomination. Almost no chance. One Democratic staff member said, ''Who knows, maybe they'll trade her off for somebody we want.''
