KNOW-HOW MILLS

BY JAMES M. BANNER JR.; James M. Banner Jr., an American historian, writes frequently on topics in education
Published: October 26, 1986

TO ADVANCE KNOWLEDGE The Growth of American Research Universities, 1900-1940. By Roger L. Geiger. 325 pp. New York: Oxford University Press. $27.50.

A MAJOR element of 20th-century American history, as of the recent history of all modern states, has been the growth of large-scale national institutions and their interdependence. Although governments and corporations are the chief of these, nonprofit associations, cultural institutions, churches, foundations and political parties must be numbered among the sizable, increasingly bureaucratic organizations that form our institutional structure.

It is one of Roger Geiger's achievements, by virtue of argument and evidence presented with engaging conciseness and strength of expression, that the position of American research universities on this list becomes secure. His working principle -that universities are analogous to governments and corporations - will always be controversial: it is often said that great seats of learning enjoy an exemption from the normal sociology of institutions because they respond so much more than others to the unpredictable course of knowledge and ideas.

Mr. Geiger, an associate research scientist in the Institution for Social and Policy Studies and the history department at Yale University, does not raise that question in ''To Advance Knowledge: The Growth of American Research Universities.'' Yet by concentrating on the social and institutional context, he provides new substantiation for placing research universities at the center of 20th-century American history.

It has been a kind of commonplace - not least, and not surprisingly, among professors themselves - that a hallmark if not a principal cause of the nation's place in modern history is its universities. Mr. Geiger studies the history of their modern evolution as a group.

His prime examples are 15 great research universities -five state institutions (Illinois, Michigan, Minnesota, Wisconsin and California), five grown from colonial roots (Harvard, Yale, Princeton, Columbia and Pennsylvania) and five private institutions founded in the late 19th century (Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Cornell, Johns Hopkins, Stanford and Chicago) - to which he adds a 16th, the remarkable and more recent prodigy, the California Institute of Technology, of which he also writes at length. Mindful of the special characteristics of each, he analyzes how they came to form a national mixed system of public and private institutions whose influence on the advance of knowledge still grows.

As Mr. Geiger tells it, by 1920 the system of American research universities, like that of so many of our institutions, had assumed the general and recognizable shape it retains today. Dependent on uncertain philanthropic support, divided over their relationship to the Federal Government, its component institutions had nevertheless by then ''become the corporations of the education industry - organized to gather the lion's share of social resources available to higher education, and committed to produce the most-valued educational products for the most-important national markets.'' Thus situated, they emerged from World War I into what Mr. Geiger calls a golden age of support, research and stature that lasted, despite setbacks in the Depression, to the next world war. TO Mr. Geiger, the history of the growth of these universities is principally the history of the graduate education and university-based research and scholarship that have nurtured the astonishing advance and diffusion of knowledge in the last 100 years. Yet he shows that the triumph of university research over research carried out in independent institutes and government bureaus was neither inevitable nor smooth; in fact, one must now ask how secure that victory remains. And he shows how heavily the fortunes of university research depended on giving through foundations, fund-raising drives and alumni contributions and on undergraduate instruction in the arts and sciences - the historic focus of American higher education.

Although Mr. Geiger's sympathies lie unabashedly with the general course of university growth, he is harshly and justifiably critical of university conduct during World War I and of the quality of university college life in the 1920's. Claiming to be citadels of the most advanced thinking, the universities mobilized for war with an insouciant enthusiasm that now seems as laughable as it was deplorable. ''Institutions,'' he argues, ''that had consistently vowed their dedication to intellectual freedom and the pursuit of truth for their faculties, or to the cultivation of critical intelligence and the development of moral character in their students, allowed these values to be eclipsed with scarcely a word of protest.''

What is more, for 10 years afterwards, in a kind of moral hangover from war fever, they tolerated and protected discriminatory admissions policies (especially quotas for Jews), rowdy student behavior and an anti-intellectualism equally at variance with their protestations of academic quality. True to his analytical strategy, Mr. Geiger attributes all these failings to the very forces that were shaping other aspects of university growth: changing faculty aspirations, funding needs and the still-uncertain place of university-based research in American life. YET Mr. Geiger's determined focus on university growth and his tendency to identify research with the natural and physical sciences compresses too much the history of these institutions. A tale that fails to record the universities' absorption and transformation of humanistic scholarship - the previously fundamental ground of most human knowledge - remains but a partial history of its subject. To call the 1920's a golden age of research universities reduces university-based knowledge to scientific learning, since the high-water mark of scholarship in the humanities and related social sciences occurred sometime between 1945 and 1970 - itself another golden age, this time of enlarged and democratized graduate and undergraduate enrollments and bountiful support from the Government.

Nor do we get from Mr. Geiger a sense of the intellectual, cultural, sometimes evangelical origins of modern American universities. Thus we confront the basic conundrum of the historiography of education: whether it can be constructed exclusive of the history of knowledge itself. After all, could we ignore the role of money and capital in writing the history of corporations?

As far as it goes, Mr. Geiger's study marks a major advance in the knowledge of its subject. Fresh observations and interpretive advances stud the book. To those who recall college and university life before 1940, this work offers perspective, compelling reading and the shock of recognition. Some will savor the implicit warnings in this history, the symmetry it reveals between past and current debates, and its account of the roots of educational realities being challenged afresh today. And for those who believe that when the history of 20th-century America is written, its great universities will prove among its most distinguishing characteristics, ''To Advance Knowledge'' provides both confirmation and pleasure.
