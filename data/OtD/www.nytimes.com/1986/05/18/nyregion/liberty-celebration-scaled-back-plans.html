LIBERTY CELEBRATION: SCALED-BACK PLANS

By CHARLOTTE LIBOV
Published: May 18, 1986

IT looked like 11th-hour action by the legislature was needed to rescue the state commission honoring the 100th anniversaries of the Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island, and that is just what the commission got.

The General Assembly appropriated $75,000, just in time to get the commission's program started with the ''Foundation of Liberty Day'' event on the town green in Guilford June 28, but the panel has been forced to abandon many of the programs it wanted to hold this year.

With the Guilford event, the Connecticut commission is starting its celebrating just ahead of the huge national commemoration in New York Harbor, but in fund raising the commission has finished a long way behind.

The nine-member commission, officially the Connecticut Centennial Commission for the Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island, spent a hard year seeking contributions, but finding itself overshadowed by the nationwide drive that raised millions of dollars to refurbish the statue, said Wilda Hamerman, commission chairman.

''Everything is very up and positive now, but before, wherever we went, people said they had already contributed to the national drive,'' Mrs. Hamerman said. ''It became very depressing.''

''There were times I'd get up in the morning,'' she said, ''and say, 'I'm going to give this up before we're all embarrassed to death.' ''

She is pleased the legislature decided to appropriate the money, but she is concerned that the delay in holding events may have buried the state's effort in all the hoopla surrounding the refurbishing of the Statue of Liberty.

''I guess we'll still be able to do it with the focus on Ellis Island,'' Mrs. Hamerman said, ''but now we'll really have to beat the drum. After all, this was the year for all the excitement.''

The $75,000 appropriation is part of the state 1985-86 budget that the General Assembly has sent to Governor O'Neill for his signature. He was expected to sign it.

''Foundation of Liberty Day'' in Guilford begins Liberty Week in Connecticut, which will be from June 28 to July 3, Mrs. Hamerman said. The Guilford celebration is to focus on the state's contribution of granite for the base of the statue. The events will include a pancake breakfast, an apple pie baking contest, ethnic music and dance performances and a re-enactment of the Citizenship Oath.

The commission has hired Event Management Associates, a Hartford public relations firm, to organize the daylong event. The company has agreed to wait for its payment until after the state funds are received, Mrs. Hamerman siad.

The commission's next event will take place July 1, with the opening of a new photography exhibit entitled ''The Statue of Liberty and Immigration in Connecticut, 1885-1924,'' at the State Library in Hartford. The exhibit will be at the library through November, and then will tour the state.

The commission and the Yale Concert Band are to sponsor a concert featuring a performance of ''Liberty Enlightening the World,'' written in honor of the Statue of Liberty by Charles Gounod in the late 1800's, and performed just twice since then, Mrs. Hamerman said. The concert is to take place in New Haven in October, she said.

The commission will remain in service through 1992, the 100th anniversary of the passage of the first immigrant through Ellis Island, and Mrs. Hamerman said she was hoping its later years would be more successful than this first one.

''I don't think we'll cause great waves,'' she said, ''but we are hoping to sponsor some major events each year.''

This year's lack of money forced the commission to abandon plans to produce a series of educational programs, sponsor essay and poster contests, hold theater performances, and stage other commemorative events concerning the statue, Mrs. Hamerman said.

The commission began its fund raising at about the same time as the Connecticut Committee for the Restoration of the Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island, a statewide drive in behalf of the nationwide campaign.

The statewide drive to benefit the national campaign has so far raised $4.5 million toward a goal of $5.5 million, according to Alan Kaiser, a spokesman for the U.S. Tobacco Company and a volunteer in the statewide campaign; Mrs. Hamerman's commission was able to raise $15,000, far short of the $50,000 that she estimated the panel needed for its full program.

The statewide drive to raise funds for the national effort is headed by Louis F. Bantle, chairman of the U.S. Tobacco Company in Greenwich. Gerald Labriola of Naugatuck served as co-chairman until January, when he declared his candidacy in the Republican race for governor, said Lois Saldukas, a volunteer from Weston. Mrs. Saldukas and Mr. Kaiser have taken over Dr. Labriola's tasks.

Dr. Labriola declined to answer any questions on the foundation's fund-raising campaign because he was no longer involved with it, he said. But Mrs. Saldukas said she was proud of the way Connecticut residents responded to the national campaign, and she said she doubted it hurt Mrs. Hamerman's commission's efforts. Instead, Mrs. Saldukas said she thought residents wanted to restore the statue first, then think about holding events.

''They wanted her to be taken care of first,'' Mrs. Saldukas said, referring to the statue, ''but now that things have turned around, they are thinking of a celebration.''
