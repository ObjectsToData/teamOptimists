COLLEGE PLANS MAJOR EXPANSION

By MICHELLE G. LEWIS
Published: August 24, 1986

MAYS LANDING— RESPONDING to what they see as community needs, officials of Atlantic Community College expect to spend $14.7 million over the next few years to develop the 546-acre campus here. Plans call for:

* New buildings for the Academy of Culinary Arts ($6.6 million). Among them will be a 200-bed dormitory -the first dormitory at a community college in the state.

* A technology center ($1.2 million).

* A library addition ($804,000).

* A developmental education/English as a second language center ($463,000).

* A fine and performing arts facility ($675,000).

A $3.2 million computer and telephone network to link the three campuses - here, in Cape May and in Atlantic City - will be installed, and $1.7 million will be spent to upgrade fire and safety systems.

The Hillier Group, a Princeton architectural company that designed the master plan, has been awarded the contract for the culinary academy, library addition and language center. Ground breaking is tentatively scheduled for next spring.

The funds, totaling almost $15 million, represent the largest investment for construction projects in the college's 20-year-history.

The state is financing most of the project through the ''Chapter 12'' program, which gives county colleges construction capital. The State Educational Facilities Authority will issue bonds for the dormitory.

Ronald Bush, president of the college, said he was confident the project would let the school adapt to changing matriculation patterns, including an increase in older and minority students.

The college has about 1,400 full-time and 2,300 part-time students, with an additional 5,000 noncredit students at its Atlantic City-based Casino Career Institute.

Since 1978, the casino and entertainment-related businesses have generated a high demand for food and beverage personnel in Atlantic County, with ''five job openings for every academy graduate,'' according to Frank Verheul, director of the Academy of Culinary Arts.

''For the first time,'' said Mr. Bush, ''the U.S.A. is exporting more chefs than it imports.'' According to the Federal Bureau of Labor Statistics, 125,000 cooks a year will be needed through 1990.

The culinary academy, established only five years ago, has already received international and national recognition, such as winning the gold medal and other awards of the Societe Cuisiniere de Paris at the Salon of the Culinary Arts, an exhibition in New York City in 1982. It was the first time students had received a gold medal in the event's 114 years.

Culinary academy graduates vie for the same positions as those of the prestigious Culinary Institute of America in Hyde Park, N.Y. Both offer similar two-year programs, but tuition at the private Hyde Park school is almost double the $1,700 to $1,900 a semester cost at the academy.

New kitchens will accommodate 800 students, twice the current capacity. The new building will also house classrooms and the school's three restaurants - a cafeteria, a family restaurant and Caremes, a gourmet French country-dining establishment.

The new technology center reflects a regional effort to supply continuing educational opportunities to engineers who will work on the Federal Aviation Administration's $12 billion, decade-long overhaul of the air-traffic control system. About $2 billion will be spent at the F.A.A. Technical Center in nearby Pomona.

The college's new center, will provide ''state-of-the-art class and laboratory facilities for all consolidated technology programs,'' said Tim Scepansky, the college's director of information technology. Majors will be offered in computer information systems, electronics technology and microcomputer maintenance and repair programs.

The program's enrollment has quadrupled since 1982, and in light of the F.A.A. project, Mr. Scepansky is confident that enrollment will continue to grow.

Since the F.A.A. announced its plan three years ago, subcontractors have already brought hundreds of employees into the area.

The college plans to install a satellite transmission system capable of receiving America Media-Based Continuing Education for Engineers video teleconferences or New Jersey Institute of Technology classes, during which students may query professors by telephone.

Plans call for increasing the number of video courses for liberal arts students. Nearly 400 students take the courses, which are offered on cable or videotapes available at the college and local libraries. Mr. Scepansky said he intended to expand the program to reach more handicapped and homebound people.

The college also wants to facilitate its own flow of information. The new voice-and-data telephone system will transform what have been long-distance calls between the three campuses into extensions.

The decision to build the technology center coincides with the South Jersey effort to attract industry.

The South Jersey Technological Consortium and the Atlantic County Research and Development Technology Corporation were recently formed to encourage hi-tech businesses to move to South Jersey and Atlantic County, respectively.

The college's developmental education/English as a second language program will also partially rely on computer-related equipment to deliver its curriculum to its 400 students.

Peter Mora, the department coordinator, said audiovisual supplements to traditional class instruction were needed because ''there are certain things that can be done visually, but not in class.''

The expanded fine and performing arts program will occupy an existing wing of Edge Hall, the original site of the fine arts, dance, theater and music area.

The fine arts department had been temporarily relocated across campus, since the Academy of Culinary Arts needed part of Edge Hall for additional production kitchens.

With the construction of the new academy, the fine arts will be reunited with its sister programs in Edge Hall, and the kitchens will be replaced by areas for ceramics, dance, painting drawing and two- and three-dimensional art classes.

The extension will serve mostly the department's noncredit workshops. Thomas Boghosian, head of the humanities division, said the 300 students enrolled in the art courses each semester were ''interested in exposing themselves to different art forms [ but ] not necessarily interested in a degree.''

Faculty members, administrators and students credit Mr. Bush with the foresight and leadership to implement the college's expansion.

But the president seeks to put the multimillion-dollar project into perspective.

The construction will contribute to growth, he said, but buildings alone -no matter how large or modern - do not form the backbone of the college.

''The greatest strength of this institution,'' he said, ''is in the faculty and students.''
