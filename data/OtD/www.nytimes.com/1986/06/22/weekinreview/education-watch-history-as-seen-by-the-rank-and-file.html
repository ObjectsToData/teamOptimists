EDUCATION WATCH; HISTORY, AS SEEN BY THE RANK AND FILE

By WILLIAM SERRIN
Published: June 22, 1986

MEMBERS of several unions gathered at a labor headquarters in Lower Manhattan one night to watch a film about the railroad workers' strike of 1877. Although the strike was a pivotal event in American labor history - more than 100 people were killed and 1,000 injured as it spread along the tracks from Martinsburg, W. Va., to Pittsburgh and other cities and turned into a general strike - few people, including union members, have heard of it.

''I grew up in Pittsburgh,'' said Vickie Williams of the Amalgamated Clothing and Textile Workers Union, ''and I don't recall anything like this ever being mentioned.''

The film, titled ''1877: The Grand Army of Starvation,'' is one of many programs being produced by the American Social History Project at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York. There, a research team has been designing ways to present history as ''more than Presidents and business leaders, national elections and battles, names and dates to memorize,'' said Stephen Brier, the project's director. Rather, he said, the project looks at the past from the viewpoint of ''artisans, slaves, farmers, working women at home, laborers, factory and white-collar workers.''

Although the study of ''ordinary people'' has been part of college curriculums since the 60's, members of the City University project hope it will broaden the audience for social history. Indeed, much of their work is designed for high schools, community colleges and trade unions. ''If you want to understand America,'' Mr. Brier said, ''you have to understand it from the point of view of working-class people.''

Joseph S. Murphy, the chancellor of City University, agreed. ''It is important to understand that it is the workers in every society who created the wealth and capital of that society, although they may not often have shared in it,'' he said. ''It is that version of American history that needs to be taught to working-class people.''

The project has received an $850,000 grant from the Ford Foundation and a $400,000 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities. Besides ''1877,'' the nine-member team is producing several slide-and-tape presentations and writing a two-volume history, ''Who Built America?'' There are plans for a film series for public television and classrooms that will deal with such subjects as the rise of the corporation and the migration of blacks from the South.

The project was set up in 1981, after Herbert G. Gutman, distinguished professor of history at the Graduate Center of City University, and Mr. Brier taught summer seminars in labor history for union leaders. Professor Gutman, who died in 1985, and Mr. Brier recruited historians and experts from other fields, including artists, film makers and writers. 'Nothing Like This'

''There's nothing like this out there,'' Mr. Brier said of the project, which stresses original sources such as diaries. ''It's a lot easier to recapture the past of elite and famous people. It's a lot harder to recapture the past of ordinary people.''

Some scholars argue that the ''new historians'' can be biased and shortsighted in their emphasis on the contributions of workers, just as the critics of mainstream history argue that such history neglects important groups and errs in representing the American past as a constant movement toward abundance.

Much of the current research into immigrants, workers, women and other groups represents ''contemporary scholarship at its best,'' Thomas Bender, a historian at New York University, wrote last year. But, he said, some social historians seem to have ''lost their sense of the central institutions of society'' and have produced ''little sense of the points of contact'' between groups in society.

The City University project is not presenting workers in a romanticized light or hiding the shortcomings of labor unions, said Josh Brown, art director of the project. The overriding aim, the project's members contend, is to generate interest in American history among students and nonstudents alike.

And in that respect, at least, the showing of the film ''1877'' was a success, according to union members who attended. ''We don't know our own heritage,'' said Gary Stevenson of the teamsters. ''This is helping to give it back to us.''
