UNIVERSITY PRESSES SLASH PRICES IN WAVE OF SALES

By EDWIN McDOWELL
Published: December 9, 1986

For a limited time, it is possible to buy all seven volumes of ''The Papers of Andrew Johnson'' for $117.25 instead of the usual $234.50, or three volumes of ''The Letters of Samuel Johnson'' for $82.50 instead of $113.50. And anyone willing to wait until next month can buy the 19 volumes of ''A Social and Religious History of the Jews'' by Salo W. Baron for $350, less than half the $786 list price.

The reason for these book bonanzas, and thousands more, is that some of the nation's better-known university presses are in the midst of major sales to raise money, reduce inventory and put their books in the hands of people who might not otherwise know of their existence.

Accordingly, the usually low-key scholarly presses - in catalogues sent to hundreds of thousands of potential customers - are offering discounts of up to 90 percent, operating 24-hour, toll-free ordering services and sometimes throwing in free books on even the smallest orders.

Not all university press officials are pleased about such sales, including some who themselves occasionally run them.

''We have done them to generate cash flow when we've been strapped, but discounts demean the value of our product,'' said Donald R. Ellegood, director of the University of Washington Press. ''What we do by having such sales is condition our major customers, libraries and scholars to wait until they can get our books at discount a year or so after publication.'' Reluctant to Discount Books

Gail Treadwell, marketing director of Harvard University Press, which rarely holds big book sales, expresses similar sentiments. ''I've always felt you don't want to sell off your books at less than value,'' she said. ''If you plan and print properly, you shouldn't have a lot of excess stock to sell off in a sale.''

But other university press officials find the sales useful and profitable, provided care and judgment are exercised. To avoid depleting its backlist, for example, Cornell University Press does not generally put a book on sale if it has fewer than 100 copies in stock. In offering 20 percent discounts on recent titles in modern European and British history, Cambridge University Press limited the discount prices to no more than three copies of any single publication.

Richard Lucas, sales manager of the University of California Press, contends that university presses have no choice but to offer titles at discount. ''Discounting of trade books has created a bargain mentality, and that affects us, too,'' he said.

Last spring, California, whose goal is to make at least $250,000 from its annual sale, offered discounts of up to 80 percent on nearly 600 titles. These ranged from the ''Book of California Wine,'' a $65 volume for $29.95, to three editions of ''Moby-Dick'': a $225 cloth deluxe edition at $99.95, a $19.95 hard-cover at $9.95 and a $9.95 paperback at $5.95.

Cornell, while it has no annual sale, does go all-out when it holds one. According to Cynthia Gration, the press's advertising and promotion manager, Cornell's 1983 sale grossed more than $340,000. Its current sale, through Dec. 31, features more than 700 titles, including the seven-volume Andrew Johnson papers, a volume of stories by W.B. Yeats, and books on ferns, falcons and William James. Half-Million Sale Notices

To publicize its sale, the Cornell press sends out about a half-million notices to names on its own lists and on some 35 other lists that it rents at prices ranging from $50 to $70 per thousand names. Most university presses rent such lists, a prime one of which is that of The New York Review of Books. Oxford University Press -whose current sale includes the three volumes of Samuel Johnson letters -advertises its semiannual book sales in The New York Review of Books, then mails out 100,000 copies of that ad to current and potential customers.

Columbia University's sales catalogue includes books from New York University Press, which it distributes. But last year, John D. Moore, Columbia's director, invited 19 other university presses to join in a combined sale featuring 600 new and recent books at savings of up to 60 percent. These included the university presses of Duke, Alabama, Missouri, Massachusetts, Nebraska, Notre Dame, Indiana, Pennsylvania, Rutgers, Southern Illinois, Syracuse, Wayne State and the University of Wisconsin.

Not only have all 19 signed up for the second ''University Press Books in America'' sale, which begins next month and will run through May 31, but six other university presses have also signed up: Chicago, Illinois, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Texas and Wesleyan.

Some of the presses that have joined forces with Columbia also run their own sales. The University of Chicago, for example, just finished its annual fall sale of nearly 600 titles in the humanities, arts, sciences and social sciences, offering discounts of up to 90 percent.

''Only about 20 percent of our titles are in the catalogue in order to help reduce inventory,'' said Carol Kasper, direct-mail manager. ''We have found for really reducing inventory, it's more cost-effective to remainder them. We're using it primarily to generate income.'' Revenues from the sale, she said, provide up to 30 percent of the direct-mail income earned by the press. Most Rely on Direct Mail

Almost all university presses rely heavily on direct mail. Jerome Singerman, direct-mail manager at Johns Hopkins University Press, which holds a big sale every other year, said that Johns Hopkins regularly sends specialized catalogues to faculty members and professional associations in literature, social sciences and other disciplines.

Many hard-cover titles are not put on sale until the book is published in paperback, but most catalogues contain a page or two of recent books at list price to make the catalogue more current.

Even that tradition is not sacrosanct, however. The Indiana University Press book sale next month, its first in many years, will include some newer books among its 400 titles. ''They will be offered at 20 percent off, just to keep the sale interesting,'' said Katie Lloyd-Lester, manager of direct-mail promotion.

One reason Indiana does not hold sales regularly, she said, is that selecting books and coping with orders requires considerable time and effort. Columbia's Mr. Moore added that the book sales were not left to chance. ''We analyze the sales afterward and if a book has not sold a certain number it's not offered again, at least the next year,'' he said. He noted that Columbia's 19-volume ''Social and Religious History of the Jews'' always sells at least a couple of dozen sets.

Because most university press books sell through bookstores whose good will is vital, many of the presses offer the stores a 10 to 20 percent discount beyond the advertised discount. ''We're not doing this to defeat the interest of the bookstores, which we treasure,'' Mr. Moore said. ''We're doing this after the bookstores have done about as much as they can do.''
