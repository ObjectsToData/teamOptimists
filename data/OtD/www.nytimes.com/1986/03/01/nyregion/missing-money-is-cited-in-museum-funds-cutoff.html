MISSING MONEY IS CITED IN MUSEUM FUNDS CUTOFF

By CARLYLE C. DOUGLAS
Published: March 1, 1986

A pattern of mismanagement that led the city to freeze financing for El Museo del Barrio last November involves the disappearance or misuse of more than $120,000, according to current and former museum trustees, city officials and documents turned over to the City Department of Investigation.

The city funds were cut off after the department began investigating the purported mismanagement, according to officials of the city's Cultural Affairs Department, which provides about two-thirds of the $600,000 annual budget for the museum, a leading exhibitor of Hispanic art.

The museum director, Jack Agueros, said in interviews this week that there may have been mistakes in bookkeeping and inadvertent failures to meet budget deadlines. But he denied there was any impropriety or mismanagement by museum officials. Mr. Agueros said that if the funds were not restored by March 6, he would recommend that the museum declare bankruptcy.

Among the irregularities are these, according to the officials and the documents:

* The museum owes $38,000 in back rent. according to the museum's landlord and Richard Bruno, a spokesman for the City Cultural Affairs Department, Mr. Bruno said the money had not been accounted for.

* Mr. Agueros was paid about $37,000 in salary from city funds over a period of 10 months last year, although payments for his salary were specifically disallowed as a condition of city funding, according to Mr. Bruno.

* The museum owes the National Endowment for the Humanities $45,000 in grant money that was not accounted for. The endowment, in a May 17, 1985, letter to the museum's board of directors, requested that the money be returned. A spokesman for the endowment said yesterday that the money had not been repaid. Founded in 1969

El Museo del Barrio, at 1230 Fifth Avenue, at 104th Street, was founded in 1969 by Hispanic artists, educators and community activists. It was underwritten by the Board of Education for several years and became an independent institution in the mid-1970's. It moved from a Third Avenue storefront to its present quarters about a decade ago with the help of Herman Badillo, then a United States Representative, and the growing support of various cultural agencies.

Since 1977, when Mr. Agueros was named executive director, the museum has grown to encompass a wide array of Hispanic cultural endeavors, though it continues to place a heavy emphasis on Puerto Rican culture. Mr. Agueros has been widely praised for his contributions to that expansion, but he has come under increasingly harsh criticism for having turned the museum into a ''personal fiefdom,'' in the words of Armando Montano Jr., an attorney who represents a group of Hispanic artists seeking Mr. Agueros's dismissal.

Similar criticism was made by Mary Hays, executive director of the State Council on the Arts, in an August 1984 letter to Carmen Nelson, chairman of the museum's board of trustees, explaining why state support of El Museo was being cut. It was reduced from $68,000 three years ago to $27,500 last year. In the letter, Mrs. Hays said Mr. Agueros sees ''the organization as his own,'' and ''though staff members have routinely been laid off in the beginning of the summer, only to be rehired in the fall, the director never divulged this fact to the Council staff.''

Money for the salaries for the entire year was provided from city and state funds, with the condition that it not be spent for anything else. Mr. Bruno said yesterday he was unsure how much public money designated for this purpose was unacounted for.

The issue of back rent was raised in a letter to Mr. Agueros from Howard Rubinstein, an assistant city culture commissioner, last Nov. 25.

In the letter Mr. Rubinstein said he had learned that El Museo had failed to pay its rent for the last five months, although the museum had received financing from the city for that rent.

El Museo's rent arrears now total $38,000, covering nearly five months, according to Robert North, the building manager and an employee of the landlord, the East Harlem Arts and Education Local Development Corporation.

In the same letter, Mr. Rubinstein questioned the fact that Mr. Agueros's $45,000 annual salary was paid for 10 months last year from funds provided by the city culture agency, even though the agency required that the salary come from non-city contributions.

Peter J. Basso, an audit official of the National Endowment for the Humanities, informed Mrs. Nelson in a letter dated May 17, 1985, that an audit by the agency had found that a total of $167,430, portions of 14 grants from the endowment, were unaccounted for. Mr. Basso wrote that after consultation with Mr. Agueros, the Endowment reduced the amount of money it said was unaccounted for and requested that $45,130 be returned.
