VAST YIDDISH DICTIONARY TAKING SHAPE, SLOWLY

By RICHARD F. SHEPARD
Published: September 11, 1986

The Yiddish language, which has amassed a cornucopia of wisdom and words in its thousand-year life, is nearly halfway inscribed in its own book of life, the Great Dictionary of the Yiddish Language, and this achievement in itself raises, as Yiddish so often does, a question.

Unlike many Yiddish questions, this one need not be answered by another question. The question: At the half-way mark, six volumes out of a dozen, how does it happen that the dictionary has so far gone through only three (maybe into part of a fourth) of the 22 Hebrew-based letters in which Yiddish is written?

The answer: It took the first four volumes of the huge work, comparable to the great Oxford English Dictionary, to finish with aleph, the first letter. Why should aleph be such a space-grabber? Because it accounts for almost all of the vowel sounds in Yiddish.

Aleph has been in print for several years now, and the news is that Volumes V and VI, embracing bayz and giml, maybe some of daled, are nearing completion. By the year 2000, when the dictionary will have captured all of Yiddish in its pages, it will have been nearly a hundred years, from concept to completion, a time no longer than a sigh in the life of a language profusely punctuated with sighs in its first millennium.

''It will have, we estimate, between 160,000 and 250,000 entries,'' said Dr. Marvin I. Herzog, Atran Professor of Yiddish at Columbia University and editor in chief of the dictionary. ''This is unlike any other Yiddish dictionary that has been compiled.'' 'Explanation' for Each Word

The dictionary defines Yiddish in Yiddish, as the Oxford defines English in English. Unlike the Oxford, it is accommodating to those who are rusty or illiterate in the language. Each entry in the Great Dictionary of the Yiddish Language will have a gloss in English and Hebrew.

''A gloss,'' explained Dr. Herzog, ''is a linguist's way of avoiding using the word 'equivalent.' It is not a one-word equivalent. A gloss gives an explanation of the word. Also, each Yiddish entry is being transliterated in Latin letters. That means that someone who doesn't read Yiddish can access the dictionary on the basis of the English language''

As with other dictionaries, the big breakthrough has come with the harnessing of computers to the task. Volumes V and VI are being inscribed by computer. Dr. Herzog said that the aleph volumes - all done manually, published independently and distributed by the Magnes Press of Jerusalem - will eventually be done over on computers and will feature the glosses and transliterations that are part of the later volumes.

Computers at Columbia University and at Hebrew University in Jerusalem link the lexicographers, accelerating the work in hand and allowing for satellite conferences as well as speeding the tracery of Yiddish letters through the firmament in one shake of a scribe's pen. Entries written in Israel, where there is a larger pool of Yiddish lexicographers and Hebrew translators, are whisked to New York for editing and for the supplementary glosses and historical information. The Latin spelling follows the YIVO phonetic system of transliteration from Yiddish. Project Ran Afoul of Dictators

The idea for a monumental dictionary of Yiddish dates back at least to the turn of the century, when Alfred Landau, a Viennese philologist, set about compiling one. He was such a stickler that he had by no means finished the job by the time he died in 1935. Soviet scholars set about doing the same thing in the 1920's, but project and scholars were squelched by Hitler and Stalin.

In the 1950's, Dr. Yudel Mark, the noted Yiddish linguist, was commissioned to undertake the task by the YIVO Institute of Jewish Research, which in Vilna (now in Lithuania) had Dr. Landau's research in hand. That painstaking work was lost with the entry of the Germans into the city during World War II, but was fortunately found after YIVO resettled in New York. The Institute for Yiddish Lexicology at the City University of New York, directed by Prof. Nathan Susskind, was established to support the dictionary project. When Dr. Mark died in 1975, Dr. Herzog was the next person in the chief editor's seat.

Yiddish is a language that in its most intimate recesses breathes the story of the wanderings of the northern European Ashkenazi Jews. It fuses Germanic, Hebrew and Aramaic, Romance and Slavic languages, carried in their cultural luggage by Jews from German-speaking cities in the 14th century when they emigrated, as refugees, to Eastern Europe and later, still as refugees, to the Americas, England, France, to the Antipodes. There were, before Hitler and the Holocaust, 11 million Yiddish speakers; today there are perhaps 4 million who know it as their first language. Young People Contributing

This Yiddish diaspora is reflected in the distribution of the dozen workers (plus graduate students) who are dedicated to the dictionary.

''There are people who know Yiddish whom we work with and by no means are they all old,'' said Dr. Herzog. ''We find increasing numbers of young people contributing to the work, among them a half-dozen graduate students.''

The path for the lexicographer in any language is strewn with formidable road blocks, and in Yiddish, with its worldwide dispersion and differing cultural colorations, the road blocks for the uninitiated are as entrapping as radar for drivers.

''I have spent a tremendous amount of time, here and in Israel, on this work,'' Dr. Herzog said. ''A curious thing, I find that the influence of modern Hebrew on Yiddish in Israel is not as great as the influence of English here.'' Americanization of Yiddish

''Yiddish has contributed a lot to English but Yiddish in America has also absorbed a lot, too,'' the editor continued. ''We may have to flag some of it in a special way. For instance, the word 'mufn,' from English 'move.' In Yiddish, as used in America, it has only one meaning, to transfer from one apartment to another. Take 'peyde,' in English, 'paid.' In Yiddish usage, it's always said with reference to receiving a salary.

''And then, there's 'tsutrayen,' from English 'try.' But in Yiddish, 'zich tsutrayen' means to get used to something. These will have to find their way into the dictionary, too.''

Another item that has to find its way into the dictionary is support. Without it, Dr. Herzog indicated, the grand project will lose the scent of the trail to the last letter of the alphabet, toph. Support, in English, Yiddish or any other language, looms large in lexicographical terminology. The National Endowment for the Humanities, and individual donors, have been giving support. The latest endowment grant, from mid-1985 to mid-1987, gives the dictionary $250,000 outright and $290,000 in matching funds.

''The bottom line is, in fact, that all our work depends on funds,'' Dr. Herzog said. ''Matching grants mean that somebody has to give funds that match or else we don't get anything from the grant. We do solicit funds.''

After all, he added, Yiddish is not dead, and the work of the dictionary merely records its vocabulary to date. The book may be monumental, he said, but there is not yet any need for eulogies.
