5 RESIGNATIONS SHAKE ARTS IN BOSTON

By FOX BUTTERFIELD
Published: July 10, 1984

BOSTON, July 9— Boston's arts world, in the midst of a major revival, has been thrown into disarray recently by the resignation of five prominent figures.

In the last month, John Williams, the successful composer, quit as conductor of the Boston Pops; Peter Sellars, the brilliant young theatrical director, resigned from the Boston Shakespeare Company; Violette Verdy and Joel Garrick left as artistic director and president, respectively, of the Boston Ballet, and Kenworth Moffett stepped down as curator of 20th-century art at the Museum of Fine Arts.

The departures of Mr. Sellars and Miss Verdy are viewed as having the most serious consequences.

''Their loss is a real blow,'' said Anne Hawley, executive director of the Massachusetts Council on the Arts and Humanities. ''It's a serious setback for their companies.''

The chain of resignations, Miss Hawley noted, comes at a time when Boston ''is at last trying to create a cultural revival'' after years of complacency and watching other cities such as San Francisco build imposing new cultural facilities and attract top artistic talent.

One sign of this renaissance is that Boston was selected as the first city to receive help from the National Arts Stabilization Fund, a new organization started by the Ford, Rockefeller and Mellon Foundations. Boston is to get $3 million from the fund, to match $3 million raised here locally.

Other signs include large recent increases in corporate financing for the arts in Boston, after years of meager support, and the arrival in town of new companies such as the American Repertory Theater.

There is no direct link between the five recent resignations. But the decisions particularly of Mr. Sellars and Miss Verdy reflect the financial and managerial problems facing new cultural groups in Boston.

Robert Brustein, who brought the American Repertory Theater to Cambridge in 1980, said that the problem for a number of new organizations here continues to be ''the lack of a steady, reliable source of financial support.''

Dependency Can Be Problem

This tends to make the organizations dependent on their boards of trustees, who are major financial patrons, and hence ''subtly affects the relations between the board and the artists,'' Mr. Brustein observed. This can lead to conflicts over artistic decisions, such as the dispute between Miss Verdy and the Boston Ballet board over her effort to cut the size of the troupe and perform fewer splashy and ambitious productions.

Mr. Brustein added that the problem ''is not for want of audiences.''

''We were playing,'' he said, ''to 94 percent of capacity this year, an unusually high figure.'' Moreover, he said, corporate support for the American Repertory Theater had finally begun to increase, rising from $2,500 in 1980 to $100,000 this year.

But Mr. Brustein said he felt that Boston's Puritan heritage still influenced patterns of giving, making it acceptable for affluent Boston families to contribute to established institutions such as the Boston Symphony, but less acceptable to donate to radical theater groups or the ballet.

''The Puritans closed down the theater in England from 1642 to 1660,'' he recalled. ''You could put on a play in that period if you called it opera, for music was sacred and theater was profane. These are the same folks who came to Boston, and it carried over.''

But Miss Hawley countered that ''the whole Puritan image thing is tired.'' It may have been true up to about 1960, she said, but now ''the real problem is that the living arts are different from the historical arts.''

Perils of the Unknown

People know they are going to like a Beethoven symphony before they go, Miss Hawley explained, so they are willing to give money. ''But how do you know whether you will like a ballet choreographed by Choo San Goh?''

She said the trouble at both the Boston Ballet and the Boston Shakespeare Company began ''because they tried to stretch themselves, reaching out for brilliant artists, and then couldn't follow through'' given their limited financial base.

The Shakespeare Company, a fledgling group whose productions were often amateurish until Mr. Sellars was hired last year, simply could not match the offer he received from the Kennedy Center in Washington, where he will now be director of the newly established American National Theater Company, in charge of three theaters.

In quitting, Mr. Sellars described the Shakespeare Company's operating budget of $840,000 in the 1983-84 season as ''peanuts,'' and indicated that the company's board had turned down his request to raise it to $1.2 million for next year. In fact, said Jack Thomas, director of marketing for the Shakespeare Company, the budget had been doubled for Mr. Sellars last year, but the board felt his proposed budget was beyond their resources from box-office sales and donations. At the Kennedy Center, he will have a guaranteed budget of $2 million.

Momentum Is in Jeopardy

Mr. Thomas conceded that the Shakespeare Company would have a hard time sustaining the momentum Mr. Sellars created in his one year here. But the 20 year-old Boston Ballet is even more imperiled. Its crisis began last May when its founder, E. Virginia Williams, died, exposing a long-simmering conflict between Miss Verdy, Mr. Garrick and the board of trustees. A series of mishaps and poor judgments have also left the ballet with a deficit of $1 million.

In leaving, Miss Verdy denounced the board's ''pettiness,'' saying the management had made two dancers representing the troupe at the New York Ballet Competition pay rent for their costumes. After Miss Verdy's resignation, several dancers, including Marie-Christine Mouis, a principal dancer, as well as the resident choreographer and ballet mistress, also quit.

At the Boston Pops, Mr. Williams stunned the orchestra when he suddenly announced he was quitting after a handful of musicians quietly hissed during a rehearsal of Mr. Williams's ''America, the Beat Goes On.''

Musicians' Interpretations

Several members of the orchestra said they felt this was an overreaction by Mr. Williams, who has gained fame and riches from composing scores for such movies as ''Jaws,'' ''Star Wars'' and ''Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom.'' As Harry Ellis Dickson, the associate conductor, pointed out, hissing at new and unfamiliar music has been a tradition at the Pops and the Boston Symphony for years.

Mr. Dickson said he regretted Mr. Williams's leaving but suggested that Arthur Fiedler, the Pops conductor for 51 years, would have simply ignored the incident.

Other musicians said they felt Mr. Williams was trying to convert the Pops into a serious symphony orchestra for his own prestige.

Mr. Williams officially performed his last concert with the Pops last Sunday, but he has met with the trustees since his resignation was announced on June 13, and an orchestra official said there was a slim chance that the conductor might reconsider his decision. Mr. Williams, who lives in Los Angeles, has refused to discuss the matter publicly.

At the Museum of Fine Arts, Mr. Moffet's departure to pursue a writing career may not displease some Boston art critics who felt he had too narrowly focused on color field abstractionism. But for the museum, whose officials had praised Mr. Moffet's contribution, his leaving means it must now find a replacement for the man who started its department of contemporary art in 1971.
