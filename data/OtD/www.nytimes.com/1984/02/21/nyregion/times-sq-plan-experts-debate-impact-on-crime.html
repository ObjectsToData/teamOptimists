TIMES SQ. PLAN: EXPERTS DEBATE IMPACT ON CRIME

By WALTER GOODMAN
Published: February 21, 1984

Where will all the street people go?

That is one of the questions raised by the recently released draft environmental impact statement on the redevelopment of the Times Square area.

The report says the redevelopment of the blocks between Broadway and Eighth Avenue and 41st and 43d Streets will displace a great number of the drug dealers, chain snatchers, hustlers, vagrants, alcoholics, loiterers and prostitutes who now make the area seem so menacing.

But experts disagree as to what effect their displacement, if indeed they do leave, will have on crime in the city as a whole.

Move Up Broadway

William Kornblum, of the City University of New York's Graduate Center, said that those now drawn to the Times Square-42d Street area could move to a nearby location, such as up Broadway to 49th Street, which already has its complement of street people and pornography outlets.

But Mr. Kornblum, whose earlier studies of life on 42d Street were cited heavily in the report, emphasized that no other area holds Times Square's attraction for the street people.

''Everything now converges on those blocks of 42d Street - transportation, action movies, pornography,'' Mr. Kornblum said. ''There's no place else in the city where these come together in such a concentration.''

''Dispersal is the basic point,'' he said. 'We Hunt Them Out'

A similar rationale was advanced last week by William J. Stern, chairman and president of the state's Urban Development Corporation, which is organizing the planned redevelopment. Referring to the area's pornography merchants, he said: ''Scattering them is the first shot. We fire the first cannonball which scatters them and then we hunt them out.''

The $1.6 billion redevelopment plan calls for demolishing or overhauling virtually the entire area and building four new office towers, a wholesale merchandise mart and a 560-room hotel, and renovating nine theaters. It also calls for construction of 170,000 square feet of retail space, almost all of which would face 42d Street.

Dick Netzer, director of the Urban Research Center at New York University, agrees that redevelopment will make 42d Street less congenial to those who now occupy the sidewalks, and more congenial to law-abiding citizens.

''But,'' he said, ''nobody has really pursued the problem of the 'problem people.' '' Dr. Netzer believes that redevelopment would reduce the number of young people who make up a large percentage of the area's regulars and transients. Such a reduction is particularly desirable, he said, because ''teen-agers have a deserved reputation for behaving better if there are fewer of them.'' Truancy Crackdown Favored

He favors more forceful efforts for getting the young off the streets. ''I've never understood why we don't deal seriously with truancy,'' he said. He suggested a return to a traditional approach - making parents obey the truancy law.

''If each and every month fines of $25 and $50 were levied against their parents, that might be effective,'' he said.

Dr. Netzer also predicted that prostitution in the area would decrease after redevelopment because prostitutes would not fit in so readily in the new setting.

''It's a simple fact that the easier it is to engage in prostitution, the more of it there will be,'' he said, ''and around 42d Street it's now very easy.'' He said that in Great Britain, the repeal of all laws against off-street prostitution, along with severe penalties for on-street prostitution, had done away with the public nuisance, if not with the problem itself. ''Why don't we try something like that?'' he said. Relocation Criticized

Herbert J. Gans, a professor of sociology at Columbia University, is skeptical of the whole redevelopment approach. ''I've never liked the idea of some people being involuntarily relocated for the benefit of other people,'' he said.

The elimination of the action-movie houses would be ''a true injustice,'' he added, since, as the report says, it would deprive low-income people of an inexpensive entertainment center. The 42d Street action-movie houses tend to charge less than other Broadway movie houses.

''Dispersal won't do anything,'' Professor Gans said. ''If the area is upgraded to attract more affluent visitors, that will be an added incentive to prostitutes and pickpockets - they can increase their take.''

The question of how to eliminate crime, he said, ''gets us back to the old question of how to eliminate poverty.'' He also saw a chance that ridding 42d Street of the people who make it ''exciting'' could result in the area becoming ''so dreary that nobody will come there.'' Influx Feared in Village

Richard Sennett, University Professor of the Humanities at New York University, said: ''In general, there's no such thing as cleaning up a city. All you can do is move the noxious elements from one place to another. In the Village, where I live, people are terrified that their neighborhood will be the next congregating place. They're not enthusiastic about redeveloping 42d Street.''

Although Dr. Kornblum believes that dispersing the undesirable elements will be good for New York, he has no illusions that it will do much to reduce crime citywide.

''The crimes committed around Times Square are relatively minor stuff compared with what's going on elsewhere - say, in the garment center,'' he said. ''It's a drop in the bucket. Although those two square blocks loom large in the public mind and take up a lot of police manpower, redeveloping them will still leave plenty of room for the same kinds of uses.''

''There will still be pornography centers and there will still be a soft-drug market in midtown,'' he added. ''The only change may be that instead of the business being done in a bazaar, it will be handled by corner vendors.''
