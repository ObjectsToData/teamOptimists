SOUTHERN CULTURE GETS ITS OWN ENCYCLOPEDIA

By WILLIAM E. SCHMIDT
Published: August 19, 1984

OXFORD, Miss.— There is to be a long section on Southern prisons and a short one on Rebel Yell bourbon. Someone is to discuss the impact air-conditioning has had on Southern economic development; someone else is to write about possums.

It is called the Encyclopedia of Southern Culture, and when a group of scholars at the University of Mississippi completes it, expected in 1986, the work will stand, they hope, as the most complete single reference volume ever produced on the mind and manners and mythology of the American South, from John C. Calhoun to ''The Dukes of Hazzard.''

''The South historically has been our most isolated, our most intense regional experience,'' said Prof. William C. Ferris, director of the university's Center for the Study of Southern Culture, which is producing the volume. ''As a result, there has been a real need, we believe, to chart the landscape of Southern culture.''

Research on the encyclopedia began four years ago and is nearly complete. Professor Ferris and his associates in Oxford have sought contributions from 800 scholars at universities across the nation. Manners, Morals and History

They have organized the volume around 24 thematic sections, including such topics as Black Life and Race Relations, Women and Family Life, and Southern Manners, Morals and History.

But within these sections and others on the communications media, law, language, class structure and music there are to be dozens of smaller sketches of the Southern scene.

Among the essays planned for the book are brief biographies on popular cultural icons like Richard Petty and Paul (Bear) Bryant, a discussion of the family reunion as a popular Southern ritual, and reviews of landscapes as diverse as William Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha County and Mayberry, N.C., the fictional setting of ''The Andy Griffith Show.''

''What we end up dealing with are many Souths,'' said Charles R. Wilson, who, along with Professor Ferris, is a co-editor of the volume. ''There is the geographical South. There is the South outside the South, which includes not only expatriate writers but Southern black communiities in northern cities. And then there is also the South as mythic reality, the South of 'Roots' and Faulkner and the blues.

''You cannot deal with one and not the other,'' he added.

So far, the work has been paid for by more than $300,000 in grants from the National Endowment for the Humanities in addition to $80,000 from private donors and the Ford Foundation. 1,000 Pages Expected

The volume is scheduled for publication in 1986 by the University of North Carolina Press. It is expected to run approximately 1,000 pages and to sell at retail for about $40.

On one level, the encyclopedia underscores a historical proclivity, some have called it an obsession, among Southerners to scrutinize their past as something apart and beyond the larger American experience.

''That's because Southerners have a real self-consciousness of being Southern,'' said Profesor Ferris, a native of Vicksburg, Miss. ''There is a memory, a long memory here, not only because the South was defeated in war, but because it has seen, as a region, the worst extremes of both poverty and racism, as well as some of the most creative writing in America.''

At the same time, Professor Ferris said, the idea of the South has had a powerful effect on people all around the world. ''The music of Elvis Presley and B. B. King, the images in 'Roots' and 'Gone With the Wind' have touched people everywhere,'' he said.

Professor Ferris said the volume also reflected a growing appetite among scholars to concentrate on regional studies of American culture.

He said a similar encyclopedia on New England culture was now in the planning stages at the University of Massachusetts at Amherst. In addition to the Southern center here at Oxford, the University of Nebraska has created a Great Plains Center and there is an Appalachian Study Center at the University of Kentucky. Growth in Regional Studies

The interest in regional studies has grown as scholars have attempted to reach a deeper understanding of American culture, Professor Ferris said. ''We are a much more complicated people than we think,'' he said.

The Center for the Study of Southern Culture was founded at the University of Mississippi in 1977. The center not only organizes an annual seminar on Faulkner, as well as conferences on other aspects of Southern culture, but also offers an undergraduate degree in Southern studies to students at the University of Mississippi. About 300 students were enrolled last year in courses in the program.

The encylopedia was conceived in 1978 as a flagship project of the center. In the past, Professor Wilson said, most works on the region have been done from the perspective of a single discipline, such as history or religion. ''What is exciting about this project is that it provides an interdisciplinary framework,'' he said. ''We do not only discuss, for example, the existence of German migration to the region, but we have an architect discussing the influence of German architecture and linguists discussing the influence of the German language.''

The encyclopedia is also scheduled to include a treasure of regional trivia. Among other things, readers are to discover that there are four ordained rabbis in the state of Mississippi; that 65 percent of the quarterbacks in the National Football League come from the South, and that while 90 percent of all office buildings in the South are air conditioned, only 60 percent of the hosptials are.
