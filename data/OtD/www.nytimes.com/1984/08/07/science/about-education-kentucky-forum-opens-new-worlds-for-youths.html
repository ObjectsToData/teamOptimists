ABOUT EDUCATION; KENTUCKY FORUM OPENS NEW WORLDS FOR YOUTHS

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: August 7, 1984

DANVILLE, KY. IT was a rare moment in legal history: Socrates and Thoreau were being tried simultaneously by a court of teen-agers who thought it natural to treat the two defendants as contemporaries. After all, their ideas - the rights of individuals versus the power of the state - had much in common.

The accused, their lawyers, the judges and the jury were among 300 high school students who, at the end of their junior year in schools throughout Kentucky, had been selected for a six-week residential program on the campus of Centre College in this small Kentucky town. An equal number of students go to Eastern Kentucky University in Richmond.

Known as the Governor's Scholars Program, it was initiated a year ago by Gov. John Y. Brown Jr. and is being continued by Gov. Martha Layne Collins. Its purpose is to open up to the brightest youths a world of possibilities that might otherwise remain closed to them. Incidentally, the sponsors hope to light a fire under the schools of a state that has the nation's highest percentage of adults without high school diplomas. The program's annual cost is $600,000, half of which is contributed by private sources in order to protect it against control by the state.

''There's so much raw talent here,'' said Robert J. Rabel, a young associate professor of classics at the University of Kentucky, one of the program's 21 faculty members on this campus. The students, nominated by their schools, must meet criteria established by the sponsors intended ''to prevent local superintendents or politicians from sending their nieces and nephews,'' one observer said.

Most of the youths would be the first members of their families to go to college. Few had ever been away from home. ''It worries some parents,'' said James L. Howard, professor of education at Centre College and dean of the program. ''We even had one case of parents 'kidnapping' their daughter after three days. We never got her back.''

Cases of mutual homesickness, however, are rarely that drastic. Counselors, one for every group of 20 scholars, are hand-picked college students who help the teen-agers find their bearings. Almost one-third of the scholars come from the poverty of Appalachia. Many had virtually no experience in writing and minimal acquaintance with literature. By last week they seemed entirely at home with such works as ''Iliad'' and ''Odyssey,'' ''Walden Pond,'' the writings of Kurt Vonnegut Jr. and a host of other works, most of them unavailable in their school libraries.

When the prosecutor in the mock trial snarled at Thoreau, ''Why don't you go somewhere else if you don't like it here?'' the student snapped back, ''Eh, America, love it or leave it?'' Socrates, faced with a sarcastic, ''Oh, you think you're enlightening people with your constant questioning?'' replied calmly, ''No, I want them to enlighten themselves.''

Every student picks a major and meets for up to three hours every morning in classes of about 20, many taught by teams of two or three teachers. Majors range from mathematics and science to history, cultural anthropology, literature, fine arts and political economy. In the afternoon a shorter period is given to the minor. Science majors must minor in the humanities and vice versa. One class last week dealt with the Presidential elections by way of computer simulation; another looked at ''heroes and monsters,'' based on prior readings from the ''Aeneid'' to James Bond. Intellectual activities are planned for the evenings. Speakers have included Nobel laureates and astronauts. One evening last week black students from Kentucky State College presented a condensed version of ''Porgy and Bess'' to wild applause.

''I really didn't want to apply,'' said a boy from rural Kentucky. ''My Dad practically stood over me with a whip until I did.'' Like many others, he had doubted his capacity to compete. One week before the program's end such misgivings had long evaporated. ''It's great,'' said a girl at lunch. ''You don't have to apologize to your friends if you go to the library.''

Youths break out their secret writings, including poetry. ''They bring us their manuscripts,'' said Milton Reigelman, chairman of the division of humanities at Centre and a member of the program's faculty. ''They can be very demanding of us now that they've discovered scholarship.''

Academic routines are relieved by sports and extracurricular activities. Student-run clubs include philosophy, karate, breakdancing, chess, debate, writing, jogging and ham radio. Lessons are available in French and tennis. Students from religious homes set up their own prayer and Bible meetings. One day, Dean Howard said, he and his wife offered to teach ballroom dancing. ''I thought a few might come just to see us old folks make fools of ourselves,'' he said. ''More than 80 showed up.'' They have been keeping up with the waltz and foxtrot ever since.

An added benefit is college counseling. Lillian Press, the Governor's resident director, said she expected all the youths would go to college, a large minority of them to elite campuses including the Ivy League.

The program's subtle side effects could challenge some of the state's, and even the nation's, education shibboleths. For example, the faculty consists of an equal number of college and high school teachers, working together in harmony. Freeing the curriculum of the straitjacket of 50- minute periods is crucial to effective learning. An education that liberates the young could set an example elsewhere.

''Six hundred youngsters a year may not sound like much,'' said one staff member. ''But it adds up. Their testimony may put pressure on the system.''

One girl said, ''I learned a lot that I can take back to my school.'' Another added, ''Here there's a lot of freedom to explore.''

Freedom affects the faculty too. Before the jury in the Socrates-Thoreau trial ended its deliberation, two teachers argued heatedly over the guilt of breaking existing laws and the charge that society had been undermined. The students joined in; they had learned that by disagreeing in front of them, their teachers were enhancing rather than subverting their education.
