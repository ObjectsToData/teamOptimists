ANTIQUES VIEW

ANTIQUES VIEW; FROM PRAGUE, A WEALTH OF JUDAICA

By Rita Reif
Published: April 15, 1984

T he most comprehensive view of a nation's Judaica ever seen in New York has been assembled in the ''Precious Legacy: Judaic Treasures From the Czechoslovak State Collections,'' an exhibition opening today at the Jewish Museum, Fifth Avenue at 92d Street, and remaining through Aug. 26.

As impressive as are many of the 300 objects and period texts presented, this is a show that will be experienced by most visitors more as history - some of it horrifying - than as art. Many of the Torah curtains, pointers, lamps, prayer books and candlesticks gathered for this exhibition were confiscated from the Jews of Bohemia and Moravia by the Nazis during World War II and sent to Prague where they were to be part of Hitler's museum of an extinct race. We are reminded of this fact in several compelling photographs of Jews being deported and the Judaic objects taken from them shown arranged in stacks and jammed on shelves.

The ritual objects, art works and books on view date from the 14th to the mid-20th century and include masterpieces as well as culturally significant selections in silver, stitchery, ceramics, glass, furniture, carved wood, metal, painted portraits, handwritten scrolls, books and kitchen utensils. All were chosen from the 145,000 objects in the State Jewish Museum of Prague, an institution of the Czech State since 1949. The museum dates from 1906 when it came into being as the Jewish Museum of Prague under the direction of Salomon Hugo Lieben, a historian and collector who was concerned about preserving the ritual artifacts in synagogues that were no longer operating.

Outstanding in this show are the Bohemian and Moravian needlework specimens, most of which are Torah valances, curtains and binders embroidered with traditional religious and patriotic symbols and Hebrew inscriptions. The stitchery skills of these peoples produced ravishing works of extraordinary refinement and sophistication. The displays include both boldly stitched and delicately wrought designs of the finest silks embellished with gold thread or the simplest linens stitched in silk.

There are glorious examples of silver - a sparely crafted 16th-century wine tankard, an elaborate neo- classical 18th-century pitcher and tray, robust Torah shields, crowns and finials - all master works and well marked, many by Jewish craftsmen. The silver Torah pointers are upstaged in this show by the carved- wood versions, all of which have the same basic shape but each of which differs according to the imagination of the artisan. The hand-shaped pointers are powerfully conceived folk sculptures connected to ''arms'' of knobs, twists, loops and shapely muscles. Memorable, too, is a glass beaker depicting in glossy enamels a burial scene showing a great crowd of men and women in native costume.

The collection was dramatically augmented in 1942 when it was taken over by the Nazis and converted into the Central Jewish Museum. By 1945 an additional 100,000 Jewish possessions - liturgical books, genre paintings, religious objects, folk crafts, kitchen utensils and synagogue implements - had been added. These art and artifacts were viewed by the museum staff as ''the mute and compelling testimony of a persecuted people,'' according to the account offered in the show's superb catalogue, published by Summit Books ($17.50). The catalogue was prepared by Vivian B. Mann, curator of Judaica and chief curator of the exhibition, and her assistant, Emily D. Bilsky, in association with museum curators and others at the museum in Prague. It was edited by David Altshuler, professor of Judaic studies at George Washington University.

Conceived 15 years ago by Mark Talisman, director of Project Judaica, the exhibition was organized by the Smithsonian Institution Traveling Exhibition Service in cooperation with the Ministry of Culture of the Czech Socialist Republic, the Federal Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the Czechoslovak Socialist Republic, the National Committee of the Capital of Prague and the State Jewish Museum of Prague. The show is aided by grants from Philip Morris, the national corporate sponsor, and the National Endowment for the Humanities. After it closes, the show will travel to San Diego, New Orleans, Detroit and Hartford.

Traditionally Judaic art and artifacts were carefully preserved by the communities and synagogues in which they originated and they were passed down from generation to generation in families. Collecting of Judaica began in the 19th century and some museums were founded in Europe at the turn of the century to house such materials. Prior to World War II, American Jews helped in removing many of the great Judaic works from European synagogues, fearful that they would be destroyed by the Nazis. The Torah lamps and curtains, prayer books, spice boxes and burial society beakers were brought to New York and stored at the Jewish Theological Seminary, and were moved to the Jewish Museum when it opened in its Fifth Avenue home in 1947.

Throughout the 1950's and 60's Judaica collecting increased slowly in this country. The antiques boom spurred further interest in the 70's and, since 1981, Sotheby's has held twice-a-year sales offering both Judaic books and objects. Today, reports Jay Weinstein, Sotheby's New York specialist in such objects, there are 200 to 300 serious collectors and many more enthusiasts who are interested in making purchases from time to time.

Sotheby's announced last week that it will auction on June 26 the earliest known Jewish Bible produced in Prague, a 15th-century illuminated manuscript which the auction house estimates conservatively will sell for about $500,000. The same sale will offer another Prague work, a picture book of the Old Jewish Cemetery near the Alteneuschul, the oldest synagogue in Europe, showing closeups of some of the gravestones and bearing the seal of the burial society of Prague. Produced possibly as a souvenir or to raise funds for the society, the book is expected to sell for up to $400.
