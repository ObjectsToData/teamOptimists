ANTIQUES VIEW

ANTIQUES VIEW; CRAFTS OF THE PLAINS INDIANS

By Rita Reif
Published: April 29, 1984

There's a tepee in Andrew Carnegie's parlor, a gilded, rococo setting that doubled as the steel industrialist's music room in his Fifth Avenue mansion at the turn of the century.

''This might not be the first brush this room has had with the Indians,'' said Richard Conn, curator of native arts of the Denver Art Museum, watching the staff of the Cooper-Hewitt Museum, the Smithsonian Institution's National Museum of Design, put the finishing touches on an American Indian art exhibition that he conceived and which opened last week in Mr. Carnegie's mansion, now the museum's home, at 2 East 91st Street. ''I'm sure the strains of 'By the Waters of Minnetonka' and 'From the Land of the Sky-Blue Water' were played here in Mr. Carnegie's lifetime.''

The show, ''Circles of the World: Traditional Art of the Plains Indians,'' comprises artifacts of 18 tribes, including two tepees, a score of examples of beaded and quilled buffalo hides, several feathered headdresses, sacred ceremonial pipes and painted ''ghost dance'' dresses and shirts. The 150 selections shown, dating from 1840 to 1930, are primarily from the Denver museum's vast holdings. The exhibition opened there two years ago and was shown in Edinburgh, Vienna, Paris and Boston. After it closes June 13, it will travel to the New Orleans Museum of Art and to the California Academy of Sciences in San Francisco.

Scores of cowboy-and-Indian films have shown the sort of beaded, quill- embellished and painted clothing, the battle regalia and tribal attire that are the focus of this show. But none in memory has explained their origins, crafting and historical meaning. Considering the broad exposure this exhibition has had among audiences who possess only slight knowledge of Plains Indian history, the organization of the show along ethnic, rather than artistic lines, makes a certain sense.

The circle theme refers to the recurrence in Plains Indian work of this image, a motif favored because it has neither beginning nor end and because it symbolizes the sun, moon, calendar year and life itself. The exhibition is divided into four parts, exploring what the Plains Indian view as the four circles of life: the family, the tribe, humanity and the unknown, including supernatural experiences.

The Plains Indians' astounding affinity for color and design is demonstrated in many extraordinary examples of crafts. An exotically colored Cheyenne fan of parrot feathers succeeds by its sheer simplicity - it is shaped freely as a bouquet of plumage. A beaded leather Crow cradle impresses by its daring in the combination of patterns and colors - stripes, crosses, diamonds and squares in blues, yellows, red and black.

Notable too is a Blackfoot woman's deerskin dress, a boldly patterned selection embroidered on the bodice and sleeves with wide undulating bands of blue and black glass rods. A Crow wedding blanket is as understated as a Minimalist canvas, striped in blues, pinks and red. Dominating a painted-leather Lokata shield is a robust ''magic'' bird - its flapping wings seem to create thunder in the skies.

While the women became masters of geometric and abstract design of stunning sophistication, the men evolved a different language using figural elements, some of which proved equally powerful. One of the most haunting is a Pawnee Ghost Dance dress, in red and blue, awash with large white stars and figures of birds and a dancing ghost.

Concurrently, the museum offers a second presentation of American Indian wares in ''Finished in Beauty: Southwest Indian Silver.'' On view are 300 selections of Navajo and Zuni silver jewelry, collected by the late Virginia Doneghy, a librarian at the University of Minnesota, who collected from the late 1930's on and gave the assemblage to the Minneapolis Institute of Arts before her death in 1981. The show, organized by the Minneapolis museum, is described as the first major exhibition of its kind to be shown outside the Southwest.

Miss Doneghy's taste was for the traditional, as can be seen in the silver necklaces, rings, bracelets, belts and earrings she acquired. Louise Lincoln, an assistant curator at the Minneapolis museum who edited the catalogue for this show, explained that the material shown is from three periods. The earliest designs, from 1860 to 1910, embody such classic motifs as squash blossoms, crosses and human hands. In the second period, from 1910 to 1940, silver had many of the same images but is more commercial - lighter in weight and more mechanical in execution. Coins, which had always been used melted down and recycled in jewelry, became a design element in necklaces between World War I and II when dimes and quarters visibly proliferate. In the post World War II period, individual expression returns in the finest designs, and often a change of scale achieves some arresting effects. After the show closes here July 8, it will travel to Denver and to several European cities.

The catalogues for both shows are excellent, documenting the history and tribal backgrounds of the Indian artisans and the artifacts they produced. Each generously illustrated volume is $14.95. Both shows have received funding from the National Endowment for the Humanities. The Plains Indians exhibition has also received grants from the National Endowment for the Arts, the New York State Council on the Arts and the American Express Foundation.

Although most of the finest selections on view would be impossible to duplicate, there is an abundance of such material in the marketplace. Prices vary from $100 for the more conventional objects to as much as five figures. The auction record for Plains Indian material is $41,250, the price paid in 1982 at Sotheby's for a quilled and beaded Eastern Plains shirt. The highest auction price for American Indian silver is $27,500, paid at Christie's in 1981 for a Navajo silver belt.
