EDUCATION; RELIGION STUDIES ARE THRIVING

By GENE I. MAEROFF
Published: October 23, 1984

NEW BRUNSWICK, N.J.

IT sounded almost like Sunday

school. The subject was God

and the discussion one day last

week revolved around the belief of Lutheranism that sin can be overcome only by faith in Jesus and that faith is a gift of grace. But the classroom was on the campus of Rutgers, the state university, not in a church.

At a time when the public schools have become the focus of a political debate over the issue of separation of church and state, religion is quietly thriving as a topic of academic inquiry at many of the nation's publicly supported universities.

The ability of secular institutions of higher education to avoid controversy while dealing with religion owes much to the fact that the public universities concentrate on the intellectual aspects and shun the devotional. In the minds of some educators, this approach illustrates that the brouhaha over prayer in the public schools has obscured a role that public education can legally play in accommodating interest in religion.

''Part of the problem is getting the public to understand the difference between the study and the practice of religion,'' said Mahlon H. Smith, chairman of the religion department at Rutgers. ''It is understandable that when religion is excluded totally it seems to some people that public education is little different from that in atheistic Russia. But it need not be that way because you can have the objective study of religion if you wish.''

In pursuing this approach, the state universities have moved far beyond isolated offerings in the philosophy department on the ethics of religion or in the English department on the literary aspects of the Bible. Since the late 1960's, there has been a proliferation of full-fledged departments and the neutral intent is underscored by calling them religion departments, not theology departments.

Interest in religion at public universities is reflected by the consistently strong enrollments at many institutions. Few students go to state universities to major in religion, but at a time of emphasis on career preparation the elective courses are nonetheless a popular way of satisfying requirements in the humanities.

'More Open-Minded' Viewpoint

''I know that taking a religion course won't make me more money as a businessman or a scientist, but I think I can get a kind of knowledge that will have a lasting effect,'' said Frederick Kleinbeck, a Rutgers sophomore, who, undecided on his major, is taking an introductory religion course. ''I went to Catholic schools and I believe in God and Jesus Christ and I think I can gain from studying religion in a state university because the point of view is more open-minded.''

Rutgers has only 35 students majoring in religion, but 60 courses in religion are listed by the department, which enrolled 1,100 students in religion classes last spring and 875 this fall.

There are 407 students in religion courses at the State University of New York at Stony Brook this fall, twice as many as three years ago. And at the University of California at Santa Barbara the religion department is so highly regarded that the California Council on the Humanities has awarded the department a grant to develop materials for teaching about religion in the state's high schools.

Courses in religion are in such demand at the University of Virginia that the department, which was created in 1967, is already one of the largest at any public university in the country with its 23 full-time faculty members. At least 300 students crowd into a lecture hall to listen to Kyle McCarter offer his course on the Old Testament and an equal number are enrolled in Harry Gamble's course on the New Testament.

'Very Powerful Teachers'

''We've been lucky in having some very powerful teachers in this department and as a result the courses have been enormously attractive to students,'' said Nathan A. Scott Jr., chairman and the occupant of one of three endowed chairs in the religion department at Virginia.

Educators at the public universities maintain that students feel drawn to courses on religion despite the secular character of their institutions.

''Religion is inherently interesting and students are naturally curious about it, whether their own experiences have been good or bad,'' said Lawrence S. Cunningham, a religion professor at Florida State University, where the newest course is tied to the current television series, ''Heritage: Civilization and the Jews.''

Mr. Cunningham's own course, ''Religion and Literature,'' examines belief and nonbelief from the vantage points of such novelists as James Joyce, Walker Percy and Mary Gordon.

Students say that a state university offers a desirable setting for studying religion because professors are prevented from taking sides.

''I wanted to study religion from the outside looking in and not with a professor who has an ulterior motive and is trying to push his views on me,'' said Eliot Maletzky, a sophomore majoring in anthropology at Rutgers, who this year is enrolled in ''Death and Afterlife.''

Students, in fact, may be quick to react if they think a professor is treading on their beliefs.

Student Rejects Concept

Last week at Rutgers, for example, Alberto R. W. Green, who teaches a course on ancient Near Eastern religions, was describing the Egyptian deity Amon to his class. He said that the Egyptian monistic concept allowed for several gods to be present in a single deity, possibly providing a basis by which Christianity was able to accommodate the idea of the Father, the Son and the Holy Ghost within a triune Godhead.

A hand shot up. ''I can't accept that,'' said Cheryl Wuensch, a senior from Maplewood, N.J., who expects to go to law school next year. ''I have spent years trying to understand the Trinity and I don't think you can just explain it away in three minutes.''

Such exchanges are a constant reminder to professsors of the tenuous ground on which they walk in teaching about religion at a public university. Jeffrey Goldstein, another of the members of the religion department at Rutgers, said, ''You have a difficult line to tread, always wondering if you are overstepping the bounds for a secular institution.''
