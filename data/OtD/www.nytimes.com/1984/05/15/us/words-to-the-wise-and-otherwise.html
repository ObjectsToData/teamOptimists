WORDS TO THE WISE AND OTHERWISE

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY
Published: May 15, 1984

WASHINGTON, May 14— Sidney Hook, the New York University philosopher and guru of either the new conservatives or the old liberals (depending on the person filling out label), lectured tonight in an ornate auditorium here on the humanities and the defense of a free society.

Professor Hook gave the annual Jefferson Lecture of the National Endowment for the Humanities, speaking at the Departmental Auditorium and competing for an audience with the nearby National Theater, which had a gala opening for the Broadway musical ''Cats,'' and with the Folger Theater across town, which held a quieter opening of its production of ''Henry V.''

Despite the competition of T. S. Eliot set to music and of history set to drama, the professor did not suffer for lack of audience. This is so because on almost any given night, Washingtonians can drop in on a marvelously wide range of talks offering intellectual entertainment. From Pompous to Provocative

Washington, properly, is best known for a torrent of words on Government, politics, war and peace that gushes practically every day from the White House, the Congress and the Supreme Court. Those words may be pompous or predictable. The lectures, by contrast, are usually provocative.

On a recent evening Clark M. Clifford discussed Harry S. Truman in a talk at the Woodrow Wilson International Center for Scholars, which has its headquarters at the Smithsonian Institution, and on another night Bernard Malamud read from his own works at the Library of Congress. On May 22, Edward L. Bernays, who is known as the father of modern public relations, will speak on his work at the National Portrait Gallery.

Most of the talking can be listened to free of charge. Much of it is in museums and Government buildings, but much of it is in colleges and universities as well.

According to Roger G. Kennedy, director of the Smithsonian National Museum of American History, what distinguishes a Washington lecture from any other in the United States is the audience, which frequently includes Congressional staff assistants.

''There is a fiscal consequence,'' he said. ''Congress will provide funds if members' staff aides attend a lecture and recommend something.'' The Doubleday Lectures

Mr. Kennedy's museum runs the most popular series in Washington, the Doubleday Lectures, so named because they are sponsored by the publishing concern. They are held four times a year and the audience, limited to 400 people each time, is invited by Doubleday or selected from a list maintained by the Smithsonian.

Asked how one gets on the list, Mr. Kennedy replied: ''One gets on the list by asking to get on the list. It may take a long time to get on.'' Mr. Kennedy is the first to acknowledge that the series has been made popular by a combination ''of show biz and interesting subjects.''

For example, he said, the choreographer Arthur Mitchell lectured on modern dance and used members of his Dance Theater of Harlem to illustrate it. The actress Kitty Carlisle Hart gave a review of her life in song. The musicologist Gunther Schuller lectured on Sarah Vaughn, insisting that Miss Vaughn's jazz was as serious an art form as opera, and Miss Vaughn herself then sang and provided what Mr. Kennedy said was proof of Mr. Schuller's thesis. Of the More Normal Variety

Lectures of the more normal stand- in-front-of-an-audience-and-talk variety are offered by the Wilson Center.

Admission to most Wilson lectures, which are held in the Smithsonian's ''castle,'' is free and on a first-come, first-seated basis. Louise Platt, a Wilson spokesman, said the center presented upwards of 300 lectures, workshops and colloquiums a year.

While the American History Museum lectures tend toward the popular and the Wilson toward the political, the talks at the Library of Congress, not surprisingly, tend more toward the intellectual. They are financed by the Gertrude Clarke Whittall Poetry and Literature Fund, based on the donor's gift to the library in the 1930's, which included the money for the lectures plus rare manuscripts and a collection of Stradivarius violins.

All the talks at the library are open to the public without charge, and sometimes they are so popular that the library issues free tickets to make sure the auditorium is not overcrowded. One That Caused a Stir

Most of the time, the talks inform or, perhaps, entertain, but rarely do they cause a stir outside the lecture hall. At a recent talk at the National Portrait Gallery, however, William L. Shirer stirred up a bit of a fuss.

Mr. Shirer, the author of ''The Rise and Fall of the Third Reich,'' was participating in the ''Living Portrait'' lectures, a series in which noted people discuss their own careers and which has attracted Robert Moses, Jesse Owens, Lee Strasberg, Karl Menninger and I. B. Singer. This is the series Mr. Bernays will take part in next week.

Mr. Shirer said the United States invasion of Grenada reminded him of the way the Nazis marched into Austria to, as the Germans said, ''prevent chaos.'' Mr. Shirer hastened to say he was not comparing President Reagan with Hitler but added, ''I wonder if it's history repeating itself.''

Mr. Shirer went on to unburden himself of other views of people he had met in his long journalistic career, including Gandhi, of whom he said, ''He had idiotic ideas about sex.''
