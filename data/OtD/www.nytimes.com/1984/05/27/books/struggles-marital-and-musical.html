STRUGGLES MARITAL AND MUSICAL

Published: May 27, 1984

Edward Rothstein is the music critic for The New Republic. AN EVENING OF BRAHMS

By Richard Sennett. 222 pp. New York: Alfred A. Knopf. $13.95.

JUST before he died, Robert Schumann wrote that

Brahms's music seemed to him like a waterfall,

''overarched by a peaceful rainbow, while butterflies played about its borders and the voices of nightingales obliged.'' But Schumann, who may have been in the late stages of syphilis, was hearing angelic voices in his head; gradually they turned hellish and drove him insane. As for Brahms, his music is more troubled than idyllic - filled with voices not just of nightingales but vultures.

''Ours is a friendly art'' is what the music professor Signor Grisi teaches in Richard Sennett's novel ''An Evening of Brahms.'' But for all the music's sweetness and light, Grisi also knows its dark side, and so does the author. Mr. Sennett, a sociologist and a professor of the humanities at New York University, is intrigued by such shadows. In his professional writing, he has described what he sees as the insinuating power of authority and the oppressive banality of contemporary life (in ''Authority'' and ''The Fall of Public Man''). In his first novel, ''The Frog Who Dared to Croak,'' the inner life of an Eastern European intellectual is shaped by outer tyrannies. Now he turns his attention to the heart of another sort of inner life - the struggle with music.

That struggle begins with a rehearsal of Brahms's C Minor Piano Quartet, a work, the novel points out, that was begun when Brahms was enraptured with Schumann's wife, Clara, and Schumann was in an asylum. It was rewritten over a period of 20 years, while Brahms and Clara continued an ambiguous, troubled epistolary romance. ''A man holding a gun to his head'' is how Brahms described the music.

Such historical detail in the novel is constant counterpoint to Mr. Sennett's tale of a contemporary romance that is also ill-fated. Alexander Hoffmann, a prodigious cellist (who may bear some resemblance to Mr. Sennett, himself an accomplished cellist) meets his pianist wife, Susan, during a rehearsal of that quartet. Three years later she is dead, run over by a taxi just as the couple was on the brink of divorce. While the quartet was Brahms's attempt to express the pains of his romance, this book is an account of Alex's attempt to comprehend his own.

Music takes center stage in the narrative; there are authentic comments about rehearsing, playing and conducting. Measure numbers are noted, key changes described. There is commentary on Schumann's ''Scenes From Childhood'' and Brahms's ''Requiem.'' A doctor gives a lecture on Schumann's syphilis. Alex lectures an orchestra, Signor Grisi his players, Alex Susan, Alex's cello teacher Alex. These lectures and descriptions are the most insightful parts of the book but the least novelistic - it is as though a pianist were to interrupt his playing to offer program notes. There are references to Mr. Sennett's themes - ''forces of darkness'' redeemed through art, as the doctor talking about Schumann puts it. Alex's struggle with the cello, in fact, cannot be split apart from his past - his parents' divorce, their broken idealism and emotional tensions. But what the author means to do is not what the book actually does.

Its style is self-consciously lyrical, but the pacing and plotting are often awkward. The prose can be precious (''The ballet of recrimination between them was set to music'') or pretentiously unclear (''A musician hearing through words is in danger of hearing everything''). The romance between Susan and Alex parallels in some tragic way Clara's and Brahms's. Alex even wants to read from that musical couple's correspondence at Susan's funeral. But in what way is Susan like Clara Schumann? And why later, in another analogy, are Susan's problems with her hands made so obviously parallel to Robert Schumann's?

These allusions to Brahms and Schumann may be meant to illustrate the way ''shadows'' in life pass into music. But the characters are relatively simple. Alex is a brilliant musician whose tensions cause problems with his ability to perform, but he overcomes them. ''On the one hand,'' Susan thinks of Alex, ''his story was unusual, on the other he came out of it almost invisible: sweet, upright, most of him missing.'' And Susan, for her part, has not tragically ''destroyed her art,'' as the novel says. She is simply a fluent musician but not a particularly good one. He feels music deeply; she lives on its surface. There is not much resonance in the couple's marital dissonances or their ''unequal gifts.''

The result, then, is like an earnest performance in which the musician has intriguing ideas that can be heard here and there in a passing phrase or an emphasized motif. Those details, however, are left to fend for themselves. The narrative - the articulation of the theme, its transformations and modulations - is stilted. It ends with a ''coda,'' as the author calls it, that leaves narrative tensions unresolved and alludes to themes left undeveloped in the novel.
