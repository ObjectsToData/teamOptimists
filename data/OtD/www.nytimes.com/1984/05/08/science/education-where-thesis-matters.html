EDUCATION; WHERE THESIS MATTERS

By GENE I. MAEROFF
Published: May 8, 1984

PRINCETON,N.J. THE courses now drawing to a close for most college seniors are their last obstacle to graduation, but for a smaller number such as those at Princeton University there is an additional hurdle: the senior thesis.

A sort of scale model of the dissertation required of doctoral candidates, the senior thesis is a special challenge that lets an undergraduate pull together the knowledge and skills gained the previous three years to produce scholarly work of the kind usually not expected until graduate school.

''It gives a student a chance not only to pursue a subject in depth, but also to accept responsibility for something of his own,'' said William G. Bowen, the president of Princeton. ''I don't think a seminar paper is the same, nor is it the same to sit through a course and take an exam. The senior thesis is the capstone to a student's education.''

Sometimes a senior thesis at Princeton ends up as a book, as did A. Scott Berg's biography of Maxwell Perkins, the book editor, that Dutton published in 1978.

Other times it generates controversy, as in 1978 when John Aristotle Phillips created a design for a nuclear bomb that was placed alongside the other senior theses for all to read in the Seeley G. Mudd Library. This year, Anne Marie Belli has caused a stir with a senior thesis documenting that a painting in the university's museum is a forgery.

Often, a senior writes a thesis to advance career goals, as did James Stewart, the actor. He was an architecture major at Princeton when he drew plans for an airport of the future in 1932.

The absence of a thesis requirement on most campuses testifies to the division over the value and practicality of the exercise. Not everyone, including those in the Ivy League, agrees that it is worth the time.

'Act of Perfunctory Obligation'

''We sense that if it were mandatory for a class of 750 seniors it would be a ponderous, relatively hollow exercise for many,'' said Michael Rosenthal, associate dean of Columbia College, which has no thesis requirement. ''I'm sure some would do a remarkable job, but for others it would be an egregious act of perfunctory obligation.''

Typically, the option of a thesis is offered to students seeking to graduate with departmental honors.

At the University of Connecticut, for example, the 86 honors candidates writing theses are a tiny portion of the 2,900 seniors. ''It would be difficult with a class of several thousand to have every senior do a thesis,'' said Carol Rocque, an aide in the university's academic affairs office.

Union College in Schenectady, N.Y., is smaller, but there, too, only the 10 to 15 percent of the seniors pursuing departmental honors must write a thesis.

Planning May Take Years

Some Princeton students start thinking about the thesis when they arrive as freshmen. The thoughts may turn to nightmares when they learn of the 25 to 30 students who do not graduate each year for failure to turn in a thesis.

There is, however, some flexibility in the requirement. Many science and engineering students, for instance, do experiments and write papers that tend to be shorter than the traditional works of up to 200 pages by those in the humanities and social sciences.

Michael Rosenfeld, a music major, is fulfilling the mandate with a play, ''Dead Give-Away,'' this week at Princeton. He wrote it and raised $10,000 for the production.

Having a good thesis adviser can be a key to getting the most out of the experience, students say. The adviser often helps a student focus on a manageable topic and think about how to organize the work, not an easy task in light of the intimidating length.

Each faculty member advises three or four seniors and most students are assigned to professors in their major areas, but some go outside the department to find an adviser with special expertise.

Interdisciplinary Orientation

James R. Beniger, a member of the sociology faculty, is considered one of the best advisers. In the last five years, six of the seniors he advised have won the prizes that each department awards for the best thesis. He teaches courses on computers and social change, displaying wide-ranging concerns that draw students with an interdisciplinary orientation.

''Interdisciplinary things tend to attract some of the best students, those who really feel good about pushing beyond the boundaries,'' said Professor Beniger, 37 years old, in whose cluttered office senior theses in their black binders mingle among disheveled stacks of books that cover his desk and extend across the floor. A beatific Bertrand Russell looks down upon the academic jumble from a huge poster that dominates one wall.

Lisa K. Vanstone, a chemistry major, has Mr. Beniger as one of her two advisers, along with L. C. Allen, a chemistry professor. She thought Mr. Beniger could aid her investigation of how the computer is changing the way researchers develop theories. She studied two theories on the bonding of molecules, theories whose relationship has been rendered more obvious by the computer.

Professors seem to vary in their enthusiasm for advising seniors.

''Some are advisers only because they have to be,'' said Robert J. Giuffra, who won the Myron T. Herrick Prize last year from Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School for a thesis studying the 1982 gubernatorial race in New York.

''Students hand in a draft to some professors and it is returned without a mark on it, offering no help,'' Mr. Giuffra said. ''Other professors almost rewrite the draft, and it is hardly the student's own paper any more. Professor Beniger is good because he puts enough notes in the margins to make you think about what you have written, leaving you to do the rewriting. That is a learning experience.''

Thesis Defended Like Doctorate

Mr. Giuffra, is entering Yale Law School in the fall after a year as a political consultant, is now trying to find a publisher for his thesis.

''Some of these students produce work every bit as good as a doctoral dissertation,'' said Professor Beniger.

Like the authors of Ph.D. dissertations, Princeton students in many departments must defend their work orally before the adviser and a second reader.

A pitfall of so arduous a process is that inevitably some students neglect their courses to complete the thesis, even though they are given the time and academic credit equivalent to a yearlong course.

David E. Wachtel is proud that he did not cut any classes while writing his thesis this year on the use of culture to promote ideology by the Socialist Party in France, where he spent last summer doing research. But recently, three days after completing the work, the tired and bleary- eyed Mr. Wachtel went to Flordia to recuperate, missing a week of classes.
