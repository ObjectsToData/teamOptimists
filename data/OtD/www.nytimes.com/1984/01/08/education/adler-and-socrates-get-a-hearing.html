ADLER (AND SOCRATES) GET A HEARING

By SUSAN SAITER
Published: January 8, 1984

CHICAGO— MORTIMER J. ADLER'S third book completing a trilogy on the Paideia Proposal, a plan to humanize and democratize the nation's public schools, hasn't even gone to press yet, but four large school systems are already giving it a whirl or are planning to do so next fall. Oakland, Calif., and Atlanta have programs involving hundreds of elementary and high school students, and Chicago and San Diego are training teachers to use the Socratic teaching methods that they say are the wave of the future.

The Paideia Proposal, a term Dr. Adler borrowed from the Greek word for the upbringing of a child, declares war on vocational education and puts students of varying abilities together in one classical education curriculum with almost no electives. Most importantly, it divides elementary and high school teaching into three equal components - acquiring information, teacher coaching and Socratic questioning - emphasizing student discussion in an effort to awaken and polish students' critical thinking skills.

Dr. Adler calls the system ''radical,'' saying it is ''a total reorganization of the one American school system,'' which would eliminate tracking students into different areas of study.

His supporters say such a revamping is needed in a world where technology and world social problems are increasingly complex. His critics say it is naive rather than radical, that it cannot work in a capitalistic society and that it deals lightly with the effects of poverty and ignores the basic inequality of human capabilities.

Dr. Adler, who is director of the Institute for Philosophical Research and chairman of the board of editors of Encyclopedia Britannica, thinks that the current school systems, in which high achievers are locked into college-preparatory programs and others into vocational courses, is elitist and undemocratic.

Taking as his philosophical mentor John Dewey, the early 20th-century educator, Dr. Adler has railed against the academic tracking to perpetuate a proletarian class ignorant of the ideas contained in classical literature and essays. Schools, he contends, first owe students a means to enjoy a better life, then the knowledge to become good citizens. Lastly, Dr. Adler feels, schools should help students in their future vocations. ''The students should be given an education to go on learning for the rest of their lives,'' he said.

The first book, published in 1982, outlines the plan of the 23 educators who make up the Paideia Group led by Dr. Adler. A three-part chart shows the components of educating - teaching, coaching and teacher-led ''Socratic'' discussion in which students react and give opinions that are to be treated as valid, even if they are not the same as the teacher's.

The emphasis is on student discussion. ''Right now, 80 percent of school time is spent with teachers talking, kids listening,'' Dr. Adler said. ''If we could reverse those proportions, we could change things overnight.''

The only elective allowed Paideia students is a choice of foreign language. Otherwise all must study language, literature, the fine arts, math, natural science, history, geography and social studies. While the actual teaching of those subjects would be traditional, relying on lectures and textbooks, the proposal diverges from current school practices in the second phase, Dr. Adler said, where coaching to develop intellectual skills is stressed. Students write, read, listen, calculate and ''exercise critical judgment.''

Thirdly, the book says, education should enlarge understanding of ideas and values through discussion of books and other works of art and involvement in artistic activities. The key to this is the Socratic method.

All four school systems that adopted the Paideia plan are temporarily using modified versions. In these initial programs, only a few hundred students out of thousands will participate, and all the systems have maintained their vocational training programs intact.

In Oakland, the system was piloted three years ago at Skyline High School, with 100 gifted seniors. Now 500 elementary and high school students, ranging in ability from average to gifted, take a two- hour block program in which American Government, English and economics are combined with philosophy and humanities. ''We haven't gotten the whole school turned around,'' said Skyline's principal, Nicholas Caputi, a member of the Paideia Group, ''but I hope we will have a full program someday.''

''EVEN lower-end students attend school with motivation now,'' he said. ''They see that they can think.'' Atlanta's year-old program, spread over several high schools, is a success with the system's primarily low-income students, according to Superintendent Alonzo Crim, also a member of the Paideia Group. ''When you talk at folks, they don't pay attention,'' he said. ''When you're talking with them, they listen.''

In San Diego, a Paideia plan is set for about 100 students, beginning next fall. ''We do well in teaching the academics,'' said Assistant Superintendent Gary J. Hoban. ''We've made strides in the coaching phase. It's the third category of Socratic teaching that we haven't done at all.''

He said that Dr. Adler's plan was opposed by many vocational-education teachers, but he added that he believed the system's vocational students would benefit from the classes. ''I choose to interpret Adler loosely on the subject of vocational education,'' Dr. Hoban says. ''You need good, strong career education. But you don't want young people to make a vocational choice in ninth grade, then be trained for a job that doesn't exist when he graduates. That's happening much too often.''

Chicago has perhaps the most ambitious teacher-training program planned. In the fall, two elementary and two high schools will begin programs, and Superintendent of Schools Ruth B. Love, who is a member of the Paideia group, is seeking foundation grants and desegregation funds to send 45 teachers to training programs at St. Johns College in Sante Fe, N.M.

''We hope to increase achievement on Iowa Test scores by raising expectations,'' said Lynn Stinnette, coordinator of the plan in Chicago, ''to raise teacher morale, improve student attitudes toward books, improve the quality of teacher-student interaction, and improve reasoning and inferential reading skills - especially the latter, where we will look for the most growth.''

One of Dr. Adler's toughest critics is Ronald E. Gwiazda, assistant headmaster of the Madison Park High School in Boston, who wrote in The Harvard Educational Review that the Paideia proposal would produce exactly opposite effect it sought. In an interview, Mr. Gwiazda said, ''A one-track system would, as it did in the past, push the lower achievers out into the labor market.'' He said also that Dr. Adler's plan to increase standards ''without improving the means for achieving them'' would produce a higher failure rate.''

''You have to find new ways to educate students who learn at different rates,'' he said, ''by moving toward something more individualized, as health-care systems do.''

Dr. Adler said that his plan was not to make society more democratic - ''it will do that itself,'' he said - but to make education catch up. And he acknowledged, ''There will be some whose lives won't be better. The plan isn't going to make them all morally virtuous, but it will give them a chance to lead a good life. Then, if they don't, that's their fault.''
