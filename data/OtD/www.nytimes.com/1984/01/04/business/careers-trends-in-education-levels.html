    Careers; Trends in Education Levels

    Elizabeth M. Fowler
    Published: January 4, 1984

    SOMETIMES, almost as an afterthought, a wide-ranging survey unexpectedly turns up trends that are hard to explain.

    For example, the Association of American Colleges, in a recent survey entitled ''Adult Students and the Humanities - An Analysis of New Data,'' found evidence that the educational gap between the most and least highly trained Americans seemed to be widening and that adults in the West were better educated, on average, than those in other regions.

    The association, which has headquarters in Washington, has an abiding interest in the humanities and wants to persuade Americans to take more courses in literature, languages, history, philosophy and other such subjects. So does Dr. Kathryn Mohrman, the author of the survey and the association's director of programs and national affairs. She is also serving this school year as associate dean of the college at Brown University.

    Dr. Mohrman is intrigued by the two byproduct findings that, she says, defy analysis and yet have sociological implications.

    In the words of the study, there is ''a widening gap between most highly trained persons in American society and the least well-educated individuals.'' The problem is that such a gap usually reflects a gap in income or perquisites received - a gap, in other words, between the haves and the have nots. Dr. Mohrman noticed the education gap in a previous survey in 1978, but, she said last week, she was startled by the extent of the change since then.

    The association offers no quick-fix solutions. It indicates the gap will continue to widen, asserting: ''The more education a person has, the more likely he or she is to seek further education or training.'' The least well educated consist of minority groups, as well as many women.

    Can the gap be closed? While the association offers no solution, one answer can be found in some businesses.

    For a person who cannot afford to go beyond high school or two years at a community college, the best bet might be to join a company that provides generous educational opportunities. These include major banks, the International Business Machines Corporation, Polaroid and Kimberly- Clark among others. When companies pay tuition, many expect the courses to be job-related, but a few, such as Kimberly-Clark, permit an employee to study almost anything.

    The second finding of the survey, that Westerners are better educated than other Americans, also raises the question of why. ''There has been strong emphasis on education in the West,'' Dr. Mohrman said, ''but I was surprised at the extent of the difference.''

    She has no sure answer, but she does observe that colleges in California have generally lower tuition rates than elsewhere. Even excluding Californians, however, the survey found people in other areas of the West to be better educated than those elsewhere.

    Along more mundane lines, the survey found ''13 percent of all American adults'' took some form of part-time educational training in the year covered, 1981. The largest proportion, 37.2 percent, was in the 25-to-34 age group, while 22.8 percent were in the 35-to-44 range.

    Financially, many were well off - 33 percent in the $25,000-to-$49,999 family income range and 8 percent in the $50,000-and-up category. And almost half, surprisingly, paid for the courses themselves, compared with the 22 percent financed by businesses.

    Noting that a large percentage of adult students ''were highly motivated by job considerations,'' the association said that business courses were taken by 20 percent of the adult students, the largest category, followed by 12 percent each in both the engineering and health fields. The humanities attracted only 7.7 percent.

    As to how people can be motivated to take more humanities courses, the association suggests that educational institutions could extol the benefits of the humanities and somehow broaden the definition of job-related education - something that few have done.

    In favor of more ''selling of the humanities,'' Dr. Mohrman argues, ''Humanities have the potential of earning a high rate of return over a long period of time.'' She contrasted that to the computer science major who probably has to take more courses in five years or so to keep up with technological change.

    In the case of foreign languages, it can be argued convincingly that they help jobholders. Taking literature courses could be more widely advertised as helping a person write and think effectively. History, especially in economic and political aspects, can also be job-related.
