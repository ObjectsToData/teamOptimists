MAN IN THE NEWS

MAN IN THE NEWS; CONCILIATOR FOR SCHOOLS: NATHAN QUINONES

By GENE I. MAEROFF
Published: March 23, 1984

When New York City's school system was forming its new high school for the humanities, which opened last fall, it seemed that the advisory committee of teachers, community activists and parents was so diverse that it would be impossible to agree on a plan for the school's operation. But Nathan Quinones, then executive director of the Board of Education's high school division, kept seeking areas of agreement and gradually helped the committee build an agenda around which the members could coalesce.

It is this ability to bring people together that his colleagues say will serve Mr. Quinones (pronounced Key-NYO-nas) well as he seeks to impart a calming influence to the school system while Chancellor Anthony J. Alvarado is on leave.

Mr. Quinones, the new deputy chancellor, is so reserved and unflappable that Robert Mastruzzi, who has known him for almost 30 years, said, ''I can count on one hand the number of times I have seen him excited.''

Began as a Teacher

Mr. Mastruzzi, the principal of John F. Kennedy High School in the Bronx, and Mr. Quinones spent many of their early years in teaching together at DeWitt Clinton High School, also in the Bronx.

The words used most frequently to describe the 53-year-old Mr. Quinones are ''gentle and humane.'' In this quiet, reserved manner, many former students say, Mr. Quinones reached into their lives in ways that have spurred them to keep in touch over the years.

''He respected and challenged his students, and he was my inspiration,'' said Gary Milgrom, a former student who is now a Spanish teacher at John F. Kennedy High School in the Bronx.

Mr. Milgrom and several other graduates of Mr. Quinones's honors Spanish class at DeWitt Clinton have gathered annually for each of the last 22 years for a small reunion with their former teacher. Questions of Leadership

While many educators believe that Mr. Quinones can restore confidence in the school system's top administration after the questions surrounding Mr. Alvarado, they are not altogether sure that Mr. Quinones is enough of a leader.

''I think of him more as a philosopher, and I hope as Chancellor he is more decisive than he was in leading the high schools,'' said Grace Rosa, principal of Christopher Columbus High School in the Bronx. ''He has had some wonderful ideas, but I have not necessarily seen them implemented.''

It is difficult for the school system's 55,000 teachers and administrators to know exactly which policies have been Mr. Quinones's, since he did not have a totally free hand as head of the high schools. He reported to Mr. Alvarado for the last year, and to Mr. Alvarado's predecessor, Frank J. Macchiarola, from 1978 to 1983.

In the last year, for instance, there was interest in creating more ''magnet'' programs, schools with special themes that allow students to bypass their neighborhood high schools. This approach was espoused by Mr. Alvarado, and while Mr. Quinones did not outwardly dissent, he was known to disagree with some of the Chancellor's plans. Rise in Minority Youngsters

Mr. Quinones gave no specific indication yesterday of the directions he might take the school system, but he said his personal background gave him special insight in dealing with some of the most pressing needs.

''My own background coincides with that of the students to the degree that we have within the school system a growing number of minority youngsters who are poor,'' he said in an interview during a break from a round of meetings with top aides.

''I also can appreciate the need that many of them have to acquire proficiency in English, which was not the first language in my home,'' he said.

Nathan Quinones was born in East Harlem on Oct. 12, 1930, the son of Natalio and Justina Quinones, who were both born in Puerto Rico.

His parents struggled financially, according to Mr. Quinones, and the family was on public assistance in the 1940's, before he graduated from the High School of Commerce. While attending City College, where he majored in classical and romance languages, he worked six nights a week at a hospital. He was graduated in 1953. Rise in School System

After military service in Korea, Mr. Quinones got a master's degree from Columbia University.

He joined the school system as a foreign-language teacher in 1957 and was assistant principal in charge of foreign languages at Benjamin N. Cardozo High School in Queens when he was named principal of South Bronx High School in 1977.

One of Mr. Macchiarola's first acts upon becoming Chancellor in 1978 was to select Mr. Quinones to head the high school division, appointing him ahead of many more experienced principals.

Mr. Quinones works long hours, rising by 5:30 A.M. at his home in Little Neck, Queens. Some friends wondered whether he was working too hard in 1978, when he had to be hospitalized for two weeks. 'Clean Bill of Health'

''I was having exceedingly severe headaches, but I got a clean bill of health after extensive tests,'' said Mr. Quinones.

One of his main activities outside the school system has been as a board member and officer of Aspira of New York, a Puerto Rican communal organization.

He and his wife, Romana, have three daughters - Daria, 24; Adria, 21, and Cyra, 16.

Mr. Quinones is man of strong loyalties, and the first person he telephoned yesterday morning as deputy chancellor was no one of high rank or political power. It was Irma Silverberg, a retired teacher in Brooklyn, who helped him learn to teach when he began with a minimum of educational credits and was uncertain about whether to make a career in the schools.

''She made all the difference for me,'' he said.
