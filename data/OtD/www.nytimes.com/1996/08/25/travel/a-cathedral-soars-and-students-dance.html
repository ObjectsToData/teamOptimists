A Cathedral Soars and Students Dance

By ERIC SJOGREN
Published: August 25, 1996

I ARRIVED in Uppsala, 25 miles northwest of Stockholm, courtesy of an Iraqi refugee now teaching advanced physics at the university in that town, after hundreds of suspicious Saab and Volvo drivers had whooshed past me as I waited on the roadside for a bus.

Foreigners have not exactly taken over Uppsala University, which was founded in 1477, but the growing internationalization of science and the humanities at Uppsala means that Swedish is the teaching language for just the first two or three terms. About a tenth of the 25,000 students are non-Swedes, some 800 of them Americans.

I was headed to Uppsala last fall, to visit my nephew for a few days. My newly found Iraqi friend dropped me off on the west bank of the Fyris River, which sharply divides Uppsala between gown and town. Virtually all academic institutions are concentrated on the west bank, with the east bank dominated by the city's commercial and industrial areas.

The vast red Uppsala Castle dominates the town from its position on the ridge that runs parallel to the river's west bank. Dag Hammarskjold, who was to become a poet and United Nations Secretary General, grew up here as the son of the provincial governor. Three hundred years earlier, Queen Christina announced her abdication and conversion to Catholicism in the castle's beautifully proportioned Hall of State.

The center of Uppsala retains a small-town look, with the student population contributing to a young and dynamic street scene. Scattered around the west bank are their ''nations,'' one for each Swedish province that sends students to the ancient university. In days gone by, these houses were living quarters for students; now they are the centers of student social life, highlighted by Walpurgis -- the May Ball -- on April 30, when young couples dance far beyond daybreak.

However, Uppsala is more than a university town. With 175,000 inhabitants, it is Sweden's fastest growing city and home to Pharmacia, one of Europe's largest pharmaceutical companies, which merged with the United States company Upjohn last November.

Uppsala's skyline is dominated by its castle and its cathedral, with the Carolina Rediviva library in the center. Just a few hundred yards separate the three buildings.

Uppsala Castle was built by Gustav Vasa (Gustavus I), Sweden's 16th-century counterpart to Henry VIII, who needed strategic strongholds to maintain the peace in the country that he had recently united and to demonstrate the supremacy of crown over church. To this day, one of the old castle guns remains trained on the archbishop's residence.

His son, Erik XIV, feeling threatened by conspiracies, instigated the assassination of several Swedish nobles in the castle dungeons. A recently restored wing of the castle houses the new Uppsala Art Museum, headed by an American, Deborah Thompson, who came to Sweden as an exchange student from U.C.L.A. 20 years ago. The museum's print room, she says, has more than 4,000 works from the 16th to the 20th century, a collection so large, only a small portion of it (usually about 100 prints) can be shown at any one time. Ms. Thompson is particularly proud of a recently acquired series of eight serigraphs by the American super-realist Richard Estes.

Carolina Rediviva, the 180-year-old university library, is next to the castle. The ocher, classically proportioned building, which contains four million books, occupies a commanding position at the top of Drott ninggatan, the main shopping street. Students gather here by the thousands on Walpurgis Night to don the white caps (Uppsala's equivalent of mortarboards). When the university's vice chancellor gives the sign, the students don the caps simultaneously, producing a sudden wave of white.

The library gained notoriety last year when its prized possession, the Codex argenteus (Silver Bible) was stolen in broad daylight by youths who sprayed tear gas and attacked the display case with hammers. The Bible, dating to the sixth century, had been brought here by the Swedes who stormed Prague in 1648, the final year of the Thirty Years' War.

The modern-day thieves soon found out that their spoils were greatly diminished. All they got of the original book was a double page (the rest of the display being a dummy) and the heavy silver covers. A month later, these were recovered. The silver cover (a 17th-century addition) had a small dent; the parchment page, made from the skin of an unborn calf in 520, resumed its original shape even though it had been folded, according to Thomas Tottie, the head librarian.

The Codex argenteus is on display again, but this time in a more secure case. A hand-painted map of Mexico and the first map of Scandinavia, both from the 16th century, and Mozart's original draft for ''The Magic Flute'' are also on display.

Uppsala Cathedral is the largest church in Scandinavia, and its twin 360-foot spires soar above the city. I was shown around the limestone and brick cathedral by a young priest named Agneta Stojkovic (the Church of Sweden was among the pioneers in ordaining women). In the summer, she told me, the cathedral has more than 4,000 visitors a day. Its most important treasure -- and the reason for its construction, which began in 1260 and lasted 175 years -- is the reliquary of St. Erik, Sweden's first Christian king, who was murdered in 1160. When it was opened some years ago, the reliquary was found to contain a number of bones, including a vertebra split by an ax or sword, and a poor king's crown of gilded copper, set with gems that are in fact bits of colored glass. The reliquary was then resealed.
