At Rutgers, Liberal Arts For Adults

By ABBY GOODNOUGH
Published: September 8, 1996

Rutgers University has long provided continuing-education programs for adults who want to return to school part time, but most courses are strictly practical. Corporate types can sharpen their computer skills, for example, and farmers can bone up on mulching techniques.

But this fall, for the first time, Rutgers will offer a continuing-education program in the humanities on its New Brunswick campuses, hoping to attract adult students with money and time to dabble in more whimsical courses. The classes, which start tomorrow, will be taught by members of the faculty of arts and sciences, most of whom teach at Rutgers College. The courses will range from 3 days to 12 weeks, and prices will range from $100 to $450. William Roney, a member of the committee that designed the program, says students will not receive credit, and homework will be minimal.

Mr. Roney says the program is modeled after the popular, expansive continuing-education programs at New York University and the New School for Social Research. ''Our target student is a humanities-oriented, somewhat upscale adult,'' he said. ''And we're not trying to create a lecture atmosphere with this. These classes are purely for self-enrichment and enjoyment.''

To plan the program, the committee sent a letter to every division of the faculty of arts and sciences, asking for a ''fantasy course description,'' Mr. Roney said.

What resulted was a course catalogue that includes ''Introduction to Yiddish Language and Culture,'' ''Mystery-Writing Workshop'' and ''Beer: Tasting the Brewer's Art.'' The fall program will offer 33 courses, including seven in English as a second language.

Faculty members signed up for the program voluntarily. ''This is not a way for them to make a living,'' said Kay Lynch, director of the English-as-a-second-language program at Rutgers and a member of the continuing-education committee. ''It's an outlet for a love they have.''

Although Rutgers officials hope the program will eventually grow to be income-producing, for now it is in the start-up phase, with fees covering advertising and instructors' salaries. To stir interest in the program, Rutgers has mailed about 10,000 catalogues to residents of Middlesex County and the surrounding area.

So far, Mr. Roney said, the most popular courses have been Conversational Chinese, Becoming an Exercise Instructor and courses on the Internet and beer making. The classes should have about 10 students each, Mr. Roney said, and if they do not attract enough people, they will be canceled.

Although continuing-education courses at other New Jersey institutions tend to be vocational, the Rutgers program will join several others that focus on the liberal arts. Princeton University allows adults to audit regular classes, but they must apply and pay $2,750 per course, and they must have attended college for at least two years. Drew University sponsors a program jointly with the Madison Public Library, offering courses that meet once a week for five weeks for $75. And Caldwell College has a ''Lifelong Learning Institute'' for people older than 49, offering about 20 short-term courses each semester and charging between $10 and $20 per course.

At Rutgers, Mr. Roney and other committee members are already planning for the spring semester. They want to add bird-watching and Dutch to the catalogue, and they are soliciting other ideas from professors and students.

People who want to register or receive more information about the program can call (908) 445-7423 or send electronic mail to fasce@rci.rutgers.edu.

Poetry and Caffeine

From the Rutgers catalogue:

CINEMA: MURDER IS THE MESSAGE (five 3-hour classes, $210). The classic themes, cultures and subgenres of the mystery in cinema. Students will view such films as ''Murder on the Orient Express,'' and ''The Big Sleep.''

RAGAMALA: MOODS OF NORTH INDIAN CLASSICAL MUSIC (ten 80-minute classes, $189). The convention and evolution of Indian classical music. Students will be introduced to the sitar, the behala and the tabla.

THE CULTURE OF CAFFEINE (three 2-hour classes, $114). From 18th-century London to the modern coffee bar. Readings will include ''Wuthering Heights,'' ''Alice in Wonderland,'' and ''On the Road.''

POETRY WRITING WORKSHOP (six 3-hour classes, $342). Aspiring poets work with successful poets, write poetry, share their work and receive feedback from peers and experts.
