THEATER;Marketing Broadway as a Cool Spot

By SCOTT BALDINGER
Published: April 14, 1996

MTV PLUGS. WEB SITES that print out theater tickets. Fliers posted in laundromats. Productions at 10 A.M. These are a few of the ideas, high tech and low, of theater producers and marketers determined to bring young and minority audiences into the ever-graying, implacably white precincts of Broadway.

They definitely have their work cut out for them.

According to a recent study by the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities, "younger Americans are unlikely ever to attend live performances of musical and dramatic theater." Their lack of interest, the report says, represents a "massive shift in taste and tradition" that bodes ill for many of the institutions that have made New York the cultural capital of the world. And no more so than for the unsubsidized, increasingly expensive Broadway.

Tapping into young as well as minority audiences is not only about being hip or politically correct today; it is about surviving. And that won't be easy. While mass marketing can affect how Americans vote, eat, dress and are entertained, play-going has been increasingly ghettoized, despite record Broadway grosses, with their strong tourist component.

Until recently, in fact, marketing Broadway has meant preaching to the converted: primarily Jewish and/or gay middle- and upper-class whites, bitten by the theater bug and reachable through the few magazines and newspapers that still cover the theater.

But a major augury of Broadway's future is about to take place: two acclaimed musical transfers from the nonprofit sector are opening later this month. If the rock opera "Rent" (whose author, Jonathan Larson, was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for drama posthumously last week for the show) and "Bring in da Noise, Bring in da Funk," subtitled "A Tap/Rap Discourse on the Staying Power of the Beat," fail to break the age and race barriers, it will be difficult to imagine what will.

Another portent involves two ambitious straight plays: "Seven Guitars" and "Buried Child." Will young and minority audiences forgo film and television for more expensive live drama? August Wilson's "Seven Guitars," which recently opened to generally excellent reviews and praise for its acting ensemble, is the sole new Broadway drama with a black theme, and its ticket sales will be watched as another indicator of Broadway's vigor. The "hot" credentials of "Buried Child" include the director, Gary Sinise (known for his roles in the movies "Forrest Gump" and "Apollo 13"), and the author, the iconoclastic Sam Shepard, making his Broadway debut.

In the past, marketing any of these productions might have meant striking the right tone in an advertising campaign or hiring a star. But what the producers of "Rent," "Bring in da Noise" and "Buried Child" are aiming for is something else: to make them "cool," that ineffable quality attained recently by grunge or moshing or the actress Jennifer Aniston's hair styles but hardly ever by a Broadway show. (Like Mr. Wilson's other works, "Seven Guitars," with its bluesy leitmotive, has a "cool" quality already familiar to its target audiences.)

None of this would be necessary if, as Cora Cahan, president of the New 42d Street redevelopment group, said, America had not "lost some generations in terms of instilling a passion for live theater" -- the kind adults get from having been exposed to it as children.

Ms. Cahan said that one result of this gap is that she and her nonprofit group, created by the state to oversee the renovation of 42d Street theaters, committed their first completed project, the restored New Victory Theater on West 42d Street, to productions for children. In one ornate swoop, the New Victory may erase from memory what Ms. Cahan called "the gestalt of poverty" often associated with children's theater.

As cutbacks in school arts programs and the National Endowment for the Arts inevitably lead to fewer young people being introduced to theater, Broadway will need all the initiatives it can devise to attract the crowds of the future.

It might learn, for example, from the work being done at the New Victory. It is 10 A.M., an ungodly hour to see a play. But the junior high school students from Brooklyn who are jamming in to see a Theater for a New Audience production don't seem to mind. They point and gaze, and talk excitedly. "I want to sit on the stage," one of them exclaims.

Just before the show begins, there is that moment when the lights go down that makes even the most jaded theatergoer tingle. This morning, as the gilded New Victory's lights dim, there is nothing less than a chorus of screams. Then, transfixed silence.

'Rent' From a Boutique To the Front Row

You wouldn't think that "Rent," which opens on April 29, needs much selling. It has received some of the best reviews of the decade and attained close to $2 million in advance sales within the first month that tickets were available. Soon, studiously with-it magazines will be on sale with multipage layouts about the show, photographed by Steven Meisel (Vogue), Marc Hom (Rolling Stone) and Mary Ellen Mark (Harper's Bazaar). Bloomingdale's has announced plans to open a boutique called For Rent, filled with clothes inspired by the show.
