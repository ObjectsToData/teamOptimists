With New Budget, Domestic Spending Is Cut $24 Million

By ROBERT PEAR
Published: April 27, 1996

The budget bill signed today by President Clinton, combined with other actions taken by Congress in the last year, will cut domestic spending by $24 billion, and most of the savings will come from housing, labor, education and Commerce Department programs.

Democrats rescued a few of President Clinton's favorites, like the national service program known as Americorps. But Republicans squeezed spending across the board in a wide range of domestic programs, and many of the cuts were substantial.

For example, Representative Sidney R. Yates, an Illinois Democrat who has served in the House for four decades, was exaggerating only a bit when he said that the 1996 appropriations bill "sounds the death knell for for Endowments for the Arts and the Humanities." The budget of the National Endowment for the Arts will be cut 39 percent, to $99.5 million from $162.3 million last year. At its peak, in 1992, the endowment received $175.9 million.

Jane Alexander, chairwoman of the arts endowment, said she was pleased that her agency had survived at all.

"The cuts are substantial," Ms. Alexander said in an interview tonight. "But the agency, which was once listed among the Republicans' top 10 targets for elimination, is alive." She said she believed the drive to eliminate the agency had lost momentum. "We are on the way up," Ms. Alexander said. "It's important for artists and arts organizations not to despair."

Still, Cherie L. Simon, a spokeswoman for the agency, said that museums and dance, music, theater and arts education programs would feel the effects of the cuts.

Money for the National Endowment for the Humanities, which supports a variety of scholarly and cultural programs, will be cut 36 percent, to $110 million from $172 million last year.

In the vocabulary of Congress, outlays subject to annual appropriations are called discretionary spending. The Congressional Budget Office said today that domestic discretionary spending this year would be reduced 10 percent to $219.7 billion, from $243.7 billion last year. Most of the savings -- $16.2 billion of the $24 billion -- result from Republican decisions to cancel authority to spend money that had been approved for the 1995 fiscal year. The remainder results from cuts enacted this year as part of 1996 appropriations.

Military spending will rise by $1.9 billion, to $264.3 billion this year, the budget office said. Together, the two categories of discretionary spending account for less than one-third of the Federal budget, which totals $1.6 trillion this year.

The agreement, which Congressional Republicans and the White House concluded this week with immense difficulty, makes a modest contribution to the bipartisan goal of balancing the Federal budget in seven years. It does not affect interest payments on the Federal debt. Nor does it affect Medicare, Medicaid, welfare and other benefit programs whose financing is set by permanent laws, rather than by annual appropriations. These benefit programs together account for slightly more than half of all Federal spending.

Among the agencies sustaining budget cuts are the Education Department, which gets $25.2 billion this year, down from $26.8 billion last year; the Labor Department, which gets $8 billion, down from $8.4 billion, and the Department of Housing and Urban Development, which gets $19.1 billion, down from $24.7 billion. The Commerce Department, which some Republicans had sought to abolish, receives $3.7 billion, down from $4 billion last year.

Legal Services Congress cut the budget of the Legal Services Corporation by 30.5 percent, to $278 million from $400 million, and imposed restrictions on the cases that may be handled by lawyers receiving Federal money.

Alan W. Houseman, director of the Center for Law and Social Policy, a public-interest law firm that receives no money from the Government, said: "Thousands of low-income people with legal problems will no longer be able to receive legal assistance. Local legal-aid offices are being closed. Lawyers and paralegals are being laid off."

The Federal program distributes money to 323 legal service organizations with a total of 1,200 offices. Under the new law, the Legal Services Corporation may not give money to any person or entity that "initiates or participates in a class-action suit." Such lawsuits have been used for years to enforce laws and protect the rights of people seeking Medicaid, welfare, housing, Social Security and disability benefits.

The law also says that lawyers getting money from the agency may not represent prisoners or inmates; may not lobby and may not engage in litigation to "reform a Federal or state welfare system."

International Aid The new Federal budget will alleviate the financial crisis that has been gripping the United Nations and many other international organizations that depend heavily on American contributions.

The budget provides $892 million for all international groups including the United Nations, up from $873 million last year. The 1996 figure is close to the $923 million that President Clinton originally requested and much more than the $700 million that would have been provided under a bill that he vetoed last fall.
