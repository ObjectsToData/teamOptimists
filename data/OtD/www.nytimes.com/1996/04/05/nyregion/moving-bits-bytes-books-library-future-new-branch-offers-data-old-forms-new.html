Moving Bits, Bytes and Books To the Library of the Future;A New Branch Offers Data in Old Forms and New

By BRUCE WEBER
Published: April 5, 1996

It was, perhaps, the ultimate illustration of style over substance. Just beyond the glitter of the Seventh on Sixth fashion shows in Bryant Park last week, a more mundane operation was trudging ahead, but one likely to affect New York for far longer.

As designers and their fast-aging models displayed the pricey wares that will be out of date by next fall, movers were trucking 1.2 million books dealing with business, economics, chemistry, physics and mathematics, plus microfilm and microfiche -- 31 miles of material, if you stacked it up side by side -- from the central research edifice of the New York Public Library at Fifth Avenue and 42d Street to their new home, the Science, Industry and Business Library, at Madison Avenue and 34th Street. There, ostensibly, it will never be beyond the reach of everyday citizens, nor will it go out of style.

This is a supreme moment for us," said Paul LeClerc, the New York Public Library president. Not only, he said, is the new science library a prototype for 21st century research, but also with the space created by the reshuffled collection, "we can now bring back the collections we have stored in the library annex on West 43d Street and create the single most important collection of publicly accessible humanities and social science documents under one roof on the continent, if not anywhere."

The 42d Street library is to be renovated over the next six years. But in less than a month, on May 2, the new Science, Industry and Business Library (known by the acronym SIBL and intended primarily for small businesses) will open on two floors of the old B. Altman department store. New York will then be home to a user-friendly, interactive research institution that may well be unmatched in the access to information it gives to the public.

Among its features are seating for 500, with each seat wired for laptop computer hookups; 100 outfitted work stations, with access to electronic databases and on-line services, and a training center at which library users will be instructed in the rudiments of electronic research.

"It's not just our collections we're moving," said John V. Ganly, the assistant director of SIBL. "When you move in all this technology, you're moving in other libraries as well. You're moving in the world."

Even in its current state of untidy incompleteness, the new library, designed by Gwathmey Siegel & Associates, seems to have little in common with the old one. The familiar feel of wood and leather and brass lamps gives way to the spanking new efficiency of the cyber-age.

But for all the technology extant, one cannot beam the books from one location to another. Since Jan. 29, workers have been yanking the stuff off the shelves by hand, putting it on wheeled carts, ferrying it half a mile through midtown traffic and then unloading it, volume by volume, onto new shelves.

"There is so much hype about electronic resources that if you allow yourself not to think about the future carefully, it's easy to believe it will be one without books," said William D. Walker, who is Andrew D. Mellon director of New York Public's five research libraries. "We're making an enormous investment in new space to hold future print collections. The book is an invention that will be difficult to bump by any technology."

Five tiers of stacks have been built in the new building's core, withpublic areas surrounding them. Books will be visible through the windows, as will a counter reminiscent of a circulation desk.

But from inside, even the smell of the place, like the newest of new cars, emanates "future." The electronic signage, the computer screens cropping up on desk tops everywhere like crocuses in a techno-garden, and the electrically operated movable stacks (not open to the public, though the first floor of SIBL will include a circulating library of 40,000 volumes taken from the mid-Manhattan branch at Fifth Avenue and 41st Street), all make a visitor accustomed to musty stacks, long oaken tables and Victorian reading lamps feel as though he is on the starship Enterprise. SIBL is a prototype, Mr. Walker said, "because it combines comprehensive electronic resources with our magnificent collection of print resources."

It is that combination -- electronic information and hard copy (also known as original documents), the past and the future -- that library officials emphasize. In the library of the 21st century, there is still a prominent place for the book.

With pride, library officials list the CD-ROM and on-line services that work-station users will be able to tap into: the addresses and sales information for 10 million American companies; complete census data, and patent descriptions from the last two decades, to name three of dozens. But there is also the Parsons collection of rare books and manuscripts on transportation and engineering.

In the Parsons collection, one finds not just texts dealing with the construction of canals and railroads and the lives of engineers, but captivating oddities, like the 50-volume Walter Weichsel transfer collection, a series of scrapbooks put together by an obscure but compulsive traveler, consisting of public transportation tickets from around the world and photographs of where they took him.
