Surgical Supplies Discarded in U.S. Help Save Lives Overseas

By JAMES BROOKE
Published: January 13, 1996

Fresh from an operating room at the Rose Medical Center here, a 55-gallon trash bag contained a valuable trove of never-used surgical supplies: sutures, Ace bandages, surgical marking pens, drape sheets and rubber gloves.

Fearing liability suits, most American hospitals would send this load straight to the incinerator. But, as part of fast-growing medical recycling movement, these supplies are destined for the bare shelves of a hospital in the former Soviet Union.

In the third world, people are dying daily because they don't have the medical supplies we throw away," said Eric Swenson, founder of H. Victory, a Denver group that started collecting unused medical supplies in seven local hospitals last year.

Mountains of never-used medical supplies, many still sealed in factory wrappers, were created by the confluence of two recent trends: a shift to disposable materials in hospitals and a fear of lawsuits from accidental infection or material failure from reuse.

At the Yale University School of Medicine, researchers calculate that American hospitals throw away 2,000 tons, or $200 million worth, of unused surgical supplies every year. With 14 million operations performed in the United States every year, the amount of recoverable material averages $14 for each operation.

"By comparison, American charities donate $400 million a year in money and supplies to hospitals overseas," said William H. Rosenblatt, an assistant professor of anesthesiology at Yale. "Just through recovery, we could increase private American medical aid by 50 percent."

Since 1991, when Yale-New Haven Hospital started a medical recycling program, it has salvaged $1.5 million in supplies. Funneling most materials to a teaching hospital in Leon, Nicaragua, Yale's "waste" now supplies 40 percent of the Leon hospital's needs for disposable goods -- dressings, sponges, syringes, surgical blades, casting plaster and tongue depressors.

Trying to attend the needs of 350,000 people in one of Latin America's poorest countries, the Leon hospital, Oscar Danilo Rosales Arguello, operates on a Health Ministry budget that amounts to $3 per bed, per day.

"We use almost everything," Norma Sarria, the hospital's supply coordinator, said when asked by telephone about the recycled material from Yale. "The ministry budget for us is nothing, nada."

About 60 United States hospitals now follow the system developed by Dr. Rosenblatt's nonprofit group, Remedy Inc., or Recovered Medical Equipment for the Developing World.

St. Anne's Hospital of Raceland, La., collects materials for shipment to Grenada. St. Vincent's Hospital of Indianopolis ships unused medical supplies to Vietnam. University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics has shipped supplies to Brazil, Guatemala and Russia. Mercy Health Systems of Farmington Hills, Mich., ships supplies to Lithuania.

From Afghanistan to Zimbabwe, diplomats and charities from 45 underdeveloped nations have solicited supplies from Remedy's office in New Haven. To handle the growing interest, the office hired its first full-time staff worker in November.

In Denver, Mr. Swenson is spreading his recycling gospel throughout Colorado and to Wyoming, catching the eye of the nation's largest private hospital chain, the Columbia/HCA Health Care Corporation.

"We definitely want to promote this throughout the hospital network," said J. Tod Fetherling, computer communications manager for the chain, which owns 335 hospitals. "Within a year, we would like that all our hospitals be aware that this program is available."

This spring, the Albert Schweitzer Institute for the Humanities plans to promote Yale's recovery system to the 5,000 major hospitals in the United States and Canada. Based in Wallingford, Conn., this nonprofit group ships unused medical supplies to hospitals in Russia and Suriname.

Yale resterilizes all equipment before shipping it overseas. Recipients are instructed to sterilize all equipment before an operation.

"They are collecting things in such a fashion that I think there is no risk of infection," said Walter J. Hierholzer Jr., chief epidemiologist at Yale-New Haven Hospital. Describing the Remedy system as "super-safe," Dr. Hierholzer added: "Most of this equipment and supplies come from supplies that are opened, but not used. Most can be resterilized."

Many hospitals, including the ones in Denver, do not resterilize before donating the goods for shipping overseas. Disclaimer labels are often placed on boxes, saying that goods are provided "as is" without warranties and not as part of the hospital's normal provision of health care.

"In Riga, they know they have to resterilize," Mr. Swenson said, noting that next week a hospital in Riga, the Latvian capital, is to receive a 40-foot-long container of medical supplies from Denver. Last year his organization sent medical supplies to Kazakhstan, a former Soviet republic.

American hospitals largely do not want to reuse disposable equipment because most manufacturers' warranties cover only one trip into an operating room. It does not matter if the operation was canceled. It does not matter if supplies were never taken out of their wrappers.
