Dispute at Club Tied to N.Y.U. Exposes Leadership Rift

By JANNY SCOTT
Published: May 21, 1996

It began with a flap over an invitation to lunch. The guest was to have been Hillary Rodham Clinton. Now it has blown up into a state of high-wattage Sturm und Drang within New York City's most venerable intellectual club.

The New York Institute for the Humanities, which for two decades has counted among its members the likes of Susan Sontag and the late Joseph Brodsky, is embroiled in a painful commotion over its leadership and its future.

It has summoned its director back from Paris for a meeting on Thursday in Greenwich Village. Meanwhile, a high-ranking group of members appealed last Friday to the president of New York University to intercede.

The dispute over Mrs. Clinton has exposed a deeper rift about how the institute is run and how best to preserve the delicate balance of academics and nonacademics that has been the institute's strength.

The episode is noteworthy because the institute is home to many of the better-known intellectuals in a city known for its intellectual life; now some of them have turned the intelligence with which they regularly tackle ideas to an internecine dispute.

In what form the institute will emerge remains to be seen. But some members fear that the entire mess could prove so unseemly that N.Y.U. might reconsider the terms under which it has long served as the institute's beneficent aunt.

The uproar within what has been described as a "talk tank" for big thinkers began the week before last, when Tony Judt, the current director, faxed a letter from Paris to the associate director, Jocelyn Carlson Baltzell, dismissing her for what he described as insubordination.

Specifically, Professor Judt, who also teaches at N.Y.U., said Ms. Baltzell had exceeded her rank by trying to help arrange for Mrs. Clinton to address the institute's members at one of their weekly Friday lunches. But the relationship between Professor Judt and Ms. Baltzell, who had run the institute's office for 10 years, had already deteriorated badly over a plan by Professor Judt to change the way the institute is run. Now Ms. Baltzell's dismissal and Professor's Judt's plan to consolidate the institute's administration with another institute he heads have divided some of the institute's most active members.

As some see it, they have split loosely along so-called town-gown lines -- with those with ties to Professor Judt and the N.Y.U. administration on one side and many of the nonacademic members of the institute on the other side. Professor Judt and some other professors in the institute say the consolidation would be more efficient, saving the university money and perhaps even benefiting the humanities institute at a time of tightening budgets. N.Y.U. spends $100,000 a year on the institute.

But some of the journalists and authors in the institute, who insisted on anonymity, fear that the it would lose the autonomy it has enjoyed within the university and that it would become more heavily academic and less the hybrid it has always been. Since its founding in 1977, the organization has been a rare mixed breed in New York intellectual life: a force that drew together thinkers from many different worlds for brainy cross-pollination.

Last January, Professor Judt, who was on a leave of absence, told Ms. Baltzell and the acting director, Anne Hollander, of his idea of merging the administration of the two institutes at N.Y.U. that he heads. Ms. Hollander said the idea made sense to her. But when she called a meeting of the executive committee to discuss it in April, she discovered "it was a bombshell of a kind that I was not prepared for," she said.

Members of the committee were upset not only because they had not been consulted in advance but also because some feared that the institute would become less autonomous, more like an academic department under the new plan.

Then came Ms. Baltzell's dismissal on May 9. With both Ms. Hollander and Professor Judt out of the country, the executive committee met again on May 13 and decided to appeal to Jay Oliva, the president of N.Y.U. The letter, signed by 11 members, including several faculty members, expresses loyalty to N.Y.U. but also to the goal of balancing the interests of both the university and the wider community of scholars, artists and intellectuals.

Meanwhile, Ms. Hollander has scheduled what she called "a meeting of clarification, enlightenment, expression of feelings of every kind" between Professor Judt, the committee and the N.Y.U. dean in whose bailiwick the institute would fall under the new plan.

Professor Judt said yesterday that he hoped people would "air their worries" and that he would be able to answer their questions. "I see no earthly reason why the institute should suffer for what is not, after all, the first brouhaha in its history," he said. "I don't think you can put anything from a dozen to 100 intellectuals, scholars, writers, artists and teachers into a room or a club or anything and not expect brouhaha."
