METRO DIGEST


Published: May 21, 1996

NEW YORK

Computer Failure Delays Planes The computer that helps guide high-altitude air traffic in the New York region failed twice last night, delaying hundreds of flights and once again raising questions about the reliability of the air traffic control system. [Page B1.]

Impasse Broken Over Aid Zones Breaking an impasse that had held up $100 million in Federal aid for Harlem and the South Bronx, Mayor Giuliani and Governor Pataki said that they had finally agreed how to share control of the agency that will dole out the money. [B1.]

Flushing Joins the Firmament The Metropolitan Museum of Art, Carnegie Hall, the Bronx Zoo and the Brooklyn Botanic Garden have earned it, but the Museum of Modern Art, the Guggenheim and the Whitney have not. The goal is the official city title of "Cultural Institution," which brings a dollop of cachet and, more to the point, a steady stream of city money. Now, the newest cultural institution has just been crowned. It is . . . Flushing Town Hall. How did it manage to net such a plum? Lots and lots of political pressure. [B1.]

Their Own No-Hitter The cheers never last, but they continue to reverberate unusually long for Dwight Gooden since his no-hitter at Yankee Stadium. Few, however, have taken his turnaround to heart as much as the 300 people trying to restore shattered lives at the Phoenix House drug-treatment residence in Long Island City, Queens. NYC by Clyde Haberman. [B1.]

Institute Embroiled in a Rift The New York Institute for the Humanities is embroiled in a painful commotion over its leadership and its future. A dispute over an invitation to Hillary Rodham Clinton has exposed a deeper rift about how the institute is run and how best to preserve the delicate balance of academics and nonacademics that has been the institute's strength. [B2.]

At Barnard, Standoff Continues The 41-day-old strike at Barnard College has left the school's administration and the 150 striking clerical workers badly bruised, but hardly closer to a settlement. What started as a polite dispute over whether the workers would have to help pay for their health insurance premiums has evolved into a test of wills in which neither side appears willing to reach out to the other. News analysis. [B2.]

Drivers Win Sabbath Dispute The Transit Authority and the Transport Workers Union have reached an agreement to accommodate four city bus drivers who were disciplined or dismissed after they refused to work on the Sabbath. [B4.]

Patronage Charged in Nassau A former Nassau County official who helped uncover a patronage scandal in the Republican Party in Garden City in the 1970's is making new charges of patronage abuses against the current Republican county administration. The former official, Paul F. Clark, said he was pressured to donate about $4,000 to County Executive Thomas S. Gulotta's campaign committee. County officials have denied the charges. [B4.]

Hard Road to Academic Honors Camara Barrett is a high school valedictorian who also happens to live in a youth shelter and who studied for his S.A.T.'s on the subway. He is one of 264 students who overcame an array of trying circumstances to excel in school, and who have received $4,000 each in scholarship money from the United Federation of Teachers. [B5.]

Youths Admit School Bomb Plot Three youths admitted that they had plotted to set off a homemade bomb in their junior high school in a suburb of Syracuse. The teen-agers also agreed in Onondaga County Family Court to cooperate with Federal agents who are investigating how the bomb-making formula was obtained over the Internet. [B7.]

REGION

January, February, August Just over a week ago, the climate got rewound to winter. In the city, the temperature dipped to the 50's. Spring, you might have noticed, either got lost or just totally forgot to come. Then it all got fast-forwarded to August. A sizzling record 96 yesterday in Central Park, eclipsing the previous record of 91 set in 1959. It warmed up from there to reach a peak of 96, well above a normal high of 73. Records also tumbled in Newark, where it was 99, and in Bridgeport, Conn., where it was 97. [A1.]

NEW JERSEY

Bridging the Education Gap The spending gap between New Jersey's poorest and wealthiest school districts has bedeviled many governors, but Gov. Christine Todd Whitman is moving cautiously. The Whitman administration has promised to increase state spending by $235 million to allow 595 local school districts to meet newly adopted statewide curriculum standards for New Jersey's one million schoolchildren. The Governor did not say how the state would find $235 million in the 1997-98 budget. News analysis. [B6.]

Officials Predict Clean Beaches New Jersey officials said that an oil spill from a tanker that fouled beaches along more than 40 miles of the South Jersey shore would probably be cleaned up in time for Memorial Day weekend. [B6.]
