METRO DIGEST


Published: May 6, 1996

NEW YORK

Subways Return After Rupture Subways in Manhattan resumed normal service and city officials predicted only minor inconveniences in the morning rush hour as an armada of workers mopped up the soggy detritus of a water main rupture in midtown and sought to pinpoint its cause. Age was one factor being considered, particularly because the segment of 48-inch cast-iron pipe that cracked open under Broadway and West 40th Street belonged to the same 94-year-old water main that burst one block away, under West 40th Street, last July. [Page B1.]

The water main break left some businesses in Times Square with thousand-dollar losses and spared others entirely. [B3.]

Choosing the School Boards When the polls open in New York City's 32 community school districts, voters will choose from a field of candidates that is smaller than any since the local boards were created in 1969. But even before voters mark their paper ballots, the infighting among candidates has left them fewer choices. In skirmishes fought before the Board of Elections and state judges, dozens of candidates, many of them incumbents, succeeded in knocking out 90 challengers on technical questions about the validity of their ballot petitions. [B1.]

Some Advice on Education Albert Shanker knows better than most what to expect from the election of community school boards. The president of the American Federation of Teachers knows that corrupt school board members will be re-elected, that hardly anybody will vote and that those who do will be confused by the complex ballots. "But how much does that have to do with whether the kids are going to read on grade level or not?" he said. "There's some correlation, but I would say not much. Because you're still not going to educate kids, because you have no educational plan out there." Metro Matters by Joyce Purnick. [B1.]

Taxi Officials Combat Rudeness Saying that rudeness among cabdrivers has been the most difficult problem to combat, the Taxi and Limousine Commission is introducing 50 courteous sample sentences into the curriculum at classes for new drivers. Officials are hoping that "Madam (sir), is there any particular route you would like today?" will replace chestnuts like "huh?" and "What's it to you?" As part of the intensified campaign to polish cabby behavior, a video showing driver-passenger conflicts is also being created to teach ways to defuse potentially explosive situations. [B1.]

Cornell Battles Anew on Race Last spring, state officials cleared Cornell University of discrimination charges involving dormitories that focus on black, Hispanic and American Indian cultures. Now, Cornell is battling accusations that it is trying to phase out those dormitories by barring freshmen from living in them. The debate has given rise to demonstrations and rallies, and has transformed the opinion pages of the campus newspaper into a daily debate on campus race relations. [B5.] REGION

Crime Drops Nationwide Serious crimes dropped across the nation in 1995 for the fourth consecutive year, led by an 8 percent decrease in homicides, according to preliminary figures released by the Federal Bureau of Investigation. President Clinton was quick to issue a statement taking some credit for the decline, but some experts cautioned that the cause of the drop is unclear. Among cities, those with a population of more than a million showed the largest decline in overall crime, averaging 6 percent. Overall crime in New York City dropped by 14.5 percent, according to the F.B.I. figures, and the city had one of the largest declines in homicides. [A1.]

Sharing Tales of Immigration The 10 immigrants who gathered at the Lower East Side Tenement Museum spoke of fantastical expectations of New York City, they spoke of their quest for freedom, and they spoke of their disappointments. In a round-table discussion and on a tour of the Orchard Street tenement turned museum, an unlikely assortment of native and foreign-born Americans had come to share their stories of migration to New York. It was the second of a two-part conversation on immigration and ethnicity in the United States -- one of three dozen such conversations around the country paid for by the National Endowment for the Humanities. [B2.]

Airport Delays Down Sharply Departure delays at all three of New York's major airports were down sharply in the last year, thanks to the addition of more traffic controllers and changes in traffic patterns to ease bottlenecks, according to officials of the Federal Aviation Administration. But the controllers' union says recent staffing cuts threaten to reverse these gains. According to the F.A.A., the number of planes delayed for more than 15 minutes declined by 49 percent at Newark International Airport from 1994 to 1995; the decline was 24 percent at Kennedy International Airport and 10 percent at La Guardia Airport. [B5.]

NEW JERSEY

Bereaved Seek Peace of Mind Friends and family members of those buried at Rosemount Memorial Park in Newark arrived at the cemetery to try to set their minds at ease after reading newspaper reports that the cemetery's operators had agreed to pay $60,000 in penalties to the state for improperly moving memorials, burying people under concrete pathways and, without authorization, reclaiming graves that had been sold to others. At Rosemount, the peace of mind that cemeteries are supposed to offer families of the dead was nowhere to be found. The settlement announced by the state only confirmed what many had long suspected -- that their loved ones at Rosemount were not always resting in peace. [B5.]
