Buddha Puts Katonah on the Map

By ROBERTA HERSHENSON
Published: February 18, 1996

WHEN the staff of the Katonah Museum in Westchester County began planning five years ago for its current exhibition, there was a sense that the show would be special. But no one expected more than 1,000 visitors to stream through the galleries on opening day last month, nor the avalanche of interest that followed.

The exhibition, "Object as Insight: Japanese Buddhist Art and Ritual," has brought a touch of the exotic to Katonah, which is more accustomed to estate sales than sutras, bodhisattvas and fire rituals.

This show is probably the first of its kind in this country," said the Rev. Sohaku Flagg, a teacher of Zen Buddhism in Manhattan. "How some of these objects wound up in a teeny-weeny museum out of New York City is incredibly wonderful but mysterious."

The show features centuries-old ceremonial objects, scrolls, sacred sculptures, monks' robes and other items -- some of which had never before left Japan -- in the context of a simulated temple. Their presence is due less to mysticism than to hard work, imagination and sufficient financing to allow the guest curators to cull items from many countries.

Anne Nishimura Morse, an associate curator at the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston, and her husband, Samuel Crowell Morse, a professor of fine arts at Amherst College, traveled to England, Zurich, Berlin and Toronto as well as to Japan, often obtaining items from private collections -- which circumvented governmental red tape in Japan -- and turning up some astonishing finds. One is a 74-foot rosary from the British Museum that had never been displayed before.

The National Endowment of the Humanities granted the museum $250,000 for the show. More support from the National Endowment for the Arts, the Westchester Arts Council and others brought the total budget to $300,000 an "unusually high" sum, said George G. King, director of the Katonah Museum.

Visitors one recent weekday clustered around Nancy Wallach, a director of the exhibition, as she spoke about Buddhism's history and religious practices in the "outer sanctum" of the temple room. The "inner sanctum," enclosed in plexiglass, displayed an 800-year-old seated Amida, the "Lord of the Western Paradise," and various implements and adornments.

The light was dim -- to protect fragile textiles and lacquers -- and the room, with its red and green temple columns and heavy draperies, was cloaked in solemnity. Arthur M. Clark, who designed the show, said that he wanted people to "talk in hushed tones" in the galleries. "The idea is that there's a rich heritage here, not just objects," he said. "When people leave, they should have a feeling of the essence of Buddhism."

Lights were low in a second room as well, but flashlights were available for viewing the rosary, known as the "Rosary for One Million Recitations." Dating from the 19th-century Edo period in Japan, it consists of 148 beads, some as large as bowling balls; many have tiny carved images inside. The rosary had gathered dust in a British Museum storeroom for 100 years before the curators came upon it. "This is its coming-out party," said Maryell Semal, a consultant to the show.

Ms. Morse explained that "unless there's a context for many of these objects, they're isolated and it's sometimes hard to explain them." The British Museum, she said, had never found an opportunity to display the rosary.

Others described the rosary's arrival in Katonah as a near-religious experience in itself. "The courier who brought it here was a charming man," said Evelyn Fay, the show's associate registrar. "He was instructing us on how to unpack it" -- the crate itself was 12 feet long -- "and we chose a chain-gang method. We passed the rosary balls from one to another," she went on, "so there would be no strain on the cord. It took 15 minutes, and as this happened all of us became very quiet."

Ms. Semal, who coordinated the shipping and installation of the objects, said the museum resembled Grand Central Terminal as couriers and staff members took pains to avoid gridlock.

Earlier, she had listened with trepidation to weather reports in New York and Japan. Each crate needed to rest unopened for 24 hours after its arrival, allowing the rare and irreplaceable cargo to become acclimated. Flights had already been canceled for several days, upsetting a carefully timed schedule. As it happened, the show was ready just minutes before its scheduled opening. "In installations, you absolutely don't break rules," Ms. Semal said.

The exhibition will remain in Katonah through March 17, after which it can be seen in the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston from April 19 through June 30. Admission is free and includes tours at 2:30 P.M. Tuesday through Sunday. More information is available by calling 914-232-9555.
