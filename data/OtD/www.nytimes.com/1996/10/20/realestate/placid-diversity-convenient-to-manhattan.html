Placid Diversity Convenient to Manhattan

By JOYCE COHEN
Published: October 20, 1996

FOR days after last month's spectacular explosion, people came to gape at the charred remains of the Hyo Shin Bible Presbyterian Church, still topped by a cross. No wonder. Such excitement is rare in Auburndale.

Normally, Auburndale is a placid place -- a middle-class Queens neighborhood that people prize for its ordinariness. It is largely populated by families buying their first homes -- or families that, after they did so, liked the neighborhood so much they never left. Historically, residents were of Irish, German or Italian descent; about three decades ago Greek immigrants started moving in. Recently, the neighborhood has become home to large numbers of Chinese and Koreans.

The church that exploded, on 166th Street near Northern Boulevard, was erected by its Korean congregation four years ago. The church, apparently destroyed by a gas leak, housed one of many similar congregations springing up throughout Auburndale and nearby areas.

Newcomers -- whether from other parts of Queens or other parts of the world -- are welcome, longtime residents say.

''Auburndale is a real smorgasbord, a nice mix of everybody,'' said Catherine Grdovic, a real-estate appraiser who has lived there for 23 years. ''It is a beautiful middle-class area with people who mind their own business. If you need neighbors to help you, they will, and if you want them to stay out of your business, they will.''

Most of the housing stock consists of single-family homes, usually with one-car garages and small yards. House prices start at slightly less than $200,000. The more expensive homes, going for up to $300,000, are east of Flushing Cemetery. The rest of the houses are built for two, three or four families. There is a smattering of apartment buildings clustered around 165th Street and 43d Avenue.

''You get good value for your money when you purchase property in Auburndale,'' said a broker, Peter Pappas of SPEC International Realty. He said he knew of homeowners who had planned to move up and out but had built additions to their Auburndale homes and decided to stay when they saw what was available elsewhere.

Part of Auburndale's appeal is its location in Public School District 25, where reading and math scores fall into the city's top quartile. (Some houses on the area's eastern edge are in District 26, known for even more rigorous academics.)

Like most of the rest of the city's schools, Auburndale's neighborhood elementary school, P.S. 107, suffers from overcrowding. This year's kindergarten classes hold 25 pupils, said James Phair, the principal. The largest upper-grade class has 34, two over the maximum desired size. The district has 22 magnet schools, centered on such themes as humanities, space sciences, art and design, and health and nutrition. Admission is selective at P.S. 130, serving kindergarten through third grade, where the focus is on science and technology.

After elementary school, students may attend nearby Intermediate School 25, a magnet school for law, or I.S. 237, a magnet school for medical and public-health sciences.

Francis Lewis High School, with 3,300 students, ranks high among city high schools, offering honors classes at all grade levels, as well as advanced placement courses in many subjects, including physics and advanced calculus.

ONE prominent parochial school is run by the St. Nicholas Greek Orthodox Church; the 400 children in kindergarten through eighth grade study the Greek language and the Greek Orthodox religion. An afternoon program teaches Greek to children attending other schools. Both church and school attract Greeks from all over Queens -- sometimes prompting complaints by the neighbors that their cars clog the streets. The church, celebrating its 25th anniversary this year, is also the host of a large festival every October. (This year's was held last weekend.)

St. Kevin's School, associated with St. Kevin's Roman Catholic Church, enrolls 500 children in nursery school through eighth grade.

The Lutheran School of Flushing and Bayside teaches 60 first-to-third graders next door to the Lutheran Church of the Resurrection.

Peck Park, where youngsters play ball, is a short walk or bike ride away. So is Kissena Park, which features nature trails, playing fields, picnic areas, even a lake stocked with fish.

The 18-hole Kissena Park Golf Course charges $17 on weekdays and $19 on weekends.

The library is another much-visited place. Bright and welcoming, the East Flushing branch of the Queens Borough Public Library offers a spacious children's section, plus plenty of material in Greek, Korean and Chinese. The East Flushing branch, opened in 1977, is not to be confused with the Auburndale branch, much farther north near 25th Avenue. That branch, formerly called Auburndale-Clearview, was established in 1930 to serve both communities.

The neighborhood is the namesake of Auburndale, Mass., a Boston suburb and the hometown of L. H. Green, who was president of the New England Development and Improvement Company. It started developing the area's farmland in 1901, the same year that Long Island Rail Road service arrived.
