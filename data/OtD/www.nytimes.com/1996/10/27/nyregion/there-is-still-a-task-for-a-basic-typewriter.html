There Is Still a Task for a Basic Typewriter

By ROBERTA HERSHENSON
Published: October 27, 1996

JUST as the rotary phone is still holding on, so the typewriter clings to life with surprising tenacity.

Typewriters remain dear to the technologically fearful as well as the computer literate, to the business clerk and the poet. Typewriter repair shops thrive on the ills that befall the machinery: paper clips jamming the works, keys sticking, parts breaking. ''Writing machines'' -- as typewriters were originally called -- are stripped and rebuilt to be sold for respectable sums. The world's leading typewriter manufacturer, Lexmark International in Lexington, Ky., sold 130,000 new machines last year. The company has a licensing agreement with International Business Machines to use its logo because I.B.M. no longer makes typewriters.

This is a far cry from the 1.1 million machines Lexmark sold in its banner typewriter year of 1978, before the computer became a standard item in homes and businesses. But Eddie R. Noel, manager of domestic and worldwide typewriter sales for Lexmark, said the steep decline of sales in recent years had leveled slightly. He predicted a steady niche for the typewriter in years to come.

''You don't give up your typewriter,'' said Pat Root, a reference librarian at the Bronxville Public Library. ''It's too easy for a small job.''

Ms. Root uses a 10-year-old I.B.M. Selectric at home for a personal genealogy project. At work, she and other library staff members use typewriters to make changes on catalogue cards, address envelopes and write short notes.

Roxane Campagna, the Bronxville library director, uses an old -- ''as basic as you can get'' -- Smith-Corona in her office for tasks like typing notices. She said that despite the staff's fondness for their typewriters, the library is up to date with three computer terminals, Internet access and E-mail.

Ed Ruzsbatzky said he sold two new typewriters and four reconditioned ones last month at Hudson Valley Typewriter Company, his shop here. ''I thought for sure they're dinosaurs,'' he said of the machines. ''I was about to retire, but I'm as busy as ever with service calls.''

At County Office Equipment, Lou Cornacchia's shop here, typewriters are piled in a basement workroom awaiting cleaning, stripping or rebuilding. Some are genuine antiques, like an Old Royal X model made in 1914. Mr. Cornacchia, in business for 50 years, plans to restore the Royal X and sell it for $200. He said he sells rebuilt typewriters to large and small businesses as well as to individuals. ''You're always going to need a typewriter to type an envelope,'' he said. ''It takes too much time to put it in the printer.''

Not uncommonly, he said, companies that become computerized feel something is missing. ''I've had people who went completely electronic and then called me six months later to buy back the machines,'' he said.

Carl Schmidt, owner of Gilvin Typewriter Corporation in Tarrytown, said every office should have a typewriter for ''the quick stuff.'' After repairing the machines for 34 years, he said he has concluded that ''as long as there are people over 50, the typewriter will survive.''

''Some people won't give up their typewriters,'' Mr. Schmidt said. ''They are comfortable with them. If someone sat down and showed them how to use a computer, they would probably throw their typewriter out the window.''

Not Aaron Sultanik. For him, typing on a typewriter is not only an aesthetic experience but an integral part of his creativity. ''I like the texture of the typewriter, the kinesthetic feeling,'' said Mr. Sultanik, who is a humanities teacher at Yeshiva University and at Berkeley College in Manhattan and a writer of books on film. ''I don't like the screen being interposed. I like my paperwork spread out in front of me.''

''It's more comfortable for me to compose this way, brainstorming, with different drafts. Clearly, for the whole creative process of writing, the typewriter is for me.''
