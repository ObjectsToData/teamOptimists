PLAYING IN THE NEIGHBORHOOD

By SARAH SLOBIN
Published: October 6, 1996

UPPER EAST SIDE

The Rescuing of Many Jews By the Righteous in Italy

''The Righteous Enemy'' is a documentary film that tells the story of how members of the Italian Army and diplomatic corps saved the life of a Jew during the Holocaust. The film, released in 1987 but not widely distributed, will be shown on Wednesday in a collaboration between Congregation Emanu-El and the Columbus Citizens Foundation, an Italian-American civic organization based in New York.

The documentary came into being when the film maker, Joseph Rochlitz, the son of a Yugoslav Jew, began a personal inquiry into his father's history during World War II. Mr. Rochlitz discovered that his father was among tens of thousands of Jews saved by Italians in the military or diplomatic corps.

Preceding the film, there will be remarks by Maxwell M. Rabb, former United States Ambassador to Italy, and Andrea Grover, a lecturer on Jewish Italy and adjunct associate professor of humanities at New York University.

''The Righteous Enemy,'' Congregation Emanu-El; 10 East 66th Street between Madison and Fifth Avenues; Wednesday, 6:30 P.M.; free; information (212) 744-1400

EAST VILLAGE

This Time, Sanitation Won't Take His Art Away

Curtis Cuffie takes objects that other people consider garbage and recycles them into outdoor sculptures that wend their way in and out of fences, drape over concertina wire and jut out of traffic circles downtown. He has lived in the East Village since the mid-1980's, mostly on the streets. And a solo show of his work has found a home under the roof of an art gallery for the first time.

''He makes work in the street,'' says the guest curator of the show at the Tribe Gallery, Carol Thompson, ''and mostly this work gets reworked or destroyed or carted off by the Sanitation Department.''

In addition to regular appearances outdoors, Mr. Cuffie's art was previously part of a group show at the Tribe Gallery and on the walls at the Flamingo East, a bar on Second Avenue at 13th Street.

''Meet Me at the Margin: Sculpture by Curtis Lee Levon Cuffie'' at the Tribe Gallery; 285 East Third Street, between Avenues C and D; through Oct. 31; free; by appointment, (212) 674-3778 or E-mail at tribes@interport.net.

CARROLL GARDENS

A Thirst for Knowledge? Check Out Brooklyn Beer

Beer in the library? That is what the Friends of the Brooklyn Public Library will be serving on Wednesday evening, in a Beer Tasting Fest with Garrett Oliver, the brewmaster of the Brooklyn Brewery in Williamsburg.

Six types of beer from Mr. Oliver's brewery will be sampled. Mr. Oliver, pictured above, will speak on beer and its major families, and will answer questions about the finer points of craft-brewed beer.

The purpose is ''to let people know more about their own Brooklyn,'' said Stacy Leigh, coordinator for the Carroll Gardens Library Friends group, a coalition of residents who gather at the branch and organize events in the neighborhood.

''A library is not just a depository,'' she said. ''It's a place where culture is shared, and where ideas are shared person to person.'' And this Wednesday, they'll be sharing some beer as well.

Beer Tasting Fest, Carroll Gardens Library; 396 Clinton Street at Union Street; $5 donation; over age 18 only; Wednesday; 8 P.M. to 10 P.M.; information (718) 625-5838.

ALSO AROUND TOWN

AFRO-CARIBBEAN -- ''Cuentos Negros,'' a musical by the theater company Repertorio Espanol, explores the musical and spiritual traditions Africans brought to the Caribbean. Based on folk tales documented by the Cuban writer Lydia Cabrera, it continues in repertory Thursday at 7 P.M. and Saturday at 8 P.M.; Gramercy Arts Theater; 138 East 27th Street between Lexington and Third Avenues; tickets $20 and up, $15 for students and those over 65; (212) 889-2850.

FLORAL FALL -- Autumn Forest Tours at the New York Botanical Garden continue weekends at 1 P.M. and 3 P.M.; 200th Street at Southern Boulevard, the Bronx; admission $3 for adults, $1 for those 3 to 16 or 65 or over; (718) 817-8700.

APPRAISING ABSTRACTION -- ''Abstract American Art of the 1930's and 1940's,'' one in a series of talks at the Practicing Law Institute, sponsored by the Appraisers Association of America. Tomorrow, 6 P.M., followed by a reception; 810 Seventh Avenue at 53d Street; fifth floor; $10; reservations: (212) 889-5404. SARAH SLOBIN
