From a Stone, Magic: A Medium That Set The Pace in Prints

By GRACE GLUECK
Published: December 27, 1996

The 200th anniversary of Alois Senefelder's invention of lithography has not slipped by unnoticed, thanks to the New York Public Library. From its own voluminous collections, it has produced a show of no fewer than 200 early examples of this once-revolutionary print medium.

''The Romance of the Stone: Lithography, 1796-1825'' details how the new medium established itself in Germany, France, England and other countries. The sprawling mix includes works by Goya, Delacroix and Benjamin West along with more than a few other artists whose names don't ring a bell today.

One of them is Nicolas Henri Jacob, whose wonderfully campy tribute, ''The Genius of Fine Arts Encouraging Lithography'' (1818), portrays a long-haired male deity, wearing a pair of sturdy wings and nothing else, ogling a young woman whose dress slips coyly off her shoulder as she pulls a poster from a lithographic press.

Senefelder, a Czech-born writer and actor living in Munich, found that if he drew on a flat stone with a greasy substance, then wet and inked the stone, the image could be transferred to a sheet of paper. Prints were thus produced without the time-consuming drudgery of etching or engraving an image onto a plate.

At first, lithography was used strictly for commercial purposes -- reproducing maps, sheet music and other documents -- rather than creating works of art. Among the earliest exhibits here are sheets of music, like Senefelder's printing of ''Sonata for Four Hands for the Pianoforte,'' by one F. Danzi, in 1797.

''Stone printing,'' as it was called, spread fast through Germany, then to France and Britain, its progress interrupted only by the Napoleonic wars. When they ended in 1815, the presses clanked again, and artists began to tune in. Until the 1820's the hottest spot was Munich, where superior know-how brought would-be lithographers flocking.

Among the strengths of the section devoted to Germany are two beautiful black-and-white lithographs by Karl Friedrich Schinkel (1781-1841), the architect and landscape painter. His finely rendered buildings, one a fortresslike castle nestled into a cliff, the other a Gothic church and its graveyard, are small masterpieces of detail and shading that show in depth the reproductive genius of the medium. A moving ''Christ Crowned With Thorns'' (1818) by Nicolaus Strixner emphasizes lithography's ability to reproduce the subtlest nuances of flesh tone and hair texture.

The first book of artists' lithographs, ''Specimens of Polyautography'' (1803-1807), was issued in London by Philipp Andre. (Polyautography, an early name for Senefelder's process, stressed that artists themselves drew directly on the stone.) The book included a thunder-and-lightning ''Angel of the Resurrection'' (1801) by the American-born Benjamin West and two impressive contributions by the Swiss artist Henry Fuseli.

The English, addicted to landscape, drew heavily on lithography for book illustration. One example is ''Views to Illustrate the Route of the Simplon'' (1822), by James Duffield Harding, a big, handsome book printed by Charles Joseph Hullmandel, a skilled lithographer whose inventive approach to the medium helped to advance its progress. Not only professional printers but also amateurs got into the act, among them J. J. Stockdale, who in 1816 published a small treatise with color lithographs, ''The Last Month in Spain; or, Wretched Traveling Through a Wretched Country.''

Caricature, too, became a staple of lithography, which offered the potential for wide popular distribution. One of the funniest works in the English section is ''Irish Rock Banditti Swearing In a New Member'' (1823), published by N. Chater & Company after one Patrick Ronan. Four smiling goons sit around a table with guns and tankards of ale, a grouping that could be an early Irish version of a scene from ''The Godfather.''

The French section of the show is rife with amusing caricatures. A prolific penman was Louis-Leopold Boilly, whose humorously exaggerated heads are typified in ''The Smokers'' (1824). It shows four older men pompously involved with pipes, while a younger one struggles with a cigar. Another master of the genre was Pierre Nolasque Bergeret, whose color lithograph ''Dawdlers of the Rue du Coq'' (1805) sharply satirizes a fashionably dressed crowd peering at the latest caricatures in the windows of a print seller's shop.

The show, organized by Nancy Finlay and Roberta Waddell of the library staff, winds up with a smattering of contributions from Russia, Spain, the Netherlands and Belgium along with the United States, where lithography arrived late. Included are the earliest known American lithographs, two small, dull views of houses by a Philadelphia portrait painter named Bass Otis (1784-1861).

If this dense exhibition has a surfeit of cows and pastoral scenes, a plethora of gnarled old trees and too many sweeping landscape views, it still absorbingly traces the early career of a medium we now take very much for granted. That the library can muster this wealth of visual material from its own resources is, once again, something to marvel at.

''The Romance of the Stone: Lithography, 1796-1825'' remains through Jan. 11 at the New York Public Library Center for the Humanities, Fifth Avenue and 42d Street.
