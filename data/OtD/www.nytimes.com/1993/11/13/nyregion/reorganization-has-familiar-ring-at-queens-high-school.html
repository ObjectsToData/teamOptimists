Reorganization Has Familiar Ring at Queens High School

By DENNIS HEVESI
Published: November 13, 1993

Educational innovation is already proceeding at Andrew Jackson High School in Cambria Heights, Queens, one of two high schools in the city that Chancellor Ramon C. Cortines wants to phase out over the next four years.

Whether the school, which has 2,347 students, will maintain its name remains to be determined, officials said yesterday. But the doors of the stately, three-story beige building on Francis Lewis Boulevard will still be open after the transformation. It is the business of learning that will be different for a school that in 1991 saw only 174 students graduate, only 10 with diplomas endorsed by the State Board of Regents.

Signs of change were evident yesterday. Seated in the bleachers of the football field -- under a 50-yard-long sign that read, "We Can Because We Believe We Can" -- Charles Kirkland, 15, said he could not wait to begin working on the computers next week. Business of Learning

Charles, a freshman, is enrolled in one of four magnet programs that started in September, though only for first-year students. In his program, Business and Entrepreneurship, he said, "I've learned definitions of business words: resource, demand, contract, obligation." But what Charles really relishes is computers. "I love them," he said.

Assuming that the Board of Education approves the reorganization plan for Andrew Jackson, said Frank Sobrino, a spokesman for the Chancellor, the four newly installed programs -- humanities, science and math, law and government, and business -- will eventually become "full-blown magnet schools within the walls" of Jackson, which opened in 1937. Beginning this year, each freshman class will be phased into the magnet-school curriculum. The board is to vote Wednesday.

Mr. Cortines said on Thursday that he planned to close two troubled schools and replace them with networks of smaller schools. Although he did not name them, other educators identified them as Jackson and James Monroe High School in the Bronx.

Under the Jackson plan, each magnet school would hire its own principal, faculty and staff, Mr. Sobrino said, and each would enroll 500 to 700 students from applicants throughout the city.

"The students currently in Jackson would graduate over the next three years as Jackson students," Mr. Sobrino said. "I don't think anybody's gotten to the point of how you name the building." Defending Magnet Plan

Standing in front of the school yesterday, Assemblywoman Barbara M. Clark, a Democrat, pointed out that the community had been working on the magnet-school plan for two years. She said she was chagrined that Jackson had been branded a troubled school.

"The community has been impugned," she said. "What really hurts is that at a time when the second year's class will be applying they say that the school is closing. It has created a major strain."

Board of Education statistics show that the dropout rate for the 1991-92 school year was 16.2 percent throughout the city and 12.1 percent at Jackson. Still, the graduation rate for the city was 42.9 percent, but only 31.2 percent at Jackson.

Several students insisted that their school was not as bad as its reputation. Phakiya Taylor, 15, said she was in the diploma-to-degree program, in which students graduate in three years if they maintain an 85 average, and are guaranteed acceptance at Long Island University.

"That was designed for Jackson, and people don't know that," she said.

Sha-Kima Larkins, 16, said, "It's not the name that makes it bad, it's the students that give it a bad name." 'Good, Hard-Working People'

Eva Duzant, treasurer of the Cambria Heights Civic Association, said the school has received "a bum rap."

"They have a mentoring program over there, of which I am a participant," Miss Duzant said. "We let the students select us and we guide them throughout their high school career, help them select and apply for colleges."

She also defended the community of about 25,000, which is largely made up of single and attached private homes.

"Most of the people here are Civil Service workers, lawyers, doctors, business people -- a lot of good, hard-working people," said Miss Duzant, an accountant.

Malik Moore, 16, said that change would benefit the school because of a lack of discipline that permeates the building.

"It's just too free," he said. "You can walk around, just go out of school."

But expressions of pride were the order of the day.

Jamal Thomas, 17, said, "It's all a lot of huff and puff. There's kids in there who are willing to put forth the effort for the future." And Kevin Williams, 15, said, "We've got teachers that want you to strive in the world."
