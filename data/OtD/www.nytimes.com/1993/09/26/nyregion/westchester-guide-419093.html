WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: September 26, 1993

LANI GUINIER TO SPEAK

Lani Guinier is traveling around the country talking about her withdrawn nomination June 3 to the post of assistant attorney general for civil rights. At 12:30 P.M. on Friday she will speak in Reisinger Auditorium at Sarah Lawrence College in Yonkers, focusing on the Clinton Administration's position that her legal writings appeared to advocate proportional representation rather than the 1-person-1-vote system.

The lecture circuit is also a platform from which she hopes to promote increased political clout for minorities and eventually create a White House Conference on Civil Rights.

Now a law professor at the University of Pennsylvania, Ms. Guinier received her bachelor's degree from Radcliffe and her law degree from Yale. She has written articles on civil rights law for the Harvard Law Review and other scholarly publications and served in civil rights areas of the United States Department of Justice and the N.A.A.C.P. Legal Defense Fund.

Her talk, "What I Would Have Said," refers to the refusal of the Senate Judiciary Committee to grant her a hearing on her positions and her record. Admission is free. The number to call for more information or directions to the college is 395-2220. 1993 JAZZ FORUM

It used to be called Bossa Nova, a slangy Brazilian term for "new thing" or "new bag," back in the 1960's when Herbie Mann, Cal Tjader and other American musicians fell under its spell. Joao Gilberto and Antonio Carlos Jobim were the leaders of the movement in Rio de Janeiro, and Mr. Mann recorded with both of them, introducing the new sound to North America.

Today Brazilian jazz is part of the mainstream, and Mr. Mann, his flute and a backup group of piano, bass, drums and percussion calling itself Jasil Brazz will open the 1993 Jazz Forum at the Tarrytown Music Hall on Saturday at 8 P.M.

The series will continue on Oct. 23 with Swing Summit, featuring George Wein and the Newport All-Stars: Clark Terry, Al Grey, Frank Wess, Eddie Jones and others, plus the Doc Cheatham Quartet. The final program, on Nov. 13, stars the trumpeter Maynard Ferguson and his nine-piece Big Bop Nouveau Band.

A series subscription is $60, individual tickets are $25. Students and people 65 and older get a $5 discount on individual tickets. The theater is at 13 Main Street, and the box office phone number is 674-2005. REPTILES FOR SALE

Wanna buy a snake? Many will be available at the Westchester Reptile Show today from 10 A.M. to 4 P.M. at the Greenburgh Nature Center, to the delight of people who fancy snakes, turtles and lizards as pets.

Only captive-bred, nonvenomous creatures will be offered by vendors, who will also carry a variety of related accessories and books on the history, care and feeding of reptiles and amphibians. The accent will be on responsible ownership, reinforced by members of the staff who will be on hand to answer questions, along with representatives of the New York Turtle and Tortoise Society and the New York Herpetological Society, both of which will have conservation displays on view.

Prices will range from $5 for small garter and water snakes to several hundred dollars for rainbow boas and king snakes. Most other snakes will cost $15 to $50.

The center's own live collection of more than 60 reptiles and amphibians will be accessible for prospective pet owners to observe and touch. Admission is $3, $2 for students and people 65 and older, $1 for children younger than 12.

The center is on Dromore Road off Central Avenue, and the phone number for more information is 723-3470. 'LOST IN THE STARS'

A concert version of "Lost in the Stars," the 1948 musical drama by Kurt Weill adapted from Alan Paton's novel "Cry, the Beloved Country," will open the Philharmonia Virtuosi's Mainstage Concert Series at the State University of New York at Purchase today at 3 P.M.

Richard Kapp, the Virtuosi's conductor and musical director, has assembled an all-black cast led by Thomas Young, tenor, and the soprano Dian Dames.

The rest of the program contains music by Mendelssohn, Mahler and Bloch, deliberately chosen because, like Weill, they were Jewish. Mr. Kapp wished to make a point of bringing together the two groups.

The performance will be held in the Concert Hall, formerly Theater A of the Performing Arts Center, and ticket prices are $18 and $22. The numbers to call for reservations are 693-5595 or 251-6200. WRITER TO TELL HOW

Language, values and technical considerations involved in writing for young people as opposed to writing for the adult market will be discussed on Tuesday at 4:30 P.M. in the Student Center of Westchester Community College in Valhalla.

Scott Johnson, author of the forthcoming book for teen-agers titled "Overnight Sensation," to be published by Atheneum in March of next year, will be the speaker in the free program, titled "Ego, Id and Teen-Ager."

Mr. Johnson, an English and creative writing teacher at Pleasantville High School, was the recipient of a Fulbright Teaching Exchange program in London and a fellowship from the National Endowment for the Humanities in the 1980's. His first novel, "One of the Boys," won the 1993 Best Book for Young Adults Award from the American Library Association. CHAMBER MUSIC SERIES

For the last decade a chamber orchestra composed of members of the American Symphony Orchestra has been performing contemporary and classic chamber works linking unfamiliar repertories with standard fare. Opening a new season at Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, the American Symphony Chamber Orchestra will be joined by Eugene Moye, the Greenwich cellist, as guest soloist in Nicholas Maw's "Sonata Notturna" on Friday at 8 P.M.

Richard Wilson will be featured as soloist in Beethoven's Fourth Piano Concerto, and Beethoven's "Consecration of the House Overture" will be performed by the ensemble in honor of the dedication of the college's new Stevenson Library, designed by the architect Robert Venturi. Admission is $12.50, and ticket-holders are invited to a preconcert talk at 7 P.M.

The American Symphony Orchestra was founded in 1962 by Leopold Stokowski, and since his death it has been led by Leonard Bernstein, Andre Previn and James Levine. Leon Botstein, president of Bard College, is the current music director. For tickets or information, the numbers to call are 758-7425 or 758-6822. ELEANOR CHARLES
