New & Noteworthy

By LAUREL GRAEBER
Published: May 16, 1993

BARN BLIND, by Jane Smiley. (Fawcett Columbine, $10.) Jane Smiley's first novel, published in 1980 and never before in paperback, deals with an ambitious woman determined to have her farm-bound children become prize-winning equestrians. The author "handles with skill and understanding the mercurial molasses of adolescence, and the inchoate, cumbersome love that family members feel for one another," Michael Malone said here.

THE CULTURE OF CONTENTMENT, by John Kenneth Galbraith. (Houghton Mifflin, $9.95.) The contented -- the top 20 percent in income in our society -- are a minority in number but a majority in power, asserts John Kenneth Galbraith. Opposed to economic reform, these are the voters who brought us the lower taxes and laissez-faire approaches of the Administrations of the last two Republican Presidents. Last year our reviewer, Robert N. Bellah, said: "I know of no book -- and I have read a number of excellent discussions of the subject lately -- that treats more incisively or more convincingly the profoundly injurious consequences of these policies to our economy." Houghton Mifflin has also reissued Mr. Galbraith's strangely prescient 1968 novel THE TRIUMPH ($9.95), in which the American Government intervenes in Latin America on behalf of a corrupt but anti-Communist dictator. Donald Barthelme found the book stuffy but, he said here, "The story is dandy."

THE DARK SISTER, by Rebecca Goldstein. (Penguin, $11.) A feminist writer named Hedda, obsessed with the literary art of Henry James, writes a very Jamesian tale that blends fiction, history and her own relationship with her sister. In 1991 our reviewer, Angeline Goreau, said that Ms. Goldstein's third novel "represents an impressively ambitious advance."

THE DISUNITING OF AMERICA: Reflections on a Multicultural Society, by Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr. (Norton, $7.95.) The author cautions against educational curriculums that celebrate ethnicity at the expense of the unified, democratic culture that has always been the American ideal. Last year our reviewer, Frank Kermode, called this a "sane and temperate book." More on the subject can be found in LOOSE CANONS: Notes on the Culture Wars, by Henry Louis Gates Jr. (Oxford University, $9.95), which argues for an open approach to the humanities based not on political correctness but on the need to understand our own diverse origins. The book "should prove especially illuminating and reassuring to nonacademics," James D. Bloom said here last year.

EVER AFTER, by Graham Swift. (Vintage International, $11.) A professor becomes embroiled in existential questions when he comes upon the notebooks of Matthew Pearce, a 19th-century man whose discovery of a fossil led him to abandon God and embrace Darwin. Last year our reviewer, MacDonald Harris, said: "The prose is rich, lush and unhurried; this is a modern British novel for the reader who is getting bored with the contemporary American mode of fiction."

IN THE REALM OF A DYING EMPEROR: Japan at Century's End, by Norma Field. (Vintage, $12.) Norma Field, the daughter of a Japanese mother and an American father, explores the state of civil rights in Japan through the cases of three citizens who questioned the culture's conformist ethic. The book is notable for its "well-researched, well-observed and completely absorbing reporting," John David Morley said here in 1991. Also available in paperback is THE OUTNATION: A Search for the Soul of Japan, by Jonathan Rauch (Little, Brown, $10.95), in which a young journalist who spent six months in the country reached the surprising conclusion that the Japanese may not be so different from us after all. Last year one critic called his book "one of the very best accounts of modern Japanalia."

LITTLE WOMAN, by Ellen Akins. (Harper Perennial, $10.) The heroine is anything but little: large and ungainly, Beauty Skinner leaves her husband and twins to set up a shelter for battered women and pursue her own ulterior motives. In 1990 our reviewer, Susan Dundon, called this novel "exquisitely written."

WOODROW WILSON, by August Heckscher. (Collier, $18.) This biography relates the achievements of Wilson's political life to the evolution of his character, also illuminating the President's relationships with both his wives. The author's "description of Wilson's personality is by far the most persuasive I am familiar with," John A. Garraty said here in 1991.

NELLIE WITHOUT HUGO, by Janet Hobhouse. (Anchor/Doubleday, $9.) In this 1982 novel, Nellie, a 30-year-old New Yorker, takes advantage of her husband's absence to have an affair with a brutal ex-lover. Although our reviewer, Katha Pollitt, found the female characters' attitudes and choices regressive, she described the author as a "lively, intelligent writer."

THE MIND'S SKY: Human Intelligence in a Cosmic Context, by Timothy Ferris. (Bantam, $13.95.) Timothy Ferris, a science journalist, explores the connection between the universe of mind and the universe of matter. His is an "informed, inspired investigation of the intellect and the infinite . . . anchored in hard science and good sense," Dava Sobel said here last year.

KISSING THE GUNNER'S DAUGHTER, by Ruth Rendell. (Mysterious Press/Warner, $5.50.) In Ruth Rendell's latest Inspector Wexford mystery, her hero investigates a gruesome triple murder in an elegant English country house. Last year our reviewer, Winfred Stout, called it an "intricate story that hinges on vanity and self-deception."
