A Clinton Report Card, So Far

By Arthur Schlesinger Jr.;
Published: April 11, 1993

Before long, the 100 days will be upon us, and we can expect a rush to judgment on the Clinton Presidency. Of course the 100 days test is silly. Franklin Roosevelt gave the 100 days mythic status, but he had a national crisis so urgent and indisputable that the country pleaded for action and Congress dared not say him nay. Except for F.D.R., no President has ever accomplished much legislatively in his first 100 days. Still, the 100 days represent a shakedown cruise that tells the people a good deal about a new President -- and tells him a good deal about the people.

For a man who a short year ago was an unknown quantity to most Americans, and a very dubious figure to some of them, Mr. Clinton has quickly established himself as an articulate, ebullient, untiring and, thus far, rather endearing occupant of the White House.

He is plainly a man of marked intellectual force and lively intellectual curiosity, a good listener and a good talker, generally cheerful though given to flashes of bad temper. He is at home with ideas, enjoys politics, understands that successful government requires persuasion and consent and appears to have a guileless faith in the latent idealism and decency of the American people. And in Albert Gore Jr. the nation has (for a change) a serious and thoughtful Vice President.

In a way, Mr. Clinton is a merger of two Democratic leaders of the previous generation who also combined professionalism in politics with idealism in policy -- John Kennedy and Hubert Humphrey. Mr. Clinton has J.F.K.'s analytical bent, his detachment and his mastery of detail, and he has Humphrey's exuberance, his gregariousness and his inability to say anything briefly.

Like Kennedy and Humphrey and Lyndon Johnson and like F.D.R. and Harry Truman before them, Mr. Clinton believes in government as an instrument of reform. Like J.F.K. 30 years ago and F.D.R. 30 years before that, he takes over from Presidents who had no use for government at all.

When Ronald Reagan became President, he signaled the change in national direction by proclaiming in his first inaugural: "Government is not the solution to our problem. Government is the problem." Get government off our backs, the Reaganites declared, and our problems will solve themselves, or rather the free market will solve them.

President Clinton took care to respond in his address to Congress, "I want to talk with you about what the Government can do, because I believe government must do more." He evidently sees himself in the apostolic tradition. One of his early acts was to make a pilgrimage, in the company of F.D.R.'s grandson and Robert Kennedy's son, to the Roosevelt house in Hyde Park. And one of his favorite quotations, used often in the campaign and since, is F.D.R.'s call for "bold, persistent experimentation. It is common sense to take a method and try it: If it fails, admit it frankly and try another. But above all, try something." Except for the part about admitting failure frankly, that was the story of Roosevelt's New Deal.

In embracing the Roosevelt-Truman-Kennedy-Johnson inheritance, President Clinton departs from neo-conservative Democrats who backed his candidacy and now feel aggrieved by lack of subsequent recognition. He differs too from Jimmy Carter, who disbelieved in government, disliked the Kennedys and refused as President even to send a greeting to a reunion of New Dealers celebrating the anniversary of F.D.R.'s first inauguration.

Oddly enough for a believer in government, Mr. Clinton has been dilatory in forming one. I cannot recall a time when a new Administration taking over the White House from the other party has taken so long in making key appointments.

The Department of Defense, for example, still lacks most of its top officials. No doubt filling out financial forms, F.B.I. investigations, etc., account for some of the delay. But the Administration's "diversity" requirement -- "looking like America" -- compounds the problem. The determination to bring in more women and minorities is praiseworthy. But the mechanical application of a diversity standard may at times sacrifice substantive competence to what John Kennedy used to call "visual reassurance."

When Mr. Clinton says he believes government must do more, he does not mean government should do everything. He has no doubt that the free market is the great engine of production, distribution and growth. But he is not under the illusion that the market by itself can cure a lot of the troubles that assail the Republic. The market by itself will not repair America's crumbling infrastructure, or produce universal health care, or protect the environment, or promote racial justice, or save the public schools, or rehabilitate the cities, or salvage the homeless. The market cannot even provide jobs for all Americans ready and willing to work. He sees no alternative to a measure of affirmative government.

Like Roosevelt in 1933 and Kennedy in 1961, Mr. Clinton has the benefit of the pent-up flood of ideas germinating in universities and think-tanks during the conservative phase of the political cycle.
