WESTCHESTER GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: July 4, 1993

THE BARD AT BOSCOBEL

With the lawn of the Boscobel Restoration in Garrison as a stage, the Hudson River as a backdrop, minimal props and costumes that rely heavily on old jeans, the Hudson Valley Shakespeare Festival has completed six successful seasons and embarks on its seventh with "The Merry Wives of Windsor."

The production opens with previews on Thursday and Friday, a first- night gala on Saturday, a performance next Sunday, then performances Wednesday through Sunday through Aug. 1.

Praise has been showered on this youthful company by The New York Times, The New Yorker and theatrical celebrities who attended the shows, including Helen Hayes and the playwright John Pielmeyer.

By doing away with doublets and hose, ponderous scenery and operatic posturing, the players present Shakespeare's works as they were performed in his own time -- trippingly on the tongue, athletically and as ribald or moving as the script demands.

The grounds of Boscobel open at 6 P.M. for picnicking before the performances, which begin at 8 o'clock. Food may be brought from home, or gourmet picnic dinners at $16 may be ordered in advance through the box office.

Preview tickets are $15; tickets for the opening-night festivities, which include a tour of the mansion, buffet supper, the performance and a champagne dessert reception afterward, cost $75. Tickets for the rest of the run are $20 and $22. The box office phone number is 265-9575. THEATER ARTS CAMP

Last year the Hudson Valley Shakespeare Festival conducted a summer Theater Arts Camp for children, which will be repeated from July 19 through July 30 at the Garrison Free School and the St. Philips Parish House, also in Garrison. Students divided into age groups from 6 through 8 and from 9 through 10 will meet from 9 A.M. to 2 P.M. Hours for age groups 11 through 13 and 14 through 17 will be from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Fees are $260 for the younger children, $300 for the two older groups. Some scholarships are available.

Members of the company and outside professionals will conduct workshops in movement, mime, voice, improvisation, scene and costume design, props and lighting. The students will attend a performance of "The Merry Wives of Windsor" and present a production of their own devising on July 31 at Boscobel's tent theater. For more information or to enroll, the number to call is 424-3960. ON AMERICAN AUTHORS

"Two Decisive Decades" (the 1920's and 30's) will be explored in a free four-part reading and discussion at the White Plains Library every other Wednesday. As part of a statewide library program called "The American Century," sponsored by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the New York Council for the Humanities, the White Plains segment will feature major American authors discussed by university faculty members who are National Endowment scholars.

Sessions begin this Wednesday at 7:30 P.M. with F. Scott Fitzgerald's "Tender Is the Night." The workshop leader will be Dr. Brenda Wineapple, professor of English at Union College in Schenectady, recipient of a Guggenheim Fellowship, and a senior fellow in American Literature at the Beinecke Library of Yale University.

Subsequent evenings will be devoted to "Uncle Tom's Children" by Richard Wright, "The Lost Lady" by Willa Cather, and "Bread Givers" by Anzia Yezierska. Workshop leaders are Thomasenia Myers-Hutchins, assistant professor of literature at the State University of New York at Purchase; Linda Anstendig, coordinator for the department of literature at Pace University, and Hedda Garza, political science consultant at SUNY Plattsburgh.

Paperback copies of the books will be available to borrowers at the library information desk up to two weeks before each program. For more information, the number to call is 422-1400. UNUSUAL EXPERIENCES

As an adjunct to "The American Century" program, the White Plains Library has scheduled a free series of one-hour video documentaries exploring the lives of some unusual Americans.

On Wednesday at 1 P.M. "Becoming American" will deal with a Laotian family's struggle to overcome prejudice and culture shock as it adapts to life in the United States.

Three other videos will be shown on the next three Wednesdays. "American Tongues" examines dialects from Back Bay Boston to Louisiana Cajun. Life among the homeless is given a close-up inspection in "Inside Life Outside." And the final piece, called "Kicking High in the Golden Years," reveals how six blacks achieved a happy and independent old age.

The library is at 100 Martine Avenue in downtown White Plains. The number to call for more information is 422-1400. SUMMER SINGS RESUME

Anyone yearning to lift his or her voice in song is welcome to join the 15th annual Summer Sings of the Westchester Choral Society, organized in conjunction with the Hoff-Barthelson Music School.

Sessions are held on Tuesdays at 8 P.M. in the air-conditioned school at 25 School Lane, Scarsdale. This week's works will be Haydn's "Mass in Time of War" and Bernstein's "Chichester Psalms." Judith Clurman will be the conductor, and scores are available for those who do not bring their own.

Handel's "Israel in Egypt" will be conducted by Lyndon Woodside next week; Harold Rosenbaum will lead three works on July 20: Mozart's "Requiem," Brahms's "Nanie" and Faure's "Cantique de Jean Racine." The series will conclude on July 27 with "A German Requiem" by Brahms, conducted by Johannes Somary.

Admission is $6 and includes the use of a score and refreshments. For more information, the number to call is 761-4714.
