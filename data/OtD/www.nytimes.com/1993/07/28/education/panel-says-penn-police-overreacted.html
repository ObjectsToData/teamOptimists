Panel Says Penn Police Overreacted


Published: July 28, 1993

A review panel at the University of Pennsylvania has found that several campus police officers overreacted in April when they tried to stop eight black students from seizing copies of the student newspaper as a protest against a conservative columnist.

The university appointed the panel of teachers and administrators, including one security official from outside, to investigate the university police department's response to the incident after five of the students filed complaints. The students said the officers had cursed at them and used unnecessary force, including handcuffing four and hitting one with a baton.

In a report on the incident released on July 13, the panel said the officers should have recognized that the students' attempt to remove all the papers, although it violated university policy, was "a form of protest and not an indicator of criminal behavior."

Instead of dealing with the matter themselves, the report said, the officers should have alerted campus officials known as open expression monitors, whose job is to observe rallies and protests to insure campus rules are followed.

Shortly after the incident, the police officer who had used the baton was suspended without pay for three days, and a museum administrator who had seized two female students as they carried off newspapers was temporarily given other duties. The students involved will be disciplined when they return to school in the fall, said Kim Morrisson, vice provost for university life.

The incident occurred when racial tensions on the campus had already been heightened by a case in which five black students filed a racial harassment claim against a white student who had called them water buffaloes. The five students later dropped the complaint.

It also came at a delicate time for Sheldon Hackney, who was then the university's president and was nominated by President Clinton to head the National Endowment for the Humanities. During Senate hearings in June, Mr. Hackney acknowledged that he should have used stronger language to dissuade students from confiscating newspapers to protest their content. The committee endorsed his appointment, and he is awaiting confirmation by the full Senate.

Barbara Beck, a spokeswoman for the university, said the panel's findings underscored the view that campus police should not operate under the same guidelines as an urban police force. "Life here is different than it is on the outside," she said.
