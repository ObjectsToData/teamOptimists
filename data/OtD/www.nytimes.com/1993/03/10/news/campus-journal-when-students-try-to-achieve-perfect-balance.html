Campus Journal; When Students Try to Achieve Perfect Balance

By ANTHONY DePALMA,
Published: March 10, 1993

Taking a midterm exam is like working with a safety net: there is always a chance for redemption.

Lynette S. Hester-Bey, an accounting major at Bloomfield College, knew that but was still nervous. Had she done enough homework? Could she remember what to do? Would she flop?

But as soon as her instructor, Ned J. Gelfars, began the test, she knew she would be all right. The squishy balls leaped effortlessly from her hands. And even though she dropped a few rings and grazed her shoulder with a club, Ms. Hester-Bey was certain she would end up on top of the world, balancing herself with easy grace somewhere near Mexico on an acrobat's giant globe.

She did well, scoring a 96.5 in what was one of the strangest midterm exams in one of the oddest college courses: Introduction to Circus Arts.

About a dozen students at this small private liberal arts college a few miles west of downtown Newark are taking the circus course, not to become clowns but to fulfill their humanities and fine-arts requirement for graduation. Besides the midterm (juggle three balls for 40 throws; balance a six-foot pole on your chin for 12 seconds), the students are graded on writing assignments ("Explain how to juggle"), their attitudes and a show in which they will perform at the end of the semester.

This is college?

Bloomfield's president, John F. Noonan, said the circus course counted as four credits toward a bachelor's degree and a big step toward understanding what it takes to succeed.

"Basically, this course is about teaching people that they can do what seems at first to be impossible," Dr. Noonan said.

There are no pretenses here about this being an intellectual pursuit. The circus course is an urban version of Outward Bound, the outdoors program that focuses on skills like cooperation, openness to criticism and a feel-it-in-your-guts love of learning.

All this is especially helpful at Bloomfield College. The 125-year-old institution has increasingly been accepting eager but sometimes unprepared students who come from nearby tough towns like East Orange, Irvington and Newark with more desire than preparation for learning.

The circus course is one of many innovative approaches that Bloomfield has tried in meeting the needs of its 2,000 students. It began in 1988 when John H. Towsen, an associate professor of performing arts, made a proposal. For him it was a natural. He is half of the clown performance team of Towsen and Yockers and an organizer of the International Festival of Clown Theater in New York City.

"It really does seem to give the students confidence, in the sense that they come to school often with lower S.A.T. scores, lower self-esteem, and without necessarily feeling that they're destined for success," Mr. Towsen said. "In the beginning they feel they would never be able to learn even one-tenth of what we're about to throw at them. But they do learn. It's pretty amazing."

Mr. Towsen said state universities in Florida and Illinois also offered circus courses but without the character-building goals that the one at Bloomfield has.

The symbolic significance of walking a tightrope or juggling balls seems obvious but how does that help anyone not planning to become a clown? Ms. Hester-Bey, a Brooklynite who wants to be an accountant, never juggled anything before the class started in January. In her midterm, she exceeded 40 throws.

"Once you can do 40 throws with the balls, you think you could do something else," she said afterward. "It makes you want to do things to see if you can do them. And most of the time you can."

Kahlil Weston, a business administration major from Merchantville, N.J., admitted he was taking the class because he thought it would be an easy way to fulfill his fine-arts requirement. But he ended up learning a lot.

"On something like the tightrope, you've just got to treat getting across it like something you really want," he said. "It can be scary, but what counts is that you make some effort."

When it was Joseph LaCognata's turn, the sophomore biochemistry major had trouble juggling. But later Mr. LaCognata fairly danced on a rolling seesaw and walked the tight wire with the greatest of ease.

"There are certain things you're great at and certain things you're not," Mr. LaCognata said. "That's the way it is with everyone."

And with life, too? "Yeah, you could say that," he replied.
