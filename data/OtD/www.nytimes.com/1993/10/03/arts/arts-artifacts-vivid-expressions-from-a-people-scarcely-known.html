ARTS/ARTIFACTS; Vivid Expressions From a People Scarcely Known

By RITA REIF
Published: October 3, 1993

The richly figured murals, sculpture and ceramics of Teotihuacan, which were widely imitated by the Aztecs but virtually unknown to all but pre-Columbian historians, are now recognized as some of the most innovative of the ancient Americas.

Teotihuacan (pronounced tay-oh-tee-wah-kahn) was Mexico's first urban state. It lay 25 miles northeast of what is now Mexico City and flourished from the first century A.D. to 750, when it collapsed for unexplained reasons. But now examples of emblematic art from this mysterious society, which left no written language, can be seen in "Teotihuacan: City of the Gods," at the M. H. de Young Memorial Museum, through Oct. 31.

More than 200 objects assembled from around the world, including 80 masterpieces from Mexican museums, tell the story of these remarkable people. At its peak in the year 600, Teotihuacan was a sprawling city of pyramids and apartment compounds. At eight miles square, it was larger than Rome, and it had a population of 150,000, as many as Athens. When the Aztecs came upon the abandoned site in the 16th century, they were so awed by its architectural grandeur and complexity that they named it Teotihuacan, meaning "place of the gods."

While no written record survives of how these people lived, there is ample evidence in art and artifacts, some of which were found in the 1980's in tombs and apartment compounds. A necklace combining teethlike shells and human jaws suggests that human sacrifice was practiced; evidence of this is also seen in the red and blue murals found on the walls of their stone and adobe temples and homes. These murals, bewildering in their complexity, mix naturalistic and abstract motifs in puzzlelike patterns and are as ornamental as medieval tapestries.

Reiterated throughout the murals are compelling images of the great goddess that was probably Teotihuacan's principal deity. Sometimes she is shown eyeless, with claws, wearing an elaborate headdress, her large open mouth bordered by waterlilies. The same dual sense of creation and destruction is expressed in the storm god, a fierce-faced being sometimes shown casting seeds on the earth as part of a fertility rite.

Half of the 15 mural fragments in the exhibition were acquired in Mexico and brought here in the 1960's by Harald Wagner, a San Francisco architect. A handwritten will found after his death in 1976 bequeathed them to the museum.

"We were flabbergasted by this gift," said Kathleen Berrin, curator of the museum's department of Africa, Oceania and the Americas, who organized the show with Esther Pasztory, an art history professor at Columbia University. "No one at the museum knew him. We found more than 70 mural fragments in his house here on Pacific Avenue."

Mr. Wagner had done rudimentary restoration to keep the fragile murals from crumbling. But the museum decided that further preservation was necessary, and Ms. Berrin contacted Mexican authorities. In 1984, conservators from Mexico came to San Francisco to work with the museum staff to prevent further deterioration. After this work was completed in 1986, the museum voluntarily returned half of its murals to Mexico, where they were exhibited at the National Museum of Anthropology in 1986.

The Wagner gift was especially important to the San Francisco museum because of the rarity of the murals, and the city's strong Mexican community. They also posed an interpretive challenge.

"What you get in Mayan art but not in Teotihuacan art," said Ms. Berrin, "is an emphasis on specific charismatic rulers. This makes Teotihuacan art more intriguing and sometimes more difficult to interpret, because there is a tendency to abstraction and no narrative material."

THE CERAMICS ALSO incorporate abstract elements. Working without a wheel, Teotihuacans devised robust vessels that are sometimes awesome in their simplicity. Among them is a round pot in a clay called thin orange ware; the surface was left blank to expose the gritty texture.

Other pieces are boldly decorated with stylized images of gods, serpents, owls, flowers or geometric motifs. A small tub on knoblike feet, blackened in an ancient fire, is handsomely embellished with rows of circles and chevrons. On a brown jar burnished to a leatherlike shimmer, snakes slither up the sides. One of the most colorful vessels depicts, in bright turquoise and red, men in goggles who appear to speak via scrolls issuing from their mouths, much as in comic-strip art. "They seem to emphasize that this is the city where we speak and don't write," Professor Pasztory said.

Among the more inventively shaped pieces are so-called host figures, which display one or more smaller figures inside their chests and stomachs. Censers, used to burn incense, are sometimes topped by lavish images of the great goddess. All that survives from one such censer is a polychrome mask of this goddess, adorned with earmufflike spools and a mouth flap hung from the nose.

More than 150,000 people have filed through the show since the opening in May, making it one of the museum's most popular. The exhibition, which will not travel, was financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the National Endowment for the Arts.
