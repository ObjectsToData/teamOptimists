Toni Morrison Casts Spell For Students at Princeton


Published: October 10, 1993

Toni Morrison, winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature, is world famous for her books "Beloved," "Jazz" and "Song of Solomon." On the Princeton campus Ms. Morrison is renowned for Studies in American Africanism, the seminar she teaches twice a week to a dozen rapt students.

The course, taught to a group that sits in an informal circle, centers on the way literary works have adapted to the presence in America of Africans and their descendants. Students must apply to qualify, and the extensive reading list ranges from Edgar Allan Poe to Herman Melville to Professor Morrison herself.

The course is demanding -- one recent week's required reading included "Moby-Dick" and "Huckleberry Finn" -- and the discussion focuses exclusively on African influences in the text. The discussion is so focused, in fact, that the students in Wednesday's seminar talked about "Moby-Dick" for two and a half hours, one student said, and "never even once mentioned the whale."

Topics ranged from how the characters in the two novels dealt with the concept of Africans and their descendants to exploring the character's use of Africa as a background in front of which they tried to discover themselves. Bringing It All Together

"She is so lyrical, so incredibly well versed," said Helen Parr, a senior from New Vernon, N.J. "Film, culture, art, music and history; she brings them together and just ties them up to paint an amazing picture."

The Princeton students talk about Ms. Morrison's striking image, the tales she weaves from the works they are studying, and the moments in her cluttered, book-filled office in the Creative Arts Building when the readings spring to life.

In a flowing skirt or dress and with a kerchief over her braided hair, Ms. Morrison is the focal point of the class, standing to lecture and sitting down to discuss.

"She just radiates this warmth about the literature," Ms. Parr said. "She has this 'This is something that fascinates me, let me share it with you' attitude about the works that you can't help but being drawn into." Timidity at First

Ms. Morrison also teaches a creative writing course during the spring semester, and each year advises at least one student with a senior thesis. She is known for enthusiasm in her meetings with students, who say that they quickly get over the timidity caused by discussing their own work with so accomplished an author.

" 'Song of Solomon' was my favorite book in high school, and it was amazing to be able to come here and actually discuss her characters with her," said Jessie Janowitz, a junior from New York City who took Ms. Morrison's writing course last spring.

Ms. Morrison won the 1977 National Book Critics Award for "Song of Solomon" and the 1988 Pulitzer Prize for "Beloved." Ms. Morrison, a professor of the humanities at Princeton since 1989, was also an editor at Random House for 20 years.

The aim of the writing course, titled Long Fiction, is for each of the six students eventually to produce a 50-page manuscript. Two students read aloud from their works each week. Classmates and Ms. Morrison then offer critiques. Keeping It Light

"She was very good at poking fun at our work and made sure no one took himself too seriously," Ms. Janowitz said. "Her abilities as an editor also really helped. I remember being so excited to get my work back to see what she had marked up."

As in her writing, in which she emphasizes the history behind characters and events, Ms. Morrison attempts to bring as many cultural elements as possible into class discussion. She is an impressive if intimidating presence who can mesmerize an audience.

"Her breadth of knowledge is mind-boggling," said Thad McBride, a junior who took a course she taught last year. "She was also a wonderful reader as she read aloud to us. I remember times when it seemed like the only sound in the world was the richness and drama of her voice."

Ms. Morrison is also respected for her commitment to teaching. She demonstrated this last Thursday, the day she was told she had won the Nobel Prize, becoming the first black American woman to do so.

"The media outside were all wondering why she was going to class and whether she was still going to teach," said Meredith Unruh, a junior in Professor Morrison's Thursday afternoon seminar section. "She laughed, and thought that it was ridiculous that they would even ask."

With the television cameras and reporters waiting outside, she walked inside to the applause of her students to teach her class.
