Conservative Is Joining G.O.P. Race for Governor

By KEVIN SACK,
Published: October 5, 1993

Herbert London, the New York University professor who ran as the Conservative Party candidate for governor in 1990 and nearly finished second, announced his candidacy today for the 1994 Republican and Conservative gubernatorial nominations.

Mr. London kicked off his campaign at an Albany news conference, where he showed both his conservatism and his glibness. He called for supply-side tax cuts, deep reductions in Medicaid and welfare payments, containment of the "homosexual agenda" and a school system in which children will not "spend more time putting condoms on cucumbers than solving quadratic equations."

He also criticized both Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, a Democrat, and Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, who has largely frozen the search for a Republican candidate by hinting that he may run. Many party leaders assume that the nomination is Mr. D'Amato's for the asking, and Mr. London declined today to say whether he would continue running if Mr. D'Amato gets into the race.

Mr. Cuomo, he said, presides over "the last bastion of socialism." If he were not running for governor, Mr. London said he would support Boris N. Yeltsin because "he has the experience of fighting socialism." Taunting D'Amato

Mr. London taunted Mr. D'Amato for failing to announce whether he intends to run.

"D'Amato has made noises, but keep in mind every time he appears to come to the batter's box, he steps out and says: 'Well not so fast. I'm not sure I'm going to do this,' " Mr. London said. "If you're going to run, then you run."

Mr. London said Mr. D'Amato's musing has "turned off the Republican spigots" and made fund-raising exceedingly difficult. He said he has raised about $100,000 and has spent all but $20,000.

He also accused Mr. D'Amato of posing as a conservative. "It's a little difficult to bring home pork for New York and at the same time say you're a fiscal conservative," Mr. London said. A Professor at N.Y.U.

Mr. London, a 54-year-old Manhattan resident, pursued the Republican nomination in 1990 but was rejected by party leaders, who chose Pierre A. Rinfret as their candidate. Running as a Conservative, Mr. London nearly defeated Mr. Rinfret, receiving 827,614 votes, or 20 percent, to Mr. Rinfret's 865,948 votes, or 21 percent. Mr. Cuomo won re-election to his third four-year term with 53 percent of the vote. Mr. London spoke today of the importance of unifying the Republican and Conservative Parties in 1994.

Born in Brooklyn, Mr. London is currently the John M. Olin Professor of Humanities at New York University. He served until 1992 as dean of the school's Gallatin Division, which is centered around the study of great books. In addition to his gubernatorial race, Mr. London ran briefly for the Republican nomination for mayor of New York City in 1989 before withdrawing.

He is the first Republican to announce his candidacy formally, although a number of others have been raising money and seeking support about the state for several months. They include two former state Republican chairmen, Richard M. Rosenbaum and J. Patrick Barrett; the Assembly minority leader, Clarence D. Rappleyea Jr.; a former Ambassador to France, Evan G. Galbraith, and former Assemblyman Jon S. Fossel. Mr. Cuomo has said he will decide whether to run for re-election by the end of the year.
