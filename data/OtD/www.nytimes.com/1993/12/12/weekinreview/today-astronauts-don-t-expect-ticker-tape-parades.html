Today, Astronauts Don't Expect Ticker-Tape Parades

By CARL H. LAVIN
Published: December 12, 1993

JOHN GLENN'S role as an astronaut of the 1960's could have been played by a chimpanzee with much the same results. But the strong, handsome test pilot soared one place no chimp will every go: into the pantheon of American heros. Ticker tape rained down. The door to the Senate creaked open. Tom Wolfe determined that he possessed "The Right Stuff."

Wendy Lawrence, daughter of a test pilot who trained with the first astronauts, was 2 years old in 1962 when Mr. Glenn first orbited the earth. After the Naval Academy, helicopter flight training, and a master's in oceanography from M.I.T., Lieut. Commdr. Lawrence joined the astronaut corps a year ago.

School children still regard her with awe. She works at her dream job, a job poll takers say rates near the top of the prestige list. But she is not counting on the hero's mantle. She readily admits to some very unheroic self doubts. "At times you get depressed and feel overwhelmed and wonder how in the world am I going to learn everything I'm supposed to know," she said in an interview as fellow astronauts finished their orbital repair job on the Hubble Space Telescope.

Over the last two decades the amazing has become commonplace and the number of astronauts has proliferated so that it's been hard to remember names -- save perhaps those of the Challenger crew. Once astronauts discovered new worlds. Now they pick up and deliver and repair and experiment -- admirable, indeed, but not awesome.

Every other year NASA reviews about 2,000 applications from people who have the paper qualifications -- at least a bachelor's degree in math, engineering, or the sciences and three years of related work experience. Perhaps 100 candidates are invited for interviews, and maybe 15 or 20 are hired. A space agency spokeswoman, Barbara Schwartz, said that most successful applicants have at least a master's, and often a Ph.D. or a medical degree. "They look for people who are team players, who are easy to get along with, who are willing to travel," she said.

That last qualification may seem the ultimate understatement, but NASA requires each astronaut to make a public appearance at least once a month. "Flying in space is really a very small part of what you do," Ms. Schwartz said. (The public speaking continues on business trips: President Clinton and Bryant Gumbel each seem to have the phone numbers of the shuttle fleet.) Prestigious Civil Servants

Recent polls have put astronauts behind only doctors and college presidents on the most admired list. But whatever social niche they fill, the astronauts themselves -- at least the half who are civilians -- still fit into the career classification system used for Federal employees. On the scale of GS-1 to GS-15, newly hired astronauts are rated GS-12, on par with entry-level scientists who test cosmetics for the Food and Drug Administration or set nutritional guidelines for school lunch programs. The GS-12's headed for space range from 28 to 45 years old. The 1993 pay scale: $40,298 to $52,385.

Even the astronauts acknowledge that prestige does not translate into the Superman stature of a generation ago. Capt. Robert L. (Hoot) Gibson, chief of the astronaut office and a veteran of four shuttle flights, was a teen-ager during the Mercury program. Those cold war astronauts, he said, were "these lone warriors carrying forward the cause of freedom."

"Over the last 30 years we've seen an evolution in the ways the astronauts are perceived," he said. "Now they are part of a scientific team, a technological team trying to push back the boundaries of what we know."

One of Tom Wolfe's old American Studies teachers, Marshall W. Fishwick, equates the changing status of astronauts with the new role of cowboys in films like "Unforgiven" or of athletes and politicians caught up in scandals. As the current shuttle mission was unfolding, he notes, an F.B.I. sting operation cast the shadow of a criminal investigation over NASA.

"At the end of the 20th century there is a strong anti-heroic sentiment going through America that is tarnishing each one of those groups," said Professor Fishwick, now a humanities professor at Virginia Polytechnic Institute in Blacksburg. The author of several books about the role of heroes in American culture, he taught Mr. Wolfe at Washington and Lee and at Yale.

Mr. Fishwick said growing concerns about crime has refocused American attitudes. "The whole national purpose is being challenged, and the astronaut no longer seems crucial to our national existence," he said.

Crises form heroes, and the risk of space flight means it will always have a certain aura. "Before you do fly you make sure you put all your affairs in order," Captain Gibson said. He met his wife, Dr. M. Rhea Seddon, in the astronaut corps, and they have three children.

"I would love for them to be astronauts and I'd probably hate it at the same time," he said. "I don't mind flying the thing myself, but when I have to stand and watch my wife fly or watch my friends fly, which is what all the astronauts are, my palms get sweaty and I pray all the way to orbit."
