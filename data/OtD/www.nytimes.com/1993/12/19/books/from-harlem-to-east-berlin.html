From Harlem to East Berlin

By Mel Watkins;
Published: December 19, 1993

WHY I LEFT AMERICA And Other Essays. By Oliver W. Harrington. Edited by M. Thomas Inge. Illustrated. 113 pp. Jackson, Miss.: University Press of Mississippi. $20. DARK LAUGHTER The Satiric Art of Oliver W. Harrington. Edited by M. Thomas Inge. Illustrated. 116 pp. Jackson, Miss.: University Press of Mississippi. $25.

UNTIL recently, black American cartoonists were extremely rare. Before the 1960's, a few labored mostly without mainstream recognition; their work was confined to black magazines, journals and newspapers. Even after strips by Morrie Turner ("Wee Pals") and Brumsic Brandon Jr. ("Luther") were picked up for national syndication in the mid-60's, they and others who were subsequently syndicated were not widely celebrated. Some, like E. Simms Campbell, whose popular panel cartoons appeared in Esquire and Playboy, were not recognized as African-Americans.

Oliver Wendell Harrington is also generally unknown in this country, but his close association with prominent black cultural and political figures gives his career an added significance. Before he created Bootsie, black America's most popular cartoon character in the 1930's, he was a friend of such Harlem Renaissance writers as Arna Bontemps, Rudolph Fisher and Wallace Thurman. Langston Hughes (whose literary character, Jesse B. Semple, resembled Bootsie) wrote that Mr. Harrington was an unsurpassed social satirist and called him America's favorite Negro cartoonist.

Later, Mr. Harrington met Paul Robeson and Adam Clayton Powell Jr.; he became art director at Powell's weekly newspaper, The People's Voice. After World War II, he was recruited by Walter White, head of the N.A.A.C.P., and spoke out against lynchings in the South. During the McCarthy era, Mr. Harrington's criticism of national apathy toward anti-lynching legislation attracted the attention of the Federal Bureau of Investigation. He moved to Paris in 1951. There he met Chester Himes and Richard Wright; they formed the nucleus of the black expatriate group that gathered at the Cafe Tournon. A year after Wright's death in 1960, Mr. Harrington went to East Berlin on a freelance assignment. When the Berlin wall was erected, he stayed in what was then East Germany; as late as 1987, friends thought he had died there.

The essays collected in "Why I Left America" outline Mr. Harrington's career and experiences in the United States and abroad. Written for American publications while he was in Europe, the pieces range from an admiring portrait of Richard Wright's last days in Paris (in which he suggests that the Central Intelligence Agency may have been involved in his death) to reviews of books by such artists as Elton C. Fax and Brumsic Brandon Jr. The book also includes "Look Homeward Baby," a long memoir of Mr. Harrington's formative years in the United States, and "Why I Left America," an address delivered at Wayne State University in 1991, which engages the personal and political reasons for his self-imposed exile. The foreword by Julia Wright, Richard Wright's daughter, provides additional documentation of the friendship her father and Mr. Harrington shared.

"Dark Laughter" -- the title is taken from Mr. Harrington's weekly cartoon panel that was first published in The Amsterdam News in 1935 -- offers a selection of the artist's best work from the 30's to the 80's. It includes many of the earlier Bootsie panels as well as the more openly satiric political cartoons that Mr. Harrington produced for The Daily World and East German periodicals like Eulenspiegel and Das Magazin from the 60's to the 80's. Mr. Harrington, who graduated from Yale University's School of Fine Arts in 1940, is a gifted painter and fine artist. His drawings, unlike those of many cartoonists, often transcend mere caricature even as they convey the impressionistic vigor and ironic thrust demanded by the genre.

Together these books, each edited with an informative introduction by M. Thomas Inge, a professor of the humanities at Randolph-Macon College in Virginia, provide not only an intriguing introduction to an unusual artist but also a rudimentary history of black American cartoonists. As it turns out, the evolution of cartoons or comic strips by black artists parallels the general development of black humor in mainstream culture. Just as stage comics had to transcend the demeaning image of the minstrel stage Negro, black cartoonists had to overcome grotesque stereotypes, established by white illustrators, that harked back to minstrel posters and "coon song" sheet music illustrations -- "a circle, black with two hot dogs in the middle for a mouth," Mr. Harrington recalls. Even after the stereotypes were eradicated, the work of black cartoonists was seldom acknowledged or published in mainstream outlets until the 60's and the escalation of the civil rights movement. A few survived the neglect, however, and Mr. Harrington was among them. As his essays and cartoons demonstrate, much of his life and work was shaped by outrage at the way he and other blacks were treated. In fact, it was anger and a desire to get back at a racist teacher that first sparked his interest in cartoons. But by funneling his rage into art, Mr. Harrington, like many humorists before him, reaffirmed comedy's ability to transform anger into laughter and, perhaps, even open a path toward reconciliation.
