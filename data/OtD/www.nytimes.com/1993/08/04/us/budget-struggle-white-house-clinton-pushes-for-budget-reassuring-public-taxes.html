THE BUDGET STRUGGLE: The White House; CLINTON PUSHES FOR BUDGET, REASSURING PUBLIC ON TAXES AND VOWING ECONOMIC GAINS

By GWEN IFILL,
Published: August 4, 1993

Declaring that "it would be irresponsible not to take decisive action" to reduce the budget deficit, President Clinton appealed to middle-class taxpayers tonight to support the economic plan that will face crucial votes in Congress this week.

"We're on the verge of breaking out of the old false choice between tax and spend and trickle down, between abandonment and entitlement," Mr. Clinton said. He brandished colored bar graphs to support his insistence that the plan is fair and includes genuine cuts in Federal spending. [ Transcript of the President's address, page A14. ]

The President, going over the heads of a reluctant Congress and appealing directly to the people, repeatedly emphasized that the burden of taxes in the budget would fall on the wealthy. Congressional negotiators reached agreement on the budget plan on Monday, but its fate is uncertain as it goes to a vote in both the House and the Senate.

The Republicans immediately struck back. Senator Bob Dole of Kansas, the minority leader, appeared on television after the President's speech and criticized the budget compromise. [ Excerpts, page A14. ] Fighting Hoarseness

Mr. Clinton, occasionally fighting back hoarseness, appeared confident and optimistic during the 19-minute address to the nation, casting himself as a lone soldier fighting Washington gridlock to solve a problem he inherited when he took office. And he said there would be dire consequences if the plan did not pass.

"Now there are only two choices," he said. "Our plan or no plan."

Seated behind his desk in the Oval Office, Mr. Clinton portrayed his plan in historic terms, saying it had been decades since a President took action to break the economy out if its stagnation. He said he intended to "revive the American dream by restoring the American economy."

"No more something for nothing," Mr. Clinton said. "We're all in this together."

Mr. Clinton said he would sign two executive orders on Wednesday: one would require that all new taxes included in the package go toward reducing the deficit; the other would put controls on annual spending in Federal entitlement programs, something demanded by Democratic moderates who wanted deeper spending cuts.

This week's budget battle was only part of an agenda of change, he said. Holding a chart showing how his plan would gradually reduce the Federal budget deficit to about $202 billion a year from $285 billion this year, he said the welfare and health care systems must also be overhauled.

He vowed to end welfare "as we know it," and asserted, "If you want the deficit to go down to zero, as I think most of you do, we have got to challenge the health care system."

Senator Dole took aim at Mr. Clinton's use of statistics and portrayed the budget plan as emphasizing new taxes rather than spending cuts. Natural growth in the American economy would create more jobs over the next four years than the President promised through Congressional passage of the budget, he said.

Mr. Dole also contended that the Federal deficit would skyrocket in the three years after 1997.

The President, whose prime-time address was broadcast live by the networks, outlined a plan developed by his advisers to improve his political standing by emphasizing the battles to come.

Aware that the struggle to get the budget bill passed may leave him looking battered, at least to some voters, the President and his aides sought today to trumpet impending victory loudly enough to drown out naysayers.

As he prepared for this week's decisive House and Senate votes on the compromise, Mr. Clinton spent the day polishing and rehearsing his speech. He consulted with a small army of advisers, including Treasury Secretary Lloyd Bentsen; Leon E. Panetta, the President's budget director, and Robert E. Rubin, director of the National Economic Council.

Mr. Clinton also met with Gov. David Walters of Oklahoma, presumably in the hope of swinging the vote of Senator David L. Boren, a disaffected Democrat, and with reporters from Nevada, the home of Senator Richard H. Bryan, a Democrat who voted against the budget when it was before the Senate. Themes to Promote

In every venue, Mr. Clinton pounded home what he has settled on as the most persuasive elements of his plan: that it is fair and that it does a responsible job of reducing the deficit by a total of $496 billion over five years.

But neither in person nor on the telephone did the President appear to be playing bare-knuckled politics. Few, if any, threats flowed from the White House. Indeed, some undecided lawmakers privately expressed astonishment that Mr. Clinton had stopped short of bluntly demanding their votes.

Mr. Clinton's mood today was upbeat, in part because the rest of the day's events had helped him considerably. The Senate, in a spate of pre-recess activity, approved Mr. Clinton's nominations of Ruth Bader Ginsburg to the Supreme Court and Sheldon Hackney to head the National Endowment for the Humanities as well as passing his national service program. In addition, the Senate Judiciary Committee approved his nomination of Judge Louis B. Freeh to head the Federal Bureau of Investigation.
