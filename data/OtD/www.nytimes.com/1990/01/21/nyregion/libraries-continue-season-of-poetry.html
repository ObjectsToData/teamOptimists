Libraries Continue Season of Poetry


Published: January 21, 1990

THE Poet's Voice, launched its 12th season with the first of six readings in November at the Ferguson Library in Stamford. Five more readings are scheduled to take place at other Fairfield County libraries this year.

Robert creeley, who first reached national recognition with his book ''For Love: Poems 1950-60'' published by Scribner in 1962, will read on Feb. 4 at the Fairfield Library. Mr. Creeley, currently Samuel Capen Professor of Humanities at the State University of New York at Buffalo, has been associated with writers of the beat generation like Allen Ginsberg and Jack Kerouac and is known for poetry that probes personal emotions. ''I write to realize the world as one has come to live in it, thus to give testament. I write to move in worlds, a human delight. I write when no other act is possible,'' he said.

The series, co-sponsored by the Manacher Fund and six participating Fairfield Libraries, was initiated in 1978 by Zelda Manacher with the establishment of the Horace E. Manacher Poetry Fund in memory of her husband.

Horace Manacher was a Greenwich resident who loved poetry. ''My husband was in the fuel oil business, which is about as far away from poetry as you can get,'' Mrs. Manacher said. She described him as a man who could be impatient with a novel, but could not put down a book of poetry. ''He felt in writing a novel thoughts could be disguised, but when you wrote in the more succinct form of poetry you exposed all of your feelings,'' she said.

When they lived in New York, she remembers attending poetry readings at the Donnell Library, when Marianne Moore and Stephen Spender might be the readers. After moving to Greenwich they were drawn to poetry readings sponsored by the Connecticut Poetry Circuit, which originated at Wesleyan University. During the 70's, they brought poets to various libraries. The poets who visited Greenwich were always invited to stay with the couple, because they wanted to introduce poets and poetry to thei children and encourage their children's interest in literature.

While the Connecticut Poetry Circuit covered public and academic circuits, the idea behind the Poet's Voice was to have a cooperative effort of local public libraries that would present poets whose work would appeal to public library audiences.

Today the six participating libraries in the Poet's Voice - Greenwich, Ferguson, Fairfield, New Canaan, Darien and Westport - are represented by one librarian each, plus three consultants, and form the Poet's Voice Committee. Each year they meet to make the annual selection of poets. ''One of the unbending rules is that the authors be published,'' Barbara Rosett, this year's coordinator said. ''We try to include younger as well as established poets.''

The poets are paid an honorarium of $300, half of which is paid by the libraries and half by the Manacher Fund. Each library is visited once during the season. Readings take place at 3 P.M., last about an hour and are followed by discussion with the audience.

On Feb. 25, Frederick Morgan, editor of the Hudson REview and author of five poetry collections, will read at the Greenwich Library. His poems share joys and sorrows, opinions and attitudes.

New Canaan

Jane Cooper, formerly a professor and poet in residence at Sarah Lawrence College who now teaches in the graduate writing division of the Columbia School of Arts, will read at the New Canaan Library on March 25. Her book ''Scaffolding: New and Selected Poems,'' won the first Maurice English Poetry Award in 1985. She is the recipient of grants and fellowships from the Guggenheim Foundation, the Ingram Merrill Foundation and the National Endowment for the Arts.

Donald Justice, who won the Pulitzer Prize for his book ''Selected Poems'' and is knows as a poet's poet, will read at the Westport Library on April 22.

The program concludes for the year on April 29 at the Darien Library when the reader is Sandra M. Gilbert, a professor of English at Princeton University.

Of the program's ability to draw people, Mrs. Manacher commented. ''One of the great things is that it brought poets out of the closet. When we started out we had maybe 10 or 12 people, including writers and readers of poetry. Now we have a full house. In the beginning a small group met upstairs in the library's gallery. Then we had to move to our auditorium.''

Last year when Joseph Brodsky, winner of the 1987 Nobel Prize for literature spoke at the Greenwich Library, which seats 365 she said, ''We had to turn people away.''
