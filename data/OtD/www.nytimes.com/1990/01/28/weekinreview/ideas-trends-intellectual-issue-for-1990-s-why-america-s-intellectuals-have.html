IDEAS & TRENDS; The Intellectual Issue For the 1990's Is Why America's Intellectuals Have Faded Away

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: January 28, 1990

IT has become commonplace to lament the passing of the day when thinkers like James B. Conant and Lewis Mumford played as influential a role in politics as today's media advisers. Even to those who consider themselves among the last holdouts, the question is no longer whether intellectuals are in hiding, but why.

''One would have to go back to the 1920's, to the Coolidge and Harding administrations, to find a time when intellectuals were as absent from public life as they are today,'' said Samuel Lipman, an essayist and publisher of The New Criterion, a journal that nurtures the thoughts of neo-conservatives. Mr. Lipman was the moderator of a panel at the 92d Street Y in Manhattan Monday night to discuss the status of ''public intellectuals.''

The panelists - two for the right and two for the left - could agree on only two things: First, that there really are such creatures, who successfully project their powers of reflection beyond their own fields to the moral and social questions of the day. And second, that the species is indeed endangered.

Peter Brooks, director of the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale, seemed to speak for all those on the platform when he said that the status of the intellectual today is one of ''divorce'' from the public and the government. He observed that intellectuals and artists like Jean-Paul Sartre, Albert Camus and Bertolt Brecht played an important role in postwar European political life, and that American writers for such ''little magazines'' as the Partisan Review made themselves heard in the corridors of power at about the same time. But he said there are no such prominent social thinkers today.

The reasons given for the change seem to vary with political persuasion. The historian Russell Jacoby, who lamented the death of the contemplative class in his 1987 book, ''The Last Intellectuals,'' was worried most about the disappearance of influential leftist thinkers. In the book, he attributed the decline to ''professionalization.'' It is next to impossible for anyone to make a living today writing reviews and essays as did Edmund Wilson and Lewis Mumford a generation ago, he said in a recent interview. So intellectuals have forsaken the public and found a home in the university. The trouble, he said, is that academia requires intellectuals ''to speak in academic jargon rather than a language anyone can understand.''

'Absolutely Lethal' Ideas

On the other hand, conservatives like Norman Podhoretz, editor of Commentary; Nathan Glazer, the Harvard sociologist, and Hilton Kramer, editor of The New Criterion, maintain that most intellectuals have lost their audience precisely because they cling to worn-out leftist dogma.

At the conference, Mr. Kramer attacked Marxist and left-wing intellectuals who, he said, have promoted ''ideas that proved to be absolutely lethal.'' As the world witnesses the collapse of the Communist movement, Mr. Kramer declared, ''this is an important moment for us to look back to see what public intellectuals and artists have been telling us.''

He cited the critics Dwight MacDonald, Mary McCarthy, Alfred Kazin, Wilson and Mumford, and artists like Leonard Bernstein, Allen Ginsberg and Norman Mailer, for having exemplified the idea that ''to be an intellectual was to be a socialist, and to be an American intellectual was to be anti-American.'' He accused Mr. Mailer in his essay ''The White Negro'' of propounding ''an existential justification for young hoodlums beating up shopkeepers,'' and said Mr. Ginsberg was guilty of having ''celebrated the drug culture.''

Mr. Podhoretz expanded on Mr. Kramer's list, including John Kenneth Galbraith, Susan Sontag and Mary McCarthy for having taken, as he saw it, a benign view of the Communist world. ''If Peter Brooks was right in saying we have no public intellectuals in this country,'' Mr. Podhoretz remarked, ''we would be very lucky indeed!''

None of those mentioned were present to defend themselves, and the liberals on the platform said they had decided not to respond to personal attacks; but Mr. Jacoby, interviewed by telephone at his home in Marina del Rey, Calif., rose to the challenge. ''Very few in the New Left were apologists for Stalinism,'' he said. ''It's hard to saddle American intellectuals with East Europe.''

Furthermore, he said, ''Even if you have individuals who were apologists for Stalin -like Lillian Hellman may have been at one time - does that mean their whole work is worthless? That's no different than saying that the work of T. S. Eliot and Ezra Pound is worthless because they were anti-Semites.''

Arthur M. Schlesinger, the City University historian who came to prominence during the Kennedy Administration, disagreed with the assertion that academia has silenced intellectuals. ''I don't see the university as being necessarily deforming,'' he said in an interview. ''It didn't affect Rexford Tugwell or Adolf Berle,'' he said, referring to two members of Roosevelt's ''brain trust.''

At the conference, Richard Sennett, a professor of humanities at New York University, linked the disappearance of public intellectuals to the decline of cities. Referring to his 1977 book, ''The Fall of Public Man,'' which asserts that the loss of 18th and 19th century values led to the impoverishment of public life, he said, ''In Balzac's Paris, people saw the city as a place of opportunity, whereas in Tom Wolfe's 'The Bonfire of the Vanities,' New York is a place where people get hurt.'' Public intellectuals require civility in order to flourish, he said.

Mr. Lipman said he hopes intellectuals will make a comeback. ''They move history,'' he said. But he wondered if their return might be prevented by a public that now questions ''the illicit transfer of competence earned in one field to another in which it has not been earned.''

Mr. Schlesinger was more optimistic. ''The tide is turning,'' he said. ''It's cyclical.''

''Intellectuals were visible during the Progressive Era, again in the New Deal, again in the Kennedy years, and, if the cyclical rhythm holds, the 1990's should be a time like the 1930's,'' he said. ''There's nothing mystical about it. Thirty years is a generation, and when you have a Roosevelt growing up in the Progressive Era and being stimulated by the ideas he hears, he then returns to those people and ideas when he comes into power a generation later.''

''Furthermore,'' Mr. Schlesinger said, ''we need ideas - people are starved for them -because we have so many problems.''
