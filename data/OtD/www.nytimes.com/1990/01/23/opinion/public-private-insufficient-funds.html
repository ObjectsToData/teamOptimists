PUBLIC & PRIVATE; Insufficient Funds

By Anna Quindlen
Published: January 23, 1990

The building that was once B. Altman stands on Fifth Avenue, solid as a rock, pathetic as an abandoned house. Two women mount the steps and peer inside the glass doors. ''I was on their bridal registry,'' said one sadly.

There are plenty of lessons to be learned when a business fails, lessons about retailing and financing and cash flow. But what shoppers think when they pass the lifeless hulk of what was once one of New York's grand department stores may be something different, something less cerebral, more emotional.

The sluggishness of the real estate market may illustrate something about interest rates, and the transformation of Bloomingdale's from Trend Central to Chapter 11 is a story of crushing corporate debt. But to those of us who come closest to reorganization when we consolidate our debt on our Visa cards, these things speak to a vulnerability that has been with us for some time. This is a land of diminishing economic expectation. The ground is not solid here. In other parts of the country this has been manifest for years, and not just when the family farms have failed or the oil boom in Texas went bust. In most American small cities and large towns, there was a store like Altman's, the anchor to the downtown, the center of commerce on Main Street. If your father made enough money, you had lunch in the tea room and bought a winter coat with a velvet collar and matching hat there. If you were poor, you dreamed of doing those things.

What you didn't dream was that some day you would come through town and see that big square building boarded up. It often had more to do with the ascendancy of the malls and the suburbs than the national economy, and yet it seemed to the people who lived in those towns as though a part of a prosperous past was gone, the part that believed in passbook savings accounts.

There is, among economists, an equation that determines when a recession exists: two back-to-back quarters of negative growth in the gross national product. One group of economists even has a committee that will sometimes meet for the sole purpose of declaring a recession.

A new New York Times/CBS News Poll shows that 36 percent of the respondents said the economy was getting worse, 11 percentage points more than a little over a year ago. Oddly enough, there's a big difference in pessimism between men and women; 43 percent of the women said things had gone downhill.

There could be several reasons for that. There's the ''last hired, first fired'' dictum which makes women especially vulnerable to layoffs. The fact that, on average, women workers are still making 70 cents for every dollar a man makes means they also feel inflation more keenly.

But perhaps it is also that women experience the economy on what you might call a reality level. We buy bread. A recession committee meets nearly every week in my neighborhood supermarket, and this is how it works. Someone puts everything on the black rubber treadmill, and when it's all toted up the bill is $20 higher than it was a year ago. At that point, a recession is declared, at least in the mind of one person.

Unscientific, but never forget that science has to share shelf space in all our minds with humanities. Much of our approach to life is anecdotal. When a pretty house down the block wears a sale sign for eight months, and when we hear that it finally sold for $30,000 less than the asking price, we feel, not that our neighbor has a problem, or our neighborhood, but that the economy does.

When he was running for President, Ronald Reagan asked Americans whether they were better off than they were four years before. That's not the question I would ask today. Ask 30-year-olds whether they are better off than their parents were at that age. For that small slice known as the upper middle class, the ones with second homes and interesting portfolios, the answer is probably yes.

But for all those young men and women who thought that $25,000 a year would buy a house and now know that it will barely buy a car, the answer is clearly no. That is based, not on economic formula, but on the way we feel about our lives. The people who stop for a minute to look at the facade of B. Altman, the people who still experience a twinge when they drive down Main Street and see the department store where their first suit was purchased now a shell, do not necessarily know the numbers. They know the feeling.
