EDUCATION; About Education

By Fred M. Hechinger
Published: September 26, 1990

How do children decide what is right and what is wrong? This was a key question in a recent survey of 5,000 children in grades 4 through 12. The answer, the survey found, is that most of them decide by listening to one another and watching what goes on around them. It also found that most adult authorities, including religious leaders, appear to have lost their power and appeal.

For example, even though 82 percent of the young people believe that there is a God and nearly 40 percent say daily prayers, only 3 percent said they would seek out a member of the clergy for help with a moral problem. And while one-third believe that their teachers care about them, only 7 percent said they would turn to them for advice in a matter of personal values.

These findings were the first results of a continuing survey directed by Dr. Robert Coles, a professor of psychiatry and medical humanities at Harvard and the author of ''Children in Crisis.'' He was aided by Louis Genevie, a former assistant professor of psychiatry at Albert Einstein College of Medicine in the Bronx. An interim report on the survey, which is sponsored by the Girl Scouts of America, the Lilly Endowment and the Mott Foundation, was published in Teacher magazine in March.

Cheating in school is a natural first test of children's sense of right and wrong. Twenty-one percent of elementary school youngsters said they would try to copy answers or glance at another student's test. In high school, 65 percent said they would.

One reason for such moral shortcuts is the pressure to succeed. And one conclusion, Dr. Coles wrote, is that ''some children merely reflect the values of their society: the notion of 'what works' is 'what works for me.' ''

''Given their membership in a highly competitive, SAT-conscious culture,'' Dr. Coles wrote, ''some children can very easily entertain the notion of cheating.

''Sadly, as so-called 'cultural literacy' grows, what could be called 'moral literacy' declines,'' he said.

Jeff Meade, a senior editor of Teacher magazine, wrote in an introduction to the Cole report: ''Parents - along with some of our more celebrated white-collar thieves - are often the unknowing font of situational ethics.''

Not unlike adults, Mr. Meade added, children at times learn to do the right things for the wrong reason. He wrote, ''They are going to see that Ivan Boesky got caught, so you could get caught.'' Hardly a moral point of view. Dr. Coles wrote: ''A lot of kids have been brought up not to be anxious or to ever feel guilty. Shame, after all, is a moral position, and some of these kids have no language to express this. We find it personally very worrisome that almost 60 percent of the children in our survey rely on moral standards that have, as their main purpose, self-gratification.''

He added with regret that ''even at Harvard, we see a lot of kids who are bright, but whose conscience is not all that muscular.''

All this recalls a warning by Dr. Karl Menninger, the noted psychiatrist who died last July. In his 1973 book, ''Whatever Became of Sin?,'' he raised the same questions of conscience, of telling wrong from right without hiding behind such cover words as the currently fashionable ''inappropriate'' and ''appropriate.''

Dr. Menninger wrote that doing the wrong thing - cheating, stealing, shoplifting, profiteering - provided the wrongdoer with a sense of winning the game. With the excuse, ''everybody is doing it,'' he continued, normal guilt feelings are readily stifled. The question the transgressors ask, he wrote, are: ''Can everyone be wrong about what's right? It's a new deal. How can it be a sin? Need anyone feel guilty?''

He then explained the impact on children: ''Pressures upon individual integrity in the face of group attitudes of this kind are particularly burdensome for children. Studies of the family by Urie Bronfenbrenner at Cornell University have tended to show that the average child of 10 in the United States has already developed a noncondemning attitude toward cheating. He is, in fact, taught by his surroundings that it is unrealistic to maintain standards of honesty that are ridiculed by his friends and ignored by his elders. Exemplary characters, which are the most powerful influence in education, are too weak to offset the evidence of his daily experience of how other people 'get by.' ''

In other words, who are the role models? Mayor Marion Barry of Washington? Michael Milken of Wall Street? Ben Johnson, the disqualified Olympic sprint champion? Defrauders of savings and loan institutions? Are their penalties equal to their transgressions?

Neither Dr. Menninger nor Dr. Coles views these dismal trends as irreversible. Dr. Menninger argued that relevant penalties, not vengeful punishment, can teach a lesson. Dr. Coles believes teachers can make a difference if they ''explain vigorously what we expect of our students and why.''
