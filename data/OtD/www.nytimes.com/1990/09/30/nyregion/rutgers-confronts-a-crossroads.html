Rutgers, Confronts A Crossroads

By PRISCILLA VAN TASSEL
Published: September 30, 1990

ACROSS from Old Queens here, workers are completing a $9.6 million renovation of Winants Hall, a stately 100-year-old building, in preparation for its dedication in November as a new administrative center. With the building refurbished inside and out and with the lawn freshly sodded, Winants is yet another example of Rutgers's decade-long drive to transform itself from a tired state university into a first-class institution of higher education and research.

But Winants Hall is also a symbol of frustration. Faced with a reduction of $26 million, or nearly 11 percent, in state aid this year, the university has had to take a number of economies, including postponing the use of its new administrative center until next year to save several hundred thousand dollars in operating expenses.

The budget cut, following a $7.9 million reduction in state aid last year, has been felt by students and faculty alike. Tuition has risen 10.8 percent, to $2,856, for full-time undergraduates; lightweight football and women's lacrosse have been dropped as intercollegiate sports, and the student health service is now closed between midnight and 8 A.M. There is a greater reliance on part-time instructors, and the full-time faculty members, including administrators, are carrying heavier teaching loads.

While Rutgers officials say the quality of instruction has so far not been affected, there are concerns that the momentum toward academic excellence will be undermined if budget cutbacks continue. Like other New Jersey state colleges and departments of state government, Rutgers has been told to plan for a further 8 percent reduction in state aid next year.

''Everybody's worried, and you need to be,'' said Dr. Francis L. Lawrence, who starts work tomorrow as Rutgers's 18th president. ''I'm concerned about maintaining faculty salaries and raising tuition. It's not just our reputation, but I'm concerned for New Jersey because this is the source of future leaders for the state.''

Whether Dr. Lawrence's leadership will be substantially different from that of his predecessor, the late Edward Bloustein, remains to be seen, but he is expected to focus much of his attention on the Rutgers undergraduate.

''What I'd like Rutgers to be is a complete paradigm - research, teaching, learning and public service,'' Dr. Lawrence said. ''We're all going to have to focus more on undergraduate instruction. In a sense, it's the highest pinnacle to which we should aspire.''

Academic deans at Rutgers have been told to make classroom instruction their highest priority, an action that, while applauded by most in the university community, may take its toll on the quality of the future faculty. ''It has been quite serious,'' said Dr. Richard McCormick, dean of the faculty of arts and sciences on the university's New Brunswick campus. ''The pain has been inflicted on research and the ability to attract new people to the faculty. I've warned all the department chairs that they may begin searches for faculty, but there's no assurance that they can consummate those searches with actual appointments.''

The financial difficulties at Rutgers came at a time when the institution seemed to be making significant strides in gaining the respect it so covets. Dr. McCormick said that the departments of political science, chemistry, psychology and computer science were on the brink of achieving the national prominence already enjoyed by its departments of English, history, philosophy, physics and astronomy, and mathematics, and that many others had well-developed plans in place to make the leap.

''This is the shame of it all,'' he said. ''There are a number of departments that are in that position. They have every legitimate academic reason to make that jump in the early 90's, but the dollars aren't there.''

The budget cuts have also created a $50 million backlog of maintenance projects, as money that had been earmarked for repairs has had to be applied to the library and to preserving classes.

In the wake of the nearly completed $600 million capital construction program, the deferred maintenance strikes an odd contrast to the many modern buildings that have appeared since the construction program began in 1986. Next to the glistening new fiber optics center in New Brunswick stands an engineering complex with doors that do not shut and a ventilation system badly in need of repair. Nearby, on the top floor of the Rieman chemistry laboratory, a pan collects water from a leaky roof.

While most state universities are facing budget problems, the situation at Rutgers seems particularly acute because of its relatively recent resolve to join the top ranks of state research universities.

''There is a delicacy about our new identity,'' said Susan Cole, vice president of administration and personnel. ''People would be perhaps less concerned about it if we had been here longer. Rutgers is such a young university to begin with, and its current identity is even younger.''

Compounding the sense of uncertainty on campus is the arrival of Dr. Lawrence, formerly academic vice president and provost of Tulane University in New Orleans, who has his own vision of what a state university, and Rutgers in particular, should be. It was under Dr. Bloustein, who served as president from 1971 until his death from a heart attack last December, that Rutgers began its effort to become a premier research center. New centers in medicine, biotechnology, fiber optics and other areas of research were developed, and the university was invited in 1989 to join the Association of American Universities, a group of leading research universities in the United States and Canada.
