CONFRONTATION IN THE GULF; To Endangered List in Gulf, Add Archeology

By JOHN NOBLE WILFORD
Published: September 16, 1990

Saddam Hussein, successor to the rulers of ancient Mesopotamia, the likes of Gilgamesh and Hammurabi, has clouded the prospects for explorations into the civilizations that produced the first writing and earliest experiments in agriculture and urban living.

Although generals and diplomats probably give little thought to archeology in the aftermath of the Iraqi president's invasion of Kuwait, scholars see troubling implications for research in the valley of the Tigris and Euphrates rivers in Iraq, the place where historical time began.

Out of the mud-brick ruins of temples and ziggurats have emerged over the last century the traces of cities whose names evoke the rise of human civilization: Babylon and Kish, Nimrud and Nippur, Ur and Uruk.

Work Suspended for Summer

No Western archeologists are known to be stranded after Iraq's seizure of Kuwait on Aug. 2. Work at all of the archeological sites had been suspended, as usual, during the hot summer season, until November or December.

But leaders of major research projects feared they would not see their dig sites again this year or for the foreseeable future and expressed concern that an outbreak of war could jeopardize priceless remains of the Sumerian, Babylonian and Akkadian cultures, which extend back 5,000 years.

They are especially worried because some Iraqi military installations, likely targets of any American attack, are believed to be near important archeological sites. And they feared that the Persian Gulf crisis would foment unrest that could disrupt archeological research in Jordan, Syria and elsewhere in the Middle East.

Files Are Now Inaccessible

Dr. Theresa Howard-Carter, an authority on Kuwaiti antiquities for the University Museum at the University of Pennsylvania, said she feared the widespread looting in Kuwait City would destroy the new museum that she had been helping develop there.

As for herself, Dr. Howard-Carter said: ''I don't know what to do. I have 18 years of research notes and materials on archeology of the Gulf states, four huge file cases over there, and I can't get at them.''

She called Mesopotamia ''the core discipline for anything you want to do in Middle East scholarship.''

Dr. Elizabeth C. Stone of the State University of New York at Stony Brook said Iraq was the ''centerpiece in archeology, with tremendously rich sites and the kinds of results that are so exciting.''

Dr. Stone had reason to be frustrated because she and colleagues had recently discovered the ''lost city'' of Mashkan-shapir and had just set up a research base there. She also worried about the effect a long delay would have on graduate students who had counted on using the excavations for their dissertations.

Pessimism on Future Work

Dr. Donald Hansen, an archeologist with the New York University Institute of Fine Arts and leader of excavations in Iraq at the ancient Mesopotamian city of Lagash, said, ''Heaven knows when we will be going back.''

Dr. Hansen had just resumed field work this year, along with experts from the Metropolitan Museum of Art, after the interruption of the Iran-Iraq war, which lasted from 1980 to 1988.

American archeologists said they doubted that they or their European colleagues would be given permission to work in Iraq any time soon. Even if they were permitted, they said, it would be foolhardy to do research there until tensions are relaxed.

Thus, all excavations would remain at a standstill, and perhaps the only archeological work to continue would be the restoration of ancient Babylon, an Iraqi project.

Archeologists said they were not particularly worried that a suspension of operations for a year or two would cause serious damage to their excavation sites, which are protected by guards and caretakers. But Dr. Stone said that military maneuvers in the Iran-Iraq war were occasionally conducted dangerously close to some 4,000-year-old ruins. ''We came back one season to find lots of trenches and mortar shells nearby,'' she recalled.

Temple of Healing in Danger

If Iraq is attacked by Western forces, archeologists said, bombs and missile could threaten several important ruins that they are situated near military sites, including places where chemical weapons are manufactured or stored. One of these, the modern city of Mosul, across the Tigrus from ancient Nineveh and close to Nimrud.

Modern Hilla is next to ancient Babylon. Baghdad itself has several archeological sites and a museum filled with Sumerian and Babylonian treasures. The ancient religious center of Nippur, where the University of Chicago's Oriental Institute has been excavating the temple of Gula, the Babylonian goddess of healing, is on the Euphrates 60 miles southwest of Baghdad.

''A bomb falling on the wrong place would wipe out irreplaceable artifacts,'' said an archeologist who has worked in Iraq but spoke on condition of anonymity. Most archeologists were cautious in their remarks to avoid antagonizing Iraqi officials who can grant or reject requests for permits to dig there.
