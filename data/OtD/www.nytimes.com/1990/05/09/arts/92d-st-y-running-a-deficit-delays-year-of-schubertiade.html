92d St. Y, Running a Deficit, Delays Year of Schubertiade

By ALLAN KOZINN
Published: May 9, 1990

After a decade of growth, the 92d Street Y - the adventurous educational and cultural center on Lexington Avenue - is trimming its programs in an effort to reduce a projected 1991 deficit of $1.5 million.

The first and most sizable target of the cutback is the Y's critically praised Schubertiade, the annual series that was started in 1987 with the goal of presenting Schubert's complete works chronologically over 10 years, with related symposiums and master classes. It was originally scheduled to end on the bicentennial of Schubert's birth, in 1997.

'A Model of a New Way'

''I consider this project to be extraordinarily innovative,'' said Omus Hirshbein, the director of the Y's arts and humanities division. ''It has made an impact far beyond what I had expected.It is being used by institutions around the country as a model of a new way to present and produce cultural programming.''

The cutbacks in the other departments have to be decided on by early June. But because musical presentations must be arranged long in advance, the decision to shelve the Schubertiade was made early.

''Cultural programs are the highest priority for us as an institution,'' said Sol Adler, the 92d Street Y's executive director. ''But our programs have grown enormously over the last 10 years. We have, for example, made more grants to dancers and performers to try out new works. And we have started programs for people with Alzheimer's disease, and for learning-disabled children.''

Mr. Adler said that in addition to the growing costs of the Y's programs, the cost of maintaining the building had increased. And in March, the Y learned that it faced an increase of $500,000 in its health insurance costs.

A Shrinking Endowment

In recent years, the Y had been drawing on the principal of a $20 million endowment to meet its growing expenses. Now the endowment has been reduced to $17 million, and the board has decided that further reductions would be unacceptable. Instead, Mr. Adler asked the directors of the Y's departments to trim their budgets to eliminate deficits. The Y has also just started a five-year $40 million fund-raising drive.

The performing arts department is the most prestigious of the Y's programs, and it has the largest individual share of the institution's annual $17 million budget. Gerald Grossman, the chairman of the Y's performing arts committee, said that the department's basic budget was about $3 million and that income from ticket sales was about $1 million.

The Schubertiade costs about $300,000 a year, and because the first three seasons have focused on the composer's earliest and least popular works, ticket sales have averaged only 60 percent of capacity. Last year, ticket income from the series was $65,000, leaving a deficit of $225,000. When the performing arts department was asked to cut $250,000 from its 1991 budget, the Schubertiade was a logical choice.

Schubertiade or the Orchestra

Mr. Hirshbein said that an alternative would have been to reduce the season of the Y's orchestra, the New York Chamber Symphony, to present at least a curtailed version of the Schubertiade. ''But an orchestra is an organism,'' he said. ''If you start tinkering with it, you're tinkering with the musicians' livelihoods.''

''What concerns me about postponing the Schubertiade,'' he added, ''is that by taking a year off, we will lose some of the momentum that the series has established. It will not destroy us. But we work in small world of artists and managers, and disappointment sits better with some than with others. And as a small institution, our credibility is built on our reputation for doing what we say we're going to do.''

Some at the Y are also upset that Mr. Adler rejected a proposal by one of the board members, who offered to cover up to $100,000 of the Schubertiade's expenses if the financing could not be raised elsewhere.

''We rejected that proposal,'' Mr. Adler said, ''because at this point we cannot be certain what the final cost of the Schubertiade will be, and therefore could not accurately say how much of the budget the offer would have covered.''

Judith O. Rubin, the chairman of the Y's board and an opponent of the postponment, said: ''The 92d Street Y does many things well, But I believe that it is the arts and humanities programs that have put this place on the map, and turned it into a major institution rather than just a neighborhood community center. These kinds of programs don't grow overnight. And the moment they are lost, the institution becomes more ordinary, and less a place that a large segment of the public is likely to seek out.''
