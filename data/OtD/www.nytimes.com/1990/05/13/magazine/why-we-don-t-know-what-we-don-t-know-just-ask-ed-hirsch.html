WHY WE DON'T KNOW WHAT WE DON'T KNOW JUST ASK E.D. HIRSCH

By Christopher Hitchens: Christopher Hitchens is a columnist for Harper's Magazine and The Nation. His new book, ''Blood, Class and Nostalgia: Anglo-American Ironies,'' will be published next month by Farrar, Straus & Giroux.
Published: May 13, 1990

CHARLOTTESVILLE, VA., IS AN IDEAL SITE FORJU the study of American rhythms and continuities. On the famous lawn of the University of Virginia is the cloister where Edgar Allan Poe had his quarters - commemorated today by a plaque reading domus parva magni poetae. On a hill 10 miles away is Monticello, the exquisite home that Thomas Jefferson built for himself, with its slave quarters still visible (even more limited in space and comfort than Poe's), and another touching memorial, a stone obelisk at Jefferson's grave recalling his role as author of the Declaration of Independence and the Virginia Statute on Religious Liberty, and as founder of the university. It modestly and perhaps ironically omits his Presidency from the list of accomplishments.

Yet according to a nationwide survey, mandated by Congress and carried out in 1988 by the National Assessment of Educational Progress, the chances of a 17-year-old American being able to say anything meaningful about Thomas Jefferson are disconcertingly slight. The chances of the same student knowing anything significant about Poe, or slavery, or of being able to translate the most elementary Latin (''a small home for a great poet''), or even being able to define the word ''ironic'' are slighter still. But, from this Virginian retreat, a self-proclaimed crusade against noneducation is getting under way. In a ridiculous half-timbered pseudo-English building in Charlottesville, the Cultural Literacy Foundation, and its president, E. D. Hirsch Jr., monitor the spread of ignorance and illiteracy in the United States and make proposals for remedying it.

Hirsch, the Linden Kent Professor of English at the University of Virginia, has achieved best-sellerdom with his books ''Cultural Literacy: What Every American Needs to Know'' and ''The Dictionary of Cultural Literacy'' and is on his way to even greater sales with ''A First Dictionary of Cultural Literacy.'' All three have been praised and mocked for trying to summarize essential knowledge in indexes or digests that run from Aaron, Henry, through agribusiness, Clemens, Samuel L., and homeostasis all the way through to noblesse oblige, yuppies and Zapata. Yet Hirsch, who has just completed a cross-country book tour, shrugs off those who laugh at his pedagogic efforts. ''At least,'' he says, ''we are trying to establish a core curriculum.'' ''Core curriculum'' is the key phrase at the foundation -the goal toward which everything is pointed. Unless and until American schools establish a common body of shared and imbibed knowledge, Hirsch argues, there will continue to be chaos and decline in the classroom. And not just in the classroom but further up. A survey by the National Endowment for the Humanities last year found that more than half of the college seniors taking a history and literature test were flunking it in absurd ways. Sixty-one percent did not know where ''the shot heard round the world'' was fired; nearly half didn't know who wrote ''Moby-Dick,'' and nearly 80 percent did not know what the Emancipation Proclamation did.

While at the foundation, I took Hirsch's test for cultural literacy, or a pilot version of it mailed out to a random sample of 500 American attorneys. They were chosen as a profession (Continued on Page 59) for their ostensibly broad academic background - undergraduate plus graduate education - and asked to answer questions like these:

a) Willy Loman

b) Stanley Spencer

c) John Smith

d) Babbitt

e) Tom Joad

A solid-state electronic device does not use:

a) insulators

b) transistors

c) vacuum tubes

d) integrated circuits

e) semiconductors Very few of the questions were more taxing than the two above - one involved knowing that Nixon rather than Hoover, Carter, Roosevelt or Truman had resigned over Watergate; another that Anne Boleyn, and not Queen Victoria, had been beheaded by Henry VIII - and the average score was 91 to 92 percent. The lawyers were trusted not to cheat in the privacy of their own offices. You will be reassured to learn that your correspondent achieved 98.2 percent when doing the test under supervision, getting the solid-state question wrong and repeating a nagging boyhood blunder about the gerund.

But what could one say of a person who scored low on such a test? How many allusions in daily reading and discourse would he be bound to miss?

Hirsch remembers the day in 1978 when, as he puts it, ''my life changed.'' He and some colleagues had been running a test on readability in prose. They were trying to see in what ways clarity of style influenced comprehension. The correlation between the two seemed simple enough, and Hirsch was encouraged to find that ''students showed a definite sensitivity to the stylistic superiority of one version over another.''

But the test that had been applied to the young people at the university hit a snag when it was applied, for comparative purposes, to the students of J. Sargent Reynolds Community College in Richmond. The decoding skills of these poor and minority students were just as developed as those of their counterparts in the sylvan surround of Charlottesville. But they broke down sooner; in fact they began to break down whenever background knowledge was involved.
