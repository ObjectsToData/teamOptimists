COMMENCEMENTS; City College

By JOSEPH P. FRIED
Published: May 26, 1990

Gen. Colin L. Powell, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, was booed loudly at the City College commencement ceremonies yesterday when it was announced that he could not appear as scheduled to receive an honorary degree because of a time conflict with his military duties.

Some members of the graduating class waved placards reading ''No Degree for Powell,'' while other students wandered through the commencement day crowd with their faces painted white to symbolize those who died in the invasion of Panama last December. ''He was the architect of that illegal action,'' said one of the students, who refused to give his name. ''And no school with as many Hispanics as City College should be honoring him.''

General Powell, 53 years old, a native of the South Bronx, was commissioned an Army second lieutenant in 1958 through the college's R.O.T.C. program. He will be given his honorary Doctor of Laws degree later, said City College's president, Bernard W. Harleston.

Other honorary degrees were awarded to Harry Belafonte, the singer and civil rights advocate; the poet Adrienne Rich, and Miriam Colon, president and founder of the Puerto Rican Traveling Theater. Lee Lorch, professor emeritus of Mathematics at York University, Toronto, received an honorary doctorate from the City University. A lifelong opponent of discrimination in education, he chose to receive his degree at the City College ceremony because of the ethnic and racial diversity of the Harlem campus.

Mr. Belafonte recalled that in his childhood, his mother often walked past the City College campus on her way to work as a domestic. ''Even in the bitter winters of her life,'' he said, ''she told me that one day I'd be in this facility - and in those days the campus was practically all white.

''Now my mother could say: 'My son received the highest honor this school has to give.''

The graduating class numbered some 1,900 students, including 1,200 candidates for the bachelor's degree. The valedictorian was Carlyle V. Thompson, an English major with a 4.0 grade average. Mr. Thompson worked full time as a subway train motorman for the New York City Transit Authority throughout his years at City College. He recently won a 1990 Mellon Fellowship in the Humanities for graduate study.
