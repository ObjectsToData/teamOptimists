WHAT'S DOING IN; Cambridge

By Cecily McMillan; Cecily McMillan lives for part of the year in Cambridge, Mass.
Published: October 14, 1990

For more than 300 years, Cambridge has pictured itself as the center - if not the birthplace - of American intellectual life. Its name invoked eminence, and the graduates of Harvard, Radcliffe and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology reflected back its glory.

Today a visitor will find a Cambridge that has cast its net of pride to the edges of its farthest neighborhoods and drawn them in. A diverse, vibrant metropolitan life of about 90,000 people has grown around the world of the universities. The squares that mark its geography are linked by an expanded subway system and long avenues of busy stores. The mix of restaurants, boutiques and residential streets that long defined the character of Harvard Square is no longer limited to it.

Cambridge has grown but hasn't lost its informal feel. Nearly one-fourth of the residents are students. Friends in conversation clog the sidewalks. Urban lampposts are gaudy bulletin boards. On any sunny day, clusters of people eat brown-bag lunches on the brick plazas or gather to hear street musicians. They may still wear rep ties and wing tips here, but the tie might be around a woman's waist, the shoes on her feet, punctuating the popular style of bright leggings and a gauzy skirt.

Out and About

The Charles River is flanked by two busy arterial roads, but its peaceful geometry, epitomized by a lone sculler in a fragile boat, offers one of the city's most restful vistas. The Head-of-the-Charles Regatta, the world's largest single-day rowing event, starts Oct. 21 at 8:30 A.M. Thirty-two races are planned, involving 930 boats. Thousands of spectators crowd the banks and bridges along the three-mile course (upstream from the Boston University boathouse, near the Hyatt Regency Hotel at 575 Memorial Drive), many of them sporting school or boating club colors, others with banners and flags. Picnics are encouraged; loud cheering is expected; alcoholic beverages are not allowed. For information: 617-864-8415. The river turns away from Cambridge near Mount Auburn Cemetery, laid out in 1831, but from the Washington Tower within its gates, there is a spectacular view of it, of Boston and, on a clear day, of the western hills and suburbs. Hundreds of trees and shrubs, many now at their peak of fall color, frame its avenues of statuary. Visitors may walk or drive through the cemetery daily from 8 A.M. to 5 P.M. or later, until standard time returns. Self-guided tour maps, historical information and bird-watching guides are available at the office (580 Mount Auburn Street; open from 8:30 A.M. to 4:30 P.M. weekdays, 8:30 A.M. to 12:30 P.M. Saturdays).

Former students often find themselves facing a landscape of memories. Hour-long campus tours can bring them up to date. Tours of M.I.T. meet Monday to Friday at 10 A.M. and 2 P.M. at the information center - at the main university entrance - 77 Massachusetts Avenue; 617-253-4795. Harvard tours run Monday to Friday at 10 A.M. and 2 P.M. and Saturdays at 2 P.M. They meet at Holyoke Center, 1350 Massachusetts Avenue; 617-495-1573. Maps for self-guided tours are also available here.

For a small city, Cambridge has exceptional museums. The Fogg Museum (32 Quincy Street) and the Sackler Museum (Quincy Street and Broadway) share Harvard's fine arts collections. The second-floor galleries of the Fogg show its strength in Impressionist and Post-Impressionist paintings. A small, scholarly exhibit of landscape painting entitled ''The Harvest of 1830: The Barbizon Legacy'' runs to Oct. 28 at the Sackler. Hours at both museums are 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. daily except Monday; combined admission is $4; free on Saturdays from 10 A.M. to noon. The main number for both is 617-495-9400.

Shopping

New World Cantabrigians have been proclaiming their independence since 1775 when George Washington gathered his militia on Cambridge Common. Today they create individual styles from stores all over the city. Broadway has its antiques row. Inman Square has a fancy toy shop and a houswares store hard by those that reflect the neighborhood's Portuguese community. Massachusetts Avenue near Porter Square is the address of today's emporia of necessities: health food stores, bicycle shops, a cafe for fine chocolates.

The old, funky part of Harvard Square has decamped to a tree-lined stretch of Huron Avenue. There is a small, old-fashioned grocery with a butcher, a corner drug store and boutiques for art, toys, even vegetables. At the Bryn Mawr book store (373 Huron Avenue; 617-661-1770) alumnae sell books from professors' libraries and family playrooms. The aging hip population, many of whom have children now, eat inexpensively at Pentimento (344 Huron Avenue; 617-661-3878), a two-room restaurant with wooden booths, unmatched chairs and a pie safe filled with homemade desserts. A meal for two is less than $20.

The two dozen bookstores of Harvard Square are magnetic. It's hard to leave them, even at midnight. Some, like Wordsworth (30 Brattle Street; 617-354-5201), sell new books at a discount, but even when the crowds are thick, the staff will patiently direct you. The Harvard BookStore (1256 Massachusetts Avenue; 617-661-1515) founded in 1932, sells used books downstairs, and new ones have been arranged in broad lanes.
