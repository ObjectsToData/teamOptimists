4 Inquiries Cite Naval Academy For Rife Sexism

By FELICITY BARRINGER, Special to The New York Times
Published: October 10, 1990

Fourteen years after the first 18-year-old women started the rigors of plebe summer at the United States Naval Academy, a ''considerable segment'' of midshipmen, faculty and staff believe women have no place there, according to an internal Academy review made public today.

''The negative attitude and inappropriate actions of this minority exert such a disproportionate influence on the Naval Academy climate that most midshipmen readily acknowledge that women are not accepted as equals in the brigade,'' the report said, adding that ''low-level sexual harassment can pass as normal operating procedure'' in some classrooms or among groups of students.

Four Studies Released

The report, by the Academy's Women Midshipmen Study Group - a seven-member panel made up of faculty, staff and students - was the most detailed of four studies made public today at a news conference. They were all commissioned last spring after it was disclosed that a former midshipman, Gwen Marie Dreyer, had been handcuffed to a urinal and photographed by at least eight male midshipmen on the eve of the Army-Navy football game.

Ms. Dreyer, now a junior majoring in mechanical engineering at California Polytechnic Institute, resigned from the Academy last spring. She did so after the men involved in the incident were given demerits and letters of reprimand. One of the midshipmen, she said in an interview last spring, threatened ''to make her life miserable'' if she did not testify on his behalf.

Rear Adm. Virgil L. Hill Jr., the Academy's superintendent, who determined the punishment, declined to revise his decision in that case but said at today's news conference that under new codes of conduct, midshipmen involved in such incidents could be subject to dismissal.

The news conference was attended by two women in the Maryland Congressional delegation, Senator Barbara A. Mikulski and Representative Helen Delich Bentley.

Family Is Pleased

Ms. Dreyer's father, Gregory, said today by telephone from his home in northern Virginia that the reports ''vindicate all that Gwen was trying to do in the face of denial and minimalization and all so forth.'' He added, ''It's a sad commentary on the Naval Academy leadership that they had to have it shoved down their throats this way.''

Ms. Dreyer was in a class this afternoon and unavailable for comment.

The internal report, the most detailed and specific of the four, touched on factors that may have contributed to the negative perceptions of women. These included the relatively low proportion, 13 percent, of female faculty members and their concentration in the humanities areas of a technically dominated curriculum, and the widespread perception among male midshipmen that women are given favored treatment.

More Women Drop Out

The statistical summaries offered in the reports indicated that since women were admitted in 1976, a higher percentage of women than men have left the school. Women account for about 10 percent of recent entering classes but their representation in the graduating classes drops, ranging from 6.3 percent to 9.7 percent in the past five years.

In the past summer, about 4.5 percent of male plebes and 7.7 percent of women plebes resigned during the stressful weeks of marching, memorizing and adapting to high-volume commands.

The reasons for midshipmen's departures vary widely. During their the four-year term, officer candidates are continually judged on technical competence, physical ability, academic ability and military performance, or performance in leadership roles.

At the Naval Academy, said Senator Mikulski: ''We pick the best. But they often come in with cultural attitudes biased against women in general and women in the military in particular. They do not check their cultural attitudes at the door.''

Examples of Harassment

According to the internal review group, one-third of male midshipmen and two-thirds of female midshipmen ''believe sexual harassment is a problem.'' The kinds of harassment cited include obscene electronic mail messages sent by computer, pornographic movies shown in the gathering areas of some of the 36 companies in the brigade of 4,400 midshipmen and various expressions that are part of midshipmen's vernacular.

Dating a female midshipman, for instance, is called ''going over to the darkside,'' a phrase describing the forces of evil in the ''Star Wars'' movies. Female midshipmen are also sometimes described by the acronym WUBA, which in the manuals refers to a particular uniform (Working Uniform Blue Alpha) but in the vernacular is understood to mean ''Woman Used By All.''

A committee appointed by the Board of Visitors, the Academy's governing body, said another factor contributing to sexual harassment was a ''breakdown of civility'' and an environment in which there was broad latitude to touch, physically restrain and play pranks on other midshipmen.

Senator Mikulski, a Democrat and a member of a committee established by the Board of Visitors, said the reports show that women find greater tolerance and acceptance in the Naval fleet than they do at Annapolis.

Representative Bentley, a Republican who was also on the Board of Visitors panel, said, ''We can't guarantee there won't be further incidents,'' ''What we must guarantee is that there is zero tolerance not only for the incidents but the attitudes.''

The internal report, the Board of Visitors' report and studies by the Navy's Inspector General and an informal review board set up by the Navy, all included recommendations for instructing midshipmen on what is expected of them and what actions are out of bounds.

A fifth report, based on an investigation by the General Accounting Office, the investigative arm of Congress, has yet to be released.
