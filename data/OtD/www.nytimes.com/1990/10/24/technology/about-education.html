About Education

By Fred M. Hechinger
Published: October 24, 1990

Ten years ago, North Carolina engaged in a daring experiment: creating a two-year boarding academy that would select able high school juniors from all over the state and immerse them in mathematics and science.

Initially, the idea of the North Carolina School for Science and Mathematics aroused heated controversy. It was selective -- and therefore, in the view of some, undemocratic -- and boarding schools were traditionally reserved for children of the rich.

Moreover, in seeking out the best and the brightest, the school would be color-blind.

The power behind the idea was James B. Hunt Jr., who had made the improvement of education the centerpiece of his successful 1976 gubernatorial campaign. Once elected, he pushed hard to make the plan a reality. "It wasn't easy," he recalled last week in an interview. "At one crucial point, we won by only one vote in the Legislature."

Now, as the school marks its 10th anniversary, he said, its progress has exceeded his fondest expectations. Last week, a new dormitory building for 400 students was opened -- until now, an old remodeled hospital served that purpose. The new building was named for Mr. Hunt, who was governor from 1977 to 1985.

"It has become truly a model school, where bright youngsters are challenged by their peers," Mr. Hunt said.

Of the 1,400 students who have graduated since the school opened in 1980, 900 answered a survey. Of those, 99 percent had gone on to four-year colleges, and 67 percent had majored in math or science. Nationally, among the top 10 percent of the nation's high school graduates, approximately 40 percent become science or math majors.

Even more important, 70 percent of the school's minority students had chosen math, science or related techological majors in college. Eleven percent of the graduates were black, 8.7 percent Asian, 1.6 percent American Indian and 1 percent Hispanic. The school's goal is to raise the total minority enrollment to 32 percent.

Before the school opened, opposition was not limited to cost-conscious state legislators. Members of the education establishment attacked a plan that would not only take some of the best students from ordinary schools, but might also cut into their budgets to pay for those students' more costly education.

Selectivity and excellence are not cheap. The cost per pupil is about three times the state's average per-pupil expenditure of $3,715, and the school turns down about 600 applicants for the 280 places in each year's junior class.

Mr. Hunt has no doubt that the results justify the cost. While in office, he said that his success in running the state would be assured if he could provide good education and good jobs. The school, which barely won legislative approval, now enjoys the widest political acclaim.

Located in Durham near Duke University, on the edge of what is known as the Research Triangle, the school gives students an opportunity, as Mr. Hunt pointed out, to "do science." Still, the students are not science freaks. About 45 percent, Mr. Hunt said, arrive on campus with musical instruments, and they also excel in other fields.

He said the school had no problem attracting outstanding teachers, including a high proportion of Ph.D.s. Teachers, he said, cherish the professional authority and rewards the school gives them. Private funds allow enough flexibility to offer attractive salaries. "It's the kind of flexibility all schools should have," Mr. Hunt said.

Mr. Hunt said the school's success also depended on being free of many of the regulations imposed by state education authorities. "I set it up under my own office, and in 1985, put it under the University of North Carolina system," he said. The school also has its own board of trustees.

One measure of its success is that it has been imitated in several states: Louisiana (with a School for Mathematics, Science and the Humanities), Illinois, Indiana, South Carolina, Mississippi, Alabama, Texas and Oklahoma.

The elitism issue, Mr. Hunt claims, has faded away. He said the school, "like a light on the hill," improves the quality of other schools, whose principals say, "Let's make ours just as good."

For the future, he sees the possibility of adding a sophomore class. While the school now admits only students from North Carolina, he would like some out-of-state and foreign students added as an enrichment.

In a broader sense, the North Carolina experience raises questions about whether public schools should rethink their traditional limitations. Can they find unorthodox new ways to offer bright children from their diverse population some of the benefits now largely reserved for children of the rich?
