PERSONAL COMPUTERS; Trips Through Geography and Time

By PETER H. LEWIS
Published: April 3, 1990

AS the school year wanes, it is time to put two educational software programs on the honor roll of the class of 1990.

PC USA, for I.B.M. PC's and compatibles, is a computerized atlas that can help students learn about the United States. It is a companion product to an earlier program, PC Globe, which is also highly recommended.

PC USA is more than a geography program. It is also a data base of statistics and other information on each state, plus the District of Columbia and Puerto Rico. The user can highlight a particular state or region and call up a variety of facts about it, ranging from broad economic statistics to the number of lawyers for every thousand people.

On a purely geographical level, the map of the United States can be broken down into its states. One view might show all of the major cities. Another might highlight the principal natural features, including rivers, mountains and lakes.

The idea of merging maps with data bases is not new, but most such programs, called geographic information systems, are expensive and highly complex. PC USA and PC Globe contain a wealth of data, but they remain accessible to pupils from elementary school on up.

The map is the entry point to both programs. The user can ''point and shoot'' at the map to select a state, using either a mouse or keyboard commands, or select states from a list. We pointed and shot at Texas, a large target, which brought a map of the state to the screen and automatically loaded the underlying data base about the state.

In one view, the state's largest cities were shown. In another, the rivers, plateaus, lakes, elevations and other natural features were displayed. At this level, the program does not show any advantages over a more conventional printed atlas.

The real strength of PC USA, and of PC Globe as well, is in the data base. The user can call up information about government, history, tourist attractions, population, age distribution, education, crime, health, ethnic groups, natural resources and agriculture, taxes, principal products, highways, and even telephone area codes and ZIP codes. Much of the information can be broken down further to the city level.

Even more useful, the data on one state can be compared with similar data on another, and the results are displayed in graph form. For example, one might wish to compare average teacher salaries in Texas with those in other states. The user can select the states to be compared or compare the ''active'' state, in this case Texas, with the 10 states that rank highest or lowest in any given category.

PC Globe has similar capabilities, but on a broader scale. Both programs are easy to use. Each time we start, we wind up spending hours just browsing through their many features.

PC USA and PC GLOBE are $69.95 each and can be ordered from PC Globe Inc. of Tempe, Ariz.; phone (602) 894-6867. Both require a minimum of 512 kilobytes of memory and a color graphics adapter. A mouse is helpful but not necessary.

For the Apple Macintosh, the star of this year's class is Culture 1.0, The Hypermedia Guide to Western civilization.

Like PC USA, Culture 1.0 combines a data base with a sort of map. However, the terrain in this case is time: a time line of history and culture, in fact. Unlike conventional time lines, which are linear, the time line in Culture 1.0 is multidimensional. It lets you see, and even hear, events and cultural developments in an interrelated way that more accurately reflects the way history unfolds.

Hypercard, a software utility that is included with each Macintosh, is an ideal educational tool. It allows links to be forged among related bits of information. Used with a data base, Hypercard permits the user to roam through text, pictures and sound, guided only by his or her imagination and interests.

In the case of Culture 1.0, the underlying information and essays have been compiled over the last 20 years by Dr. Walter Reinhold, a professor of music history and the humanities at New York University.

In his classes, and in Culture 1.0, Professor Reinhold urges students to consider historical events in context, not in isolation. It is one thing to memorize the dry fact that Beethoven was a Romantic composer who lived from 1770 to 1827 - snore - and quite another to see him, hear a passage of his music (even in computer beeps) and place him amid the political, theological and artistic environment in Europe at the time. Who were his contemporaries? What great painters and architectural masterpieces might Columbus have admired before setting off for the New World?

From any starting point, the user can explore the history, music, philosophy, architecture, politics and art of the major periods of Western history from ancient times to the 19th century. The 20th century will be added later this year, the program's developers say.

For those who prefer a more structured way to explore the program, Cultural Resources offers a $35 companion guide called ''Interdisciplinary Lessons Covering Various and Sundry Things Every Secondary School Student Should Learn and Every College Student and Graduate Should Know.''

Culture 1.0 for the Macintosh is $175 from Cultural Resources Inc. of Cranford, N.J.; phone (201) 709-1574. It requires about five megabytes of hard disk space.
