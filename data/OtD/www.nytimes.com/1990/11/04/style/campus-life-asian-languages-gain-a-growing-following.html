Campus Life; Asian Languages Gain A Growing Following


Published: November 4, 1990

With the emergence of Japan and Korea as world economic powers, East Asian languages are getting more attention at universities across the nation.

The University of Chicago, the University of Wisconsin at Madison and the University of California at Berkeley have long offered a wide assortment of languages. Now they are among a growing number of schools offering more classes and spending more money on Asian languages, particularly Japanese and Korean.

Student interest in foreign languages in general has increased in recent years, but the largest increase has involved Asian languages, administrators say.

"The biggest change in the language picture has been the Eastern languages," said Richard Strier, master of the humanities collegiate division at the University of Chicago. "The most serious crowding we've had was in Japanese and Korean last year."

Data being collected by the National Foreign Language Center, an independent research organization based at Johns Hopkins University, shows that enrollment in Japanese language study has risen 68 percent since 1986 at the 171 colleges surveyed. Decline in French

Jonathan Wolff, a research associate for the center, said that about 50 of the 171 schools had begun offering courses in Japanese since 1986. At schools with more established programs, Mr. Wolff found that classroom space and teaching staffs as well as annual budgets are being strained by increased student demand.

At the University of Chicago, since the 1985-86 academic year, the number of courses in Japanese has increased to 5, from 2, while the number of language courses in French fell to 9, from 17. During that time, the university's overall enrollment increased and a stiffer language requirement was instituted.

Over the last year at Chicago, the number of introductory and intermediate language courses has remained the same in every area except for French, Italian, Portuguese and Spanish, which decreased, and Japanese, in which two sections were added.

Geoffrey Cox, director of financial planning and budgets at Chicago, said that while the total language budget is up 93 percent from 1985, the increased costs in Asian languages has been partly offset by increasing the teaching load of graduate student instructors and professors.

Columbia University, which has one of the oldest Asian studies departments in the nation, is paying for its program, in part, by attracting more outside gifts. In particular, gifts from Japanese donors are on the rise, said Paul Anderer, acting dean of the Graduate School of Arts and Sciences and an East Asian language professor. Classes for Koreans

At Berkeley, the Oriental Languages Department "can't accommodate all the students who want to take the courses" in Japanese, said Jeffrey Riegel, chairman of that department.

Eric Broque, assistant provost for administration and budget in the College of Letters and Science at Berkeley, said that the budget for Oriental languages is up 10 to 20 percent in the last 10 years, while Romance languages have seen a "modest inflationary growth" of 4 to 6 percent.

The University of Wisconsin at Madison has been cutting undergraduate enrollment in the last five years to decrease class sizes and make the school more selective. This has led to considerably lower instructional budgets in Romance languages: 20 percent lower in French and Italian and 30 percent lower in Spanish and Portuguese.

But in East Asian languages and literatures, the budgets have been increased 75 percent since 1980 and 30 percent since 1985. There have been smaller increases in Semitic languages, African languages, Scandinavian studies, classics, Slavic languages and South Asian languages. Second-Generation Koreans

Administrators at all three universities pointed to growth in Korean language classes, which they related to the recent influx of second-generation Koreans who often are able to speak Korean but unable to write it.

The University of Chicago introduced Korean in 1985 to meet demand from Korean students, who now make up the university's largest ethnic minority. When Yale introduced Korean to its curriculum this semester, 52 students enrolled, forcing the department to add two extra sections to the one originally planned.

In contrast, most of the students enrolled nationwide in Japanese studies are not of Japanese descent. Mr. Strier at Chicago and Mr. Riegal at Berkeley attributed the increase to growing interest in Japanese culture and international business.

But Akira Miura, a professor of East Asian languages and literatures at Madison, said that the practical concerns prompting students to study Japanese are not often enough accompanied by a cultural interest.

He foresees a "stagnating pool" of new Ph.D.'s in the area because the number of people studying elementary Japanese is not matched by the number interested in teaching it.

"It's really not that interesting to teach people only interested in business," Mr. Miura said. He said he would rather teach people interested in "Japan as a country, not just Japan as a business."
