The Two Faces of Southern Populism

By Dan T. Carter; Dan T. Carter, a professor of history at Emory University, is a fellow at the National Humanities Center.
Published: November 10, 1990

George Wallace, that Southern ghost of elections past, still haunts us as we enter the 1990's.

If Guy Hunt's re-election campaign as Governor of Alabama was a kinder and gentler version of the politics of race, Jesse Helms's senatorial campaign in North Carolina offered a chilling reminder that the old-time religion of "Nigger! Nigger! Nigger!" can still bring out the frightened faithful. Both victories vindicate those Republican strategists who have always wanted to exploit the racial fears so skillfully aroused by Mr. Wallace in his Presidential campaigns of the 1960's and 70's.

It is a strategy that seems particularly suited for our increasingly subliterate television culture. In that world in which each new day begins afresh -- without past, without future -- 30-second appeals to glandular reflexes ("quotas," "parasitic underclass") are infinitely more accessible than complex discussions of race relations or economic exploitation. The current economic downturn makes such squalid appeals to scapegoating even more effective.

What we should not forget is that this is neither something new nor the last word, but the latest chapter in a century-old struggle between two quite different strands of Southern populism.

Mr. Wallace began his career in 1946 as a down-home social democrat with little enthusiasm for race-baiting. To be sure, when hebecame the high priest of racial segregation in the early 1960's, he brought to Alabama a level of vindictiveness that left wounds still unhealed. And his Presidential campaigns gave voice to some of the darkest fears and hatreds in American society.

But when the number of black voters in his state passed the 300,000 mark in the early 1970's, the Alabama Governor reversed direction and embraced black voters and politicians. Political opportunism is not an edifying spectacle. Compared with the unwavering racism of Mr. Helms, it has its charms.

In some ways, George Wallace was simply returning to his political roots, as one of the proteges of a Southern populist who spent his career fighting for, rather than against, civil rights: James E. (Big Jim) Folsom.

Through two terms as Governor of Alabama and 40 years of campaigning, Folsom evolved his political catechism: governmental laissez-faire inevitably allowed the powerful to prey upon the weak; adequate welfare programs were the fundamental obligation of a democracy; women were entitled to full equality; the state of Alabama should guarantee black citizens equal justice, equal opportunity and complete access to the ballot box; there were no problems that couldn't be solved by a "good strong purgative of pure unadulterated democracy."

In the end, Folsom's personal failings (too much whisky, too many women, too few honest friends) were as conspicuous as his 6-foot 8-inch frame and his size 16 shoes. His challenge to Mr. Wallace collapsed in the 1962 governor's race when he appeared on statewide television too drunk to recognize his own children. When he ran for governor against Mr. Wallace in 1974, he got less than 5 percent of the vote.

And now, as the economy falters and the bills for Ronald Reagan's fantasies come due, the voices James Folsom fought are returning to join the David Dukes. The politics of victimization are over, we are told. Now black Americans can once more take their historical place as the scapegoats of a troubled society.

Folsom had seen it all before. The "Ku-Kluxers, the race-baiters, and some of the selfish interest groups" would always be present, he warned in 1949. They would be "spreading their filth, their lies, their old and ancient hatreds . . . trying to boil up hatred by the poor white people against the Negroes . . . trying to keep the poor white from progressing by keeping the Negro tied in shackles."

Still, I take heart from the only conversation I ever had with James Folsom. He was nearly blind and occasionally confused as we sat and talked in a truck-stop restaurant outside Cullman, Ala. But he retained an almost childlike faith in the same voters who had rejected him. The working people of this country would eventually see through this "blasphemous smoke screen" of racial hatred, hepredicted in our talk. And they would understand that the promise of this nation lay in the challenge of guaranteeing "equal justice, equal opportunity and equal freedom for every man, woman and child."

That's a far cry from the "populism" of Jesse Helms.
