Dostoyevsky, With All the Music

By Andrei Navrozov; Andrei Navrozov is the author of "Second Nature: Forty-Six Poems by Boris Pasternak" and a forthcoming literary biography of Pasternak.
Published: November 11, 1990

THE BROTHERS KARAMAZOV A Novel in Four Parts With Epilogue. By Fyodor Dostoevsky. Translated by Richard Pevear and Larissa Volokhonsky. 796 pp. San Francisco: North Point Press. $29.95.

HAVE you read "The Brothers Karamazov"? A new translation of Dostoyevsky's novel is here, and those who remember that question from their undergraduate days must prepare themselves to hear it asked again. To understand just what it was that everyone has missed until now, think of opera.

"Chur!" exclaims Boris Godunov in the opera named after him, staring into space. Were it not for Mussorgsky's genius, this terrifying cry would not move a foreign audience aware only that the Russian Czar was cursing. Nor would the Shakespearean "Aroynt thee," if used by an English librettist instead of the more familiar "avaunt!," curdle the blood of even the most literate operagoer who did not know English. Fortunately, there is the music.

With this example in mind, it may well be argued that the semantic universe of a Russian writer like Dostoyevsky is forever closed to the foreigner. The word chur ("get out," "leave me alone"), for instance, is etymologically contaminated by chort ("devil") and cherta ("line"). If, as the philosopher Vasily Rozanov suggests, all of Dostoyevsky is a study in the agony of individual spiritual sovereignty, how can the English word "crime" be expected to support his critical case when Dostoyevsky relies on prestupleniye (as in " Prestupleniye i Nakazaniye, " the title of the novel known in English as "Crime and Punishment"), meaning literally "overstepping" or "transgression"? Dostoyevsky's heroes, after all, are best apprehended just as they cross the line ( cherta ) by violating the delineated limits of nature and the individual. Such lines are supernaturally solemn, drawn by the ancient ancestors of the pagan Slavs and imbued with all the power of living ritual. The existence of the devil "on the other side" of the line is as plain as a log of firewood, known as churka in common speech after a notched ancestral idol it resembles. Sovereignty of the individual is the underlying theme of many a children's game, with " Chur menya! " a common refrain. The translator who renders that as "Get thee hence!," however accurately, will not convey the idea that the hallowed ground of individual freedom is circumscribed by deities more ancient, and more sacred to the Russians, than the Christian God.

It would indeed be hopeless were it not for the music. For no translator is expected to transport the reader into the semantic universe of the original by means of a single word. The context, stylistic as well as substantive, is there to illuminate the reader by increments, educating him in the book's meaning and handing him its ultimate revelation like a diploma. In short, it is on the musical whole of the original that the translator, like the audience of Mussorgsky's opera, must rely for elucidation. And it is in this new translation by Richard Pevear and Larissa Volokhonsky that one finally gets the musical whole of Dostoyevsky's original.

Yet ever since the days of Constance Garnett, who published the first "unaltered and unabridged" translation of Dostoyevsky's last novel, "The Brothers Karamazov," in 1912, the contextual whole of this writer's work has been treated like the libretto of an opera that would never be performed. Meanings of the words were carefully considered, sentences were judiciously composed, and eventually English versions of the novels came into being. In Garnett's case, the Edwardian result had much to recommend it, while David Magarshack, who translated "The Brothers Karamazov" in 1958, could be said to have effectively modernized Garnett's prose. But the translator's fundamental approach, based on the certainty that the music of the original could never sound, remained the same: emendation. To make the literary experience of a libretto stand in for the total experience of an opera, no distortion was too fanciful, no paraphrase too contrived, no gloss too smooth.

That the collaboration of Mr. Pevear and Ms. Volo khonsky on a new translation of "The Brothers Karamazov" achieves what has been possible all along becomes clear as soon as we open their version at Part One, Book One. "The History of a Family," Garnett emends the original, probably thinking of a family in a Dickens novel. "The History of a Family," Magarshack translates nearly half a century later. "A Nice Little Family" is what Mr. Pevear and Ms. Volokhonsky give for Dostoyevsky's " Istoriya Odnoi Semeiki ." Had Dostoyevsky wanted to say sem'ya for "family" instead of the always sarcastic semeika, he would certainly have saved Garnett and Magarshack the trouble of having to correct him. In and of itself, this sort of iconoclasm is hardly more than elementary literacy at the college level. It is not radical enough to reveal the semantic universe of Dostoyevsky in a single word like sem'ya, with its connotations of semya, seed. But cumulatively, once the combined felicities of the new translators add up to a context, the English reader of "The Brothers Karamazov" is assured of hearing Dostoyevsky's music for the first time.
