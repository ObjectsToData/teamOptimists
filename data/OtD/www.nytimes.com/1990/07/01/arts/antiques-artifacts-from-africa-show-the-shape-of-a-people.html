ANTIQUES; ARTIFACTS FROM AFRICA SHOW THE SHAPE OF A PEOPLE

By RITA REIF
Published: July 1, 1990

Many of the artifacts of Zaire on view at the American Museum of Natural History - curious people-shaped boxes, flamboyant feathered hats, exotic harps and fetish necklaces - are being shown for the first time since they were collected in Africa 75 years ago.

The exhibition, ''African Reflections: Art From Northeastern Zaire,'' which remains in New York through Jan. 6, comprises 456 objects, most of which were among the 4,000 items acquired by the museum's zoologists, Herbert Lang and James Chapin, during their African expedition from 1909 to 1915. Some of the 54 tons of material they brought back - rare mounted mammal specimens, including okapis and white rhinoceroses, birds and flora - were incorporated into the museum's displays after World War I. The rest of the meticulously documented items from the Belgian Congo, now called Zaire, remained in storage. The sculpture, furniture, pottery, baskets, hats, tools, weapons and jewelry are identified as made by the Mangbetu, Azande and Barambo people of the region.

About six years ago, a major aspect of what had long been accepted as a traditional form of art by the Mangbetu was questioned by Dr. Curtis A. Keim, a professor of history and political science at Moravian College in Bethlehem, Pa. This spurred Dr. Enid Schildkrout, the museum's curator of anthropology, to propose a thorough review of these holdings. The results are documented in this comprehensive exhibition, of which they are curators, and in the 272-page catalogue ($45 cloth, $27.50 paper), which they wrote. ''Curt Keim came to the museum and claimed there is no such thing as Mangbetu anthropomorphic art,'' Dr. Schildkrout said in a recent interview. ''He said the Mangbetu didn't have anthropomorphic art in the 19th century and they don't have it today. We debated this for several years, and we finally concluded that something happened in the Colonial period, from the 1890's to the 1920's, that made anthropomorphic art flourish as an art style among the Mangbetu.'' The people-shaped objects found on the expedition - bark boxes, ceramic vessels, musical instruments, pipes, tools and knives - were labeled to show they were made by the Mangbetu or by their neighbors, including the Azande.

''The Mangbetu were the group the Europeans admired, so that art depicting them became desirable,'' Dr. Keim said. ''Anthropomorphic art existed in the region and was adapted by the Mangbetu when it became esthetically and socially interesting.'' Mangbetu earthenware pots and pitchers take the form of busts of women of regal bearing, their elongated heads crowned by elaborate fan-shaped coiffures, their faces scarified with beadlike tears. These vessels appear to be almost line-for-line depictions of the wives of important Mangbetu men - beautiful women whose heads had been bound at birth to lengthen them and who were photographed in the Congo at the time these vessels were found, about 1910. This head-binding practice was abandoned decades ago, Dr. Schildkrout said. Bark boxes for trinkets and charms are cylinders on wood pedestals that become armless torsos when topped by the carved head of a man or woman. Wood or ivory harps and sanzas, which are small musical instruments played by plucking, were produced by the Mangbetu or Azande with necks in the form of men or women and resonator boxes covered with leopard skins or pangolin scales. An iron dagger with an ivory handle carved as the head of a man is as stunning in its abstract simplicity as a Cycladic sculpture.

The Mangbetu and their neighbors also made hats of woven plant fibers sprouting feathers - fantasy creations that were worn only by men at the turn of the century. The intricately woven straw hats are topped by an abundance of floppy feathers, white or striped, from such creatures as the guinea fowl, the owl, the eagle, the touraco, the gray parrot, the chicken and the paradise flycatcher. Equally imaginative in conception are the animal-shaped images, such as a copper ring with the head of a rabbit with Bugs Bunny ears, which dates from about 1850, and a wood slit drum in the shape of a buffalo, which was carved about 1910.

Jewelry was worn as more than ornaments. A boldly plaited rope belt dangling a hank of wart-hog hair was donned by Mangbetu men to guard against harm. Charms on necklaces of iron, copper and horn warded off evil. Leopard belts with leopard and lion teeth were worn only by rulers at court as symbols of their power.

One of the most arresting objects in the exhibition is a Barambo healer's necklace of wood, whistles, seedpods, antelope horns and a gourd filled with what were believed to be magical powders. ''The magic for them had to do with the species of wood,'' Dr. Schildkrout said. ''Each different kind of wood had a different kind of power. Each medicine treated different kinds of problems. The long pieces are whistles. Each would have another meaning and might have been used to cause sickness or death, financial gains or losses, luck or happiness.''

To augment the museum's African collection are more than 50 objects lent by the British Museum, the Musee Royale de l'Afrique Centrale of Tervuren, Belgium, the Pigorini Museum in Rome and the Brooklyn Museum. The exhibition, which is sponsored by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the New York State Council on the Arts, is to travel next year to the National Museum of African Art of the Smithsonian Institution in Washington and the High Museum of Art in Atlanta.
