A SPECIAL REPORT: HOT POTATOES; The Fourth R

By Anthony DePalma
Published: August 5, 1990

TEACHERS are jugglers, balancing competing interests, and, sometimes, competing ideologies, to deliver to their students enough accurate information to help them make reasoned judgments.

It isn't easy, particularly when formerly taboo - and still sensitive - subjects like AIDS and religion are added to the curriculum. It is a performance in which parents, students and educators all have a stake.

This report offers an overview, followed by an examination of some of the most troubling subjects.

AFTER decades of skirting the subject of religion, public schools from California to North Carolina are concluding that what Thomas Jefferson called the ''wall of separation'' between church and state has been too high, depriving students of a complete understanding of how history, literature and the arts have been influenced by faith.

Guided by directions from the Court, some districts have made the academic study of religion the Fourth ''R'' in their curriculums, offering elementary and high school courses like ''World Religions'' or ''The Bible as Literature'' that teach about religion, as opposed to offering any sort of spiritual indoctrination.

The problem is that religion, so thoroughly discouraged as a course of study for so long, is absent from most textbooks and teacher-training courses, and teachers who make a go of it often are on their own.

Some observers warn that this lack of methodology could lead to religious doctrine leaching into the classroom, just as schools had so long feared. ''To take a teacher whose specialization is American history and turn him loose in a classroom and say 'You're going to teach comparative religion' is just a real indiscriminate mess,'' said R. Joseph Hoffmann, a professor of humanities at California State University in Sacramento. ''There's a great opportunity for abuse.'' Despite such warnings, many school districts are inserting religion into their curriculums and looking for materials with which to teach it. Several new developments may answer the need.

The most significant of these may be the California Department of Education's current review of social-studies and history textbooks, which have been revised to meet the state's recent decision to purchase texts that fairly deal with religion, as well as race and the role of women.

Experts nationwide are also responding. An assistant professor of philosophy and religious studies at Western Illinois University in Macomb, John K. Simmons, is producing television workshops on teaching religion. A national association of curriculum specialists has just published a book on teaching religion in American history courses. North Carolina, which recently included religion in its social-studies curriculum, is holding summer seminars to train teachers about teaching religious awareness without imposing particular views.

''Religion is out there as an issue now,'' said Charles R. Knicker, a professor of secondary education at Iowa State University and editor of Religion and Public Education, journal of the National Council on Religion and Public Education. The council consists of more than a dozen organizations including the American Jewish Committee, the Baptist Joint Committee on Public Affairs and the Islamic Society of North America.

Mr. Knicker said several factors had spurred this new interest in religion. ''We must make some acknowledgment that conservative forces in the country had an impact in bringing it up,'' he said. Mr. Knicker also believes it has taken a while for educators to feel comfortable enough with the Supreme Court's landmark school decision in 1963 to bring religion into the classroom.

That decision, in Abington v. Schempp, rrbanned school prayer but left open, and some say encouraged, teaching about religion. ''The justices said, 'One's education is incomplete without the study of religion,' '' said Nicholas Piediscalzi, a professor of religious studies and visiting scholar at the University of California at Santa Barbara. Mr. Piediscalzi set up the first clearinghouse for information on the subject in 1971. He said the push now was to integrate religion into other subjects already in the curriculum, such as social studies, literature and art. ''This is something we always emphasized, because religion occurs at every level of life, impacts on every dimension,'' he said.

Two teachers in St. Louis Park, Minn., a suburb of Minneapolis, have developed a series of texts for integrating religion into the curriculum. Lee H. Smith, chairman of the social-studies department, and Wesley J. Bodin, a teacher, have written five paperback anthologies on world religions: Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Islam and Judaism. A sixth deals with a broad range of religious expression.

The booklets contain excerpts from primary sources such as the Bible and the Koran and are designed to be used with 16 audiovisual filmstrips that have been converted to videotape.

One teacher who uses the material is Robert J. Dilzer Jr., chairman of social studies at Newtown High School, in Newtown, Conn. Mr. Dilzer has been teaching a popular one-semester elective course in world religions for 20 years. ''The rationale for the course is that knowledge about religions is absolutely essential for living in a world of diversity,'' Mr. Dilzer said.
