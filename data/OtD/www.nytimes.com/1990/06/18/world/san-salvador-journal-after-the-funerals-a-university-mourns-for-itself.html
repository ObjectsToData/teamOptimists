San Salvador Journal; After the Funerals, a University Mourns for Itself

By LINDSEY GRUSON, Special to The New York Times
Published: June 18, 1990

The rage rose as news spread of the November killings of six Jesuit priests. But bit by bit their colleagues' fury passed, replaced by a smoldering bitterness.

''There is still a sadness,'' said Jon Sobrino, a friend and colleague. ''We have had several recent Masses, and the people weep. It's not the rage or indignation that one may have felt at first. There is a small sense of being orphaned.''

The slain priests' friends and associates at the University of Central America took solace in the belief that the priests would be survived by the power of their ideas. The university's Jesuits have been critics of social inequities and the power of the military, and while the ruling elite and the armed forces have viewed them as intellectual authors of El Salvador's decade-long leftist rebellion, the Jesuits have never been as militant as the right presumed.

'The Best Homage'

Throughout the school, colleagues have decided to take up the slain men's yoke as a leading voice of reason in a society where fanaticism seems all too prevalent.

''Never before have the dead done so much for the living,'' said Francisco Escobar, a poet and the dean of the university's humanities department, adding that the institution ''is committed to living on, to continue putting its face out front.''

''The best homage was to continue working for them,'' he said. ''The decapitation of the institution obligated many people to assume the work they had been doing.''

But so far, many faculty members acknowledge, the success of the effort has been limited. Life at the school has returned to normal. The slain Jesuits were quickly replaced from a flood of volunteers, and the rolling campus soon returned to the quiet rhythms of students studying and teachers teaching. A Two-Legged Stool? But the university has yet to resume its former role as this society's dominant intellectual institution. Faculty and students compare the university to a two-legged stool. While it remains the best academic and research institution, attracting talented students who can not afford to go abroad, it has lost much of its social role, its ability to project itself beyond the walls of the serene campus and define the national debate.

Antonio Canas, a protege of one of the slain priests and editor in chief of the university's authoritative weekly, Proceso, said the school continued ''to maintain its function as an institution, but it has lost its soul.''

In the last three years, previously unimaginable space has opened up in this country. Dozens of leftists have returned from exile. Sworn enemies who once were more likely to exchange bullets now argue on daily television programs. But unwritten limits remain. And the existing political space is tenuous, a matter of timing rather than principle.

Remembering a Visionary

''The world of reason is incompatible with the world of terror,'' said Mr. Canas, who has been rereading the writings of the Rev. Ignacio Ellacuria, the slain rector of the university. ''I'm in awe of his vision. His 1972 warnings of a social explosion -and a period of grave violence that would be very difficult to break out of - have all come to pass. The single most difficult thing to replace is this quality of a visionary.''

The university's vice rector, the Rev. Ignacio Martin-Baro, and its dean of social sciences, the Rev. Segundo Montes, also were killed in the Nov. 16 attack on the Jesuits' residence on campus. The others slain were the Rev. Joaquin Lopez, the Rev. Amado Lopez and the Rev. Juan Ramon Montero, and the priests' housekeeper, Julia Ramos, and her daughter Celina. An army colonel, two lieutenants and five lower-ranking military men have been charged with murder, but court investigators have complained that evidence was destroyed, and questions remain about who ordered the killings.

A New Uncertainty

The school still seems to be feeling its way. The loss of direction was illustrated last month when a local television station invited the vice director of the weekly Proceso to appear on a talk show about the political future of the leftist guerrillas.

In the past, it would have hard to imagine such a discussion without a leading figure from the university; acceptance would have been all but automatic. But this time the idea prompted serious reflection. Proponents argued that they had a responsibility to lead the country's political debate; opponents said that they would only attract unwanted attention and that it would be wiser to maintain a low profile. In the end, the invitation was accepted.

Consensus has been more difficult to reach in other matters. The result has been that the slayings widened the schisms that have long divided the faculty and the 7,000 students, most of whom are from the middle and upper classes and tend to be apolitical or conservative. The school's graduates have included some of the country's top military officers and many children of the ruling elite.

The Army Feels Betrayed

The school was founded in 1965 as an alternative to the National University, a social and political caldron that produced many of the guerrillas' senior leaders and most ideologically charged adherents. But the new university's Jesuit faculty members soon became disciples of liberation theology and by the early 1970's had emerged as leaders in El Salvador's strident social and political debate.

The oligarchy and the army felt betrayed. Ignoring the social inequities that were driving many leftists into the mountains as guerrillas, they came to view the university's Jesuits as the cause of the civil war, which by now has claimed 70,000 lives.

Although staunch critics of the status quo and military domination, the Jesuits were much less than solid supporters of the guerrillas. In the months before he was slain, Father Ellacuria even became convinced of the good intentions of President Alfredo Cristiani and his rightist Government. More and more, he and his Jesuit colleagues became the country's most articulate proponents of negotiation.

As a result, the deaths here have left a lingering pain, a political vacuum that is proving particularly difficult to fill. ''This year is the 25th anniversary of the U.C.A.,'' one administrator said, ''but there's isn't much desire to celebrate.''
