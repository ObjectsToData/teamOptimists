When the (New) World Was Portugal's Oyster

By RICHARD F. SHEPARD
Published: June 1, 1990

First of all, they were great seafarers, the people of Portugal in the 15th and 16th centuries for whom exploration became a national way of life. Their voyages led to the discovery of Brazil, to a route around Africa to India and to an impressive exhibition that is to open tomorrow at the New York Public Library.

''Portugal-Brazil: The Age of Atlantic Discoveries'' is an imposing reminder of the Portuguese role in pushing back the horizons of the known world, including the world known as the new one. It comes when preparations are being made elsewhere to celebrate, also with merited eclat, the Spanish discovery of the Americas through the skill of the Italian navigator Columbus, who lived in Portugal for many years and might have done so under Lusitanian colors but for a royal decision against such globe-trotting.

A Policy of Exploration

''The main point is that Portugal played a really crucial role in spearheading the earlier discoveries overseas,'' said Gerald Lombardi, director of special projects of the Brazilian Cultural Foundation, which organized the exhibition. ''People from all over the Mediterranean converged on Portugal. The crown had made it an official policy to stimulate exploration.''

Mr. Lombardi said it had cost $1 million to do research and put the project together. Of this, he said, $325,000 came from the National Endowment for the Humanities. Other financing came from the Portuguese Government and from corporations and foundations in the United States and Brazil.

The exhibition is suitably displayed in the appropriately regal and marbled Gottesman Exhibition Hall, where columns and arches emphasize the significance of each of the 150 objects on view. The collection, selected from more than 50 libraries, archives and museums the world over, is not one for the visitor to breeze through. Precious documents and ancient parchments are there, as are books, maps and paintings that show, step by step, the Portuguese primacy in exploration.

Through Trial and Error

Appropriately, the four-section exhibition starts with a small model of a caravel, the 50-ton, two-masted, lateen-rigged cockleshells that were the instrument by which the Portuguese found and conquered much of the world. The section called ''The Art of Astronomical Navigation'' shows that they did so with the help of the astrolabe, like the one on view, made in 1555, and the magnetic compass, represented by a 1711 example.

The second section, ''Exploring the Atlantic,'' attests to the trial and error that typified early navigation. A replica of a 1492 globe outlines Eurasia and Africa rather recognizably, but between eastern Asia and western Europe, there is seen only blue water where cartographers now place the Americas. Yet the early charts, like those overseen by Prince Henry the Navigator and used by Vasco da Gama on his epic voyages to Asia, and firsthand descriptive reports of new lands are marked in detail, reflecting a realization of the importance to posterity of what was being logged.

Among a plethora of old papers in which popes and kings asserted claims and defined borders is the original Treaty of Tordesillas, one of the most publicized and precious historical keepsakes because in 1494 it divided the world between the Portuguese and the Spanish, one of the first European attempts to define who was entitled to what when it came to sharing the globe.

Amid all this technical detail are paintings, like the large ''Panoramic View of the Monastery and Plaza of Belem,'' depicting an African stop on the route to India.

The Way to Brazil

The third section, ''Portugal-Brazil: The Encounter Between Two Worlds,'' includes two wooden panels with bas relief showing Indians chipping and shipping brazilwood, the source of a red dye sought by France's textile industry and the source of Brazil's name. The discovery of Brazil by Pedro Alvares Cabral on March 9, 1500, came ''at vesper time,'' in the words of the report to the King by the clerk of the armada's factor, and is detailed in map and letter.

''Cultural and Literary Echoes of the Portuguese Discoveries,'' the fourth section, exhibits early copies of classics, among them the first Portuguese edition of ''The Lusiads'' in 1572 and the famous poetic fiction by Luis de Camoes describing da Gama's voyage to India.

There is also a 1516 edition of Thomas More's ''Utopia,'' and a 1719 printing of Daniel Defoe's masterpiece, denominated on the title page as ''The Life and Surprising Adventures of Robinson Crusoe of York, Mariner Who Lived 8 and 20 Years, All Alone, in an Un-Inhabitable Island on the Continent of America, Near the Mouth of the Great River of Oroonoqui.''

A full-color, 270-page, $75 catalogue, featuring essays by experts, is on sale in the library shop, next to the exhibition hall.

The exhibition, through Sept. 1, is open, free, from 10 A.M. to 6 P.M. Monday to Saturday. Free tours are given at 12:30 and 2:30 P.M. Information: 930-0501. Free slide-lectures are to be held on 6 P.M. on Monday and on June 11, at the Mid-Manhattan Library, 455 Fifth Avenue, at 40th Street.
