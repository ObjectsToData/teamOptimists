Provost From Michigan Named to Head M.I.T.

By FOX BUTTERFIELD, Special to The New York Times
Published: June 13, 1990

Charles M. Vest, the provost of the University of Michigan and a former dean of engineering there, has been selected to be the new president of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, school officials announced today.

Dr. Vest's selection, by the executive committee of M.I.T.'s governing board, ends an unusually difficult search process that began a year ago when Paul E. Gray announced that he would step down as president at the end of this month.

Two men initially considered the leading candidates, John Deutch, M.I.T.'s provost, and David Baltimore, a Nobel prize-winning biologist, both took themselves out of the running. In February, another distinguished biologist at M.I.T., Phillip A. Sharp, accepted the presidential nomination, but then stunned the school a week later by changing his mind and saying he preferred to remain a professor.

Dr. Deutch, a chemist, withdrew his name after opposition surfaced from some professors and students critical of his ties to the military and to private business. He is now considering leaving M.I.T. for a new professorship at Harvard University, officials of both institutions said today. That switch would be a coup for Harvard, whose division of applied sciences has lagged well behind M.I.T., its neighbor down the Charles River.

Dr. Baltimore has accepted the presidency of Rockefeller University.

'The Right Choice'

The selection of Dr. Vest, 48 years old, still requires the approval of the full 65-member M.I.T. governing board, which is expected to meet in a special session in Cambridge next Monday, school officials said today.

A spokesman for the University of Michigan said Dr. Vest would have no comment until his confirmation. Dr. Vest has been provost and vice president for academic affairs there for 18 months. He was dean of engineering from 1986 to 1989, and was credited with helping develop a new $100 million industrial technology institute.

''M.I.T. made the right choice,'' said James J. Duderstadt, the president of the University of Michigan. He described Dr. Vest as a man of ''extraordinary vision and depth'' who is also ''very persuasive, with that rare ability to get people to do things by believing they want to do them.''

Dr. Duderstadt predicted that as president of M.I.T. Dr. Vest would emerge as a ''national spokesman for science and industry.'' He noted that Dr. Vest had developed close relations with leaders in both the automobile and aerospace industries.

Dr. Vest is one of a number of university presidents to come from the University of Michigan in the past few years. Harold Shapiro, who became president of Princeton University in 1988, had been president and before that provost at Michigan, and Linda Wilson was picked as president of Radcliffe College last year after being a vice president at Michigan.

'He Filled the Template'

Robert Solow, a Nobel laureate in economics and head of the M.I.T. faculty committee that helped pick a new president, said Dr. Vest was chosen because ''he filled the template of what an M.I.T. president should look like.''

Dr. Solow noted that Dr. Vest was both an engineer by training, which fits one of M.I.T.'s traditional strengths, and an experienced administrator. Michigan has 35,000 students, while M.I.T. has 10,000.

''I thought he was a reflective and thoughtful person with very broad academic sympathies, not just for engineering but for the humanities, social sciences and hard sciences,'' Dr. Solow said.

Dr. Vest has no previous connection with M.I.T., having received a bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering from West Virginia University and master's and Ph.D. degrees in mechanical engineering from the University of Michigan. Of M.I.T.'s 14 presidents, only three have had M.I.T. degrees, and six had no previous ties to the university either as students or as faculty members.

Dr. Vest is a specialist in three-dimensional imaging and image reconstruction, and has written a standard textbook, ''Holographic Interferometry.''

His experience with the manufacturing sector was also a factor in his selection, said Dr. Solow, because M.I.T. has taken an increasing interest in examining reasons for the decline in competitiveness of American industry.
