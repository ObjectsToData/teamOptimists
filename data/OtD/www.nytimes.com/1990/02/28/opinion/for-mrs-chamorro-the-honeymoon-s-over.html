For Mrs. Chamorro, The Honeymoon's Over

By Paul Berman; Paul Berman is a fellow at the New York Institute for the Humanities at New York University.
Published: February 28, 1990

The Nicaraguan election, far from having clarified a murky situation, has plunged that country into a series of ever murkier questions.

There are, to begin with, 10,000 armed contras. Will President-elect Violeta de Barrios Chamorro and her coalition succeed in controlling these soldiers? For that matter, does anyone know who controls them now?

Is it the C.I.A.? If so, the C.I.A. has some explaining to do, since contra military activity was supposed to have ended long ago, but never entirely did. If the contras are autonomous, does anyone know what in particular they want from this election, apart from their vague phrases about democracy? Some of Mrs. Chamorro's top advisers, and one of her sons, are former civilian contra chiefs, which some people may view as helpful for exercising influence over those troops. But the civilian chiefs quit the contra movement precisely because they never could get the military contras under control.

Who will guarantee that contras do not steal into remote villages at night to settle accounts with the Sandinistas? President Bush? Do the contras listen to him? What a testimony it is to U.S. policy that after tens of thousands of deaths and hundreds of millions of dollars, no one quite knows what the contras are about.

And what about the Sandinistas? How much power will they agree to turn over to the new government?

Mrs. Chamorro may pledge with perfect sincerity not to threaten the livelihood of legions of Sandinistas with a government purge. But sooner or later, in the face of thousands of armed Sandinistas of uncertain loyalty, a weeding out and restructuring will occur. She will have to guarantee that the Sandinista police and army officers answer to her own orders, not those of the Sandinista Directorate.

Sandinista leaders will discover that whole wings of their party are being dismantled, and these wings, made up of army officers and secret police, will turn out to be the party elite. If Daniel Ortega, gracious in his promise to ''respect and abide by'' the election results, has achieved a Gorbachev-like tranquility, he may accept this catastrophe and call it a blessing, which would be wonderful.

But there's a good deal of experience by now in Eastern Europe to show that, despite what top leaders say, armed sectors of a formerly vanguard party do not rejoice at orders to disband. In Nicaragua, orders will issue forth to stop spying, to name everyone who served as a police informer and to stop harrassing anti-Sandinistas. Those orders will not, in fact, be respected and abided by.

So there will be an obscure and ominous back-room political struggle over defense and interior. In that struggle, Daniel Ortega and other top Sandinistas will play a good role or a bad role or a role in the middle, but the struggle will go on nonetheless.

Its outcome will depend on how much political strength Mrs. Chamorro can summon up. How much will that be? The 14 parties in her coalition are so weak that, during the campaign, she started to ignore them in favor of her in-laws, the Chamorro clan, who have ruled intermittently since the early 19th century.

But if Mrs. Chamorro's government reverts, in an old-fashioned Nicaraguan way, to being a subsidiary of the Chamorro family, how will she face down the Sandinista agents and officers who run the interior and defense ministries? There are many Chamorros, but not that many. And who will defend the poor from exploitation in that case? Will it be the Sandinistas, the way that Polish Communists currently claim to defend the masses against Solidarity?

Mrs. Chamorro obviously needs a monumental boost from abroad. But who should devise and provide that monumental boost?

There are, as it happens, non-Nicaraguan political leaders who, over the years, have not lacked for political victories. In 1979, the social democrats of Costa Rica and Venezuela, joined at the last minute by Jimmy Carter, managed to help Nicaragua overthrow the Somoza dictatorship. Just now, the social democrats of Costa Rica and Venezuela, joined at the last minute by Mr. Carter, helped Nicaragua outvote the Sandinistas.

This kind of social democratic, liberal coalition turns out to be the only force with a record of successfully influencing Nicaraguan events - possibly because, when Nicaraguans first made their revolution, social democracy was what many of them thought they were getting. Even now, in a far more conservative moment, social democratic inspirations are still a popular instinct. Social democrats and liberals from abroad do well because they know how to vibrate to the peculiar pitch that is Nicaraguan.

In a better world, the White House, too, would be dominated by social democrats and liberals. Yet the White House now is dominated by a political tradition whose record in Nicaragua has been less than sublime.

White House Republicans were over-interventionist early in this century and dispatched the Marines to Nicaragua, which was a calamity. They were under-interventionist in the 1950's and failed to support the anti-Somoza conspiracies of Mrs. Chamorro's husband, Pedro Joaquin Chamorro. The Republican record in the 1980's is too grim to mention, except to recall that the trials in Washington are still going on. When it comes to Nicaragua, White House Republicans are, in short, a natural disaster, like an 80-year earthquake.

Since White House Republicans must nonetheless respond to Mrs. Chamorro's hair-raising predicaments, they might consider, as a guide in their perplexity, these maxims: Distrust your own instincts. Call on Jimmy Carter, the thinking person's Richard Nixon. Foreswear imperialism. Go multilateral. Ignore polls. Remember that peasants are other than geopolitical pawns.

Of all the questions raised by the election, the murkiest is this: Will the White House keep that last, most difficult maxim in mind?
