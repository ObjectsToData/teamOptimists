LONG ISLAND GUIDE


Published: February 25, 1990

MARDI GRAS TIME

When Mardi Gras is celebrated at Bethpage State Park in Farmingdale, it is as a Family Fun Day, an event that concludes the Long Island State Park Region's Winter Arts Festival.

Today's the day for the Mardi Gras Gala, and from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. there will be entertainment by Mark Schussmann's World of Magic and Peter Abbott's Touring Mime Circus, folk and square dancing led by Primo Fiore, live animal displays (courtesy of the Suffolk County Farm) and balloons and lollipops for the children.

Young and old alike will be able to have their faces painted as clowns or have caricatures drawn by Lou Puglisi, watch woodcarving demonstrations by Victor Taglieri, take Funny Fotos and even create posters.

Everything is free.

'ROMANTIC RUSSIANS'

The Russians are coming to Brookville today. Not just any old or, for that matter, new Russians, but ''Romantic Russians.''

They are composers defined as such by Kenneth Klein, music director of the New York Virtuosi, and the chamber orchestra will perform their works at the Hillwood Commons Lecture Hall on the C. W. Post campus of Long Island University.

The presence of Tchaikovsky on the program is not surprising. He will be well represented, not only by his ''Souvenir of Florence'' and his ''Variations on a Rococo Theme for Cello and Orchestra'' featuring the cellist Michelle Djokic, but also by Arensky's ''Variations on a Theme by Tchaikovsky.''

Mr. Klein also sees a romantic side to Stravinsky, and so his ''Suite No. 2 for Small Orchestra'' will be performed. The concert is at 3 P.M.

Information: 626-3378.

JOPLIN'S 'FRIEND'

Dennis Kobray is a piano teacher-turned-performer, but when he performs it is not just by tinkling the ivories.

Rather, his specialty is ''Meet the Musician,'' a kind of living history concert for which Mr. Kobray dresses up as his subject matter, plays music associated with the composer or musician and, using slides, tapes and words, fills in details about the subject's life, times and work. Accordingly, sometimes Mr. Kobray is Beethoven; sometimes, Mozart. This being Black History Month, and since Mr. Kobray is not black, he is currently billing himself as a ''Friend of Scott Joplin.'' That is, the production is devoted to the ragtime composer as seen through the eyes of an actual student and friend of Joplin's.

Today, Mr. Kobray brings his show to the South Country Library, 22 Station Road, Bellport, for a free performance at 3 P.M.

ON THE FLY

Yes, Virginia, there still is fly fishing for trout on Long Island, and those who doubt it might want to take advantage of some clinics planned to spread the word about how to keep those big ones from getting away.

On Tuesday, master fly-tyers will demonstrate the latest salt- and fresh-water patterns for stripers, trout and bass.

The free seminar, to be held at Eisenhower Park's 19th Hole in East Meadow, begins at 7:30 P.M.

On Saturday, the Long Island State Park Region begins a series of indoor and outdoor workshops conducted by members of the Long Island chapter of Trout Unlimited.

Consisting of casting instructions, fly-tying demonstrations and tackle and equipment displays, the three-hour clinics start at 9 A.M., with the first one to be held at Connetquot River State Park in Oakdale.

They then continue March 10 at Caleb Smith State Park in Smithtown, with reprises on March 17 in Oakdale and March 24 in Smithtown.

The sessions are free, but there are park admission charges of $1 a person. Preregistration is a must (581-1005 in Oakdale and 265-1054 in Smithtown).

'HEDDA, HEDDA, HEDDA'

Can it be that ''Hedda Gabler'' is 100 years old? Ibsen's masterpiece marks its centennial this year.

In honor of the event, Long Island Stage starts a three-week run of the classic Tuesday. Rolf Fjelde, founding president of the Ibsen Society and author of the translation the theater is using, says the play ''deals convincingly and provocatively with the universal human emotions of greed, fear and sexual hostility and confronts the eternal conflict between reality and illusion.''

Mr. Fjelde, incidentally, will serve as a panelist at a free public symposium, ''Shaw and Ibsen: The Centennial Year of Shaw's Quintessence of Ibsenism,'' scheduled for March 11.

It will be the first entry in a major three-day humanities conference on ''The Once and Future Shaw'' that Long Island Stage will hold in April.

Meanwhile, ''Hedda'' arrives at the Hays Theater on the Molloy College campus in Rockville Centre and stays through March 18.

The large cast includes Nancy-Elizabeth Kammer, the former artistic director of the BoarsHead Theater in Michigan, in the title role of the independent, free-spirited woman struggling to achieve her rightful place in a male-dominated society; Humbert Allen Astredo, a Broadway, television and regional theater veteran, as Judge Brack, and Long Island Stage regulars Steven Haworth and Bjorn Johnson as Eilert Lovborg and George Tesman, respectively. Clint J. Atkinson, the Stage's artistic director, directs. Tickets: 546-4600.
