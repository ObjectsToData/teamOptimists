MUSIC; A New Concert Series Begins at Grace Church

By ROBERT SHERMAN
Published: March 11, 1990

THE Grace Episcopal Church in White Plains is home to an endeavor known as Downtown Music at Grace. ''The charming Victorian architecture and fine acoustics of the church provide a warm, intimate setting for all types of music,'' said the group's music director, Timothy Lewis.

In addition to a monthly series of late afternoon Sunday recitals (now in its second year), Mr. Lewis has begun a weekly cycle of half-hour Wednesday noontime concerts at the church that will continue through April. ''All of the artists are young professionals,'' he said, ''and all deserve to be heard.''

Because the church has a brand new Guilbault-Therien pipe organ, the venerable so-called King of Instruments will be called into play twice this week. At a 12:30 P.M. matinee on Wednesday, Susan Randall, the organist of St. Matthew's Episcopal Church in Wilton, Conn., performs. Then the internationally renowned Gillian Weir plays Bach, Pachelbel, Franck, Vierne and more at the official inaugural recital next Sunday at 5 P.M. (This despite a warning from one critic who wrote, ''The danger of having Miss Weir dedicate your new organ is that you may never hear it played so well again.'') Call the parish office at 949-2874 for information about these and future concerts at Grace Church, including such varied fare as oboe recitals, several more organ programs and even a salute to Irving Berlin.

Another noted organist, Richard Heschke, choirmaster at the Episcopal Church of St. Mary the Virgin in Chappaqua, has an 8 P.M. Friday recital scheduled at Concordia College in Bronxville (where he is also chairman of the organ department). The first half of his program offers music of the German Baroque - by Buxtehude, Sweelinck and Bach - while after intermission it is all French, including works of Widor, Durufle and Messiaen. For reservations, call the college at 337-9300.

Much has been written about the stripping away of music from the American school curriculum, so when the trend is reversed, it is time for bravos. Such would seem to be the case with the Children's Opera Company of Ossining, recently formed by the Ossining Public Schools, with the cooperation of the music department at the State University at Purchase.

The idea is to have elementary school students perform the singing and acting roles, as well as carrying forward the technical functions involved in opera productions.

What is more, the company is bowing with the world premiere of a new opera, based on ''The Pied Piper of Hamlin'' by Arthur Jay, a teacher in the Ossining system.

''I am interested in developing works for children to perform for each other,'' he said, ''not in works for adults to perform for children. That, in fact, is my definition of children's opera.''

Nor is Mr. Jay about to play down to the kids: ''Anyone coming to see a story with cute jingles or Broadway-style songs is going to be disappointed. I consider this a serious work for children, and I have tried to preserve the basic tragedy of the story. Children should have some serious theater in their experience, not just be fed a diet of theatrical pablum.''

It's a challenging point of view, and the results of the intensive planning and preparation it has engendered will be on display twice, first on Wednesday at the Ossining High School and then on March 20, in the Humanities Building at the State University. Both of the performances are at 7:30 P.M. and feature fourth and fifth graders in the major roles. The two performances are free and open to the public.

''During this production,'' Mr. Jay added, ''we have put into place a staff and structure that will enable the company to continue in the future, and we hope to present new works by other composers next season.''

Some attractive events this week include a 2 P.M. concert today when Kenneth Cooper conducts six vocal soloists and the Bronx Arts Ensemble Orchestra at the Fordham University Church. The program lists Henry Purcell's Ode for Queen Mary's birthday, ''Come Ye Sons of Art,'' and what is billed as the New York stage premiere of ''The Judgment of Paris,'' by the English composer's countryman and contemporary, John Eccles (212 549-1899) Also at 2 P.M. today, the Kaleidoscope Dance Company is presenting another of the free children's programs sponsored by the Mount Kisco Concert Association. This one, designed ''to share with children the joy and excitement of dance as an art form,'' will be held at the Fox Lane School (666-6072).

David Starobin, director of the guitar program at the State University of New York at Purchase, was, in 1988, the first guitarist to receive an Avery Fisher Career Grant Award. At 3 P.M. today he plays pieces by Sor, Guiliani and Elliott Carter in a recital at the Westchester Conservatory in White Plains(761-3715).

The string contingent of the Westchester Camerata, led by Victor Lionti, concludes its season at Westchester Community College in Valhalla today at 4 P.M. The attractive lineup lists Bach's Third Brandenburg Concerto, the Mozart Divertimento in F, Puccini's only String Quartet, ''Crisantemi'' (Chrysanthemums), and Tchaikovksy's gracious Serenade for Strings (285-6600).

Tonight at 7 P.M. it is ''mezzo-mezzo,'' as two Westchester singers -Carole Maloof of Scarsdale and the Met's Nancy Williams of Crestwood - offer a program with the pianist Donald Comrie at the Hoff-Barthelson School in Scarsdale. The music will range from duets of Mendelssohn and Brahms to theater songs by Sondheim and Andrew Lloyd Webber (723-1169).

On Thursday, Marymount College in Tarrytown presents the first of three weekly, illustrated lectures on the operas of Mozart by the noted broadcaster, critic and Hastings-on-Hudson resident George Jellinek (631-3200).

The superb Orpheus Chamber Ensemble is presenting an all-Mozart concert at the State University at Purchase at 8 P.M. Friday (251-6200). And at 8 P.M. Saturday, it heads up to the Peekskill Paramount where Mozart's Divertimento, K.113, prefaces a mixed program that includes Dvorak's Nocturne for Strings, the Reverie and Caprice of Berlioz and Haydn's Symphony No. 85 (739-2333).

Don't expect Mozart at ''A Cantor's Cabaret.'' Steve Sher of Temple Beth Abraham in Tarrytown is devoting his 8 P.M. Saturday concert with Marty Dunayer, a pianist, to lighter selections from Broadway, Hollywood and the Yiddish theater. This, by the way, is part of a new series at the temple devoted to many forms of Jewish music (631-1770).
