ARTS BRIEFING

By Ben Sisario
Published: December 2, 2003

HIGHLIGHTS

NEW ZEALAND: 'RINGS' PREMIERE DRAWS 100,000 -- About 100,000 fans lined the streets of Wellington, New Zealand, yesterday for the premiere of ''The Return of the King,'' the final film in the ''Lord of the Rings'' trilogy. Peter Jackson, the director of each of the films, walked in a street parade for three miles through New Zealand's capital along with the stars Elijah Wood, Sir Ian McKellen, Liv Tyler and Orlando Bloom, and Helen Clark, the country's prime minister. Mr. Jackson was born in New Zealand and filmed most of the trilogy there. The first two films in the ''Rings'' trilogy have already earned $1.8 billion worldwide. ''The Return of the King'' is to open in the United States on Dec. 17.

ACCOLADES -- The playwright August Wilson will be given the Heinz Award for Arts and Humanities on Thursday at a ceremony in Pittsburgh. The $250,000 prize, given by the Heinz Family Foundation, is one of five, including equivalent prizes for work relating to the environment, public policy, technology and the ''human condition.'' The awards were created in 1993 by Teresa Heinz Kerry in honor of her husband, Senator H. John Heinz III of Pennsylvania, who died in 1991. (She married Senator John Kerry of Massachusetts in 1995.) Mr. Wilson, 58, who was born in Pittsburgh and has won the Pulitzer Prize twice, is scheduled to give a reading at the 92nd Street Y in Manhattan tomorrow, where he will be introduced by James Earl Jones. . . . The Brazilian writer Rubem Fonseca, above, has won the 2003 Juan Rulfocq Literature Prize, a leading award in Latin America. Mr. Fonseca, 78, is the author of ''Agosto,'' ''Vast Emotions and Imperfect Thoughts'' and other novels.

MOURNING AT LINCOLN CENTER -- Richard L. Fisher, a real estate executive who serves on Lincoln Center's board, was to be honored last night at Lincoln Center's gala performance ''American Songbook: Celebrating the Music of Leonard Bernstein's New York,'' at Avery Fisher Hall. But no one expected Mr. Fisher to be there to receive his distinguished service award. His 22-year-old son, Andrew M. Fisher, died on Thursday from injuries sustained in a car crash in Waldoboro, Me. The elder Mr. Fisher is a senior partner at Fisher Brothers, one of New York's leading real estate firms. Richard Fisher's mother, Emily Fisher Landau, is a managing director of the Metropolitan Opera. In April, Mr. Fisher's brother, M. Anthony Fisher, also a senior partner at Fisher Brothers, died at 52 in a plane crash along with his wife, Anne Fisher, 41.

WASHINGTON: TCHAIKOVSKY FESTIVAL -- The John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in Washington begins an extensive Tchaikovsky festival today, with performances through Jan. 4 by the Kirov Ballet and the Kirov Opera, among others. The Suzanne Farrell Ballet opens the festival with an all-Tchaikovsky program tonight and the first concert is tomorrow night with the National Symphony Orchestra under Leonard Slatkin, playing Tchaikovsky concertos and other works with the pianist Yefim Bronfman, the cellist Yo-Yo Ma and the violinst Gil Shaham. A highlight of the festival will be the Kirov Opera and Orchestra, conducted by Valery Gergiev -- an increasingly frequent visitor in both New York and Washington -- in a rare performance of ''Mazeppa.'' The company will also perform ''Eugene Onegin'' with many new sets and costumes; much of its decorations for that opera were destroyed in a fire at the company's warehouse in St. Petersburg in September.

FIDDLING FOR A HARLEM SCHOOL -- If it sounds like a movie, that's because it was one: a decade ago a music teacher in East Harlem whose program was canceled in a round of budget cuts held a benefit concert to raise the money she needed to keep the program going. Phone calls were made and soon enough Itzhak Perlman, Isaac Stern and other stars were onstage at Carnegie Hall, playing alongside the students, which made the teacher, Roberta Guaspari, very happy, and earned her program, Opus 118 Harlem Center for Strings, more than $300,000. (It also attracted Hollywood: Meryl Streep played Ms. Guaspari in the 1999 film ''Music of the Heart.'') The current city budget being what it is, tonight in Carnegie Hall Ms. Guaspari is having her sixth benefit, and one look at the list of performers is enough to explain why it is called ''Fiddlefest'': the lineup includes Joshua Bell, below left, Regina Carter, Mr. Perlman, Arnold Steinhardt, Pinchas Zukerman and Natalie MacMaster, along with the nonfiddlers Bobby McFerrin, Yo-Yo Ma, Mark O'Connor and Dave Grusin. Opus 118 operates music programs in public schools, trains music teachers and has a community program that offers afterschool lessons.

FOOTNOTES

Montecore can rest easy. Roy Horn, the Las Vegas showman of ''Siegfried and Roy'' fame, is slowly recovering from the injury he received at the claws of that 600-pound white tiger on Oct. 3, his partner Siegfried Fischbacher said yesterday on NBC's ''Today'' show. ''He is moving now, little by little,'' Mr. Fischbacher said, adding that Mr. Horn has been off his respirator for two weeks. . . . A persnickety book about grammar has become a surprise best seller in Britain. ''Eats, Shoots and Leaves,'' by Lynne Truss (subtitled ''The Zero Tolerance Approach to Punctuation''), is ranked No. 1 on Amazon.com's British site, the BBC reported. The book takes a particular interest in the misuse of apostrophes, as in signs for ''Mens Toilets'' and the film ''Two Weeks Notice.''
