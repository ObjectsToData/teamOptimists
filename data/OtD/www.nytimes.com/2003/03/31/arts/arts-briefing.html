ARTS BRIEFING


Published: March 31, 2003

HIGHLIGHTS

NEW SEASON, NEW MONEY AT COVENT GARDEN -- The Royal Opera House in London announced a busy 2003-4 season last week, as well as a major financing boost. The season, to open on Sept. 12 with a revival of Francesca Zambello's staging of ''Don Giovanni'' from last year, will have 20 productions, including a commission by 32-year-old Thomas Adès and the Royal Opera's first musical, Stephen Sondheim's ''Sweeney Todd.'' Mr. Adès has written an opera based on ''The Tempest'' by Shakespeare and will conduct its performances beginning on Feb. 10 with a cast that is to include Simon Keenlyside and Ian Bostridge; it will be produced with the Royal Danish Opera and the Opera du Rhin in Strasbourg, France, and Tom Cairns will direct. Among the other productions next year are Handel's ''Orlando,'' directed by Francisco Negrin; Shostakovich's ''Lady Macbeth of the Mtseksnk District,'' directed by Richard Jones; and Britten's ''Peter Grimes,'' directed by Willy Decker. ''Sweeney Todd'' is to begin on Dec. 15 in a production with Chicago Lyric Opera that was first seen in Chicago in November. Antonio Pappano, the music director of the Royal Opera, told The Guardian of London that he wanted to ''open the windows'' of the traditional repertory with the musical. Last week the company received nearly $4.9 million in new funds from the Arts Council of England in recognition of its ''commitment to new work, new artists and new audiences,'' the BBC reported.

THE FATHER OF ACTION PHOTOGRAPHY -- The New York Institute of the Humanities at New York University tonight opens ''Was Eadweard Muybridge the Father of Us All?,'' a symposium on Muybridge's pioneering work in action photography. The panelists are to be the portraitist Chuck Close; the photographer Joel Meyerwitz; the historian Simon Schama; the institute's director, Lawrence Weschler; and Rebecca Solnit, the author of an acclaimed new study called ''River of Shadows: Eadweard Muybridge and the Technological Wild West'' (Viking). Ms. Solnit's book puts Muybridge's studies of horses and other creatures in motion -- captured with fast shutters that were cutting-edge technology in the late 19th century -- into a broad historical context. The symposium is at 7:30 at the Angel Orensanz Foundation, 172 Norfolk Street, on the Lower East Side. Admission is $5 at the door; no advance reservations will be taken.

AFRICAN-AMERICAN ART TOUR KICKS OFF -- The Smithsonian American Art Museum in Washington has recently sent five exhibitions of its collections to travel to 30 cities in 20 states through 2005. The first show, ''African-American Masters,'' opens tomorrow at the New-York Historical Society with more than 60 paintings, sculptures and photographs by a who's-who of African-American artists of the 20th century: William H. Johnson (painter of ''Cafe,'' above), Jacob Lawrence, Gordon Parks, Richmond Barthé, Romare Bearden, Horace Pippin and Mel Edwards. The show will run through June 1 and then travel to the Cheekwood Museum of Art in Nashville. Information: (212) 873-3400.

A MONTH OF POETRY -- Now in its eighth year, National Poetry Month was instituted in 1996 by the Academy of American Poets. The academy will post a National Poetry Map of America tomorrow on its Web site (www.poets.org), with state-by-state listings of poets laureate; journals and publishers; bookstores with ''strong poetry sections''; and festivals, conferences and other events. A celebrity reading tomorrow night at the Juilliard Theater at Lincoln Center, called ''Poetry and the Creative Mind,'' is to include Meryl Streep, Caroline Kennedy (the event's honorary co-chairwoman), Laurie Anderson, Frank McCourt, Jessye Norman, Zadie Smith, Henry Louis Gates Jr. and others. Tickets: (866) 468-7619.

FOOTNOTES

Last week the director of the Venice Biennale announced that this year's festival would reflect global struggles in the face of crises like the war in Iraq. That proved true when Scotland and Wales pulled out of the British pavilion in the prestigious contemporary art festival, The Guardian reported, for display spaces of their own. A spokesman for the Welsh pavilion said, ''We want to raise the profile internationally of Welsh artists and hopefully help with Cardiff's bid to become European Capital of Culture.'' . . . Last week Myung-Whun Chung announced that he would not renew his contract at the Accademia Nazionale di Santa Cecilia, where he has been the principal conductor since 1997, The Associated Press reported. Mr. Chung is said to have a tense relationship with Luciano Berio, the orchestra's chairman, though the orchestra stated that the decision was made ''in complete harmony.'' Mr. Chung's contract is to expire in September 2005.
