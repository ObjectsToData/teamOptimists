Double Exposure

By Bruce Bawer
Published: March 16, 2003

SHROUD

By John Banville.

257 pp. New York:

Alfred A. Knopf. $25.

IT can feel like a leap back to a distant era to be reminded of the case of Paul de Man, which provides the inspiration for John Banville's hypnotic new novel, ''Shroud.'' When de Man, a Yale professor and a founder of deconstructive criticism, died in 1983, he was a veritable cult hero in humanities departments worldwide. Four years later, it was revealed that as a young man in Nazi-occupied Belgium he had contributed anti-Semitic articles to a collaborationist newspaper, Le Soir.

Yet many of de Man's colleagues and disciples, putting into practice the deconstructionist premise that even the most lucid-seeming texts contain covert meanings and self-contradictions, argued that the articles didn't suggest what they appeared to. De Man's bigotry was, they insisted, really its opposite; his guilt, innocence; his lies about his early career, truth-telling; and his utter silence about his wartime writings, abject confession. For some observers, however, these paradoxes only affirmed an uneasy ideological consistency between de Man's youthful support of Nazism and his later zeal for deconstruction. As David Lehman noted in ''Signs of the Times: Deconstruction and the Fall of Paul de Man,'' the ''briefs for de Man provided a dossier of proof that deconstruction is not a value-free science but a program that promotes a reckless disregard for the truth and a propensity for hero-worship.''

Certainly ''disregard for the truth'' is a defining trait of Axel Vander, the protagonist of ''Shroud,'' who is based on de Man but who, unlike de Man, lives to confront the discoverer of the Nazi-era journalism that bears his name. The book's title refers to the Shroud of Turin, a recurring symbolic image in these pages because Vander, like the shroud, is at once a fake and an object of veneration. ''All my life I have lied,'' confesses Vander, who narrates most of the novel. ''I lied to escape, I lied to be loved, I lied for placement and power; I lied to lie.'' He lies to us, too, serving up a vivid account of childhood experiences in Antwerp (de Man's actual hometown), only to tell us afterward that he made it all up. How much of what he says should we believe? His advice: not much. ''There is not a sincere bone in the entire body of my text,'' he warns -- though, like other arrogantly unreliable narrators in the Banville oeuvre, he eventually discloses something of his own truth in spite of himself.

''Shroud'' is the latest of several Banville novels that take the form of urgent confessional monologues by men who have done great wrongs and lived a lie. Vander isn't the first of these men to be based on a historical figure. ''The Untouchable'' drew on the life story of the art critic and Soviet agent Anthony Blunt -- like de Man, a respected scholar who turned out to have served totalitarianism. As that novel opens, its narrator has just been unmasked as a spy; similarly, at the beginning of ''Shroud,'' Vander is convinced that a young Irish researcher, Catherine (Cass) Cleave, is about to expose him.

He has received word that she has unearthed his wartime articles, the gist of which, he tells us, was that ''nothing of consequence would be lost to the cultural and intellectual life of Europe . . . if certain supposedly assimilated, oriental elements were to be removed and settled somewhere far away, in the steppes of Central Asia, perhaps, or on one of Africa's more clement coasts.'' (De Man's Le Soir article ''The Jews in Contemporary Literature'' made the same argument in similar language.) Positive he's facing professional ruin, Vander, now an elderly widower living in a California college town, arranges to meet Cleave in Turin, where he plans to attend a Nietzsche conference.

Yet there's a twist in store, a secret beyond the secret. For Vander, it turns out, is not really Vander. The real Vander -- who wrote the anti-Semitic articles -- was a gentile friend of his who died during the war. The narrator, himself a Jew, used his friend's name to escape from occupied Europe. It's this information that Vander (or whatever his name is) considers shameful and potentially career-destroying. But why? He claims not to know the answer, though he's already admitted to being driven by ''fury at being what I am not, fear of being found out for what I am.'' His identity was once his death warrant; he's fled from it ever since. What's more, though he didn't write those long-ago articles, his ''deepest, dirtiest secret'' is that he agreed with them: ''We would have, I would have sacrificed anything to that transfiguring fire. I whisper it: and I still would.'' Such is the measure of his icy alienation from his own identity, and from humanity.

Indeed, Vander -- who professes ''a passionate and all-consuming belief in nothing'' -- claims he first crossed the Atlantic because he felt that in America he ''would not be required to be anyone, or to believe anything. . . . I would be pure existence there, an affectless point moving through time, nihilism's silver bullet . . . shooting holes in the flanks of every moth-eaten monument of so-called civilization. Negative faith!'' (The ethos of deconstruction -- though the movement is never mentioned here by name -- could hardly be better described.)
