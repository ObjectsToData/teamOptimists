Georgia Plans an Utterly Virtual Encyclopedia

By BONNIE ROTHMAN MORRIS
Published: July 24, 2003

NOT too long ago, publishing an encyclopedia was a daunting proposition reserved for publishers with deep pockets and a willingness to tackle weighty and wordy subjects.

But the computer has changed many elements of publishing, and encyclopedias are no exception. Encyclopedias published online or on CD-ROM are busting out all over, and the New Georgia Encyclopedia is a case in point.

People wanting to know all about Georgia's history and culture will be able to consult this virtual reference work, which is to go online this fall at nge.gsu.edu.

Printed versions of state encyclopedias exist -- Texas has one, for example -- but Georgia's is the first to be conceived as a virtual project and published online. It will not be issued in a print version.

Like many reference works available in electronic form, the online encyclopedia will be filled with 1,000 articles that are creatively experienced as much as they are read. Many include short films, songs and recorded speeches, including some by Georgia authors, sports heroes and craftsmen.

Because the project will live on the Web as a free resource, the editors' hope is that not only a schoolchild in Athens, Ga., but even an adult in Athens, Greece, will log on and learn. The potential reach of the book is ''far more attractive than the limited readership of a print version,'' Kelly Caudle, the encyclopedia's managing editor, wrote in an e-mail message.

That may be one reason that when the project directors went looking for support from repositories of everything Georgian, they found it in abundance. The Georgia Humanities Council started the project, it resides at the University of Georgia Press, and many institutions have contributed money and resources to it.

The National Endowment for the Humanities provided two grants, and The Atlanta Journal-Constitution, the Georgia Sports Hall of Fame, the Jimmy Carter Library and numerous historical societies and museums gave encyclopedia researchers access to their collections. A galleries section will showcase some collections, and encyclopedia entries will often include links to relevant, mostly noncommercial Web sites.

Once the encyclopedia becomes available to the public, the editors hope to update it regularly.

''Encyclopedias are notoriously expensive to produce,'' Ms. Caudle said. ''And when it's printed there will be things that are already out of date.'' Publishing it online, she said, creates the potential for constant revisions.
