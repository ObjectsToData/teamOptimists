ARTS BRIEFING

By Lawrence Van Gelder
Published: November 6, 2003

HIGHLIGHTS

TELEVISION: 'SKIN' CANCELLATION -- ''Skin,'' the heavily promoted Jerry Bruckheimer-Warner Brothers drama series about a romance between the daughter of a porn king and the son of a crusading district attorney, has been dropped by the Fox network after three broadcasts that drew low ratings. Called a ''slick, clever melodrama'' by Alessandra Stanley in The New York Times, the show, likened to Fox's more popular series ''The O.C.,'' about rich teenagers in Newport Beach, Calif., faltered in its time slot, at 9 p.m. on Mondays. Mr. Bruckheimer's television productions include the successful series ''C.S.I.'' and ''C.S.I.: Miami,'' and his recent film credits include ''Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl'' and ''Veronica Guerin.'' In ''Skin'' his partners were Warner Brothers Television and Jim Leonard's Hoosier Karma Productions. Mr. Leonard created ''Skin,'' which starred Ron Silver as a Los Angeles pornography magnate, Kevin Anderson as the district attorney and Olivia Wilde and D. J. Cotrona as their 16-year-olds.

IDOLS WITHOUT PROFIT IRK MTV -- The president and chief executive of MTV Networks Europe yesterday joined a chorus already led by stars like Elton John, George Michael and Bono of U2 in denouncing pop-idol-breeding television talent shows. Saying the shows manufacture performers who help television ratings but contribute nothing to a troubled music industry, the executive, Brent Hansen, told Reuters, ''These programs make good TV, but from a musical point of view they do not have any value.'' Mr. Hansen spoke in Edinburgh on the eve of the MTV Europe Awards. He said that the music industry, suffering from a decline of nearly 11 percent in global sales in the year's first six months, should take the time to nurture talent. He said, ''Disposable and ephemeral will, I hope, become less of an attraction both to people who are signing the artists and to the audience.''

COUNTRY MUSIC HONORS -- Song of the year honors went to Stevie Nicks, left, of Fleetwood Mac for ''Landslide'' (made into a country hit by the Dixie Chicks) in Nashville on Tuesday night at the Country Awards presented by the performing rights organization BMI. Jeffrey Steele, with ''Chrome,'' ''The Cowboy in Me,'' ''My Town'' and ''These Days,'' was named songwriter of the year. A highlight of the evening was a surprise tribute to Dolly Parton, honored as a BMI Icon for her ''unique and indelible influence on generations of music makers.''

PARIS: PRIVATE PICASSOS -- Some 90 rarely seen or never exhibited works by Picasso are to go on view tomorrow in Paris at a new privately financed museum, the Pinacothèque. On view through the end of March, the art comes from the private collection of Picasso's widow, Jacqueline Roque, who met him in 1954 when she was 28 and he 73. With Marc Restellini, director of the Pinacothèque, as curator, the show, ''Picasso Intime,'' commemorates the 30th anniversary of the artist's death at 92, an event said to have prompted Roque's suicide 13 years later. The art, nearly all of it featuring Roque, comes from a collection inherited by Catherine Hutin, her daughter from a previous marriage, Agence France-Presse reported.

CHICAGO: LEGENDARY MUSIC -- Andy Razaf's hundreds of credits as a lyricist include songs like ''Honeysuckle Rose,'' ''Ain't Misbehavin' '' and ''Memories of You.'' Eubie Blake's compositions include not only ''Memories of You'' with Razaf but also ''Bugle Call Rag'' and ''I'm Just Wild About Harry.'' Together, Razaf (1895-1973) and Blake (1883-1983) collaborated on 80 songs and 2 major musicals. ''Blackbirds of 1930,'' staring Ethel Waters, played Broadway, but the Broadway-bound revue ''Tan Manhattan,'' which they called their masterwork, folded after tryouts in 1941 in Washington and the Apollo Theater in Harlem. At 7:30 tomorrow, at the Northwestern University School of Law in Chicago, a reconstructed ''Tan Manhattan'' will be presented by the Chicago Humanities Festival with the Tony Award-winner Heather Headley, above, the actor André De Shields, the singers Karla Burns, Billy Porter and Lawrence Hamilton, the jazz pianist Dick Hyman, the ragtime entertainer Max Morath and the Chicago Sinfonietta.

LANNAN WINNERS -- Lannan Literary Awards totaling $905,000 to celebrate excellence in poetry, fiction and nonfiction were announced yesterday by the Lannon Foundation in Santa Fe, N.M. Literary Awards of $125,000 each in fiction go to Edward P. Jones of Arlington, Va.; Alistair MacLeod of Windsor, Ontario; and John McGahern of Foxfield, Ireland; and in nonfiction to Rebecca Solnit of San Francisco. Seven Literary Fellowships totaling $405,000 were awarded to Chris Abani and Mary Rakow, both of Los Angeles; Geoff Dyer of London; Deborah Eisenberg and Linda Gregg, both of New York; George Evans of San Francisco; and Chris Offutt of Iowa City, Iowa. The awards, established in 1989, recognize writers who have made significant contributions to English-language literature.

FOOTNOTES

Julie Kent, a principal dancer with American Ballet Theater, will take maternity leave after performing in the company's engagement through Sunday at City Center. Miss Kent and her husband, Victor Barbee, the troupe's associate artistic director, are expecting their first child in April. Ballet Theater said Miss Kent planned to return in September.
