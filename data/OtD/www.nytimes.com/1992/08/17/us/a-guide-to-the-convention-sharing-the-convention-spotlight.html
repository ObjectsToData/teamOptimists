A GUIDE TO THE CONVENTION; Sharing the Convention Spotlight


Published: August 17, 1992

Richard N. Bond The Republican national chairman will call sessions to order.

The 41-year-old Mr. Bond has been active in Republican politics for more than 20 years. In 1979 he was an aide to George Bush's Presidential campaign. He was deputy chairman of the party in 1982, and in 1988 was deputy director of the Bush campaign. He became national party chairman in February, replacing Clayton K. Yeutter. Mr. Bond was in the news last week after saying that Hillary Clinton was someone who would "liken marriage and the family to slavery," which drew a rebuke from Barbara Bush. Will he tone down his rhetoric? Lynn Martin The Labor Secretary will nominate President Bush on Wednesday.

A former high school teacher, Ms. Martin, 52, was a five-term Congresswoman who gave up her House seat in 1990 for an unsuccessful Senate race. She succeeded Elizabeth Dole as Labor Secretary. A supporter of Mr. Bush since his 1980 campaign, Ms. Martin has opposed him on his veto of family leave legislation and on abortion. Next to Barbara Bush, she will be the most prominent woman speaking. The party always counts on her to do her partisan duty, and she is not expected to mention her support for abortion rights directly. William J. Bennett Ex-Education Secretary will nominate Vice President Quayle.

Mr. Bennett first came to national prominence in 1981 as Ronald Reagan's chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, a position he used for advancing his conservative views on education. President Bush named Mr. Bennett the nation's first Director of National Drug Control Policy in 1989. After he resigned the post in November 1990, he accepted, then declined, the job of national party chairman, citing financial reasons. He is a favorite of conservatives, so many people expect a strident attack on Bill Clinton, the Democratic nominee. Mary Fisher Founder of Family AIDS Network will speak Wednesday night.

A 44-year-old mother of two, Ms. Fisher contracted the virus that causes AIDS from her former husband. The organization she founded aims to help the families and supporters of people with AIDS, and she is also a consultant to the National Commission on AIDS. Ms. Fisher worked in the Ford White House and her father, Max, is the honorary chairman of the Bush-Quayle '92 National Finance Committee. Ms. Fisher's speech is intended to match the eloquence of the two AIDS patients who spoke at the Democratic convention. Senator Phil Gramm Texan will give the keynote address on Tuesday night.

After three terms in the House as a Democrat, Mr. Gramm resigned in 1983 after a dispute with the leadership over a committee assignment. He switched parties and was re-elected as a Republican in a special election. He was elected to the Senate in 1984. Mr. Gramm is probably best known for his co-sponsorship of the Gramm-Rudman-Hollings budget deficit reduction law of 1985. He is considered one of the party's best speakers and gave the nominating speech for President Bush at the party's 1988 convention in New Orleans. Wynonna Judd Country music performer will sing the national anthem Wednesday.

Ms. Judd began a solo career after her mother, Naomi, retired from the duet act, the Judds, because of illness. Her first solo album, released in March, has sold almost two million copies. Ms. Judd and Tanya Tucker, who will sing the national anthem tonight, should be a hit with President Bush, a fan of country music. A lesser-known singer, Fred McClure, former assistant to the President for legislative affairs, will sing the anthem at today's morning session. Ms. Judd will perhaps help shore up support in the Bible Belt, where she is extremely popular.
