College Plan Leads to Criminal Inquiry

By JON NORDHEIMER,
Published: September 30, 1992

An effort to reach out to nontraditional students in this tiny community of Hasidic Jews has created multimillion-dollar troubles for Rockland Community College and led to a criminal investigation into possible fraud, theft and conspiracy.

The difficulties began in a most unlikely way: the desire of this college to offer classes in Judaic studies at an unofficial off-campus branch in this incorporated village, which is home to some 600 Hasidic Jewish families who do business with the outside world but conduct the rest of their lives in virtual social isolation.

Then the real problems began. State investigators, looking closely at the outreach program, questioned if it was more theological than academic. They concluded last year that it was -- a conclusion that could have serious financial repercussions for the college and the community, which supports the institution through taxes.

According to the investigations, there were clear examples to support their claim. For example, rabbis were among those hired as instructors or mentors to conduct classroom and individual courses of instruction. Warnings About Religion

And when an occasional science course was offered, the investigators said, it had an Orthodox twist. A course described as Biology 140, for example, was in practice "a training course for kosher slaughter," they said, and the reading list "includes the code of Jewish law but no textbook in modern zoology." If a language course was offered, it was Hebrew; the only history courses covered the Bible and Israel.

In several warnings to college administrators since 1988, state investigators said the program was "indicative of religious education" and did not fulfill the college's liberal-arts mission of providing "a broad world view and cross-cultural experiences."

Thus, the state concluded, students in the program were not eligible for financial aid. The college, which suspended the program in 1989, has been ordered to repay nearly $10 million in aid that went to 3,120 students over seven years: $4.7 million from the state and $5.1 million from the Federal Government. Now the Rockland County District Attorney is investigating whether any of the money ever ended up in students' hands, or was diverted elsewhere.

But some college officials say the dispute is not in fact about money, but about anti-Semitism. And that has given a sharper edge to the controversy in a county where a growing population of Orthodox Jews has already produced tensions over cultural differences and zoning laws. Problems Cited

Officials from the State Department of Education and the Comptroller's Office likened the program at New Square to the classes at a yeshiva, advising college officials about several problem areas:

*Students were segregated by sex even when taking the same courses.

*Course material was presented from an Orthodox Jewish perspective almost to the exclusion of other branches of Judaism.

*Even though the college offered Judaic studies as part of its liberal arts program, few of the students took classes that broadened their academic experience beyond religious studies.

*All of the 3,120 students enrolled in the New Square program received some form of financial assistance.

*None of the students, as far as state investigators could determine, were ever awarded academic degrees from the college.

Under State Education Department regulations, students enrolled in liberal arts and science programs in community colleges may take a maximum of 15 credits in "specialized study" out of the 60 needed for an associate's degree. The rules require that the remaining 45 credits reflect a broader selection in the arts, sciences and humanities.

At New Square, the investigators found, this ratio was reversed, with students taking more than 75 percent of their courses in Jewish religion and culture. In a sampling of students receiving financial assistance who completed four to six semesters -- some accruing as many as 84 credits -- none earned a degree. None took classes at the main campus at Suffern, which has 8,000 full- and part-time students.

The state audit of the New Square program began in 1988 after the Comptroller's office completed an investigation into similar programs in Israel operated by Kingsborough Community College in Brooklyn and Friends World College on Long Island. A computer match of student records showed that most of those who took these courses formerly studied in Rockland's program at New Square.

Throughout the investigation, college officials kept the growing problem with Judaic studies to themselves.

"We were misled and snookered into believing there was no problem with the audit," said John Grant, the County Executive.

The college's President, F. Thomas Clark, in fact, told county officials after receiving word of the final audit that he had filed suit to block the repayment. But Mr. Clark did not mention the size of the penalty, Mr. Grant said; the $4.7 million disallowance of state aid was not mentioned until March, he said, followed a month later by the $5.1 million "thunderbolt" disallowing Federal assistance.

"That's when the roof fell in," Mr. Grant said.
