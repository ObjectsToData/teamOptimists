Israel-China Relations Nearly Official

By SHERYL WuDUNN,
Published: January 23, 1992

Foreign Minister David Levy of Israel arrived in Beijing today for a four-day visit that is expected to lead to the establishment of diplomatic relations between China and Israel.

Israel's 40-year pursuit of ties with China is likely to end on Friday, when Mr. Levy and Foreign Minister Qian Qichen sign a joint communique to mark the new ties.

While the two nations still differ over Mideast issues -- China recognizes the state of Palestine that was proclaimed by the Palestine Liberation Organization -- the speed with which relations have warmed reflects a new pragmatism, particularly in China.

Mr. Levy's visit comes immediately before the Moscow round of the Middle East peace talks, which China is expected to attend now that it has recognized Israel. China has been eager to play a role in the peace process, but first had to accede to Israeli demands that China extend to Israel the same diplomatic recognition that it gives the other parties.

Yang Fuchang, China's Deputy Foreign Minister, was quoted by the Israeli radio as saying he would take part in the peace talks next week. The radio said Mr. Yang had told Israeli reporters accompanying Mr. Levy that his country "wants to help promote the peace process in the Middle East."

In the past, China was reluctant to recognize Israel for fear of offending Arab countries. But on a visit to China in December, Yasir Arafat, leader of the Palestine Liberation Organization, also asked China to take part in the peace talks. Diplomats read that as a sign that the Palestinians would tolerate Chinese-Israeli ties.

Although Mr. Levy arrived today, his public schedule does not show any formal meetings with senior Chinese officials until Friday, when he meets Foreign Minister Qian and Prime Minister Li Peng. Israeli officials said Mr. Levy's visit would be considered private for the first two days and include sightseeing at the Great Wall and the Forbidden City. Taking Care of Business

It is not clear whether he will also meet discreetly with military officials. In November, the Israeli Defense Minister, Moshe Arens, reportedly made a secret visit to China, presumably to discuss military sales and exchanges and perhaps to express concern about China's arms sales to Arab countries.

Although annual trade between the two countries is only about $30 million, according to Israeli estimates, Israel is believed to have sold China significant amounts of military equipment, including missile guidance systems and tank guns. China, however, has sold weapons to several of Israel's enemies, including Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq and perhaps Syria.

Because of the Sabbath, Mr. Levy will not hold meetings on Saturday. Nor will he and his delegation be served any meat because of China's lack of kosher food. Recognition, but No Diplomats

Israel was one of the first countries to recognize the People's Republic of China, shortly after it was founded in 1949, but because of the Korean War and China's growing links with the Arab world, diplomatic ties were never established.

Two years ago, Israel set up an office of the Israeli Academy of Sciences and Humanities in Beijing and the Chinese set up a branch of China International Travel Service in Israel. Those offices are expected to expand into embassies.
