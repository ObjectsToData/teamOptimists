So Maybe the Dog Ate Your Invitation

By ENID NEMY
Published: November 19, 1992

SOCIAL standing won't do it. Ancestors, other than top-of-the-line royals, don't mean a thing. Money helps, as always, but it's no guarantee. Achievement? Now you're getting closer, but the competition is fierce. Physical beauty? Nice but not enough, unless it's an adjunct to fame or notoriety in some field.

So just how are those guest lists made up, the lists that are an open sesame to the glittering, glossy parties that titillate the gossip columnists and lure the paparazzi, the lists that validate, in their own eyes and the eyes of others, the importance of the men and women on them?

And equally important, certainly to the excluded who don't want to be: is there any way an ordinary mortal can get on some of those lists?

In a word, no.

But if those ordinary mortals blossom into talk-show hosts, best-selling authors or hot recording stars, or if they manage to accumulate really big money and know how to go about dispensing it, there's hope.

Some lists are easier to crack than others. Although charity benefits have their own select invitation lists for their $500- and $1,000-a-person events, the objective is, after all, to raise money. Few such organizations will reject a request from an unknown if that request is accompanied by a check, provided the event is not sold out. Still, as one benefit organizer said, "Getting in and getting a good table are two different things."

To many people, their placement at a party is just as important as being there, and this is perhaps the trickiest part of arranging any big event.

"We try scattering people around; we don't like Siberias," said Lewis A. Ufland, a partner in Ufland Jannes Ltd., which coordinated the tribute celebrating Pauline Trigere's 50 years in business, held at the Fashion Institute of Technology in September. The event, which drew 650 guests, raised $240,000 for six AIDS service organizations.

"Some people treat seating like a marriage or a commitment for life, but after all, it's only two and a half hours at a table," Mr. Ufland continued. "Some threaten to stop their check if they don't like their table, but some really secure people don't care. They'll give up their place to someone new to encourage them in charity."

Who gets the good tables? At the top of the totem pole are the men and women who, over a period of years, have given substantial support to not just a specific charity but to a variety of worthwhile causes, who have become integral cogs in the community wheel. This group can be cracked, with good works, good manners, designer clothes, the right amount of deference to those who have already made it and very big money. And one year won't do it: it takes several seasons. In this arena, a mere two tickets to a benefit is as evanescent as a raindrop.

George Trescher, a marketing counselor who has been behind some of the most prestigious public and private parties in New York for two decades, said that when it came to money, it could take anywhere from $500,000 to $750,000 in a given season to establish an unknown but ambitious couple.

"There's a 10-month season, September through June, and buying a benefit table once a week for 40 weeks, at an average price of $8,000, is $320,000," said Mr. Trescher, who arranged one of the Parties of the Year, the 90th birthday celebration for Brooke Astor. Held last March at the Seventh Regiment Armory, it raised $750,000 for the Citizens Committee of New York City. He also organized the 200th anniversary of the New York Stock Exchange in May, which raised $1 million to start a foundation to develop programs for New York City public schools.

"Getting oneself even better known would be underwriting a benefit or two, at about $100,000 each," Mr. Trescher said. "And then there's the clothes, hair, limousines."

Mr. Trescher and his staff read and clip the gossip columns daily to keep up with new names, but, he said, "you have to know the fabric of a city, the warp and woof." "It's a very complicated thing and constantly changing," he added.

On almost every guest list, he said, are the well-known columnists and people like Mrs. Astor, Sid and Mercedes Bass, Mario Buatta, Bill Blass, Arnold Scaasi, Norman and Norris Church Mailer, Barbara Walters and a new addition, the talk-show host Charlie Rose. Others, like Geoffrey Beene, the fashion designer, and David Easton, the interior designer, are equally in demand but rarely accept invitations to big events.

"The minute you show you want to be active and concerned about New York, you'd be amazed at how you can get on lists," said Harriet Weintraub, who handles public relations and special events for a number of fashion-related concerns, among them Comite Colbert, an association of French luxury-goods makers; Galeries Lafayette; Carolee Jewelry, and Asprey. But, Ms. Weintraub conceded, money is an integral ingredient.

Although Ms. Weintraub has the usual categorized master lists, she contends that the core of most lists "exists in the air."

"You just know the people who should be invited, who represent what you think New York is all about," she said. "The kind of person who made news 10 years ago is different now. Years ago, it was mainly social prominence; now it's a blending. It's more of what you do, more creative people, artists and writers. In recent years, Hollywood and recording stars were added to the list, and even more recently models, photographers, art directors. Now there's a great blend of uptown and downtown: Steven Meisel, Tim Hunt, Tama Janowitz, Marky Mark, along with Blaine and Robert Trump, Cecile Zilkha, Jerome Zipkin."
