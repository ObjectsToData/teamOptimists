Q and A

By Carl Sommers
Published: February 9, 1992

Viewing the Kentucky Derby

Q. I would like to see the Kentucky Derby. Do you have information on viewing the race? -- Rosemary N. Bireline, Cocoa Beach, Fla.

A. There are two routes for spectators at the Kentucky Derby, held the first Saturday in May -- May 2 this year -- at Churchill Downs in Louisville, Ky.: watching from a reserved seat or getting standing room.

Getting reserved seats, which are $39 to $500 each, is a long shot. Of the 50,000 seats at Churchill Downs, there are only 100 to 150 not yet reserved for this year's derby, a spokesman for the track said. Those who do get seats are given the option to renew, and virtually everyone does, accounting for the lack of new tickets. To apply, write a request to Harriet Howard, Churchill Downs, 700 Central Avenue, Louisville, Ky. 40208. Letters should state the number of seats you want and whether you prefer seats in the grandstand (you have a slightly better chance for these) or clubhouse. Requests will be considered by a ticket commmittee. The committee's criteria for choosing are confidential, but some connection with the derby -- say, an ancestor who rode a horse in a previous race -- or a business tie with Louisville helps, the spokesman said, adding that the claims are investigated. If you do not get a seat, you are advised to keep trying each year, noting how many years you have been applying.

For those without seats, an unlimited number of standing-room tickets go on sale the day of the race. They are grandstand standing room tickets, $20, which allow the holder into the stands or the infield, and clubhouse standing room, $30, which provides space in the clubhouse garden. Chairs may be brought onto the infield, only through gate No. 3, off the Fourth Street entrance. Most of the 150,000 spectators expected at the derby watch the races from the infield, but the betting lines are shorter and concession stands more numerous in the grandstand and clubhouse. Studying in Florence

Q. Are there any courses for adults in or near Florence that offer study in the Italian language as well as classes in Italian culture taught in English? -- Barbara Kaye, Indianapolis

A. Here are some programs, offered by universities but open to nonstudents. Costs are for each of two people staying in a room.

Fairfield University in Connecticut offers 10-day and one-month programs in Florence. The monthlong program is in July at the Florence Institute for Arts and Humanities, with a wide choice of courses (Italian opera, cooking and language among them), each including field trips. The cost is $3,300 a person, including air fare from New York, hotel accommodations and all meals. The 10-day trip is an art-study tour, which goes to Rome, Florence, Venice, Orvieto, Perugia, San Gimignano, Assisi and Siena; the cost is $3,300 for noncredit participants. Information: Fairfield University, Fairfield, Conn. 06430, (203) 254-4220.

Gallatin Abroad in Italy, a program offered by the Gallatin Division of New York University, is a seminar on Italian Renaissance art and literature given in Florence during the last three weeks of June. The cost is $2,220 including tuition, hotel and daily breakfast. Information: New York University, Gallatin Division, 715 Broadway, Sixth Floor, New York, N.Y. 10003, (212) 998-7370.

Villanova University and Rosemont College offer a month's study based in Siena, 30 miles south of Florence, May 25 to June 25. The courses emphasize late medieval and Renaissance culture and art, and Italian language courses are also offered. Among other subjects are art history, landscaping and Dante (read in English or Italian). The cost is $2,635 a person, including hotel, all meals and travel to Florence, Rome and Venice. Information: Villanova University, Department of Art and Art History, Villanova, Pa. 19085, (215) 645-4610.

From July 1 to 31, the University of South Florida offers a program on Italian civilization and culture and the history and art of Italy, at the Italian University for Foreigners, in Perugia, 70 miles southeast of Florence. Language courses are also available. The cost is $1,068, including course fees and accommodations, usually in private homes. Meals are not included, but students can eat at the university dining hall for about $3.15 a meal. Information: Division of Special Programs/Travel-Study, LLL 012, University of South Florida, Tampa, Fla. 33620, (813) 974-3218.

Skidmore College offers a noncredit study vacation tour in Florence, leaving New York May 21 and returning June 4. The program consists of lectures and field trips in the city and around Tuscany focusing on the region's art and culture. There is no language program. The cost is $3,200 a person, including air fare between New York and Rome, ground transportation, hotel, breakfasts, two lunches and a dinner. Information: Skidmore College, Office of the Dean of Special Programs, Saratoga Springs, N.Y. 12866, (518) 584-5000, extension 2264. London Rembrandt Show

Q. How can I get tickets to the Rembrandt exhibition at the National Gallery in London in the spring? -- Ellen Hanley, Muskegon, Mich.

A. The National Gallery, March 26 to May 24, is the last stop of a three-city European tour for the show. (It is in Amsterdam until March 1, although the drawings are no longer on display there.) The exhibition contains 50 paintings, 40 drawings and 40 prints by Rembrandt, along with 45 works once thought to be his but later attributed to his students. Tickets will be sold at the door for about $9.25 ($:5), but as the museum will limit the number of people attending, you may not get in at the time you wish. First Call, a ticket service in London, is selling tickets in advance, for an additional fee of about $1.85 ($:1) a ticket, that have a specific day and time (within a half hour) of admission. To order from the United States call (44 71) 240 7200. Taking Pets on a Cruise

Q. Are there cruise ships that allow passengers to take their dogs? -- C. Heneberg, Wolcott, Conn.

A. One ship that takes pets is the QE2, which has a kennel that takes passengers' dogs and cats. Passengers can visit their pets in the kennel and walk them in a special area on the signal deck, which is the highest on the ship. The pets may not be taken to any other part of the ship. The cost for taking a dog or a cat on a cruise is $70 a day. The cost of taking along a dog on a trans-Atlantic voyage is $400, a cat $225. (However, Britain, like many other countries, quarantines for six months animals that are brought in, and therefore only passengers moving there with their pets take them along, Cunard says.) Pet owners must carry their animals on board in a portable case. Information: Cunard Line, 555 Fifth Avenue, New York, N.Y. 10017; (800) 221-4770.
