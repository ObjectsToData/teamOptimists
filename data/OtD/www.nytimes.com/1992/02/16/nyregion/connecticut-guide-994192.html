CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: February 16, 1992

WHITNEY SHOW

Sol LeWitt, Eva Hesse, Carl Andre and Jonathan Borofsky are among the 14 artists featured in the exhibition at the Whitney Museum in Stamford, titled "Immaterial Objects: Works from the Permanent Collection."

The show, which was organized by Richard Marshall, one of the museum's curators and remains on view through April 8, explores areas of art that the public frequently views as incomprehensible or worse.

For example, Mr. LeWitt's "Lines to Points on a Six-Inch Grid," created in 1976, is newly drawn on the wall of whatever museum it inhabits, re-created from the artist's written instructions.

Bruce Nauman's work called "Untitled" (1965-66) assumes a different configuration every time it is dropped on the floor. It is made up of several floppy strips of burlap soaked in latex and connected at one end.

The Whitney is at 1 Champion Plaza, corner of Atlantic Street and Tresser Boulevard. Visiting hours are 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday and admission is free. Gallery talks are given Tuesday, Thursday and Saturday at 12:30, free of charge, and free parking is available in the Champion garage on Tresser Boulevard. Call 358-7652 for more information. SPERM WHALES

"Studying the Living Sperm Whale" is the subject of Dr. Hal Whitehead's lecture on Thursday evening at 8 P.M. in the Imax Theater of the Norwalk Maritime Center, at 10 North Water Street in South Norwalk.

Dr. Whitehead, a professor of biology at Dalhousie University in Nova Scotia, is the author of "Voyage to the Whales," among many published works. His research on whales, carried on in the West Indies, the Galapagos Islands, the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean, provided some of the most comprehensive information on the creatures' social behavior, mating, communication and migration, gathered by swimming with the whales, photographing them and observing a birth.

Previously, information had been based on studies performed on whale carcasses. Dr. Whitehead has been a major force in reducing the killing of whales for research.

Tickets are $9. Call 852-0700, extension 206, for more information or reservations. CHANGING BUSINESS

The Connecticut Business and Industry Association has scheduled two workshops for companies whose futures are threatened by the loss of defense contracts in the state.

Diversification is perceived to be the solution, and the workshops will offer a practical approach to formulating a new business plan. Information on state and Federal assistance available to defense-dependent companies will be distributed.

Both workshops will be conducted by Ken Wexler, consultant on defense diversification to the State Department of Economic Development. Speakers include Joseph McGee, Commissioner of the department, and Louis Auletta, manager of strategic planning for Bauer Aerospace in Farmington, where diversification has already begun.

The first workshop is scheduled on Wednesday from 8 A.M. to 12:30 P.M. at Mohegan Community College in Norwich. The second session, at the same hours, will take place at Mattatuck Community College in Waterbury. The fee is $75. Call 244-1900 for reservations of more information. INDIVIDUAL IN ART

"Human Conditions," the exhibition on view at the Walsh Gallery of Fairfield University through March 11, contains 20th-century American works focusing on the ordinary individual.

Among the works on display are Paul Cadmus's "Fleet's In," banned by the United States Navy for almost half a century because of its raucous depiction of sailors on shore leave; Isabel Bishop's paintings of working women in lower Manhattan during the 30's and 40's, and Reginald Marsh's earthy scenes of Broadway bars and "The Gaiety Burlesk."

A long list of equally notable artists from the first half of the century includes Jacob Lawrence, John Sloan, Arthur B. Davies, Marsden Hartley and Jared French. Among the more recently created entries are Hugh Steers's paintings that address the AIDS crisis and Abby Shahn's "Salvador" and "Eco-Terror: Baghdad."

Visiting hours at the Regina A. Quick Center for the Arts, the gallery's site, are 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday. Admission is free. For more information call 254-4000, extension 2975. DISSENT IN HISTORY

A public symposium on "Justice and Its Discontents: Dissent in the Renaissance" will be held at the Whitney Humanities Center, 53 Wall Street, in New Haven, on Friday from 9 A.M. to 6 P.M.

The program, which is sponsored by the Yale Journal of Law and the Humanities, the Yale Law School and the Smart Family Foundation, will examine the beginnings of the modern concept of justice, and how changes wrought by dissent redirected the future of literature, the arts, politics and philosophy.

Specific topics under discussion include the conflicts between the individual and the state, religious movements in the context of new notions of justice that tolerated other beliefs, and the early changes in gender role definition and woman's voice in society.

Guido Calabresi, dean of the Yale Law School will moderate a panel of speakers from Dartmouth, Columbia, Rutgers, Northwestern and Yale as well as from the Universities of Alabama, Connecticut and Pennsylvania, Stanford Law School and the University of Virginia School of Law.

Call 432-4037 for more information or to arrange overnight accommodations. AMERICAN HISTORY

Portraits of Rosa Parks, Ralph Abernathy, A. Philip Randolph, Malcolm X, Roy Wilkins, Frederick Douglass and Martin Luther King Jr. are among 22 paintings dealing with the civil rights movement on view at the Museum of American Political Life of the University of Hartford thorugh March 7.

They were painted by Robert Templeton of Woodbury over a 20-year period but never before shown at a museum in his home state. Mr. Templeton died in July.

The show may be seen Tuesday through Saturday from 11 A.M. to 4 P.M. The museum is at 300 Bloomfield Avenue; admission is $3, $1.50 for children, students and the elderly. Call 243-4090 for more information.
