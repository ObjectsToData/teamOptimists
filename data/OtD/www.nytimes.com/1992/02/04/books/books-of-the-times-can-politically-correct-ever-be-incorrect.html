Books of The Times; Can Politically Correct Ever Be Incorrect?

By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: February 4, 1992

Debating P.C. The Controversy Over Political Correctness on College Campuses Edited by Paul Berman 338 pages. Laurel/Dell. $8, paper.

Should a college student be punished for using language offensive to minorities, women or homosexuals? Is Tolstoy a great artist who speaks to the world, or a DWEM (dead white European male) who is representative of a Eurocentric cultural bias? Is democratic pluralism still a viable social ideal, or has American culture become a fragmented collection of small warring groups?

These are the sorts of questions raised by the acrimonious new debate over "political correctness." It is a debate that is being waged on dozens of college campuses and in the pages of journals across the country. At stake are the future and direction of American education and the role of culture in a democratic society.

On one side are the radicals, who argue that traditional courses at American universities have been unjustly dominated by the culture of white males. They want the achievements of women, blacks and other minorities better represented in the curriculum, and some of them also argue that speech codes must be adopted to protect members of such groups from verbal abuse. On the other side are the traditionalists, who argue that the radicals have subordinated the teaching of the humanities to political imperatives, substituting ethnic cheerleading for objective standards of excellence. They warn that the radicals' influence is leading to a Balkanization of culture and the curtailment of free intellectual exchange.

In "Debating P.C.," the scholar and cultural critic Paul Berman has assembled a collection of 21 provocative statements on the subject of political correctness. The articles have been selected from popular, academic, intellectual and political journals, and the spectrum of opinion is wide, introducing the general reader to a range of arguments and issues. Included are essays by Nat Hentoff, Irving Howe, Henry Louis Gates Jr., George F. Will, Edward W. Said, Roger Kimball and Barbara Ehrenreich.

As Mr. Berman notes in his introduction, the roots of the P.C. debate go back to the 1960's, with the advent of radical intellectual theories in France and radical political movements in America. While the post-modernist philosophers in Paris were launching cynical attacks on the very wellsprings of humanism and rationality -- people should give up their foolish belief in individual will, they argued, and stop trying to make sense of the world -- activists in America had begun working for women's rights, gay liberation and black nationalism. This phenomenon of "identity politics" combined with bits and pieces of ideas generated by the French theorists became a cornerstone of the radical academicians' new multi-culturist agenda.

The thinking behind this viewpoint, Mr. Berman says, goes something like this: Groups, not individuals, produce culture (that is, there are cultures of white men, of black men, of women, of black women, etc.), and different cultures are engaged in a continuing struggle for power. The culture of white males has achieved domination in this war, using "terms like rationalism, humanism, universality and literary merit to persuade other people of their own inferiority. But by shining the light of race/ class/gender analysis upon it, this success can be revealed as the power play that it is."

Clearly some of the thinkers in this volume, writing on both sides of the P.C. debate, have interesting and important observations to make. Regardless of the reader's own point of view, one is stimulated by the incisiveness of these writers' arguments and goaded to re-examine some of the cultural questions raised by the issue of political correctness.

The critic Irving Howe suggests there is "something grossly patronizing in the notion that while diverse literary studies are appropriate for middle-class white students, something else, racially determined, is required for the minorities." Henry Louis Gates Jr., the W. E. B. Du Bois Professor of the Humanities at Harvard, points out that academic critics must not confuse triumphs won for "the marginalized and demonized" in the classroom with actual changes achieved in the outside world. And the poet Katha Pollitt observes that academics on both sides of the debate regard reading as "medicinal"; they share "the anti-pornographer's belief that there is a simple, one-to-one correlation between books and behavior."

Nat Hentoff's report on the curtailment of free speech on college campuses is intelligent, impassioned and chilling in its findings. And the philosopher John Searle reasonably points out that while the dominant tradition for the United States remains the European tradition -- "the United States is, after all, a product of the European Enlightenment" -- "you do not understand your own tradition if you do not see it in relation to others."

Other writers, however, get carried away on the subject of political correctness, resorting to hyperbole and ideological posturing. Even their language grows inflated and inflammatory. Dinesh D'Souza, the author of "Illiberal Education," has called radical academicians "Visigoths in Tweed," while Mary Louise Pratt, a professor of comparative literature at Stanford, is quoted referring to Allan Bloom, William Bennett and Saul Bellow, three well-known traditionalists, as "the Killer B's." George F. Will warns that "to cure the offensiveness of others, therapists and thought police are proliferating on campuses," while Barbara Ehrenreich charges that the American right has put multi-culturalism in the place of the international Communist conspiracy as its "evil, all-powerful ideological enemy."

Most disturbing in this volume are a few essays by radicals who shamelessly put their own ideological concerns before the basic principles of democratic freedom and liberal education. In an essay on multi-culturalism at the University of Texas, two professors, Ted Gordon and Wahneema Lubiano, write: "What we are talking about here is no less than transforming the university into a center of multi-cultural learning: anything less continues a system of education that ultimately reproduces racism and racists."

In another essay, Stanley Fish, a professor of English and law at Duke University, goes so far as to attack the First Amendment. "Speech, in short, is never and could not be an independent value," he writes, "but is always asserted against a background of some assumed conception of the good to which it must yield in the event of conflict." Such statements can only leave the reader with the conviction that some of the traditionalists' fears about political correctness are very real.
