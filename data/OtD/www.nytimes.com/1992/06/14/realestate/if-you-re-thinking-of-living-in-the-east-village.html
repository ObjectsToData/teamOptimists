If You're Thinking of Living in: The East Village

By BRET SENFT
Published: June 14, 1992

LAST month, after eight years uptown, Lisa Russell moved to the East Village. "It's funkier, and a lot more fun," said Ms. Russell, a People magazine writer. "Most of my life was happening here anyway -- there are more cafes, bars, movies."

In a Manhattan sobered by recession and social ills, those in search of a counterculture life style may find its last vestiges here. Rents are high, but food is cheap. And the multicultural mix includes a handful of anarchists serving as guilty conscience to a neighborhood seeking, privately, at least, to improve its qualtity of life without betraying its history of social consciousness. They hold mini-demonstrations and Friday night speakouts.

"This neighborhood is an example to the world of standing up against oppression and the police state," said John Potak, one of a group of loosely organized activists who, among other things, have set up a RAGE-ON! (Revolt Against Gentrification Erasing Our Neighborhood) hot line -- 713-5743 -- for details of coming demonstrations.

The area has known quieter days. It began as a farm, or bouwerie -- embracing what is now Fourth Avenue to the East River, 17th to Fifth Street -- bought by Peter Stuyvesant from the Dutch West India Company in 1651. To the east, the property's salt marsh was filled in and in 1834 became Tompkins Square Park.

Part of Stuyvesant's property in 1799 became the site of St. Marks-in-the-Bowery, a landmark church at Second Avenue and 10th Street that was damaged by fire in 1978 and restored in 1984.

Poor immigrants, initially Irish and German, flooded the area in the 1850's, spurring tenement construction. Italians and Eastern European Jews arrived in the early 1900's, Poles and Ukrainians after World War II, Puerto Ricans in the late 1940's, beatniks in the 50's and artists from Greenwich Village in the 60's.

Beth Flusser and her husband, Richard, moved to Stuyvesant Street 32 years ago and raised two daughters. "Back then it was an affordable place to live, with a 'new frontier' quality about it," she said. "There was no commercial activity."

Then came gentrification. Suzanne Wasserman, on Stuyvesant Street from 1982 to 1988, has since moved to Stuyvesant Town. "There were incredible changes during those years," she said. "It went from very quiet, all Ukrainian little shops, to yuppie restaurants everywhere and heavy traffic."

Changes include new, upscale Italian restaurants on East Fourth Street: Frutti di Mare, La Strada and Cucina di Pesce. Stingy Lulu's Luncheonette, a 24-hour, 50's-style diner, opened last month at 129 St. Mark's Place. Then there are the Indian restaurants -- about two dozen on Sixth Street between First and Second Avenues, with some on the avenues.

PERFORMANCE venues include La Mama E.T.C., 74A East Fourth Street; Theater for the New City, 155 First Avenue; the Living Theater, 272 East Third Street, and two multiplex theaters: Loew's Village Theater VII, 66 Third Avenue, and Village East City Cinemas, 189 Second Avenue.

A thriving gallery scene in the mid-80's all but vanished by the end of the decade. Pat Hearn, for one, had galleries on East Sixth and East Ninth from 1984 to 1989, but is now onWooster Street.

"The galleries needed more storage and office space," she said. "And the rents kept escalating -- for the price of a small storefront, people went to Broadway between Houston and Broome, for example, to buildings renovated specifically for galleries."

The neighborhood's funkiness seems to include graffiti-covered buildings, garbage strewn by the homeless collecting redeemables and heavy drug traffic.

"This is where people come to get drugs," said Rick Carmen, chairman of Community Board 3. "And for many without other economic opportunities, it is our neighborhood's biggest employer."

Yet the allure of bohemian decadence keeps housing prices up. The building stock includes "more five-story walk-ups than anything else," said Gary Brenner of City Estate Agency. Rents in these and in brownstones and renovated spaces, he said, are $600 to $1,000 a month for studios, $750 to $1,400 for one-bedrooms and $1,200 to $1,800 for two-bedrooms.

Luxury buildings went up during the 1980's. But more than half the owners in the Christodora House, an 85-unit condominium on Avenue B overlooking the park, have rented their spaces, waiting out the recession before selling, according to James Roman, sales manager for the Halstead Property Company, a brokerage, and Red Square, a high-rise rental at 250 East Houston built in the late 80's, "still has empty apartments and a steady turnover."

St. Mark's Real Estate, which handles rent-stabilized apartments, said studios fetch $600 to $700 and two-bedrooms, $1,000 to $1,100.

Tompkins Square Park, where there have been summer riots since 1988, was closed last June for renovation and is scheduled to reopen late in July. Its bandshell is being replaced by a portable stage.

"It was obsolete, an eyesore, a symbol for the anarchist-thugs," said Councilman Antonio Pagan, who also seeks limits to the area's social services burden, most recently, a proposed shelter on East Seventh Street for people with AIDS.

The Lower East Side has one-third of all such services citywide, many in the East Village, said Mr. Pagan, adding: "Such an over-concentration, in any neighborhood, is not healthy."
