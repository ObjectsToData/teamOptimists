Sex Without Fear

By Daniel J. Kevles;
Published: June 28, 1992

WOMAN OF VALOR Margaret Sanger and the Birth Control Movement in America. By Ellen Chesler. Illustrated. 639 pp. New York: Simon & Schuster. $27.50.

Ellen Chesler, in "Woman of Valor," brilliantly reveals and revitalizes Margaret Sanger in the context of the tempestuous public battles for birth control that she waged through the first half of this century. Sanger once remarked to a lover that she was "not a peaceful or restful person to know at all." Ms. Chesler has come to know her about as well as anyone could at this distance from her life -- she died in 1966 -- by interviewing friends, associates and relatives and by exploring a vast body of published and unpublished material, ranging from sizable caches of private papers to her file at the Department of Justice. The letters and diaries expose a great deal about Margaret Sanger that she did not want the public to learn during her lifetime, but that, as Ms. Chesler notes, she apparently wished history to see.

Ms. Chesler is an independent scholar whose many years of work on this biography were interrupted by extended service as chief of staff to former New York City Council President Carol Bellamy. She brings a keen sense of political nuance to the conflicts that surround issues of reproductive rights. She approaches her subject with critical sympathy and informed psychological insight. "Woman of Valor" is at once richly detailed and capaciously conceived -- authoritative, readable and rippling with the energy of the life it conveys.

Margaret Sanger, nee Higgins, grew up in Corning, N.Y., in an Irish-American family in which, Ms. Chesler contends, "one parent taught her to defy, the other to comport." The teacher of defiance was her father, Michael Higgins, a ne'er-do-well stonecutter who was an apostate Roman Catholic, militant freethinker, labor-rights advocate and social pariah. The model of comportment was her mother, who remained pious throughout her life, bearing 11 children and dying, worn out, at the age of 50.

MARGARET HIGGINS went to New York City to study nursing, and there, in 1902, she met and married William Sanger, a handsome young architect and aspiring painter. The product of an observant Jewish family, he was, like his new wife, a Socialist and a rebel against religious orthodoxy. In 1910, having been driven out of a suburban house following financial reverses, the Sangers returned to Manhattan, short on cash and with three young children to support. While Margaret Sanger worked as a visiting nurse and midwife, the couple got involved in the Socialist Party and with many of the day's radical reformers, including William (Big Bill) Haywood, the head of the Industrial Workers of the World, and the anarchist and feminist Emma Goldman.

Ms. Chesler emphasizes that Margaret Sanger's conversion to the cause of contraception owed a great deal to Goldman, who for some years had been insisting that much of the solution to what European Socialists called "the woman question" lay in birth control. Emancipated from the reproductive consequence of sex, Goldman reasoned, women would be freed from repressive forms of marriage and motherhood, liberated to realize themselves sexually and spiritually. Emma Goldman's doctrines moved Sanger toward an increasing impatience with the attitudes of male Socialists like Haywood, who spoke of an economic commonwealth where women could have "all the babies they pleased," and against the indifference of most radicals to Socialism's call for women "to revolt from sex slavery."

Sanger's sexually radical thinking was reinforced by her midwifery, which brought her into contact with impoverished women who resorted to self-induced abortions, risking death to end pregnancies that they did not know how to prevent. In 1914, Sanger began publishing a periodical entitled The Woman Rebel, declaring that women were enslaved by "sex conventions," "motherhood," and "middle-class morality" and promising that she would provide information on contraception to help emancipate them.

In August 1914, she was arrested and charged with violations of the Federal Comstock laws, which prohibited the dissemination of obscene materials, and -- since an article in her magazine had discussed political assassinations -- with incitement to murder. She fled to Europe to avoid prosecution, returning in 1915 as the public debate on the issue of birth control was mounting rapidly. In February 1916, after much of what she had published about contraception was now common magazine fare, all charges against her were dropped. Suddenly she was famous.

Sanger adjusted rapidly to celebrity, shedding her radical rhetoric to promulgate the doctrine of birth control as a means to achieve both sex without fear and social transformation through lowered birth rates, without class warfare. In 1916, she established the first birth control clinic in the United States in the Brownsville section of Brooklyn, advertising the facility in handbills written in English, Yiddish and Italian. During the early 1920's, she began publishing The Birth Control Review and organized the American Birth Control League. She fought opposition to birth control with persistence and flair. In 1929, when civic authorities forbade her to speak in Boston's Ford Hall, she stood silent on the stage, her mouth taped shut, while the Harvard University historian Arthur M. Schlesinger Sr. read her prepared statement.
