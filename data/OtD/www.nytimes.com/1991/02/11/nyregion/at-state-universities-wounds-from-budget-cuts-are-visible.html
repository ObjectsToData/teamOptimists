At State Universities, Wounds From Budget Cuts Are Visible

By SAM HOWE VERHOVEK, Special to The New York Times
Published: February 11, 1991

At the State University Center at Binghamton, courses once limited to 25 students now have 45. Sophomores trying to fulfill lab requirements in physics must wait until senior year because of cutbacks in the equipment budget. The running track has so many potholes that sprinters practice in a parking lot.

At the main campus of New Jersey's state university, Rutgers, in New Brunswick, class sizes are also growing, library hours have been cut and the wait for students to use a computer has stretched to as much as 36 hours.

At these universities and at dozens of other public colleges and universities in New York, New Jersey and surrounding states, the term "budget crisis" is no longer an abstract threat. As state governments cut spending to deal with growing budget shortfalls, the effects are being felt on campus. Withering of 'Public Ivy'

As a result, another crisis is growing: one of confidence in the ability of public colleges to maintain their hard-won standards.

"We are pulling every trick we know to keep up with our reputation as a public Ivy," said Peter E. Wagner, provost and vice president for academic affairs at Binghamton, one of the State University of New York's four flagship university centers. "But our ability to keep doing so is very limited."

Students and administrators complain of the erosion in quality that stems from cutbacks in everything from maintenance to faculty hiring. Alison Gillespie, a senior English major at Rutgers, said: "What bothers me is this feeling that you really have to put up a fight to get your education. Since I first came here, class sizes have increased unbelievably. You're not just competing for electives; you're competing to get into your required courses."

New Jersey's direct support to its universities and community colleges was cut to $765.4 million this fiscal year from about $800 million the year before. Gov. Jim Florio recently recommended a budget of $703.8 million for the coming year, representing a two-year drop of 12 percent.

Rutgers's appropriation is $216 million this year, down from $238 million the previous year, and a further 8 percent cut is expected for the next year.

In New York, the university system is preparing to cut nearly 10 percent from its $1.5 billion budget. At Binghamton, which says it needed $74.6 million this year to maintain all programs, a series of cuts left the campus with a revised 1990-91 budget of $71 million. Connecticut Also Hurting

The University of Connecticut has been told its $150 million state appropriation will be cut to $137 million. And Gov. Lowell P. Weicker Jr. has warned state agencies that a 20 percent spending cut may be necessary. That would reduce state financing for UConn by an additional $17 million. A university spokeswoman, Karen Grava Williams, said a $30 million cut could force the university to limit enrollment to 21,000. It now has 25,000 students.

Similar strains have been felt in all but a handful of states in the last few years, as the rate of increase in state financing for higher education has dropped to a 30-year low, according to a survey by the Center for Higher Education at Illinois State University.

But the effects have been starkest in the Northeast. The survey found that in maintaining spending compared with two years ago, Massachusetts ranked 50th, New Jersey 49th, Rhode Island 48th, New Hampshire 47th, Connecticut 46th and New York 44th.

The budget cuts are affecting every public campus, including the jewels of the systems, like Binghamton, which accepted only 36 percent of its applicants in 1989, and Rutgers, where its University College accepted only 29 percent. Cutbacks in Engineering

At Binghamton, budget cuts mean the engineering school will offer 20 fewer classes, down from 260, this semester, all because of delays in hiring replacements for departing faculty members. The nursing school has made similar reductions. The size of remaining classes has been increased by 5 to 15 percent, officials said.

The engineering school is already considering cutting the number of students it accepts next fall.

In the history department, lack of money for new faculty has left it without full-time appointments in colonial history and constitutional history.

In the library, subscriptions for more than 600 academic journals were canceled in the last year -- about 10 percent of the library's total. Legacy of the 80's

In some ways the campuses struggling with questions of where to cut are victims of their own past optimism. Having expanded programs and even enrollment during the 1980's, when department budgets sometimes grew well beyond the rate of inflation, they now have to scale back.

But to a large degree the schools are constrained in how they can deal with new fiscal realities. Personnel costs, which account for more than two-thirds of the budget at Binghamton, are mostly beyond the university's control; salaries and benefits of nearly all employees are negotiated by unions and the Cuomo administration.

Gov. Mario M. Cuomo recently called for a $500-a-year increase in annual tuition at the university, to $2,150. That would come on top of a $300 increase approved at the end of last year. At Rutgers, tuition for New Jersey residents has increased about 10 percent a year since 1982, to $2,856.
