Sullen Hero

By Camille Paglia;
Published: July 21, 1991

BRANDO A Life in Our Times. By Richard Schickel. Illustrated. 218 pp. New York: Atheneum. $21.95.

Marlon Brando is one of the most brilliant and charismatic artists of the 20th century. Like Elvis Presley, he is a supreme sexual persona, an icon who has entered our dreams and transformed the way we see the world. All contemporary American actors owe a debt to Mr. Brando and are in some sense in his shadow. He took the self-analytic and ensemble-based Stanislavskian Method of the Actors Studio in New York City to Hollywood and helped put an end, for good or ill, to the old paternalistic studio system, with its corporate populism and armylike cadres of polished technicians.

Today's young actors, emerging from comfortable, respectable homes and lacking access to the hardening experiences of factories, freighters or battlefields, search for masculinity by aping Marlon Brando. Sean Penn, Mickey Rourke, Sylvester Stallone -- the list of Brando imitators goes on and on.

Arrogant and manipulative, seething with raw sensitivities and burning rage, alternately harsh and kind, selfish and generous, Mr. Brando is a monumental personality of profound complexities and contradictions. He must be approached from the direction of other major Western artists suffering spiritual conflicts and thwarted ambitions: Byron, Keats, Caravaggio, Michelangelo.

Marlon Brando deserves a better biography than Richard Schickel has written. "Brando: A Life in Our Times" approaches vandalism in the dogged way that it strips everything dark, dangerous and titanic from its subject. What we have here is Marlon Brando reborn as Richard Schickel -- polite, bookish and perpetually worried. One of the grandest and most disorderly personalities of our era has been reduced to a pedestrian narrative of terminal blandness.

Mr. Schickel, a movie critic for Time and the author of biographies of Cary Grant and James Cagney, unwisely prefaces "Brando" with a long, unctuous, smarmy letter to the actor. A hand-wringing lamentation over "this wretched moment," when Mr. Brando's son Christian has been charged with the fatal shooting of his half-sister's lover, turns into a droning litany of all the wonderful similarities between the doting fathers Schickel and Brando. Mr. Schickel's suffocating, condescending compassion is a literary affectation forced onto a major performing artist, who operates in a totally different imaginative realm.

The book proper begins with a wandering account of the Group Theater and Stella Adler, Mr. Brando's New York acting teacher and mentor. Then we skip backward for a listless and patchy account of the actor's birth in 1924 and his upbringing in Nebraska and Illinois. The cultural origins of the family are ignored. Mr. Schickel latches onto one bright idea -- "Brando was the child of alcoholics" -- and runs it into the ground for the rest of the book. It explains nothing, for the world is full of children of alcoholics who did not become Marlon Brando.

Dropping out of military school, Mr. Brando at the age of 17 moved to New York, where his sisters were living. Mr. Schickel, pushing the parallels between Mr. Brando and himself (both are shy; both played the drums), now rhapsodizes about their arrival, 10 years apart and forever -- alas -- unknown to each other, in New York in its heyday. We follow Mr. Brando through his acting apprenticeship and first bit roles, leading to his Broadway appearances in "I Remember Mama" in 1944 and Maxwell Anderson's "Truckline Cafe," directed by Harold Clurman, in 1946.

Sparks began to fly when Mr. Brando was cast opposite Tallulah Bankhead in Jean Cocteau's short-lived play "The Eagle Has Two Heads" (later derisively nicknamed "The Turkey With Two Heads"). At their first meeting, Tallulah Bankhead was soon making drunken sexual advances. When the play opened in Wilmington, Del., he upstaged her by squirming, leering at the audience and staring into the wings. He was fired before the play got to New York. Mr. Schickel, whose psychological insight seems nonexistent, is stumped at Mr. Brando's simmering aggression and benignly labels it "sheer youthful devilry" or noble "disgust with the status quo."

Mr. Brando's big break came in 1947 when he won the leading male role in Tennessee Williams's "Streetcar Named Desire," a part that the producer first thought of offering to John Garfield. Mr. Schickel, lazily assuming everyone alive knows all about this, flubs badly. Marlon Brando's raw, brute, comic performance as Stanley Kowalski in the play and the 1951 film, both directed by Elia Kazan, was one of the most spectacular and explosive moments in modern art. It has come to symbolize American theater. Mr. Schickel just spreads a little schmaltz and hurries on.

Though he acknowledges that "rumors of homosexual behavior were rife" during Mr. Brando's early career, Mr. Schickel curtly dismisses them as pranks by the actor's roommate in New York, the comedian Wally Cox. There is also evidence of epic womanizing that Mr. Schickel mentions but loftily announces that he disdains to tell us about.
