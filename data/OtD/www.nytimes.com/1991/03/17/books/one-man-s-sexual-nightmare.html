One Man's Sexual Nightmare

By John L'Heureux; John L'Heureux's most recent novel is "An Honorable Profession." He is Lane Professor of Humanities at Stanford University.
Published: March 17, 1991

CHICAGO LOOP By Paul Theroux. 196 pp. New York: Random House. $20.

American by birth and itinerant by nature, Paul Theroux is in the great tradition of English writers who do it all and do it well: novels, short stories, travel books, literary criticism, books for children. Indeed, for all his critical and popular success in this country, he is perhaps even more popular in Britain, where he is regarded, rightly, as a major literary figure. Few American writers enjoy such trans-Atlantic success; few deserve it as richly as Paul Theroux.

Each of his books comes with the guarantee of a new, enriching and often disturbing experience. The newest, "Chicago Loop," is a study in dementia, an examination of one man's sexual nightmare, and if it charts new and unexplored territory for Mr. Theroux, it is nonetheless as absorbing as anything he has written.

It is summer 1988. Heat scorches the Midwest, fires burn out of control in the national forests, used syringes and bloody bandages wash up on the beaches in New York, there are muggings and hijackings, and the Presidential campaign is in full swing. In Chicago the bitter smell of rusting iron poisons the air and the sensational Robert Mapplethorpe exhibit is on display at the Museum of Contemporary Art. Chicago is sweltering in the unremitting heat and people are crazy, kinky. But none is so crazed and kinky as Parker Jagoda, a successful real estate developer and a good family man, who is about to commit a hideous murder.

Parker Jagoda has family money, but he works anyhow. He is an architect who has become a real-estate developer. Once he designed houses in meticulous detail -- not just the structure but the doors, the window frames, the shutters, every lock and shelf and ornament. Now, however, he demolishes buildings, guts them, finds ingenious ways of supporting classic, elegant facades that he can use to conceal jerry-built apartments, boutiques, expensive restaurants. He preserves appearances and trebles the profits. He controls the business with only minimal help. He likes control; he controls his life, his diet, and -- he thinks -- his appetites. His wife, Barbara, has taken off the last two years to have a child and is now about to return to work as a high-fashion model. They have careers and a baby, they have a BMW, they have good sex. And they work at keeping their rich and busy life exciting.

It is a life full of facades: the buildings, the glossy advertisements that sell products by promising sex, the wigs and the makeup and the incongruous clothing Parker sees everywhere, and the lies he tells, arbitrarily, compulsively. His daily life provides the perfect facade for concealing a murder.

Parker has set it up and he is about to make it happen. He has placed advertisements in the personals columns, each ad slightly different, each coded to appeal to the hunger, the need, the secret prurience of some special reader, somebody who's just asking for it. The personal ads are his undoing.

"He felt he was being absolutely impartial. They were no more than words. If a woman acted on them she had to take the consequences." Sharon -- "SWF, 5'4", 108 lbs, 'streaky blonde' " -- answers. She meets him in a bar, flirts with him, says "Touch me" and, in Parker's mind, determines her own fate. "She wants this to happen. She is making this happen," he thinks. " I am doing nothing except letting this happen. " He is merely an instrument, he tells himself, and for a moment he hates her and he hates being a man.

Then it happens: he kills her, horribly. The rest of the book unfolds -- ineluctably but unpredictably -- from this first and final murder.

Parker succumbs at once to a kind of willed amnesia. He represses the murder so completely that he cannot really believe he has done it. But events bring it home to him: a visit to the raw, challenging Mapple thorpe photographs, his urgent seduction of another potential victim who turns out to be a policewoman, and finally, incredibly, a kinky date with his wife, who dresses as a man and meets him in a seedy hotel room -- for a treat, for a surprise. He is stricken first with knowledge, then with remorse. He knows who he is at last and, in knowing, he loses control over his life.

Slowly, painfully, however, he regains a semblance of control as he works out in his mind just how he will satisfy his need for punishment. It must be apt. It must be total. He sets about looking for someone who will do to him exactly what he did to his victim. He is looking for his own killer. From this moment on he moves inevitably toward the awful, wrenching conclusion of this book, an ending that is right and just and completely unexpected.

This is a hard and compelling book. It is also unrelentingly bleak and grim. The world we see here is Parker's world. We see it wholly and only through his eyes, and what he sees behind the suburban facade of white houses and green lawns and smiling children is psychic and moral emptiness; what he sees behind the slick renovations of the Chicago Loop is a soulless sexuality that numbs the mind and dries up all appetite. Parker's is a narrow, ugly, empty vision of the world.
