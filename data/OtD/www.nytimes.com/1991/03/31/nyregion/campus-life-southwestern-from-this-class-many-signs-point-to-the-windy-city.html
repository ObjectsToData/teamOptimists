Campus Life: Southwestern; From This Class, Many Signs Point To the Windy City


Published: March 31, 1991

Once the social-science laboratory for the University of Chicago, the city of Chicago has become the site of an urban studies experiment for an interdisciplinary humanities course taught about a thousand miles away.

Some 40 students from Southwestern University in Georgetown, Tex., visited the city last week to see firsthand what they had been studying since January in University Studies Class 216, popularly called the Chicago course.

"Texans, in general, don't automatically see Chicago as a preferred destination," said Martha Allen, a history professor who teaches the course with Daniel Hilliard, a sociology professor, and Farley Snell, a religion professor.

But Professor Allen said a reading of literature from different academic disciplines pointed to a distinct trend: Chicago as a center of modern American thought. "Every area we looked at -- architecture, higher education, economics, art, literature, urban planning, social work -- they all pointed toward Chicago," she said.

Last week, her students were pointing at Chicago, specifically at the Art Institute; Clarke House, the oldest house in Chicago; the Pullman community, the company town that became a site of the infamous 1894 Pullman strike; the city's historical society to view the exhibit "Chicago in the 1890's," the very subject of their classroom work, and both the Frank Lloyd Wright home and studio and the Wright-designed Unity Temple in the suburb of Oak Park.

"I wanted to learn more about the history of the city," said June Chen, a senior biology major from Austin, Tex., who had visited Chicago on her own last summer. "Like the Frank Lloyd Wright tour -- His work is more interesting in person because we learned about it in class."

Developed under a National Endowment for the Humanities grant, the Chicago studies course was structured to teach students the interrelationships between humanities disciplines. Of the 18 credit hours that students receive, six are based on the study of Chicago.

With an enrollment of 42 students, Chicago studies is one of the most popular courses on campus. Indeed, Chicago itself seems to have captured Southwestern University's fancy. The college library has built a collection of more than 400 books about the city, while students, faculty and staff members have all worked to finance the class's Windy City trip.

Fund raising included helping to take inventory at the Austin Montgomery Ward; auctioning off student labor to do odd jobs for faculty and staff members, and holding a garage sale.

Then there was "the Jug," an empty bottled-water container for any spare change that students wanted to cast its way. "Even students who weren't signed up to go on the trip contributed," said Professor Allen. The jug total: $90.

"We studied it to be such a big city," said Harland DeWitt, a senior in American-studies from Houston. "But now that I'm here, it has a kind of weird, small-town feeling to it."
