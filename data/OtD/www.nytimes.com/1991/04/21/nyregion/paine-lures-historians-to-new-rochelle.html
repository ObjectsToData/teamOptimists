Paine Lures Historians To New Rochelle

By IAN T. MACAULEY
Published: April 21, 1991

"WHEN it shall be said in any country in the world, my poor are happy, neither distress nor ignorance is to be found among them; my jails are empty of prisoners, my streets of beggars; the aged are not in want, the taxes are not oppressive . . . when these things can be said, then may that country boast its constitution and its government."

Two hundred years later these words, which the British-American political theorist and author Thomas Paine presented in his 1791 classic, "Rights of Man," are still being contested. The Thomas Paine National Historical Association, the College of New Rochelle and the New Rochelle Public Library have sponsored a study of Paine's works and will present a daylong international symposium at the college on Saturday, beginning at 8:30 A.M.

"This is a chance for Westchester residents and other members of the public to get, free, a first-hand look at the works of a truly great thinker and at the same time sample the flavor of the controversy of the man," said Douglas A. Cooper, a lawyer in New Rochelle and the president of the Paine association, which also has maintains the local cottage where Paine lived part of his adult life. Memorabilia and Debate

Mr. Cooper said that as well as viewing a continuing archival exhibition of original and rare editions of Paine's works and memorabilia at the Thomas Paine Museum at North and Paine Avenues, the public can hear five international scholars at the symposium discuss and debate the merits of Paine's philosophy.

With his pamphlet "Common Sense," Thomas Paine "provided the intellectual basis for a separation from the British Crown as well as a compelling argument for the Declaration of Independence," according to a booklet published by the Paine association. And it was in his first "American Crisis Paper" that Paine spurred worn and weary Revolutionary soldiers with his words, "These are the times that try men's souls."

Moderated by Dr. James Schleifer, professor of history at the College of New Rochelle, the symposium will include the following addresses:

*"From the Rights of Man to the Rights of God: Paine's Ultimate Challenge" by Dr. Bernard Vincent, professor of history at the University of Orleans, France.

*"The Rights of Man and Rights of Nations" by Ian Dyck, professor of history at Simon Fraser University of Burnaby, British Columbia.

*"From Common Sense to Reason: The Progress of Equality" by Dr. Louise Marcil-Lacoste, professor of history at the University of Montreal.

*"The Rights of Man in America: The Celebration and Rejection of Thomas Paine" by Dr. Alfred F. Young, professor of history emeritus, Northern Illinois University in De Kalb.

*"The Influence of Thomas Paine on the Popular Press" by Dr. Helio Osvaldo Alves, professor of history at the University of Minho in Portugal.

Mr. Cooper said the idea for the symposium evolved from the June 1989 celebration in Paris of the bicentennial of the French Revolution. There Mr. Cooper met with Professor Vincent and the former British Labor Party leader Michael Foot. Two years later, he said, the idea is a reality, thanks to the efforts of the symposium's three sponsors and a grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities. From the Convention to Prison

Paine wrote "The Rights of Man" in France, Mr. Cooper said, where he contributed to that country's revolutionary cause, believing that "sharing in two revolutions was living to some purpose." His efforts got him into the French National Convention, the revolutionary governing body; imprisonment for 10 months by Robespierre, who felt Paine had become a threat to France, and eventual freedom thanks to the intervention of James Monroe, then American Ambassador to France.

The year after publication of "The Rights of Man," Paine, in absentia, was tried and convicted for treason in Britain, to which he never returned during his life. As the story goes, however, his bones, disinterred from his grave in New Rochelle, were sent back to England for burial in his homeland -- but disappeared.

"Our examination of 'The Rights of Man,' " Mr. Cooper said, "offers the opportunity to study the writer who fought for freedom and who believed that dignity was a right, not a charity."

But Paine had many opponents, Mr. Cooper noted, and one obituary in a New England newspaper described the writer thus: "Completely unprincipled, he was always uniform in conduct and never deviated from it into virtue or honor. His ends were to destroy fixed principles, and he never was scrupulous about means. He was useful to this country in the Revolution, but it was from vanity, not patriotism."

For information about the symposium, call the College of New Rochelle at 632-5300.
