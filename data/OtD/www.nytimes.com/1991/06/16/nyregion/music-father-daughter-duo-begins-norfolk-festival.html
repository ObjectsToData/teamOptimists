MUSIC; Father-Daughter Duo Begins Norfolk Festival

By ROBERT SHERMAN
Published: June 16, 1991

THE Norfolk Chamber Music Festival is about to begin its second half-century, as the Yale Summer School of Music takes up residence on the 70-acre Ellen Battel Stoeckel estate. From July 6 through Aug. 6, student recitals are given in the Music Shed on Thursday evenings at 7:30 and Saturday mornings at 10:15. But the Friday and Saturday programs have made the festival a major cultural event, since they feature faculty and guest artists like the Tokyo String Quartet, the Golub-Caplan-Carr and the Frankl-Pauk-Kirshbaum Trios, the flutist Samuel Baron, the clarinetist David Shifrin and the conductor Gustav Meier.

The festivities begin on Thursday afternoon at 4 with a benefit recital by the father-daughter, violin-piano duo of Claude and Pamela Frank, their program listing the Beethoven "Kreutzer" along with Sonatas by Mozart, Debussy and Schubert. Mozart pops again at most of the other recitals, including the annual Sunday afternoon concert by the Litchfield County Choral Union, on July 28 at 3 P.M., with Jonathan Babbitt conducting two Mozart settings of "Regina Coeli" plus the Grand Mass in C minor (K. 427). There also seems to be a Brahms-Dvorak festival within the festival, with nine major works on the schedule.

Newer music has always played an important role at Norfolk, and it does so again. An all-contemporary program presents premieres by young composers: on June 28, Vincent Persichetti's Serenade No. 6; on July 6, Ezra Laderman's "Celestial Bodies"; on July 19, a setting by Martin Bresnick of an Elizabethan dance "Lady Neil's Dumpe." The Aug. 10 finale is a Mozart-Copland sandwich, the American master's "Appalachian Spring" surrounded by the Overture to Mozart's "Magic Flute" and the A major Clarinet Concerto. For reservations, a season's brochure or driving directions, call 542-5537.

On the university's campus, Boris Berman has organized the Yale Summer Piano Institute. The pianist and professor said the three-week program "stands out from other training opportunities due to its unique blend of intensive piano performance study with master classes, lectures on various subjects in music and the humanities and visits to Yale's museum collections." Joining Mr. Berman on the faculty are Donald Currier, Seymour Lipkin and Ilana Vered.

Three student recitals and a benefit concert for the institute are open to the public. The free programs are scheduled on the next three Friday evenings (June 21 and 28 and July 5). The benefit -- an all-Mozart program by Mr. Currier and Mr. Berman, with three faculty colleagues -- the violinist Syoko Aki, the pianist Elizabeth Sawyer Parisot and the tenor Jerold Siena -- is set for June 25. All the recitals begin at 8 P.M. in Sprague Hall (432-4158).

The Chamber Music Conference at Kent is a similar training program, but with emphasis on strings. This year 60 amateur players are being coached by the violinist Rachmael Weinstock; David Clampitt, chairman of the music department at the Kent School, and the members of the Manhattan String Quartet. All are presenting recitals at the school this week in St. Joseph Chapel: the Manhattans play Bartok and Schumann Quartets on Tuesday at 8 P.M., Ravel, Shostakovich and Beethoven on Saturday at the same hour.

Both violinists will enjoy the piano partnership of Norman Carey. Mr. Weinstock's Wednesday concert is devoted to works of Brahms, Dvorak and Franck; Mr. Clampitt's Friday program is given over to Sonatas of Faure and Beethoven (672-0315).

Most orchestras have ended their spring seasons, but the Stamford Chamber Orchestra's season finale comes at 8 P.M. on Saturday at the Stamford Palace. The orchestra's music director, Laurence Gilgore, has chosen a romantic program, including Bizet's First and Schumann's Fourth Symphonies, plus the Violin Concerto of Samuel Barber, with Joseph Silverstein the guest soloist (359-0009).

The South Shore Music Club is presenting the Aureole Ensemble, winner of the 1989 Fischoff Competition, in its Young Artists Summer Series, at 8:30 P.M. on Friday at the Pequot Library in Southport. The Aureole -- which comprises Mary Hammann, Laura Gilbert and Barbara Allen -- has chosen an appealing program, including Trios of Arnold Bax, Harold Genzmer and Josef Jongen plus Debussy's Sonata for Viola, Flute and Harp (227-6943 or 226-1076). The ensemble will also open the Silvermine Chamber Music Series in New Canaan next Sunday evening at 7:30 (966-5618).

Bach, Haydn and Handel are the composers of choice this week at the Connecticut Early Music Festival. This evening at 6, in Harkness Chapel in New London, Rachel Rosales, Mark Bleeke and William Parker are each featured in one of Bach's solo Cantatas, then join voices in the "Coffee Cantata."

At 8 P.M. on Friday, at the Old Meeting House in Chester, vocal and instrumental artists will perform music written by Haydn in London during the same period (1793-1795) that the citizens of Chester were constructing the Meeting House. Saturday evening at 8 it's back to Bach with the complete "Musical Offering" (plus two Sonatas) played at Noank Baptist Church. And at 6 P.M. next Sunday at Harkness Chapel, Will Crutchfield, a former music critic for The New York Times, conducts a concert performance of Handel's opera "Amadigi" (444-2419).

At Music Mountain, the Chester String Quartet is in residence this week, playing Beethoven, Piston and Dohnanyi today at 3, and returning next Sunday at that same hour with more Beethoven -- the Op. 18 No. 6 Quartet, the Third Shostakovich and, with guest artist Karen Tuttle, Mozart's Viola Quintet in D (K. 593).

In between those recitals, the Saturday evening crossover series begins at 8, with original music composed (or improvised) and played by the flutist Rhonda Larson and the pianist Paul Halley, both members of the Paul Winter Consort (496-2596).
