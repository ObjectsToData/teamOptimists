BOOKS OF THE TIMES; English Intellectuals Between the Cataclysms

By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: May 14, 1991

Our Age English Intellectuals Between the World Wars: A Group Portrait By Noel Annan 479 pages. Random House. $30.

In "Our Age," Noel Annan, the eminent British writer and academician, takes on a sprawling and much documented subject. He proposes to write an intellectual history of that generation of British writers, thinkers, artists and opinion makers who came of age during the convulsive social period between the two World Wars.

Although it ranges over material examined by such earlier books as "Children of the Sun," by Martin Green, "The Great War and Modern Memory," by Paul Fussell, "The Auden Generation," by Samuel Hynes, and "The Brideshead Generation," by Humphrey Carpenter, Mr. Annan's book takes a decidedly more personal tone and point of view. A former provost of King's College, Cambridge, and vice chancellor of the University of London, Mr. Annan writes as a member of this generation, and as a colleague of many of the people he discusses. The result is an engaging, irreverent book that serves as a kind of testament to the author's own values of pluralism and liberal humanism.

How does Mr. Annan characterize the generation he so loftily calls "Our Age"? As he sees it, it was a generation that "played variations" on its "predecessors' themes." "Our Age produced no socialists comparable in invention and importance to Shaw, Wells, the Webbs and Tawney," he writes, "no writers of the stature of T. S. Eliot or Joyce or D. H. Lawrence, no thinkers of the originality of Russell or Keynes."

In other respects, he says, it was a generation that defined itself largely in adversarial terms. Having grown up with what Mr. Annan calls "the insufferable ideal" of the English gentleman -- an ideal of duty, diligence and good manners that was inculcated by Britain's public schools -- many "Our Age" intellectuals found themselves challenging the whole notion of an Establishment, with its implied privileges and prerogatives. In Mr. Annan's view, the two movements that shaped this outlook were modernism, with its gospel of alienation and radical originality; and pacifism, the legacy of World War I's horrors, which left Britain profoundly wary of involvement in another war and initially susceptible to the idea of appeasing Hitler.

In trying to convey the sensibility of "Our Age" intellectuals, Mr. Annan uses the cameo and the group portrait as means of illustration and example: perfect devices, given his wicked gift for observation and his easy familiarity with the works of individual writers and artists.

He juxtaposes portraits of Virginia Woolf and Lytton Strachey with descriptions of Brian Howard and Harold Acton to illuminate the disparate attitudes that members of Bloomsbury and the Oxford Wits took toward intellectual seriousness and hedonistic pleasure. And he contrasts the values of these two groups with those of the influential critic F. R. Leavis to demonstrate the continuing role that class -- or the perception of class values -- played in clashes among British intellectuals.

Orwell -- "a biting, bleak, self-critical, self-denying man of the idealist left" -- is contrasted with Cyril Connolly, "the circus master of London literary life," who regarded "frugality and austerity" as the "most repugnant words in the language." And C. P. Snow, who argued in "The Two Cultures" that science had a clear ascendancy over the humanities, is shown in a philosophical duel with Leavis.

"He cultivated to perfection the sneer which he used like an oyster-knife," says Mr. Annan of Leavis, "inserting it into the shell of his victim, exposing him with a quick turn of the wrist, and finally flipping him over and inviting his audience to discard him as tainted and inedible."

His descriptions of other intellectuals are considerably less judgmental, though no less entertaining. Of Wittgenstein, he writes: "His silences and fits and starts of speech as he struggled to weld his thoughts together defied anyone to scoff, and such was his charisma that his gestures -- the same groping for words and the same stammer -- were copied by his pupils."

He describes Virginia Woolf as "the protagonist of the private life," who "seemed to prove that the mind, which gave shape to feelings, was the ultimate in life and art." And he describes Anthony Blunt, the art historian who was exposed as the fourth man in the Cambridge spy ring, as "a fascinator," who "baited his conversation with gossip, inside gossip, gossip to which only he had access."

When Mr. Annan leaves the specifics of history and character behind to speculate about his generation's overall attitudes, he tends to make some overly broad generalizations. It's impossible to sum up what an entire generation thinks about changes in the literary or moral climate, and attitudes toward individual politicians and issues (feminism, the Common Market, gay liberation, etc.) are even harder to sum up.

The reader, however, may happily skim over such fuzzy passages, while enjoying Mr. Annan's elegant, erudite meditations on his friends and colleagues. All in all, this is a spirited, intelligent and provocative book, a history of ideas that is at once a portrait of and an elegy for a fascinating generation.
