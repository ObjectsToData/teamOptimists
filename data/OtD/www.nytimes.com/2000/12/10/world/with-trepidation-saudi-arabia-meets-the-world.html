With Trepidation, Saudi Arabia Meets the World

By SUSAN SACHS
Published: December 10, 2000

Prince Sultan bin Salman is a familiar face in Saudi Arabia. A strapping former Air Force pilot, he flew as an astronaut on an American space shuttle 15 years ago, is a grandson of the nation's founder, the son of a well-known governor, and now the nation's tourism chief.

But on a recent trip to a village market near Taif he saw firsthand what it might be like to arrive a stranger in the conservative and provincial kingdom.

The prince and his aides pulled out their cameras to take photographs of the market scene, all part of their research for the budding tourism industry. It took but an instant, the prince recalled, before they were the center of hostile attention from the local people. One old man, sure they were outsiders with bad intentions despite their traditional Saudi dress, grabbed a big stick and chased them.

The incident underlined the challenges facing Saudi Arabia as it attempts to open up to influences, and visits, from the world outside. Its lesson was not lost on Prince Sultan. ''You just can't bring thousands of people in and let them out and about,'' he said, in an analysis that applies to much more than his own tourism project. ''We are going to move carefully and cautiously.''

Though they have yet to bear fruit, reforms have been set in motion that aim to diversify the economy and create jobs. But the overriding question is whether the royal family can execute economic reforms and open up the kingdom of 22 million people without also sharpening demands for political change.

Crown Prince Abdullah, Saudi Arabia's de facto ruler, has proposed several steps over the last two years. The government has for the first time offered foreigners the chance to own property and open a business without a Saudi national as a majority stakeholder. And a new General Investment Authority has been set up to seek foreign investors in gas development, power industries and desalination of sea water.

The government has said it intends to set up a capital market, which would require new standards of openness for Saudi companies. It has also started work on reforming its legal system and trade regulations, all in pursuit of membership in the World Trade Organization. And it has signed international treaties and conventions on human rights.

Each of these steps would mean that Saudi Arabia, a country that follows a combination of Islamic law and ancient tribal traditions, will be pressed to acknowledge international norms. And in those cases where its own norms differ, Saudi Arabia will be pressed to explain itself as never before.

''At the high levels, the interest is there and the thinking is clear,'' said Saleh Mutabbakani, a businessman in Jidda who has put together ventures with foreign partners.

But many Saudis simply resist change. ''They might not know why they are against it, but they are,'' he said. ''One problem is that religion and tradition have melted together and people don't think about the difference.''

For a society accustomed to its king and princes, where foreign publications are censored, where the study of Western philosophy is banned, and where women may appear publicly only if swathed in black cloth, there are minefields ahead.

''We are motivated to join the W.T.O. and be part of the wider world and its institutions,'' said a senior Saudi official. ''But it's a trial. As we look at how we are reflected in the eyes of the world, we are going to see what's missing in our reflection.''

The kingdom has responded fiercely to unexpected change in the past. In 1965, then King Faisal, a modernizer with a flair for dramatic gestures, introduced television. Tribal leaders and members of the king's own family, including a nephew who ultimately assassinated the king, denounced the newfangled invention as the work of the devil.

When American and European soldiers, male and female, poured into Saudi Arabia for the Persian Gulf war 10 years ago, a group of educated women in Riyadh, emboldened by the presence of Westerners, flouted the ban on women drivers. They were arrested, and those who worked lost their jobs. Alarmed at the intrusion of the soldiers, radical Islamic preachers attacked the Saudi ruling family. The preachers were arrested.

Liberals and conservatives alike sent letters to the king asking for more participation in decision making. They were arrested or vilified in semiofficial newspapers, but King Fahd finally made good on a promise by his elder brother in 1956 to create an appointed council to advise him.

Introducing foreign ideas about competition, freedom of movement and human rights could again provoke a public backlash, some Saudi officials acknowledged, but not an earthquake. ''The dynamics here have changed,'' said Prince Sultan. ''The country is different than it used to be.''

Still, each change may have a ripple effect on the country's political dynamics, many Saudis said. For example, if companies are required to follow standard accounting rules, demands may also intensify for more openness in the government.
