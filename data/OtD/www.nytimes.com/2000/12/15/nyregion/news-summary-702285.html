NEWS SUMMARY


Published: December 15, 2000

INTERNATIONAL A3-22

Yemeni Leader Steers A Delicate Course

President Ali Abdullah Saleh said he would support American efforts to seize Osama bin Laden, the F.B.I's most wanted terror suspect, if Mr. bin Laden were found to have inspired or directed the bombing of the destroyer Cole in Yemen on Oct. 12. A22

U.S. Posts in Turkey Closed

United States consulates in Istanbul and Adana, in southern Turkey, will be closed indefinitely because of fear of terrorist attacks, officials said. A5

Push to Revive Mideast Talks

Israel's foreign minister, Shlomo Ben-Ami, and Gilad Sher, chief of staff to Prime Minister Ehud Barak, began meetings with Yasir Arafat to try to renew peace talks. A19

Putin Meets Castro in Havana

President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia and President Fidel Castro of Cuba pledged to reinvent their countries' relationship, which foundered after the Soviet Union's demise. It will be based on a modest agenda of trade and commerce and a bit of ideological alignment. A18

Russia Frees American

Edmond Pope, 54, a businessman and former Navy officer convicted of espionage by a Russian court this month and sentenced to 20 years in prison, flew to Germany after being pardoned by President Vladimir V. Putin. A18

Closing Down Chernobyl

The last operating reactor at the Chernobyl nuclear complex in Ukraine will be shut down today, 14 years and 7 months after an explosion and fire that spewed radiation across Central Europe. A3

International Crime Syndicates

The Clinton administration, saying networks of international criminals are a real and growing national security threat, plans to issue a study today that documents the rise of transnational criminal syndicates that know no boundaries to their illicit activities. A9

Clinton's Reminder on the Poor

President Clinton urged developed countries not to forget the poor in an era of high technology and globalization. He spoke in Britain, concluding what was likely to be his final overseas trip as president. A18

Chirac Denies Accusations

President Jacques Chirac of France forcefully denied knowledge of kickback schemes to aid his party while he was mayor of Paris, though he said it was widely understood that all parties had relied on illicit fund-raising up until 1995. In the television interview, Mr. Chirac, who was mayor from 1977 to 1995, said he knew nothing about such matters because he never dealt with party finances. A13

New Life for Congo Mission

The United Nations Security Council lengthened the duration and doubled the number of monitors of a troubled advance mission preparing the way for a peacekeeping force in Congo. A7

World Briefing A12

NATIONAL A24-37

Bush and Cheney Begin Reaching Out to Democrats

President-elect George W. Bush and his running mate, Dick Cheney, signaled their interest in working with Democrats during a series of meetings and phone calls. Mr. Bush is to meet today with Senator John B. Breaux, the centrist Democrat from Louisiana who produced a plan for overhauling Medicare similar to Mr. Bush's own. Mr. Cheney said the transition team would be ''aggressive'' in seeking Democrats for high-ranking administration jobs. A1

Some conservative leaders argued against appointing Democrats and moderate Republicans. A34

Speaker J. Dennis Hastert said he did not favor the $1.3 trillion across-the-board tax cut proposed by Mr. Bush, his fellow Republican, preferring cuts aimed more narrowly at married couples and heirs. A1

The presidents of M.I.T. and Caltech said their science-and-engineering universities would team up to try to improve the voting system. A35

Budget Fight Over Sea Lions

Congress and the Clinton administration are poised to finish the government's $600 billion annual spending plan, though the White House and an Alaska senator squared off for one more fight -- over sea lions. A32

Warning on Military 'Burden'

Gen. Henry H. Shelton, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, said the military faced ''an unsustainable burden'' caused by shrinking forces, old equipment and the pace of operations. A26

Faulty Drug Tests

The Transportation Department announced rules to protect transportation workers who undergo mandatory drug testing, but critics said they did not go far enough. The move came as new evidence emerged that tests could mistakenly brand innocent workers as drug abusers. A24

Manhunt in South Texas

The authorities were searching for seven armed men who escaped from a maximum security prison near Kenedy on Wednesday by impersonating a civilian maintenance crew and overpowering their guards. A24

NEW YORK/REGION B1-16

City Offers Landlords Water at a Flat Rate

New York City is offering landlords of 72,000 buildings with six or more units the option of a flat-rate water bill, rather than metered service, no matter how much water tenants use. Landlords must show that at least 70 percent of the plumbing fixtures are types that conserve water. A1

Neediest Cases B4

EDUCATION

State Aid Plan Draws Fire

Schools Chancellor Harold O. Levy denounced a $14.5 billion school aid proposal by the State Board of Regents, calling it ''grossly unjust'' because New York would get less per pupil than other cities. B1

SCIENCE/HEALTH

Ruling on Bias and the Pill

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission ruled that employers discriminate against women when they offer insurance coverage for preventive health care like drugs to lower blood pressure, but exclude prescription contraceptives. A1

Altered Gene Extends Life

Researchers said they had doubled the life span of fruit flies by slightly disabling a single gene that is involved with their metabolism. A24

SPORTS D1-8

WEEKEND E1-36; E37-52

National Medal of the Arts

President Clinton recognized 24 people in the arts and humanities, including Maya Angelou, Quincy Jones, Toni Morrison, Itzhak Perlman, Harold Prince and Barbra Streisand. E32

OBITUARIES C15

George Montgomery

The actor who went from prizefighter to stuntman to star before turning his energies to sculpture, furniture-making and painting was 84. C15

BUSINESS DAY C1-16

AOL-Time Warner Deal

The F.T.C. approved the proposed merger of America Online and Time Warner, clearing the way for the creation of the world's biggest media company. The companies agreed to stringent conditions intended to ensure competition in providing Internet services and the interactive television systems of the future. A1

Health Care Fraud Settlement

HCA-the Healthcare Company, the nation's largest hospital company, agreed to pay $95 million in criminal penalties and plead guilty to charges that it had obtained some of its money by cheating government health care programs. HCA has now agreed to pay a total of $840 million in criminal and civil penalties so far this year, with part of the civil investigation still to be resolved. C1

A Warning from Microsoft

Microsoft said after the markets closed that earnings and revenue in the current quarter would be lower than previously projected because of weakening economic conditions. The statement echoed recent warnings from computer makers that are Microsoft's leading customers. C1

Bankers Lower Their Sights

Executives of Chase Manhattan and J. P. Morgan warned that fourth-quarter earnings would disappoint investors, though they said the weakness in their trading and investing businesses would not affect Chase's pending acquisition of Morgan. C1
