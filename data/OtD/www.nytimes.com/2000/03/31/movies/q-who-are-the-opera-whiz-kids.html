Q. Who Are the Opera Whiz Kids?

By ANTHONY TOMMASINI
Published: March 31, 2000

If you tune in tomorrow afternoon to the Texaco Opera Quiz, the popular intermission feature that is part of the live radio broadcasts of the Metropolitan Opera, don't be surprised if the three panelists sound rather young to be experts.

Rachel Speight, 15, is a sophomore in the vocal music department of the High School for the Performing and Visual Arts in Houston. Mahan Esfahani, 16, a classical pianist and theater enthusiast, is a junior at Richard Montgomery High School in Rockville, Md. And Evan Diamond, 17, a senior from East Meadow High School on Long Island who plays saxophone in his school jazz band, has become an opera enthusiast through taking college-level Italian courses.

If these students are not yet experts, they are at least experts-in-training. They are the Texaco Quiz Kids, as the producers of the broadcasts are calling this first-ever panel of high school students. Their knowledge and suitability have been tested through a series of regional competitions in the New York, Houston and Washington areas. A total of 57 students from 18 schools competed. Three semifinalists were selected in each region. Final rounds in the three cities produced the three panelists who will be on the air tomorrow during the second intermission of Wagner's ''Walkure.'' (The quiz should start about 3:35 p.m.)

These panelists will hold the same seats once occupied by such renowned authorities and raconteurs as the conductor Julius Rudel, the critic and translator Andrew Porter and the playwright Terrence McNally.

For making it to the panel, each student has won a crystal trophy; gift certificates to Texaco's online store; a parcel of CD's; a free trip to New York for themselves, a parent and a teacher; seats in the Texaco box for tomorrow's performance; a private backstage tour of the Met; tickets to ''Kiss Me Kate'' on Broadway; and, of course, the experience of being a panelist on the quiz program, heard by millions of opera buffs on more than 325 stations in North America, Australia, New Zealand, Brazil and 27 European countries.

The producer of the intermissions features, Michael Bronson, thinks presenting what he expects to become an annual Texaco Quiz Kids panel is a logical step.

''The original mission statement from Texaco when the company started sponsoring the broadcasts in 1940 was that the intermission features should be entertaining and informative,'' Mr. Bronson said. ''One area I felt we could do better with was in making listeners aware of the importance of music education: that young people can and do enjoy opera. This is critical if we are going to have people sitting in the seats 20 or 30 years from now.''

Mr. Bronson said that his office often receives letters and quiz questions from listeners seeking advice on how to introduce opera to children. The idea of a student panel grew out of talks among Mr. Bronson; Diane Herzman, who is the manager of the Texaco-Metropolitan Opera sponsorship; and David Dick, the director of education for the Metropolitan Opera Guild, which was founded in 1935 and is the oldest outreach department of any American opera company.

Mr. Bronson proudly reported that for the final round in Washington in February, held at the auditorium of the American Film Institute in the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts, a teacher from a participating school brought her whole class to see the final face-off. Mr. Esfahani, the winner, was presented his award by Supreme Court Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg, a big opera fan.

Because this is the first year of the Texaco Quiz Kids project, the organizers limited the search for panelists. ''The idea was to reward schools that already included the study of opera, and the attendance of operas, as part of their curriculums,'' Mr. Bronson said. The Met's Opera Guild has long run such outreach programs, as has the Houston Grand Opera and the Washington Opera, which is why those cities were the first selected.

A chance to see how the competition worked came in mid-February, when the three semifinalists from the New York region assembled for the showdown at the studios of WQXR in Manhattan, which carries the opera broadcasts. The oldest regional semifinalist was Ali Kagan, 18, a soft-spoken and articulate woman who attends the Gunnery, a private school in Washington, Conn. Her musical background is limited to a few years of flute lessons.

''I do listen to the opera broadcasts on occasion,'' she said before the final round started. ''At least when I am not with friends on Saturday who don't really appreciate opera as much as I do.'' Being a young opera fan can still brand you among your peers, she said. This is something the Texaco Quiz Kids project is working to change.

Ms. Kagan was introduced to opera by the teacher who runs the humanities program at her school, Tom Adolphson, whose dogs are named Fasolt and Fafner after the giants in Wagner's ''Ring.'' He organizes regular school trips to dress rehearsals at the Met. ''These kids are closer to this art form than they realize,'' Mr. Adolphson said. ''I always tell them that everything they see on the opera stage in the evening is played out in terms of drama at the student center every day.''
