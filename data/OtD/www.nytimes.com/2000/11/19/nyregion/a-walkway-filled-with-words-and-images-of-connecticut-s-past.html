A Walkway Filled With Words and Images of Connecticut's Past

By ALBERTA EISEMAN
Published: November 19, 2000

THE crew of a whaling ship in New London harbor, circa 1907; a smiling young Algonquian man, portrayed in 1645; munitions workers during World War II; a Yankee peddler in his horse-drawn wagon; the legendary Charter Oak -- these are some of the images, well known or unfamiliar, chosen to tell the story of Connecticut in an exhibition mounted along the walkway between the Capitol and the Legislative Office Building in Hartford.

The 25 historical images, collectively entitled ''Imagining Connecticut,'' set the scene for a group of interconnected programs designed to introduce the state's history to the thousands of residents and tourists who visit the two adjoining structures: the grand, gold-domed Victorian Capitol and the sleek office building dedicated in 1988.

''The whole story of Connecticut is not found anywhere else in the state, and this is one of our great public spaces,'' said Bruce Fraser, executive director of the Connecticut Humanities Council, which produced and financed the project with the support and assistance of the Joint Committee on Legislative Management.

''People come here to visit, to attend meetings, to lobby their representatives: little kids, seniors, casual visitors with little understanding of Connecticut and people who spend a great deal of time here and are intimately connected to the process. We wanted to reach them all, so we needed to set up a series of programs that would satisfy the varied audiences,'' he said.

Nothing was left to chance. To create ''Imagining Connecticut,'' Mr. Fraser gathered together a panel of historians who hammered out the themes for the display.

''What essential 15 or 20 things do we want people to appreciate about our distinct past?'' Mr. Fraser asked at their first meeting. ''What images best capture these points and tell us who we are?''

And so the viewer is presented with an early painting of the three churches on New Haven's town green, a choice intended to emphasize the importance of the state's strict religious origins; a daguerreotype of workers at the Eli Terry Clock Factory, which pays homage to Connecticut's inventors and their key role in the industrial revolution. Mark Twain and his neighbor, Harriet Beecher Stowe, represent the state's cultural heritage; a portrait of James Mars -- a little known, but noteworthy former slave who led a movement to extend voting rights to African-Americans -- reminds visitors that the struggle for equal representation was long and hard, and that slavery was not abolished in the state until 1848.

The most ambitious component of the project, on view in the atrium of the Legislative Office Building, is an eight-panel, 500 square foot, permanent exhibition on the history of self-government in Connecticut, whose title, ''An Orderly and Decent Government,'' is a quotation from the Fundamental Orders, drawn up in 1639 by early colonists.

''To mayntayne the peace and union for such a people there should be an orderly and decent Government established,'' wrote the doughty Puritans. They also proclaimed the radical ideas that the authority of government came from the ''free consent'' of those governed and that ''representation,'' or letting the people choose their own leaders, was the way to make government work.

''Their handiwork,'' the exhibition states, ''was the world's first document putting in practice the principles of self-government'' -- in other words, the basis for Connecticut's claim to the title ''Constitution State,'' an assertion some scholars find debatable.

Dense with pictures and text, the exhibition presents the history of the state in detail and shows the influence of the general assembly on it. Each panel is divided into three concurrent tracks. The upper third presents major trends and developments at the national and state level -- wars, migrations, economic and political upheavals -- and how they shaped the period under consideration. The middle track, ''Searching for the Common Good,'' explores the major issues created by these forces, and how the legislature addressed them. At the bottom, ''Making Self-government Work,'' traces the story of the legislature itself as it evolved over time, from a single legislative chamber to the complex institution of today, from a gathering of white, male property owners to the diverse House of Representatives and Senate of our time.

Unhurried visitors will be rewarded with dozens of intriguing episodes: the religious controversies of the 1740's, for example, as Anglicans, Quakers, Baptists and other groups entered the colony and pressured the General Assembly to recognize their religious practices; or, after statehood, the westward migration of thousands of the state's people in search of fertile land -- 750,000 men and women left Connecticut between the years 1760 and 1840; the exploits of the ''privateers,'' privately owned vessels authorized by the state to attack British ships during the War of 1812, or the rise of the ''Know-Nothing'' party, which took control of the General Assembly in 1855 and quickly passed a tough anti-Irish, anti-Catholic program.

No effort is made to gloss over the less appealing aspects of Connecticut's heritage.

''What we worked hard on in this exhibition is trying to tell the truth,'' Mr. Fraser said.

History buffs and other visitors who want to learn more about a particular topic can turn to ''History on Demand,'' an interactive video station located between the two exhibitions. The station offers 15 brief vignettes on such issues as the Fundamental Orders, the legend of the Charter Oak, the clash between Yankees and immigrants or the struggle of women to win the vote.

A handsome, short brochure on the history of the General Assembly and of the state is available as part of this multi-faceted program. Moreover, a collection of resource materials and teacher guides is being readied for fall distribution, so that tomorrow's voters can better understand the heritage of Connecticut.

The Capitol and the Legislative Office Building are open to the public Monday through Friday from 8 a.m. to 5:30 p.m. Tours of the two buildings are offered by the League of Women Voters at quarter after the hour from 9:15 a.m. to 2:15 p.m., free of charge. Tour information is available by calling (860) 240-0222.
