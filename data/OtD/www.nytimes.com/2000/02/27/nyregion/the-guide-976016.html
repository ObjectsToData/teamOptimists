THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: February 27, 2000

Dali and Lorca

In conjunction with the Salvador Dali exhibition, the Wadsworth Atheneum will honor one of the painter's friends, Federico Garcia Lorca, the poet, on Thursday at 7:30 p.m. in the museum theater.

The relationship between the two men will be discussed by Wayne H. Finke, professor of modern languages at Baruch College in New York. His talk will be followed by readings of Lorca's poetry in Spanish and English by Naomi Ayala, a poet, educator and community activist, and by Rennie McQuilken, poet and director of the annual Sunken Garden Poetry Festival at the Hill-Stead Museum in Farmington.

Lorca, known in Spain as a poet of the people, and recognized widely as one of his country's most celebrated literary figures, is probably best known for his lyric drama ''Blood Wedding,'' and for ''Gypsy Ballads.'' He died in 1936 at the age of 38, when he was executed by the Fascists for his Republican-left sympathies and, some scholars believe, for his homosexuality.

The Atheneum is at 600 Main Street in downtown Hartford. Admission to the program is free. For more information call (860) 278-2670.

Rubin Carter's Story

The story of Rubin (Hurricane) Carter will be told by the former boxing champion himself at 7 p.m. Wednesday at Sacred Heart University's theater, 5151 Park Avenue in Fairfield.

Twice convicted in a triple murder case in 1966, he was sentenced to three life terms. His fortitude during his long ordeal in prison has been dramatized in the new film, ''Hurricane,'' starring Denzel Washington.

In 1974 his autobiography, ''The 16th Round: From Number 1 to Number 45472,'' attracted international attention that intensified with the retractions of testimony by the state's two key witnesses, who confessed to lying at his trial.

A new trial was held, but he was again convicted. Finally, in 1985 he was released and the indictment was dismissed in 1988.

Today he is a board member of Human Rights in Atlanta, Alliance for Prison Justice in Boston, and the Association in Defense of the Wrongfully Convicted in Toronto. He has lectured at numerous universities including the law schools at Yale and Harvard.

Admission will be $5 and the number for more information is (203) 365-7614.

Visions Forum

Fairfield University's next Visions Forum, to be held tomorrow at 7:30 p.m.in the Quick Center for the Arts, will feature Ken Burns, creator of documentaries for public television.

While Mr. Burns began producing programs in 1981, including ''Brooklyn Bridge,'' ''Huey Long,'' and ''The Statue of Liberty,'' his fame as a producer, director, writer, cinematographer, editor and music director, took a leap in 1990 with his 11-hour series, ''The Civil War.'' It won 40 awards including two Emmys and two Grammys.

In 1994 he presented ''Baseball,'' an 18-hour documentary that is said to have been the most-watched series in PBS history, and he has continued to research and present American icons on film with ''Lewis and Clark'' in 1997, ''Frank Lloyd Wright'' in 1998, and ''Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Susan B. Anthony'' in 1999.

Tickets are $15, $10 for adults over 64, $5 for students. The box office number is (203) 254-4010.

Irish Arts and Letters

''The Dead'' (1987), John Huston's last film, opens the Fairfield University Festival of Irish Arts and Letters today at 2 p.m. Based on the James Joyce classic, with a cast headed by Huston's daughter Angelica, it will be shown in the Kelley Theater of the Quick Center for the Arts. Donal Donnelly, a member of the cast, will introduce the film.

Five additional films will be screened weekly through March 26, dating from ''The Informer'' (1935), directed by John Ford and starring Victor McLaglen, to ''The Butcher Boy'' (1998) directed by Neil Jordan, with Stephen Rea, Eammon Owens, Milo O'Shea and an all-star cast. Tickets are $5.

Additional offerings include an art exhibition titled ''Contemplative Myths: Images by Contemporary Irish Women Artists,'' opens today through April 8 in the Quick Center lobby. Viewing hours are Tuesday through Saturday from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m., Sunday from noon to 4.

''By The Bog of Cats'' by Marina Carr will presented as a dramatic reading on Thursday and Friday at 8 in the Kelley Theater. Admission will be $5.

Call (203) 254-4010 for tickets or more information.

Antiques and History

Guilford holds its 31st Antiques Show and a concurrent History Exposition on Saturday from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. and next Sunday from 11 to 5 at the Adams Middle School on Route 77, a half-mile south of Interstate 95 Exit 58.

About 40 selected Northeastern dealers will display painted country furniture, oriental rugs, garden accessories, redware, 18th- and 19th-century English ceramics, period native American jewelry, European formal furniture, fireplace accessories, export porcelain and paintings.

In addition, a broad range of unusual items includes 19th- and early 20th-century botanical prints, tall clocks, Civil War firearms, antique lighting, period walking sticks, English and American tea caddies, and Victorian drapery tiebacks.

This year's exposition theme is ''Care of Old Houses and Collectibles,'' to be explored in five lectures on Saturday, hourly from 10:30 a.m. to 2:30 p.m. Admission of $5 covers the show and the lectures.

On Sunday a walking tour of the show will be offered at 10 a.m. for a $10 charge that includes show admission and refreshments. From noon to 3 p.m. verbal appraisals will be available from a panel of dealers. The fee is $5 an item, or $10 for three items. For more information call (203) 458-9087.

National Security

Sidney Drell, a physicist and arms-control specialist, will deliver two lectures at Connecticut College in New London this week.

On Wednesday he will discuss ''Science and U.S. National Security'' in the Blaustein Humanities Center, and on Thursday he will talk about ''The Rudman Report: The Investigation into Security Problems at U.S. Nuclear Labs by the President's Foreign Intelligence Board.'' The second program will be held in Oliva Hall of the Cummings Art Center. Both talks begin at 8 p.m. and are open to the public free of charge.

Dr. Drell, who is professor emeritus at the Stanford Linear Accelerator Center of Stanford University, is an adviser to the Clinton Administration on arms control and foreign intelligence. His published papers and books on theoretical physics have been widely translated, and he has been chairman on many government panels on nuclear weapons, technology and intelligence. His awards include two Guggenheim Fellowships, a John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation Fellowship, the Leo Szilard Award, and Ernest Orlando Lawrence Memorial Award, among others.

The college is at 270 Mohegan Avenue. Call (860) 439-2508 for more information. ELEANOR CHARLES
