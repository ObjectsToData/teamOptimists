SCREEN GRAB; On the Trail of the Underground Railroad

By MICHAEL POLLAK
Published: February 24, 2000

MUCH of the history of American slavery has long been left unexplored out of shame and ignorance, but also because the search for truth has been too quickly abandoned.

''A lot of historians and history books have excluded the Underground Railroad,'' said Anthony M. Cohen, a writer and lecturer on black history, ''usually citing the idea that because it was an illegal system and a secret system, they didn't keep records. Well, they did.''

There has been an explosion of writing about slavery in the last decade, particularly about slave resistance, and Mr. Cohen's site is one of many emerging on the Web.

In 1996 and 1998, Mr. Cohen, 36, of Silver Spring, Md., made two long walks north along routes used by escaping slaves. He traveled on foot along some of the same one-lane country roads, through swamps, and occasionally by boat and train. Recreating the escape method used by Henry Brown (nicknamed Box), Mr. Cohen even had himself crated and shipped by freight car. Like Mr. Brown, he was almost misrouted by a wrong address label.

Mr. Cohen is descended from a black man who was born in Philadelphia during the Civil War, orphaned and later adopted by a Jewish family in Delaware. His interest in the Underground Railroad was stimulated in part by his desire to learn more about his black roots. He has established the Menare Foundation (www.ugrr.org) to help document, preserve and restore safe houses on the Underground Railroad. (The foundation takes its name from the Italian word meaning ''to conduct,'' which was a password used by escaped slaves crossing the Ohio River.)

Mr. Cohen's account of his 1998 journey is published on the foundation's Web site, along with advice on documenting historic houses. The site, which is being expanded, includes guides for teachers and students. One of its links is the Underground Railroad Project of the Vermont Council on the Humanities (www.cit.state.vt.us:80/vhs educate/ugrr.htm).

In a telephone interview, Mr. Cohen recounted the wealth of little-noticed evidence that has identified the once-secret safe houses: family letters, diaries, trap doors and secret rooms over eaves and under barns, and interviews with former slaves in state archives and the Library of Congress.

''People are carriers of history,'' Mr. Cohen said. ''I met people along the route who were 80 or 90 years old who had met former slaves who had themselves been 80 or 90 years old, and who could share direct personal experience.''

There is also the work of scholars like Dr. Wilbur H. Siebert of Ohio State University, who compiled a list in 1898 of ''station masters'' who guided slaves north. Mr. Siebert's list, sometimes of questionable accuracy but a valuable starting point, is on the Menare Web site.

While he was retracing the escape paths, Mr. Cohen wrote of the friendship of strangers. He felt, he said, as if he were being passed from one station to another. The surviving safe houses, he said, are ''a tangible reminder of man's humanity.''

Among other resources on the World Wide Web, the National Underground Railroad Freedom Center www.undergroundrailroad .com/home.html is planning an $80 million interactive museum and research building to open in 2003 in Cincinnati, an important stop on the railroad. Its Web site already has articles on fugitive slaves and those who helped them.

The National Park Service's sites are among the most thorough on the Internet. Aboard the Underground Railroad (www.cr.nps.gov/nr/underground) is a travel itinerary detailing 45 sites on the service's National Register of Historic Places. A color map shows escape routes, including those into Mexico.

The site also points out that although there were courageous white allies, ''the majority of assistance to runaways came from slaves and free blacks, and the greatest responsibility for providing shelter, financial support and direction to successful runaways came from the organized efforts of northern free blacks.''

Another Park Service site, Researching and Interpreting the Underground Railroad (www.cr.nps.gov/history/exugrr/exuggr1 .htm), points out the pitfalls of oral history and local legend, while providing a bounty of potential sources.

The National Geographic has a site with a moving account of a slave's journey to freedom at www.nationalgeographic.com /features/99/railroad.

Another Web site, www.nyhistory.com /harriettubman/life.htm, has a biography and links to other sites on Harriet Tubman, the Moses of the railroad, an escaped slave who is credited with making 19 return trips to save more than 300 others.
