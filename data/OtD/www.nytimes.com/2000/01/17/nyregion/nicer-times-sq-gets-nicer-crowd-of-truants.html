Nicer Times Sq. Gets Nicer Crowd of Truants

By RANDY KENNEDY
Published: January 17, 2000

The police roundups are never accompanied by a reading of the suspects' Miranda rights. Instead, the lieutenant in charge usually asks two questions: ''Do you want to work at McDonald's for the rest of your life? Do you want to super-size my fries?''

The suspects are junior high and high school students, and they are playing hooky in the kind of place that has lured truants for more than a generation: a shiny new mall. The only difference is that this place is called Times Square.

And if any doubt remains that it has become a mall, just ask the nearest teenager.

Like Juan Morales, a clean-cut eighth grader from Queens who skipped class with three friends last Tuesday and took the No. 7 train to Times Square ''because of, like, the new stuff -- MTV and the Virgin Megastore.''

''And,'' he quickly added, ''to check out girls, man.''

He and his friends managed to check out only one girl before they were rounded up by the Police Department's truancy patrol. ''I guess we learned our lesson today,'' Juan said sarcastically upon his release. Then he grinned, his braces glinting, and the four headed back to Times Square.

Of course, there has probably never been a time -- from nickelodeons and burlesque right up through kung fu movies and peep shows -- when the area has not been a haunt for teenagers gone AWOL.

But police officials say that now, the troubled truants looking for pornography or fake ID cards or drugs have been replaced by even more students of all kinds from around the metropolitan region, who are cutting classes to seek out the relatively wholesome alternatives offered by corporate America in the new Times Square. They come for high-priced compact discs, high-priced movies and the rare chance to stand on a crowded sidewalk for hours for a fleeting glimpse of a rock band appearing at the MTV studios.

Lt. Helen Rossi, who has been patrolling Midtown for truants since 1983, said that during most of her years on the job, the 42nd Street area attracted primarily ''runaways, and kids who had warrants, and juvenile delinquents.'' Searches of the truants often turned up guns and drugs.

''Now,'' she said, ''kids are coming to see 'N Sync.''

Searches, when they are conducted, usually turn up cell phones with Mom's number on the speed dial.

It is hard to tell exactly how many truants are showing up in Times Square on an average school day, police officials say. Most are coming from schools in the five boroughs, although officers also commonly find students from Long Island, New Jersey and Westchester.

Throughout Manhattan, the police find far fewer truants every year -- 5,000 to 6,000 -- than they did a decade ago, when the number sometimes reached 10,000. The truancy patrols do not appear to be any less aggressive or frequent than they were during Times Square's more troubled past.

Of the 100 or so teenagers who are picked up every week in Manhattan, about 60 are found in or near Times Square and most of the rest at the Manhattan Mall on 34th Street, said Chief Allan H. Hoehl of the Manhattan South police command.

But on the busiest days in Times Square -- almost always when a popular band shows up for an afternoon interview at MTV's glass-walled studio on Broadway at 44th Street -- the official count does not help a lot. Lieutenant Rossi's five officers try not to pluck students from behind the barricades where they start to gather by the hundreds in the early afternoon to stake out a prime spot to see their favorite band.

Officers also try not to pull students off long lines inside the Virgin music store at Seventh Avenue near 45th Street when they throng to wait for a recording artist's autograph.

''I'm not looking to cause a riot,'' explained Lieutenant Rossi, who has two daughters of her own, 11 and 15, and so knows about these things. Besides, she said, ''if you wanted to, you'd have to bring buses to take everybody away.''

And the officers can easily fill up their roving vans just by concentrating on students who wander off from the crowds alone or in small groups to the area's new high-tech arcades or McDonald's or the Virgin store.

This was the tactical mistake made by Rebecca Miller, 15, and three of her friends from West Morris Mendham High School in New Jersey, who skipped school late last fall to see Korn, a rap-metal band that attracts almost fanatic crowds when it shows up for MTV's interview show called Total Request Live.

It was the first time the students had cut school, and they did so with the blessing of their parents, though not, of course, their school. An older sister of one student worked in Times Square, and they caught a ride with her.

Like many teenagers interviewed in the area starting last fall, Rebecca and her friends seemed to lack the seditious spirit normally associated with truancy. Instead, they had asked permission, planned and gone with concrete goals: see their band, buy compact discs, maybe get on television so that their classmates would believe they were there.

By 10 a.m., the students had already scouted out the best spot near MTV. They walked around, staring up at all the jumbo television screens and at the only parental presence anywhere around: the huge, smiling faces of Charles Gibson and Diane Sawyer on a ''Good Morning America'' billboard.
