At Yale, a $500 Million Plan Reflects a New Age of Science

By KAREN W. ARENSON
Published: January 19, 2000

Concerned that it will not be able to stay in the top academic ranks without a greater presence in science, Yale University plans to announce tomorrow that it will invest at least $500 million in new science and engineering facilities, including the construction of five new buildings and the renovation of several old ones.

Universities everywhere are spending heavily on genomics centers, computer laboratories and other facilities to foster cutting-edge scientific fields, but Yale's $500 million commitment appears to be one of the largest one-time building plans ever.

Yale's decision to try to become more of a science and technology powerhouse illustrates the growing importance that universities are placing on scientific research. It also represents a serious bid to reposition Yale's reputation; it is better known for its prominence in the humanities, social sciences and arts, and for its professional schools, than for its work in the sciences.

''Yale is committed to remain on everyone's short list of the best universities in the world,'' said Richard C. Levin, Yale's president, an economist who studies the role of technological innovation. ''In the 21st century, you must excel in science and engineering to maintain that position.''

Modern space for scientific research is crucial, Mr. Levin said, in attracting top professors and students. ''We find ourselves in stiffer competition in the sciences than in other fields,'' he said.

E. William Colglazier, executive officer of the National Academy of Sciences, said many universities were recognizing the need to spend more money on science. ''I think this is the wave of the future, and we'll see more of these announcements,'' he said.

''This is a reflection of the fact that science and technology are reshaping the world, and the universities need to make these types of investments to remain in the forefront,'' he added. ''Unfortunately it's expensive, but universities like Yale have decided they have to make this kind of investment.''

Aided by its medical school, Yale is stronger scientifically than its reputation might suggest, Mr. Levin contends. He said it was among the top four universities in grants from the National Institutes of Health and among the top four in scientific articles most cited by others. Most of its science and engineering departments are ranked among the top 12 nationally, he said, and two -- physiology and pharmacology -- are No. 1 in graduate department rankings.

Sixty-three Yale professors are members of the National Academy of Sciences, placing it fifth after Harvard (158), the University of California at Berkeley (115), Stanford (111) and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (100). But the Yale faculty includes only one Nobel laureate in the sciences: Sidney Altman, who won the Nobel Prize in chemistry in 1989 for research on RNA.

About a quarter of Yale's 5,200 undergraduates are in science or engineering, up from about 17 percent a decade ago, and about a third of its 1,800 graduate students are in those fields.

The university has begun to try to upgrade its science offerings in recent years. Mr. Levin announced his intention to strengthen engineering when he became president in 1993, and he brought in Dr. D. Allan Bromley, a nuclear physicist and former science adviser to President George Bush, as a dean.

The new construction plan grew out of a report last fall on Yale's needs for expansion and renovation of its physical plant. Yale has erected only two new science buildings in the last 30 years, one connected to the medical school and the other for a structural biology center. The new buildings will expand the university's science and engineering space by about 25 percent.

Of the $500 million investment, about 40 percent will go for new buildings, and the rest will be used to refurbish existing space. The money will come on top of existing spending in science and engineering.

Many other colleges and universities, including Haverford, Hofstra, Middlebury, Princeton, Williams and Wooster, have announced new science centers in recent years, and some, like Harvard, are undertaking more than one.

Harvard, for example, has said it plans to spend about $200 million over the next five years on five new and refurbished science centers, including one for physical sciences and one for life sciences. It recently opened a new computer sciences building and is nearly finished with a new chemistry laboratory.

Although the Yale plan covers only capital spending, Mr. Levin said it would probably result in more hiring and spending in these disciplines, too. He said the university had already committed to adding four positions in engineering and six in the biological sciences, which it is filling now.

The new buildings, which Yale hopes to complete in six to eight years, will include new facilities for chemical, biological, and environmental research as well as for engineering and for the School of Forestry and Environmental Studies. Yale also plans to build two new lecture halls for introductory science courses.

A $24 million gift from John Malone, chairman of Liberty Media and a 1963 graduate of Yale's engineering program, will help finance the new engineering building, and Yale hopes to attract other private gifts. In the meantime, it will borrow to pay for construction.

Connecticut officials are cheering Yale's new building plan. Gov. John G. Rowland and his senior economic adviser, Arthur Diedrick, will join Yale officials tomorrow morning for the formal announcement.

''It's a great thing for Connecticut,'' Mr. Diedrick said yesterday, adding that research at the university had led to the creation of 35 new companies over the last decade and that an expanding research base could lead to even more.

''This will just further education in Connecticut in the sciences and engineering,'' Mr. Diedrick said, ''which will be greatly needed, as economic development focuses more on science and technology fields.''
