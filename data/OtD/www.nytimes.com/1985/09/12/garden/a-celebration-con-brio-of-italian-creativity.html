A CELEBRATION CON BRIO OF ITALIAN CREATIVITY

By SUZANNE SLESIN
Published: September 12, 1985
Correction Appended

TWENTY-FIVE years ago Bloomingdale's held its first promotion to showcase Italian wares. Now, Ecco L'Italia, a seven-week event that opens to the public today, is a storewide show celebrating Italy that will not only appeal to confirmed Italophiles but also should garner additional devotees. The energy, bravura and good taste of Italy's classic and modern wares are represented in everything from fashion to food to home furnishings.

''It's the biggest country event in our history,'' said Carl Levine, the store's senior vice president for home furnishings. ''And in the planning since 1982.''

In the last 25 years, Mr. Levine said, Italian ready-to-wear has ''become much more important.'' The store's fashion presentation is called La Moda Italiana. It includes a collection of scarfs and sportswear made of Memphis/Milano-designed fabrics; tulle petticoats, twin sets and cinch belts in the Roman Holiday boutique; Emilio Pucci and Gucci accessories, and designs by such well-known Italian fashion names as Fendi, Armani, Bottega Veneta and Andrea Pfister.

Marvin Traub, chairman of Bloomingdale's, and his wife, Lee, and Robert Tammero, president of the store, and his wife, Margaret, along with a cast of costumed entertainers acted as hosts for the Carnevale Alla Veneziana. Guests were invited to eat prosciutto and melon, Italian-style rice with red, green and yellow peppers and plenty of pasta.

Ecco L'Italia officially began last night at 8, with 1,700 guests invited to a $200-a-person Ballo in Maschera. Requested attire was black tie, with festive mask as an option. Maxwell Rabb, American Ambassador to Italy, and his wife, Ruth, were the party's chairmen. Lily Auchincloss and John T. Sargent were the co-chairmen of the event, which benefited the American Academy in Rome, a center for advanced study in the arts and humanities.

Marchese Emilio Pucci, the fashion designer, who sported a wide red, white and green ribbon over his tuxedo, was talking animately with Susanna Agnelli. ''I'm here tonight at the request of the Mayor of Florence,'' he said. ''That's why I'm wearing this decoration.''

Mayor Koch was also there along with an elegant crowd that mixed Italians with Italophiles. Included were Franco Maria Ricci, the publisher of FMR magazine, and such architects and designers as Michael Graves, Mario Buatta and John Mascheroni. Massimo and Lella Vignelli, who came with their son Luca, were some of the early arrivals. ''So far there are very few Italians here,'' she said. ''But you know we're always a little bit late.'' Mrs. Vignelli wore a strapless red gown. ''It's Valentino, of course,'' she said, ''and just a little bit tight.''

Other Italian fashion designers were also well represented. Donata Sartorio, who works at Donna, an Italian magazine, wore a flowing white organdy overblouse by Gianfranco Ferre. And some guests, including Neal Furie and Bob Franceschini, donned the requested masks. ''They're from Venice, of course,'' said Mr. Furie.

Gabriella Mariotti was kicking herself for not having worn her carnival costume. ''I'm really Venetian, after all,'' she said.

''I'm here because I just made a film in Italy,'' said Ismail Merchant, the Indian director, who was waiting in the receiving line with Asha Putli, who said, ''I am a No. 1 star in Italy and all my gold albums come from there.'' Victoria Kaunitz, a swimwear designer, was in a celadon floor-length 1940's dress. ''I got it at the Spence-Chapin Thrift Shop for $20,'' she said. Mirella Petteni, one of the most elegant women present, wore a burnished gold tunic and black velvet pants. ''By an Italian designer?'' she was asked. ''Donna Karan,'' she said. ''American.'' One of the most important areas of the show is on the fifth floor, with its newly installed series of model rooms and adjacent Street of Shops, where Italian merchandise of all eras and styles is represented. It ranges from 17th-century reproduction furniture and accessories to custom pieces by Memphis, the Italian avant-garde design group. ''It's all the things indicative of Italy: Art Deco, wrought iron, baskets, ceramics, alabaster, marble, terra cotta and tin,'' said Richard Knapple, the store's vice president of interior design. ''We have them all,'' said Barbara Deichman, the furniture fashion director, ''classic shapes and contemporary forms.''

Julian Tomchin, senior vice president and fashion director for home furnishings and cosmetics, was especially enthusiastic about Il Palazzetto, a small shop crammed with real and fake marble objects. ''Fake finishes are a concept in which we all believe very strongly,'' he said.

Particularly intriguing were a number of overscale ceramic vases and platters glazed to look like blue-veined marble or malachite. Prices for the merchandise ranged from $8.50 for a candlestick to $400 for an overscale bowl.

Ecco La Casa is the title of a series of model rooms. Most striking is a dining room by Aldo Cibic of Sottsass Associati, a Milan-based design and architecture firm. The room has been furnished with the lively and exuberant Memphis designs and patterns, including wallpaper and carpeting.

''I consider this my return engagement,'' said Barbara D'Arcy, the store's vice president, director of merchandise presentation, who designed one of the model rooms, ''A Palladian Chamber for the 20th Century.'' Instead of doing a copy, Miss D'Arcy chose to interpret the Palladian style. ''There's no way to reproduce what's there,'' she said. ''I created a background and used the store's Palladian-inspired pieces as well as contemporary pieces.''

Also included is an urbane, contemporary interior by Mr. Knapple that features sprayed walls, and a bedroom with tiger-skin-patterned carpeting that Miss Deichmann called ''Rome goes Hollywood.'' Mr. Knapple's Pompeii-inspired room had Etruscan-style furnishings and fake plaster reliefs.

A bedroom meant to be in a villa in Siena, also by Miss Deichmann, had a well-worn terra-cotta floor that looked especially effective. ''It's been here for years,'' said the designer, ''and I wanted it to look 300 years old so I asked for all the holes and imperfections to be left in. It was perfect.''
