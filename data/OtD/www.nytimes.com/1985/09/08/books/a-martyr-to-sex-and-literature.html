A MARTYR TO SEX AND LITERATURE

By Thomas Mallon; Thomas Mallon, who teaches English at Vassar College, is the author of ''A Book of One's Own: People and Their Diaries.''
Published: September 8, 1985

OUR THREE SELVES The Life of Radclyffe Hall. By Michael Baker. Illustrated. 386 pp. New York: William Morrow. $17.95. UNA TROUBRIDGE The Friend of Radclyffe Hall. By Richard Ormrod. Illustrated. 340 pp. New York: Carroll & Graf. $18.95.

AS the year 1928 opened in England, Radclyffe

Hall was a well-regarded middlebrow novelist, a practicing Catholic, a political conservative, an admirer of the Brownings, a homeowner with occasional servant troubles. That August she published ''The Well of Loneliness,'' about which the editor James Douglas wrote in The Sunday Express: ''I would rather give a healthy boy or a healthy girl a phial of prussic acid than this novel.''

''The Well,'' a frank treatment of lesbianism and a plea for its toleration (''Acknowledge us, oh God, before the whole world. Give us also the right to our existence!''), was, after stormy proceedings in Bow Street Magistrates Court, successfully suppressed by the Home Secretary in accordance with the Obscene Publications Act of 1857, achieving for Radclyffe Hall a sexual and literary martyrdom that Michael Baker, in his fine new biography ''Our Three Selves,'' convinces us she craved.

Radclyffe Hall was born in 1880 to a philandering father and quarrelsome mother. She grew up prosperous, but was lonely and poorly educated. She reached adulthood without a vocation and spent much of her 20's pursuing women she usually lost to marriage. Certain all her life that she was a ''congenital invert,'' she had her first long relationship, between 1907 and 1915, with Mrs. Mabel Batten (Ladye), whose male admirers included the Prince of Wales and the poet-adventurer Wilfrid Scawen Blunt. Shortly before Ladye's death in 1915, John (as Radclyffe Hall always called herself) embarked on an affair with the sculptor Una Troubridge, the wife of an admiral, that would last for 28 years. John had a strong guilty sense that her infidelity had hastened Ladye's end, and to assuage it she and Una pursued contact with Ladye's spirit through a medium. For years Una lived with Ladye's strong posthumous presence (hence the ''three selves'' of Mr. Baker's title), as later in the relationship she would have to cope with a more material rival named Evguenia Souline.

Until she was past 40, Radclyffe Hall published only small volumes of verse, about which Mr. Baker tactfully says: ''John's poetry represented her passport to prose.'' But in the 1920's, with Una's encouragement, she became a prolific and successful novelist. ''Adam's Breed'' (1926) won both the Prix Femina and the James Tait Black Prize; the only other novel that had won both was ''A Passage to India.''

The theme of ''inversion'' had been discreetly present in ''The Unlit Lamp'' (1924), but by 1928 John and Una were both, in the latter's phrase, ''sick to death of ambiguities.'' The explicit result was ''The Well of Loneliness.'' Mr. Baker, who is a film and television writer in London, does an able job of setting forth the legal and cultural complexities involved in its suppression (Virginia Woolf was willing to testify on the novel's behalf, ''but it pained her sensibility that the case rested on such a poor book''). Radclyffe Hall faced the court's denunciations with considerable courage, and she has won on posthumous appeal. The ''Well'' was republished in 1949, and in the winter of 1974 Mrs. Grundy could even tune into the BBC to hear it presented, in 17 episodes, on ''Book at Bedtime.'' While contemporary readers may be bored by some purple boilerplate in the ''Well'' 's prose, the good sense and passion of the author's indignation remain moving. In the decade following the ''Well'' controversy, John still ranked as a considerable literary figure, but novels like ''The Master of the House'' (about a modern Christ figure) and ''The Sixth Beatitude'' failed to win the kind of critical approbation she hoped for. Romantic upsets and illness dominated the last years of her life.

Radclyffe Hall was, in Una's words, ''a born fanatic.'' Mr. Baker finds in her ''a positive lust for suffering'' that was of considerable use when applied to a just cause, but which took a personal toll in fits of frustration and illness. She was often humorless, aggressively masculine in manner and dress and emphatically the ''husband'' in the household she made with Una. Alec Waugh thought the two of them ''an austere Edwardian couple who expected conventional behavior from their guests and hosts.''

John's view of lesbianism was not wholly celebrato-ry: she ''regarded her homosexuality as a handicap which had often made her unhappy.'' In fact, her Catholicism tempted her to believe that it was a part of God's intended suffering for her. But common sense convinced her that the ''invert'' had to be allowed into the mainstream, permitted to contribute to a society to which she was inevitably bound by all sorts of considerations other than the sexual. Shortly before the storm over the ''Well,'' she gave an interview to The Daily Mail as part of its series ''How Other Women Run Their Homes.'' In most ways hers was an ordinary upper-middle-class one. Indeed, Mr. Baker's book is confirmation of how one's sexual and general politics do not always run parallel. Radclyffe Hall may have been in the sexual vanguard, but like those other well-off homosexual writers of her day, Siegfried Sassoon and Vita Sackville-West, she had little sympathy for modern literature and modern life. Her feminism was grudging, and when in 1912 a suffragist campaign of civil disobedience, conducted during a miner's strike, turned violent, she wrote anonymously to The Pall Mall Gazette: ''According to Mrs. Pankhurst, they are resorting to the methods of the miners! Since when have English ladies regulated their conduct by that of the working classes?'' Fanaticism aside, Radclyffe Hall was, in a profound way, part of the majority - in whose chorus of determinants money speaks louder than sex.
