NATIONAL ENDOWMENT FOR THE ARTS IS HONORED AS 20TH BIRTHDAY NEARS

Special to the New York Times
Published: September 1, 1985

WASHINGTON, Aug. 31— In hindsight, everyone agrees the American Ballet Theater's brush with extinction could not have come at a better time.

It was the winter of 1965, only a few months after President Lyndon B. Johnson created the National Endowment for the Arts as part of a cultural development program ''to make fresher the winds of art in our land.''

''Art is a nation's most precious heritage,'' President Johnson said as he signed the program into law on Sept. 29, 1965, ''for it is in our works of art that we reveal to ourselves, and to others, the inner vision which guides us as a nation. And where there is no vision, the people perish.''

As the American Ballet Theater watched its funds dwindle that winter, it feared that it might perish. But on Dec. 20, 1965, Vice President Hubert H. Humphrey presented the dance company with $100,000, the first check ever issued by the Government in direct support of the arts. The ballet theater had survived and the National Endowment for the Arts, the Federal grant-making agency, had just gotten under way.

Two decades later, the endowment is the largest single arts contributor in the country, and it is promoting its 20th anniversary as an occasion for reflection and celebration, with special events planned across the nation.

Commemoration Under Way

The birthday is a few weeks away, but the yearlong commemoration of the arts, which was organized by a 20th-anniversary committee with the actor Charlton Heston and First Lady Nancy Reagan as co-chairmen, began some time ago.

In March, the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences awarded an Oscar to the endowment in recognition of ''its dedicated commitment to fostering artistic and creative activity and excellence in every area of human genius.'' In April, President Reagan awarded the first National Medal of Arts to seven internationally known artists and five longtime arts patrons.

The finale comes during National Arts Week, Sept. 23 to 29, when more than 600 exhibits, activities and performances around the country will coincide with the endowment's anniversary.

About 70 museums will highlight works of art that have been purchased or created with the support of the endowment. Other institutions, including state arts councils and community centers, plan lecture series, seminars and workshops on the endowment and the arts, and many dance and theater performances have been dedicated to the anniversary.

In New York City, Mayor Koch will honor the arts endowment and local artists with a ceremony at City Hall on Sept. 18. Among other projects in the city, the Museum of Modern Art will highlight the preservation of film, which the endowment has helped finance; the Metropolitan Museum of Art has printed posters saluting endowment support, and the Circle in the Square Theater has dedicated the run of ''Figaro'' - Richard Nelson's new version of the Beaumarchais ''Marriage of Figaro,'' directed by Andrei Serban - to the endowment's anniversary. The play opens Sept. 22.

Creation of Two Agencies

President Johnson observed in 1965, ''Somehow, the scientists always seem to get the penthouse, while the arts and the humanities get the basement.'' To make amends, his cultural program created two Federal grant-making agencies: the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities, with budgets of about $2.5 million each. The humanities endowment, which finances scholarly and academic pursuits in many fields, has announced no plans for the anniversary.

Since then, the arts endowment has seen its programs, its budgets, and American appreciation for the arts grow. This year its budget of $163 million finances undertakings in music, dance, literature, visual arts, theater, arts education, architecture, planning and design, public media, museum and expansion arts and opera-music theater.

Some have criticized the arts endowment for spending more on established art projects than on small, grass-roots efforts. ''The endowment has an insider-outsider networking system,'' said Derek Guthrie, publisher of The New Art Examiner, a national arts magazine that frequently runs articles about endowment policy - and that has been refused Federal grants in the past.

''In a time of no expansion in the arts, younger artists are coming up against a middle-aged generation of arts administrators who make up the grant-giving panels,'' Mr. Guthrie said. ''Consistently, you get the highest grants if you're on the panel.''

But Frank Hodsoll, chairman of the endowment, insists that the ''peer review panels,'' whose recommendations are the basis for distribution of grants, fairly represent ''the disciplines and other aspects of arts across the country.

Decisions Based on Recommendations ''We do not make decisions because the chairman thinks that this or that institution ought to receive a grant because he or she likes this or that kind of theater,'' he said. ''We make our decisions on the basis of recommendations of experts from the field.'' While specific eligibility for endowment financing varies by program, all qualifying arts organizations must be nonprofit and have an established record of exceptional artistic merit.

The number of arts organizations has increased greatly in the past 20 years. There are 192 orchestras eligible for Federal support today, compared to 58 in 1965; 102 opera companies, compared to 27, and 389 theaters, compared to 22. The American Film Institute, founded to encourage pursuit of ''the 20th-century art form'' and partly financed by the endowment, did not exist in 1965. And since 1968, the endowment's Dance Touring Program has exposed many audiences to ballet and modern-dance performances for the first time, expanding audiences considerably.

The endowment estimates that in 1965 the national dance audience numbered about one million people, 80 percent of them New Yorkers, who could see only 37 dance companies. Today, 20 million people watch 213 dance companies around the country.

Since President Reagan took office there have been annual threats of reductions to the endowment budget. The Administration proposed funding of $144 million for 1986 - a cut of more than 11 percent - but Congress is expected to push for more. Before its August recess, the House approved $166.6 million; there has been no Senate action to date.

Also, in recent years, private giving to the arts has increased, encouraged in part by the endowment's Challenge Grant Program requiring arts organizations to match Federal dollars with donations from other sources. In 1965, the private sector contributed $250 million; last year, according to endowment figures, it gave $4.6 billion.
