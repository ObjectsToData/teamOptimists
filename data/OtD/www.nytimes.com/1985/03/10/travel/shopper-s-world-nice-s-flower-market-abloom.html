    SHOPPER'S WORLD; NICE'S FLOWER MARKET ABLOOM

    By Robert Packard; Robert Packard is a professor of the humanities at Stevens Institute of Technology.
    Published: March 10, 1985

    Because the residents of Nice are passionately fond of flowers, the city's flower market is visited daily by what appear to be as many residents as visitors. Even in the most modest Nice hotel entrances visitors will find elaborate floral arrangements that are changed every day or so; flowers are used matter-of-factly as an integral part of the decor in restaurants and shops as well as in many homes. ''I can't remember when I didn't have fresh flowers in my apartment,'' a woman who has lived in Nice for 10 years said. ''They have become a part of my life.''

    The flowers offered in the Ni,cois market come from the foothills of the maritime Alps, a Mediterranean region where 500 million cut flowers are harvested annually.

    Prodigality begets generosity. ''Take them,'' a vendor in Nice's flower market said to me as she thrust a bunch of eight or nine long-stemmed yellow roses into my arms. I was already holding two dozen, bought from her at bargain prices, and I scarcely needed more. ''No, no,'' she said, ''you don't understand. As a gift. Put them in your kitchen.''

    The flower market is in the Cours Saleya, in the heart of the old city of Nice, one block from the Quai des Etats-Unis, the boulevard that runs along the Mediterranean Sea. For at least a century this open square has been a site for the selling of flowers, but the large rectangular area has served as a market place since the time of the Romans.

    In recent years the market has been given over to pedestrians. Beneath it, four levels are available for parking, and the flower vendors bring their produce to their stalls by elevator.

    A triumvirate of merchants share the marketplace: flower sellers, produce vendors and antiques dealers. The flower sellers, who predominate, offer their cut flowers and plants daily except Monday. At the western end of the Cours Saleya, vendors of fresh vegetables, fruit, nuts, olives and cheese maintain stalls daily except Wednesday and Saturday afternoons. At those times they are replaced by the antiques dealers.

    On Sundays it all comes together: camellias, Camembert and candelabra vie with one another for customers. Despite heavy competition the camellias always win out, and in fact the Cours Saleya is also known as the Marche aux Fleurs.

    Customers enter the flower market by way of Rue St. Fran,cois de Paule. Three avenues of banked flowers extend as far as one can see. About 50 vendors have individual stalls of about 160 square feet each, with striped green and white awnings to protect the flowers from direct sunlight.

    On a stroll through the market even an amateur botanist could identify the masses of carnations of every imaginable color, anemones of deep pink as well as red and purple, calla lilies, irises, freesia and petaled flowers such as marigolds, astors, chrysanthemums and African daisies.

    The effect is momentarily dizzying. All around is the practical bustle of the market: dogs bark, customers maneuver to get near the booths, children laugh, vendors call out.

    The fragrance of roses permeates the air. Roses, in countless varieties and hues, are, along with carnations, the most prevalent flower. Beyond are great bunches of daffodils, cyclamen, azaleas, and bruy ere (a delicate heather-like bloom). Pots of gardenias stand beside miniature flowering cumquat, grapefruit and mandarin orange trees.

    Some merchants arrange their flowers to display the long-stemmed varieties such as gladiolus, lilies and roses as a backdrop for the shorter-stemmed blooms at the front of the stalls. Massed bouquets marked ''voyages'' are prepared to enable visitors to take them home by plane or car; such bouquets are tightly packed and the flowers themselves are just beyond the budding stage.

    The vendors differ in their approach to passers-by. ''Profitez, profitez!'' shouts a man, exhorting the public to take advantage of his sacrificially inexpensive flowers; ''Some mimosa, perhaps?'' asks a soft- voiced woman, holding up an exquisite cloud of yellow blossoms; ''Sir - look this way, please,'' calls a youth proffering 50 tightly packed deep pink long-stemmed roses, ''bring joy to your wife with a memorable souvenir of Nice.''

    A little bargaining and haggling goes on (especially at the end of the day when flower stocks are near depletion) but for the most part prices are set - which doesn't prevent the vendor from adding an extra sprig or two if you are thought to be ''sympathetique.''

    Flowers in southern France are a big business. About 300 acres in Provence are devoted to roses, 200 acres for jasmine and orange blossoms, and 40 acres for violets. As many as 460 businesses are involved in some aspect of turning the essence of the flowers of the south of France into perfume.

    Last year the total production of flowers along the French Riviera amounted to 23,000 tons, of which almost 6,000 were carnations and 2,500 roses. Mimosa and smaller flowers made up much of the remainder. Perhaps one third of the flowers are grown in greenhouses, the rest in the open, sometimes with coverings for inclement weather. Directly north of Nice, Antibes and Cannes, vast areas are given over to flower growing: visitors to towns like St. Paul de Vence can stand on embankments and lose count of the coverings that dot the landscape.

    Nice is a city with public areas everywhere that are embellished with flowers. The citizens set scrupulous examples for visitors in treating their parks and gardens with respect. The city's planted flowers and decorative fountain-sprayed gardens are sacrosanct: no one pilfers, no one tramples, no one even touches the gorgeous and sometimes rare blooms. These civic plantings, along the boulevards and at bus stops, in front of public buildings and railway stations, are tended by a corps of gardeners on the municipal payroll (The Department of Green Spaces) whose sole and demanding duty it is to water, trim, prune, and transplant the exquisite variety of flowers on display.

    BRIEF GUIDE FOR LOVERS OF FRESH FLOWERS

    WHERE

    The flower market in Nice is in the Cours Saleya at the eastern end of the Rue St. Francis de Paule, one block north of the seafront boulevard, close to the Opera and the Palais de Justice. Underground parking is available. The market is open daily from 6 A.M. to 5 P.M., except Mondays.

    HOW MUCH

    Flower prices vary according to season, to availability, and even according to individual vendors; but they tend to be quite low by New York standards, partly because of the strength of the dollar and partly because of the volume being raised so near the market in which they are being sold.

    WHEN

    It is beter to visit the flower market in the early morning when the display is at its height; unless you have ironclad resistance, be prepared to return to your hotel directly afterward or you will spend the day carrying armloads of flowers. If you visit in the late afternoon prices may be lower but stocks will be depleted.
