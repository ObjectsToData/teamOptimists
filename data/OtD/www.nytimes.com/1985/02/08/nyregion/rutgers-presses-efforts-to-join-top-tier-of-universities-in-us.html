RUTGERS PRESSES EFFORTS TO JOIN TOP TIER OF UNIVERSITIES IN U.S.

By JONATHAN FRIENDLY
Published: February 8, 1985

NEW BRUNSWICK, N.J., Feb. 7— For years, Rutgers, the State University of New Jersey, has been overshadowed by its Ivy League neighbors of the East and the great state universities of the Middle West and the West Coast.

But over the last five years, a series of changes at Rutgers and in the State Legislature have put the school on the road to national standing as a center for research and learning, according to educators in the state and Rutgers officials and faculty members.

''Rutgers is in the middle of a significant transition,'' said T. Edward Hollander, the state's Chancellor of Higher Education. ''It is moving from the second tier of universities to the first. It's not there yet, but it will be.''

Quality and Reputation

The developments at Rutgers provide a case study of the forces that shape academic quality and institutional reputation.

At the same time, Rutgers's evolution raises complex questions about what a public, land-grant university should be in an age when the lines between ''pure'' and ''applied'' research are becoming blurred and the potential of corporate support can seem more compelling than breeding a better tomato.

Some faculty members worry that the changes at Rutgers will open the door for big business to dictate what goes on in the research centers that the university will build. Others see it as possibly slighting the humanities for the sake of the sciences.

A Time of Optimism

But in a score of interviews, faculty members and administrators said the changes were worth the risk. And all insisted that the belief that the university could earn national stature was a much more optimistic outlook than previously recorded at Rutgers.

For most of the period from the end of World War II to the end of the 1970's, Rutgers was, by its own description, a middle-level public university. It had a full enrollment, thanks in part to the baby boom, and had individual pockets of noteworthy academic activity but little of the world-class talent that went to the great public universities such as Michigan, Illinois and California.

If ambitious or accomplished pro-fessors thought about New Jersey, they thought about Princeton.

But over the last decade the state government at Trenton has upgraded the state and community colleges, relieving Rutgers of the responsibilities of educating students at the lower end of the ladder.

''We are, for the first time in my years at Rutgers, at a point where optimism is realistic rather than just politic,'' said Dr. Edward J. Bloustein, the president of the university and a nonstop cheerleader for changes that he helped set in motion after he was named to the job in 1971.

Doctoral Programs

As evidence of improvement, the university points to a 1981 national poll of 5,000 professors. It gave high marks to 14 doctoral programs at Rutgers - ranging from English and history to chemistry, microbiology and psychology.

The ranking - behind Indiana and the University of North Carolina, for example, but ahead of Purdue, Ohio State and the City University of New York - gave Dr. Bloustein a new talking point in his endless wooing of the Legislature.

In the summer of 1980, Rutgers began to make a significant change in its internal organization.

The university's undergraduate colleges on the main campus in New Brunswick - Rutgers, Douglass and Livingston - had grown up independent of each other, in some cases duplicating or triplicating course offerings. The governing board overruled the sometimes bitter complaint of faculty members, who feared for their autonomy, and ordered the departments consolidated.

W. Robert Jenkins, the dean of Livingston College, who was an outspoken foe of reorganization four years ago, now praises its results. In his own field, biology, for instance, three independent groups became a department with 100 professors and a good chance at more research funds and better graduate students.

Degree Now 'Means More'

Because of the merger, ''a Rutgers degree means more than it did five years ago,'' the dean said.''People in New Jersey are now beginning to recognize what we have here.''

Starting in 1981, a faculty committee began to review and rate every graduate program. Some, it concluded, were excellent and should get more money for expansion. Others were deemed so vital to the university's future that they had to be encouraged to improve. A few, it decided, should be allowed to shrink.

The Rutgers administration used the ratings to justify shifting 120 faculty positions, about 6 percent of the university total, between departments. And Daniel Gorenstein, a mathematician who headed the committee, said the threat of losing faculty was a spur to self-improvement in many departments.

''The mediocrity that was spread around the university is upgrading itself,'' he said.

The university will not say which programs it has cut nor give criteria by which it judges some disciplines expendable, other than to say that declining enrollment is one factor.

A Gradual Process

T. Alexander Pond, the executive vice president, acknowledged that the choices involved important issues of public policy, but he said that identifying the departments would needlessly hurt them and hasten what should be a gradual process of attrition.
