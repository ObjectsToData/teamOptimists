New-York City. Houses Wanted.

Published: June 26, 1865

For those possessed of fortunes, or those having liberal incomes, this city is immeasurably the most desirable place on the American continent in which to live. The fact that it is the recognized metropolis of the nation; the fact that it is the largest and most populous, the richest and among the oldest of American cities, are circumstances greatly in its favor. To these advantages must be added its location, admirable in every respect, and, altogether, unequalled in its natural healthfulness, its pleasant climate, its grand surroundings, the facility of access to it from all parts of the country and from all countries of the world. To those again must be added its splendid streets, and edifices and parks, and its hundred miles of commodious and luxurious residences. To these also, its cosmopolitan and liberal spirit, its scientific and literary supremacy, its artistic treasures and opportunities, and all the innumerable appliances or gratifying the rich man's ordinary wants and ministering to his higher nature. To men of fortune or of means, possessed of taste, culture, intellect or genius, and desirous of making as much as possible out of his life and out of themselves, New-York City has a superiority which is unquestionable, and which is tacitly confessed by the continual gravitation toward it of these classes from all parts of the country.

But it is a city of varied and violent contrasts. To those of small incomes it is greatly inferior as a place of residence to many other American cities. To the great body of mechanics, craftsmen, artisans, workingmen and laborers -- to the vast and dominant multitudes whose earnings range from ton to eighteen or twenty dollars a week -- in other words, to four-fifths of its entire population -- New-York furnishes the worst place of residence of any city in America -- the worst as regards suitable household apartments, the worst as regards health for adults and children, as regards streets and markets, supplies and economy, air and cleanliness, and as regards the preservation of public and domestic morals. We do not speak at this time of the sinks and slums and cellars in which the lowest forms of depravity and wretchedness fester and rot, and which were lately described in our columns. We have masses of human beings in this city -- few of them native to American soil -- whom it seems almost hopeless to attempt to elevate, and the improvement of whom must at all events be the work of long time. But it is to respectable, intelligent, industrious people we refer -- people whose skill and industry secures them a return in money that ought to be sufficient to procure for them such household accommodations, comforts and surroundings as are essential to the health and happiness of themselves and their families. Yet what sort of places of abode is furnished in this city for its working masses, for those who are the creators and sustainers of its wealth and greatness? Do they possess any of those characteristics of commodiousness, airiness, cleanliness and general comfort, which are so absolutely essential, which can be furnished so economically, and which have such a vital influence on the well-being and morals of the people? Or are their characteristics crampedness, closeness, crowdedness and filth, with obnoxious yards in the rear and reeking, slimy streets in the front? And can even such tenements as we have for workingmen be obtained for anything like a reasonable rental? Or have not the rents they are compelled to pay become so intolerably high as to trench on their daily necessities, to crowd them into smaller and smaller spaces, and drive thousands of them from the city? Lot the answer come from that densely populated peninsular district extending from Chatham-square to Four-teenth-street, where laborious myriads reside who are engaged as founders, workers in brass, plumbers, shipcarpenters, tailors, cordwainers and journeymen of crafts innumerable. Or let it come from the north side of the city, from Canal-street and upward for a couple of miles, inhabited in great part by similar classes. The fact is, those who have taken the trouble to make themselves familiar with the way in which our industrial classes and their families are now crowded and crammed in many of the streets of the localities referred to, can see no prospect but for a great increase of poverty, degradation and disease in the future, unless some effective measure of improvement be promptly adopted.

The politico-economical law is that demand induces supply, and if capital can be more profitably employed in house-building than otherwise, it will be so employed. The general proposition is true in ordinary times, and so also is the second, with limitations. But the condition of things of which we are complaining had its origin, or, at all events, was greatly aggravated during the four extraordinary years of war, when house-building was almost entirely stopped, though our population grew enormously, and when capital did not follow its ordinary course, because of uncertainty and timidity regarding the future. It must also be remembered that in this country capital looks not only to profitable, but to immediate returns, and pays but small respect to the humanities.

We need thirty thousand more houses than there are in this city at present to properly accommodate the population actually resident in it. We need them imperatively and immediately. Their erection would pay well as an investment, and in relation to the present and future well-being of the city would pay a hundred-fold. There is no likelihood that the city will diminish in population, but every indication that it will continue to grow in coming time more rapidly even than in the past; consequently there is no danger of depreciation in property or of loss by the investment. We have already, in the interests of the crowded and suffering industrious masses, urged this matter upon the attention of property-owners and capitalists, and we do so again. A good plan, in addition to individual efforts, would be to get up building companies, in which the combined capital of moneyed men and real estate owners could be employed. Let substantial blocks of comfortable houses be erected, such as have of late years been built in London and other English cities; and there is no doubt that they would be as profitable an investment here as they have invariably proved to be there, and would greatly redound to the prosperity of this great city. We broached this subject in the TIMES the other day, and subsequently received a note from a responsible gentleman offering to put ten thousand dollars into such a scheme.
