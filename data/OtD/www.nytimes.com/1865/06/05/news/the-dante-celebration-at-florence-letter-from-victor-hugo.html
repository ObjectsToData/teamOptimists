The Dante Celebration at Florence.; LETTER FROM VICTOR HUGO.

Published: June 5, 1865

The inauguration of the statue of DANTE took place at Florence on Sunday, the 14th ult., in the midst of a display of patriotic feeling and enthusiasm such as have been rarely withnessed in the city of the Medicis. From a very early hour in the morning the streets were astir with eager crowds, the balconies were hung with drapery, and national flags waved out on every side. At a little before 9 o'clock the procession started from the Piazza di Santo Spirito, the cannon of the fort San Giovan Battista announcing its departure. The representatives of the Italian and foreign press led the way, followed by those of the Italian dramatic art, with their banner; then came in long line the representatives of the provinces, the communes, and the institutes of every part of Italy, and the foreigners invited to the ceremony. More than 700 banners were borne in the procession. At a little before eleven the cortege reached the Piazza di Santa Croce, where it was shortly afterward met by the King, who was received with hearty and long continued applause. The Mayor of Florence then delivered a short speech, and after another from Padre GLANBATTISTA GIULIANI in honor of DAN[???]E, the covering of the monument was removed. The enthusiasm at this point of the ceremony rose to the highest pitch, and the scene was altogether most impressive. Not the slightest disorder is reported to have occurred throughout the day. The pate, the feeling of the crowd frequently found vent in sympathetic cheers for Venice, Rome and Turin.

The London Times correspondent thus describes the most important part of the ceremony: Accompanied by several bands of music, which played in turn as they entered the square, and bearing many hundreds of rich banners, having emblazoned upon them coats of arms, inscriptions, appropriate to the day, portraits of DANTE and of other celebrated persons, the long proccession slowly marched in, forming up in close order and with great regularity on the spacious steps which rose, in gentle ascent, from the level of the arena to the inner parapet around it. The collection of banners, all seemingly quite new, borne upon velvet-covered staves, surmounted with gilt ornaments and splendid with golden tassels and tresses, was such as I do not remember to have seen surpassed in any ceremony of the kind. Genoa, the rich city, sent two standard-bearers, clothed in scarlet and embroidery in charge of her magnificent colors. A whole day might have been spent in studying and deciphering the meaning of the devices on the flags and affixing names to the portraits upon many of them. Then came a loud cheer from the entrance to the square, and "the King!" was the cry. Nothing could hold the ladies in front, and they were on the benches with the activity of acrobats. Shouts of indignation in the rear led to official interference, and the restless female element again subsided. No King was there: the applause was addressed to a banner draped with black crape. The American was of opinion it was out of respect for poor LINCOLN, but the work "Venetia" told a different tale. Fresh and vehement plaudits were called forth by a banner bearing the she wolf and boys, and inscribed "Roma." When the whole of the procession was formed up round the square, several thousand strong, while the uniformed bands, comprising many hundreds of musicians, drew up at the east end below the orchestra, the sight was really gorgeous, as the brilliant sunlight played among the vivid colors, and was reflected from the gilt battle-axes, medallions and other ornaments in which the flag-staves terminated. Exclamations of admiration and delight burst forth continually from the spectators, although the view of many of them was impeded by the serried line of banners which now surrounded the square. Hardly were all the component parts of the sumptuous pageant stationary in their places when the King arrived, attended by Gen. LA MARMORA and a numerous suite. Considering that the vast assemblage only gradually become aware of his presence, his reception seemed to me excellent The cheers were hearty and reiterated. What remained to be done took but little time. The band struck up a grand symphony, the Gonfaloniere of Florence, Signor DE CAMBRY DIGNY, made a short speech, which was, doubtless, much to the purpose, but which nobody heard; and then, at the pull of a rope, the covering of the statue parted in the centre and lay in folds upon the ground. The music played louder than ever, the multitude cheered, the bells of the Palazzo Vecchio rang out a noisy peal, from the summit of the lofty tower a shot was fired, and a rocket rose into the air like a streak of white smoke across the blue firmament Some more speaking ensued -- I believe by Prof. GIULIANI -- and then came the Hymn to DANTE, executed by a band of vocalists and by the great orchestra, while the notary proceeded to the registration of the solemn act. And thus concluded one more brilliant spectacle, to add to the many which in former days were witnessed on the same square of the Holy Cross, once the favorite rendezvous for tilts, tournaments and carroussels, for sham fights and masquerades, and many other courtly and chivalrous divertissements common in the palmy days of splendid Florence.
