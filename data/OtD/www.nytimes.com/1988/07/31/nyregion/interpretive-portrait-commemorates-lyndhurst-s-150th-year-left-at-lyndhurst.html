Interpretive Portrait Commemorates Lyndhurst's 150th Year , left, at Lyndhurst.

By ROBERTA HERSHENSON
Published: July 31, 1988

One might think, from a glance at Renata Rainer's coming lecture schedule, that the accomplished photographer, art historian and teacher had hung up her camera, spilled out her darkroom chemicals and turned her lab into a library.

A visit to the Hudson River Museum of Westchester here, on the other hand, would provide reassurance that the 60-year-old Irvington artist continues the vigorous exploration of fine photographic printmaking she began in the early 1970's, fresh from her earlier career at Pocantico Central School as an art teacher and graphics printmaker.

''Motif: Lyndhurst,'' an exhibition continuing through Oct. 30 in the upper main gallery of the museum in Yonkers, consists of 47 black and white photographs by Ms. Rainer commemorating the 150th anniversary of the Tarrytown institution best known as the former residence of the 19th-century railroad magnate, Jay Gould.

The pictures do not offer a documentary portrait of Lyndhurst, whose mansion has been called an outstanding example of Gothic Revival design and which is now a property of the National Trust for Historic Preservation. Rather, the show represents the vision of an artist who seeks out the unseen, presents unexpected views of the oft-seen and reveals through light, shadow and special techniques the spirit of a place as perceived by the imagination. Photographer's Intention

''I intend my work at Lyndhurst to be a contemporary interpretive portrait of a past age,'' Ms. Rainer writes in the foreword to the 16-page illustrated catalogue, with an essay by Amelia Peck - an assistant curator at the Metropolitan Museum of Art - that was published by Lyndhurst in connection with the show.

Ms. Rainer made the images over a 10-year period, without, initially, a view to their collective exhibition. The photographer, who lives about a mile from Lyndhurst, came to know the estate and the way light shaped its trees, shrubs and structures at different times of day and in different seasons.

''I've seen it on my skis and when I've just been walking,'' Ms. Rainer said in describing her frequent hikes along the Croton Aquaduct, which runs through the property.

In two of the earliest pictures, ''Elegance Remembered I and II'' of 1978, the artist depicts the peeling wallpaper and crumbling plaster of rooms past their prime.

As her work evolved, however, Ms. Rainer sought instead to capture the ''romantic'' quality of the estate - ''the harmonious integration of ''Nature's design' with the poetic rhythms of 'human design,' '' she wrote in the catalogue. (The restoration of Lyndhurst's interiors is a long-range goal, said the institution's assistant director, Barbara Hammond. Ms. Hammond noted that since 1978, the National Trust for Historic Preservation had spent ''close to $750,000 on systems-upgrading to achieve a sound outer shell.'' The trust is ''now on the verge of addressing'' further restoration, she said.) Ms. Rainer has brought her impressive command of darkroom technique to the Lyndhurst photographs. She uses negative imagery (reversing blacks and whites in a picture such as ''Nocturne''), split-toning (tinting interior views both deep red and gray), montage (sandwiching negatives to create a layered effect) and infrared film with special filters. Each technique is used selectively to create a mood. Merging Past and Present

The montages, especially ''Fairytale Castle,'' in which the mansion's tower is superimposed on a gnarled tree, and ''Memento of Helen and Dorothy,'' in which a marble statue of two young former residents of the estate hovers ghostlike above a view of the grounds - combine past and present in one seamless image.

Ms. Rainer, who used both a 35-millimeter Olympus camera and a Hasselblad for the project, said she found that by shooting with film sensitive to infrared light rays - rays just beyond the red of the visible spectrum - ''the more expected, cliched views of the place fell away and one saw it afresh.'' Using infrared film with a dark red filter deepens the sky, gives a ''fleecy'' look to clouds and brings a luminous, shimmering quality to foliage, the photographer explained.

The infrared technique was especially suited to her spring shooting (as seen in pictures such as ''Greenhouse'' and ''The Japanese Split-Leaf Maple''), Ms. Rainer said, because of ''the way it captures the inner energy of things that are alive'' and beginning to bloom at that time of year. 'Hauntingly Beautiful Images'

Summer photography can be enlivened by the use of infrared - ''in the summer, black and white is boring because all the greens turn gray,'' conceded Ms. Rainer - because of ''the element of surprise'' in the technique, the photographer added. The ''hauntingly beautiful images'' infrared can produce are ''unpredictable a lot of the time,'' she said.

''Straight'' photographs are also included in the exhibition. There are atmospheric portraits of trees in the snow and views of such rooms as the library, the parlor and the dining room.

Ms. Hammond, closely connected with Ms. Rainer's project even as Lyndhurst's administrators changed (Susanne Brendel Pandich is the current director), said she felt the exhibition would ''truly give the viewer a new look'' at the 67-acre estate.

Thomas Holman, director of the Hudson River Museum of Westchester, called the exhibition ''a multimarriage'' between the museum - with its own historic Trevor Mansion - Lyndhurst and Ms. Rainer. He added that the museum was ''delighted, pleased and proud'' to honor its upriver neighbor on its anniversary and to enable people ''to appreciate Lyndhurst in the abstract'' through Ms. Rainer's photography.

Ms. Rainer, who has been named a ''Speaker in the Humanities'' for 1988-89 by the New York Council for the Humanities, will discuss ''100 Years of Landscape Photography'' at Lyndhurst at 4 P.M. on Sept. 18 in the Carriage House, and will speak on the same topic later in the fall at the Hudson River Museum. She will also present a series of six illustrated lectures called 'Re: Viewing the Landscape'' in October and November at the Greenburgh Town Hall for the Town of Greenburgh's Arts and Culture Committee. In addition, Ms. Rainer will serve as a visiting artist for the Council of the Arts in Westchester and will initiate a 10-session workshop in September at the State University of New York at Purchase called ''Rethinking Photography,'' which will include portfolio critiques for the skilled amateur or professional photographer.
