THEATER; GURNEY'S 'AMERICAN LIFE' REVIVED AT LONG WHARF

By ALVIN KLEIN
Published: January 31, 1988

WITH the current Long Wharf Theater revival of one of A. R. Gurney Jr.'s early works, the 1970 ''Scenes From American Life,'' and the opening Off Broadway of his latest produced play, ''Another Antigone,'' at Playwrights Horizons, chroniclers of Mr. Gurney's career have a rare chance to see the playwright in perspective, and to contrast his past and present concerns.

''Scenes From American Life,'' which began performances on Jan. 22 and runs through March 6, was first seen in 1970 at the Windsor Mountain School in Lenox, Mass., and then at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where Mr. Gurney, who is 56 years old and currently on an extended leave of absence, has been professor of American literature and the humanities for 26 years. The following year, the play had a brief, noncommercial run (36 performances) at the small Forum Theater, which is now called the Mitzi E. Newhouse, in Lincoln Center.

With a title that the playwright said ''echoes George Eliot's 'Scenes From Provincial Life,' '' the playwright seeks ''to chronicle the demise of a class - and the decay of a city.''

That city is Mr. Gurney's native Buffalo; the play, which has no conventional plot, no chronological sequence and no central characters, is, Mr. Gurney explained, ''a mosaic of short, staccato vignettes'' - 36 in all. It spans four decades, beginning with the 1930's and going on to depict a fascistic, futuristic society, replete with military takeovers and detention camps. Eight actors are required to play more than 100 roles.

* For the Long Wharf production, Mr. Gurney rewrote four scenes -''like touching up an old painting,'' he said - two of which he expected to be retained after opening night Friday. ''Since I pinpointed the future as the 80's and now we're near the end of that decade,'' he said, ''we've taken out some specific dates.''

Explaining his concerns when he wrote ''Scenes From American Life,'' Mr. Gurney said: ''My attitude was one of dismay at my own inability -and the inability of those I knew from that privileged world - to respond with responsibility, resilience, vigor, leadership and intelligence, to the changing times, to the war, to the civil rights movement. Democracy was imperiled. So the play, written in anger, was an attack on myself and the world I grew up in. I saw myself and them as ineffectual and useless.''

Referring to his own father's reaction to the play, Mr. Gurney once said in an interview, ''When he saw 'Scenes From American Life,' in which I satirized Buffalo's WASP's, he found it difficult to speak to me for about a year.''

Now that the playwright can reassess his 1970 view of the future, he looks back more with forgiveness than with anger. ''My attitude is more generous about that world and less paranoid about the future of democracy,'' he said.

* As for his current Off Broadway play, the conventionally structured ''Another Antigone,'' which did not receive enthusiastic reviews, concerns the ideological confrontation between an old-guard classics professor and a rebellious student who accuses him of anti-Semitism, and it has a darker tone than his previous works. In contrast to ''Scenes,'' which, he said, ''is epic in scope and takes place all over the city,'' Mr. Gurney's later plays have ''smaller psychological and focused issues.'' And the play on which he is currently working, ''The Cocktail Hour,'' has four characters and spans one-and-a-half hours - its actual running time.

About the ongoing pertinence of ''Scenes,'' its director, John Tillinger added: ''We still realize our democracy is very vulnerable and fragile, and we have to hang onto it and protect it. Maybe we've forgotten what it was like - children running to Canada because of Vietnam - but if we're living in a Gorbachev euphoria, we have to be careful; things could turn awry.

''This was a sequestered world, and it's symptomatic of any cross-section of American society. It's a play about duty and responsibility, and a sense of tradition that's slowly being lost, themes. He couches his concerns in light terms, but he cares; the laughs pack a punch.''

While Mr. Gurney Jr., who is nicknamed ''Pete,'' has been an unusually prolific and widely produced playwright, he has not had a significant commercial breakthrough, nor has he been blessed with more than an occasional rave notice. With his urbane style and sophisticated, often insulated characters, he has been compared to Philip Barry, S. N. Behrman and Terrence Rattigan.

''Sweet Sue,'' the one play of Mr. Gurney's to get to Broadway, was, in the playwright's word, ''plastered'' by the critics, and in spite of the star presences of Mary Tyler Moore and Lynn Redgrave, under the direction of Mr. Tillinger, it retrieved only half its investment, one of the play's producers, Arthur Whitelaw, said recently.

* Although Mr. Gurney's works -among them ''What I Did Last Summer,'' ''The Golden Age,'' ''The Perfect Party'' and, most particularly, ''The Dining Room'' - are often seen countrywide, Connecticut's leading regional theaters have been slow in coming around to producing them.
