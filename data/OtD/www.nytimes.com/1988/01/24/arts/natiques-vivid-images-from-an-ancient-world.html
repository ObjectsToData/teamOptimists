NATIQUES; Vivid Images From An Ancient World

By RITA REIF
Published: January 24, 1988

ANCIENT CARTHAGE - A city of immense wealth where commerce and culture flourished - left a legacy of some of antiquity's most compelling mosaics, 17 of which are on view at the American Museum of Natural History through May 1.

The highly realistic images of wildlife, landscapes, feasting and the hunt - created with hundreds of bits of colored marble and stone cemented into floors - dominate the exhibition ''Carthage: A Mosaic of Ancient Tunisia.'' Organized over eight years by Prof. David Soren, a classicist and classical archeologist at the University of Arizona, the show was produced for the Museum of Natural History in cooperation with the Bardo Museum in Tunis and other Tunisian institutions. Dr. Soren, who is curator, was co-author with Aicha Ben Abed Ben Khader of the catalogue, published by the museum in association with W. W. Norton ($19.95 in paper; $35 in cloth).

The presentation of this architectural material - the largest selection of such floor mosaics exhibited in this country in memory - comes at a time when ancient art enthusiasts and scholars are increasingly curious about the highly expressive images found in mosaics. The interest lies in both what these mosaics reveal about life in antiquity and the level of sophistication achieved in this art form.

The mosaics of Carthage are subtly colored works in brown, gray, black and white, punctuated by thick strokes of jewellike cubes - the colors of emeralds and rubies. These boldly patterned pavements range from fragments two feet square to major sections of floors, 28 feet long. The images are decidedly robust, and many are dramatically framed by abstract and geometric designs -some with optical illusions. Borders also incorporate realistic images and stylized foliage, as can be seen in one depicting a hare and a dog leaping through spirals of acanthus leaves.

Carthaginian craftsmen originated the mosaic art form, Dr. Soren said last weekend by telephone from Tucson. Excavations have uncovered a number of floors with flat-surfaced mosaics of stone cubes in cement that date from the fifth century B.C. These earliest mosaics were plain, he said, and the art form flowered in Carthage after the city was destroyed by Rome in 146 B.C. The more sophisticated tableaux were devised under the Romans and later - from the first century to the fifth century A.D., the period of the mosaics in the show.

Dr. Soren said that most of the mosaics sent from Tunisia were found largely intact in houses excavated over the last century, some as recently as a decade ago. Several of the panels have never been seen by the public until now, because they were restored in Tunisia at the Bardo with funds provided by the Museum of Natural History, he said.

Although mosaics dominate, this exhibition of 97 selections also includes jewelry, pottery, coins, sculpture and metal artifacts, dating from about 800 B.C. to A.D. 500. The story told of ancient Carthage begins with its founding by Berbers and continues through the third century B.C., when it rivaled Rome in wealth and power. The Punic Wars fought by these two Mediterranean powers raged, on and off, over 120 years - with Hannibal leading the Carthaginian army and 37 elephants across the Alps in 218 B.C. to attack Rome. In 146 B.C. Cato's insistent cry to the Romans, ''Carthage must be destroyed,'' was fulfilled. The city was leveled.

Raw intensity characterizes the wildlife scenes depicted in several mosaics. The grisly episodes include two lions devouring a wild boar, a tiger attacking two wild asses, and a python coiled in a death grip around an elephant, fiercely biting its abdomen. The variety of art styles includes a surrealistic study of a score of fish rising from - or falling into -a basket.

Five men in high spirits at a feast are depicted, as in modern comic books, with their comments spelled out above their heads: ''Let us amuse ourselves,'' says one. ''Enough talk,'' comments another. And, from a third: ''Silence, let the bulls sleep.'' Pre-Byzantine mosaics include several portraits with halos, an uncommon touch in fifth-century mosaics, the most notable being the large-eyed lady of Carthage.

''The golden age of mosaics in Carthage was the second century through the fourth century A.D.,'' Dr. Soren said. ''When you walk through a village in ancient Tunisia dating from Roman times you see mosaics even in lower-middle-class houses. There were mosaic floors everywhere.'' In the villas of merchants, they were in the rooms set aside for entertaining guests - including courtyards, dining rooms and bedrooms. But they have also been found in utilitarian areas like baths and works areas.

The exhibition, which will travel for at least two years after it closes in New York, to Houston, Los Angeles, New Orleans and Albuquerque, has been funded in part by a grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities.

Mosaics of the same period crafted in North Africa were the subject of an exhibition last March at the Edward H. Merrin Gallery, 724 Fifth Avenue. ''There is a strength in the images have,'' Mr. Merrin said. The market has been active in this material over the last decade, especially in the 1980's, he said, because several important Lebanese collectors have fled Beirut since civil war broke out, moving to Switzerland, where they have sold their mosaics.

Mr. Merrin is showing several mosaics in his booth at the Winter Antiques Show, on view through next Sunday at the Seventh Regiment Armory, Park Avenue at 66th Street. A sparely drawn strutting peacock, with sticklike legs and a burst of feathers behind, is $8,000. Other works that he handles in his gallery range from $5,000 to $125,000.

Two late Roman mosaics from the Middle East are currently on view in the window of Antiquarium, 948 Madison Avenue (at 75th Street). One depicts a woman wearing rich jewelry in her hair and around her neck, The other is of a young man holding a vase. The heads of both are framed in halos. The 30-inch-square mosaics are $45,000 each.
