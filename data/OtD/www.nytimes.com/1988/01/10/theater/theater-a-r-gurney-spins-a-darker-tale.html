THEATER; A. R. Gurney Spins a Darker Tale

By JEREMY GERARD
Published: January 10, 1988

IN 1971 A. R. GURNEY JR. WROTE ''The Old One-Two,'' a play about a classics professor whose beloved Euripides and Seneca are about to be displaced by timelier courses taught by young teachers yearning to be relevant.

The playwright knew whereof he wrote. A teacher of American literature and the humanities at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Mr. Gurney is a product of privilege (son of a Buffalo businessman) and of academe (educated at St. Paul's, Williams College and the Yale School of Drama). With such plays as ''The Middle Ages'' (1978) and ''The Dining Room'' (1982) - he would emerge over the next decade as an American Terence Rattigan, writing with an interweaving detachment and no small sense of nostalgia intricately crafted scenes of a Protestant culture challenged by Jews, blacks, women and others seeking a share of the American pie.

''I've taught the classics,'' Mr. Gurney said, during a recent conversation at the Upper West Side apartment he shares with his wife. ''Seventeen years ago, I wrote a one-act play called 'The Old One-Two,' about a typical absent-minded professor who falls in love. As far as I know, it's only been performed once, at Brandeis.''

If that small play has not been produced professionally, its subject has rarely been far from Mr. Gurney's heart. ''Another Antigone,'' which opens Thursday at Playwrights Horizons, also concerns a classics professor deemed an anachronism by nearly everyone around him. But it is a very different work. ''Another Antigone'' represents a significant shift in tone for an author best known for his light touch (as in last season's ''Sweet Sue'') and his skeptic's eye (as in the 1986 comedy, ''The Perfect Party'').

In the new play, the main challenge to ProGrizzard) comes from a persistent Jewish student named Judy Miller (played by Marissa Chibas), who insists that he accept her updated version of Sophocles' tragedy as her term paper for the course. He refuses, and in the ensuing contretemps they grapple with topics ranging from the value of ancient writing to nuclear disarmament.

Anti-Semitism, however, becomes the issue that hurtles teacher and student toward their inevitable showdown. The world according to Professor Harper is an ongoing dialectic between Athens and Jerusalem: ''There it all is,'' he tells an unconvinced student. ''The private conscience versus the communal obligation. Jew versus Greek. Miss Miller versus me.'' ''The Old One-Two'' was a farce primarily concerned with challenged traditions. ''Another Antigone'' is a much darker work, the playwright said, whose subject - anti-Semitism as practiced by those who would never imagine themselves prone to the charge of prejudice - has been touched on, if not explored, in his other works. ''Jews have appeared in most of my plays,'' Mr. Gurney said. ''I've always been interested in the attitudes of Gentiles toward Jews.'' In the trophy room of an exclusive men's club that is the setting of ''The Middle Ages,'' for example, one of the main characters automatically pronounces disdain for everything Jewish-sounding (including Harrisburg, Pa.). The couple invited to ''The Perfect Party'' are affronted by their host's confession that he has invited them because they're Jewish. ''Once the party gets going, you both should feel free to explore your own Jewishness,'' he tells them in the comedy, ''even in front of our shallowest Protestant friends.''

Mr. Gurney wrote ''The Perfect Party'' at the same time as ''Another Antigone.'' The original plan was for Playwrights Horizons to mount the two plays together. '' 'The Perfect Party' was sort of a satyr play for this, a other Antigone' is an antidote to the lightness of 'Perfect Party.' '' As it turned out, the Off Off Broadway company couldn't afford to do both plays. So ''Party'' opened at Playwrights Horizons and then transferred to a commercial Off Broadway run. ''Another Antigone'' had its premiere last year at the Old Globe Theater in San Diego. John Tillinger, who staged Mr. Gurney's ''Golden Age'' (1984) and ''Sweet Sue,'' directed both of the new plays.

Mr. Gurney remains a tenured professor at M.I.T., though he has taught there only sporadically since the success of ''The Dining Room.'' He still smarts from accusations that a work of his, written while he was teaching there, was anti-Semitic.
