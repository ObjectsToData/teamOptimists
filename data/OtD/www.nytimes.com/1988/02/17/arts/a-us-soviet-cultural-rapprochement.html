A U.S.-Soviet Cultural Rapprochement

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: February 17, 1988

A little more than two years after Secretary of State George P. Shultz and Foreign Minister Eduard A. Shevardnadze of the Soviet Union signed the General Exchanges Agreement at Geneva, a remarkable number of cultural exchanges are bursting out between the two nations and prompting a mixture of awe, delight and occasional doubt and scorn.

Not even the legendary impresario Sol Hurok, who pioneered the booking of Soviet cultural attractions in the United States two decades ago, foresaw that so many of the foremost arts institutions in the two superpowers would become engaged in cultural interchange.

But this spring the New York Philharmonic is to visit the Soviet Union for the first time in 12 years, possibly with the pianist Van Cliburn as a soloist, the Metropolitan Museum of Art has signed agreements with the Hermitage Museum in Leningrad and the Pushkin Museum in Moscow for four major exchange shows over the next two years, and the Maly Dramatic Theater of Leningrad later this year will become the first major Soviet theatrical troupe to visit the United States since the Moscow Art Theater toured this country nearly a quarter of a century ago.

Smaller groups such as Dance Theater of Harlem, the Brandywine River Museum in Chadds Ford, Pa., and Pavel Virsky's Ukrainian State Dance Company are also participating, and, for the first time Soviet artists are not merely presenting their customary star turns but are also rehearsing together with their American counterparts in the production of new work.

Moreover, top-ranking Soviet cultural managers and bureaucrats have been criss-crossing the United States in recent weeks whipping up enthusiasm for new exchange projects.

It has taken this long after the signing of the Geneva agreement for productions to get started because major cultural institutions must lay their plans a year or two in advance of actual presentation.

There is nothing in the Geneva agreement which would prevent defections of Soviet artists while visiting the West; some observers believe that the Soviet Union is less apprehensive about this matter than in previous years because the Government learned from Mikhail Baryshnikov's defection that limited artistic opportunities had tempted Soviet artists to defect. Accordingly, in recent years, the Soviet Union has permitted some top artists such as the dancer Maya Plisetskaya, the mezzo-soprano Elena Obraztsova and the virtuoso dancer Vadim Pisarer to appear internationally. 'Something Besides Missile Throw Weight'

''It's a thrill to have our two Governments talking about something besides missile throw weight,'' said Senator Alan K. Simpson, Republican of Wyoming, at a recent meeting in Washington with Culture Minister Vasiliy G. Zakharov of the Soviet Union.

William H. Luers, the president of the Metropolitan Museum and a former Ambassador to Czechoslovakia, sees the current burst of cultural exchange as an opportunity for Americans to participate in Mikhail S. Gorbachev's glasnost. ''We now have a chance to be part of this expansion of the role of intellectuals in their society,'' Mr. Luers said. ''We want that.''

Sarah Caldwell, who, with Rodion Shchedrin, a leading Soviet composer, is serving as co-director of a small army of Soviet musicians and dancers descending on Boston next month for the three-week ''Make Music Together'' festival is jubilant about the prospect.

''It's tremendously exciting and tremendously important for us to be speaking with each other,'' she said yesterday. ''We're so ignorant of the Soviets and they're so ignorant of us, there is a just frightful lack of knowledge on both sides. This extends not only to music but to life styles and what we believe. We are just beginning to try to understand one another.''

Others run against the general mood of euphoria.

''I don't approve of it,'' said Hilton Kramer, editor of the neo-conservative New Criterion magazine. ''It reminds us of what short political memories American artists and cultural bureaucrats have. They can so easily put aside the history of suffering that artists and ordinary citizens have endured and continue to endure in the Soviet Union. I think it's deplorable that just out of pure self-interest these exchanges are so hotly pursued.''

''There's no question but that their motives are propagandistic,'' said Philippe de Montebello, the director of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, ''but as long as they don't bludgeon their curators into sending works which should not travel, or don't belong in a given show, then these exchanges serve the public of both nations.'' The Major Exchanges And the Major Visitors These are among the major upcoming Soviet-American cultural exchanges are the following:

* Soviet musicians, dancers, composers and poets numbering 285 will join a group of American artists under the co-direction of Miss Caldwell, the artistic director of the Opera Company of Boston, and Mr. Shchedrin, the Russian composer, to produce the ''Make Music Together'' festival in Boston beginning March 11. The festival, which will be presented in at least five major Boston theaters and concert halls, will feature the United States premiere of Mr. Shchedrin's opera ''Dead Souls,'' based on the novel by Gogol. Also, major ballets, symphonic works and an oratorio never before heard in the United States will be rehearsed and performed by Soviet and American musicians.
