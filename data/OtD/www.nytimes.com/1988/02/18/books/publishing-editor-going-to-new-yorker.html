Publishing: Editor Going to New Yorker

By EDWIN McDOWELL
Published: February 18, 1988

Pat Strachan, who started as an editorial assistant at Farrar, Straus & Giroux and rose to associate publisher, will leave the independent house on March 10 to become a fiction editor at The New Yorker.

''I'll be leaving one fine firm for another fine firm,'' she said yesterday, ''with the expectation I'll have even more time to read and edit established writers and to bring new writers to a very wide audience.''

A 17-year veteran of Farrar, Straus, Ms. Strachan has had an especially successful past year or so, having edited the current No. 1 fiction best seller, ''The Bonfire of the Vanities'' by Tom Wolfe, and ''Paco's Story'' by Larry Heinemann, which won the National Book Award as the best work of fiction in 1987. Some of her other authors include Marilynne Robinson, Edna O'Brien, Jamaica Kincaid, Padgett Powell, Seamus Heaney and Rodney Hall.

Asked who started the discussions, Ms. Strachan circumspectly replied, ''The New Yorker is indirect, so it's hard to say.'' Nonetheless, she made her decision to accept last Thursday and told Roger Straus, the company founder, the same day.

Unlike Robert Gottlieb, the editor of The New Yorker, who continues to edit certain authors for Alfred A. Knopf, which he headed for many years, Ms. Strachan will not continue to edit books for Farrar, Straus & Giroux. It has not yet been decided who will edit her authors after she leaves. ''We haven't gotten to that stage yet,'' she said. ''But they'll be well taken care of, I have no doubt about that. Farrar, Straus knows how to hire well.'' Doubleday Reversal

The Doubleday Book Shop on Fifth Avenie at 53d Street was scheduled to close at the end of this month, but now it will remain open at least through spring of 1989.

''We would have liked to stay forever,'' said Gary DelVecchio, the president of the 28 Doubleday stores. ''But we could never come to terms with the landlord about money or the length of the lease, so eventually we will have to leave. But the owners have asked us to stay on into next year.''

The store has been in its present location since 1968, and before that it was at 52d Street and Fifth Avenue for many years. But once it closes, Doubleday has no plans to relocate it elsewhere on Fifth Avenue. ''We'll continue to look for stores, maybe in the financial district or upper Madison Avenue,'' Mr. DelVecchio said, ''but our plans for Fifth Avenue are just to continue with our store at 57th Street.''

That store, which stays open until midnight every night except Sunday, is the flagship of the Doubleday chain, which has stores in 13 states. What's more, Mr. DelVecchio said, it probably sells more new trade books (that is, books of general interest) than any other bookstore in the United States. Southern Writers Unite

Almost two dozen prominent Southern novelists, poets, historians and literary critics recently formed the Fellowship of Southern Writers. Its purpose, according to Cleanth Brooks, the noted critic and the prime mover of the fellowship, is ''to nurture literature in the American South through recognizing distinguished achievement'' and to establish prizes and fellowships for outstanding young writers.

Mr. Brooks was elected chancellor of the fellowship by the 19 other founders. The novelists and poets are Eudora Welty, James Dickey, Walker Percy, Robert Penn Warren, Andrew Lytle, George Garrett, Reynolds Price, William Styron, Peter Taylor, Fred Chappell and Elizabeth Spencer. The historians are C. Vann Woodward, John Hope Franklin and Shelby Foote, and the critics George Core, Louis D. Rubin, Lewis P. Simpson, Blyden Jackson and Walter Sullivan.

Membership in the organization will be limited to 36 writers at any one time, according to Mr. Core, editor of the Sewanee Review in Sewanee, Tenn. ''Many of us have academic affiliations,'' he said, ''but we didn't want an academic organization, we want an organization for writers.'' The University of Tennessee will provide an office at its library in Chattanooga, and Mr. Core said this was one organization that would not be saddled with bureaucracy or large overhead.

Southern literature is justly celebrated, and Mr. Core said neither its influence nor vitality was in immediate danger of disappearing. But many promising younger Southern writers don't have the financial support and fellowships they need to keep at it, he said, or anywhere near the kind of support provided by some states. To help rectify that, the fellowship intends to raise money from foundations, regional publishers, state commissions on the humanities and private donors. 'Atomic Bomb' Pays Off

Paperback reprints usually appear a year or more after the hard-cover edition, which by then has run its course. But after ''The Making of the Atomic Bomb'' by Richard Rhodes won the 1987 National Book Award for nonfiction, Simon & Schuster sped up its publishing schedule by a couple of weeks, hoping that its Touchstone trade paperback edition could cash in on the publicity.

But the publicity for the book increased considerably when the book also won the National Book Critics Circle Award last month as the best work of nonfiction published in 1987. After a first printing of 22,500, there are now 27,500 paperbacks in print. Moreover, the hard-cover edition has sold about 35,000, and it too continues to sell. Those sales should tail off before long - unless, of course, ''The Making of the Atomic Bomb'' pulls off a literary hat trick on March 31 by also winning the Pulitzer Prize.
