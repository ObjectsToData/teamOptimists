EDUCATION; American Teaching Of the Arts Is Assailed

By WILLIAM H. HONAN, Special to the New York Times
Published: May 4, 1988

A potentially controversial plan to steer arts education away from performance and exhibition and toward more disciplined study was made public today by Frank Hodsoll, chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts.

''The problem is, basic arts education does not exist in the United States today,'' said the 182-page document, ''Toward Civilization.'' Without such basics, ''the artistic heritage that is ours, and the opportunities to contribute significantly to its evolution, is being lost to our young people.''

Congress commissioned the report in 1985 as part of legislation governing the endowment that year.

''Trying to create or perform the nonliterary arts without skills and knowledge is like trying to write without vocabulary and syntax,'' the report said.

''The arts are in triple jeopardy,'' it continued, because ''they are not viewed as serious; knowledge itself is not viewed as a prime educational objective; and those who determine school curricula do not agree on what arts education is.'' Stringent Standards Sought

The report recommends the development of stringent standards, the recognition of masterpieces and the intensification of study throughout the primary and secondary school system.

''It's down with finger painting and up with Rembrandt,'' said an arts administrator familiar with the report.

Although the report is only a recommendation, it is expected to be widely discussed. It will soon be the subject of hearings by a House subcommittee on education, and Mr. Hodsoll and his staff plan to make what he described in an interview today as ''an intensive advocacy campaign in all regions of the country over the next 12 months, beginning with a gathering of educators from six states in St. Louis on May 9.''

Furthermore, to promote the report, the endowment is collaborating with the Department of Education in establishing and funding research centers on arts and literature education around the country and has joined the J. Paul Getty Trust to develop a television series on arts education.

''Toward Civilization'' is based on research and consultation over two years by about half a dozen staff members of the endowment and professional writers and researchers. It also incorporates advice and comments from members of Congress, outside experts and members of the National Council on the Arts, which advises the endowment. Report Recommendations

The report makes these recommendations:

* State education agencies and local school districts should establish ''the minimum knowledge and skills'' in the arts that all students must possess before graduating from high school.

* Instruction in the arts should take about 15 percent of the school week throughout elementary, junior high and middle school levels.

* High schools should require all students to take an art course through two of the four years of high school.

* High schools should offer optional specialized arts courses for exceptionally interested or talented students.

* ''Knowledgeable experts'' should be engaged to coordinate arts curriculum development and evaluation.

* Budgets should provide for making all ''appropriate arts materials'' available.

Other sections of the report advocate concentrating on ''the great works of art from all times and cultures,'' shifting the emphasis of endowment policy from visiting-artist programs to school-generated programs and testing in arts education.

The ideological break that ''Toward Civilization'' makes with conventional liberal wisdom of the recent past becomes apparent when the document is compared with ''Coming to Our Senses,'' a report on arts education produced 11 years ago by the American Council for the Arts in Education and paid for by the endowment in co-operation with private sources.

Both reports call for sweeping change, but whereas ''Coming to Our Senses'' stressed such liberal virtues as ''enriching our emotional selves'' and ''the rich world of sensation, emotion and personal expression,'' the new report emphasizes such conservative orthodoxy as ''cultural literacy,'' ''hard work and discipline'' and, above all, ''knowledge and understanding.''

''Toward Civilization'' draws its intellectual capital from two ideologically conservative sources. The first is a report published in 1980 by the Heritage Foundation accusing the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities of supporting much nonart for political purposes, thereby relegating culture to ''mere entertainment.'' Criticism of Education

The second source is the sharp criticism of American education in a series of reports and books, beginning with ''A Nation at Risk,'' a 1983 study by the National Commission on Excellence in Education, which used such military terminology as ''an act of war'' and ''educational disarmament'' to make its point that the public school system had declined.

Two leading neoconservative intellectuals - William J. Bennett, Secretary of Education and the author of ''A Nation at Risk,'' and Samuel Lipman, publisher of the New Criterion and the author of the 1980 Heritage Foundation report - strongly influenced ''Toward Civilization.''

Nevertheless, Mr. Hodsoll, a moderate Republican who has been a supporter of the Presidential campaign of Vice President Bush, was in charge of producing ''Toward Civilization'' and had final approval of the text, and his principal researcher and writer was Brent Wilson, a professor of art education in the School of Visual Arts at Pennsylvania State University, who describes himself as a registered Democrat and a liberal. Departure From 'Touchy-Feely'

Such ideological fusion illustrates that although the provenance of ''Toward Civilization'' is unmistakably neoconservative, many moderates and liberals in the arts and education have come to believe, as Mr. Wilson put it the other day, that ''American educators have too long ignored the fact that mastery of content and subject matter are what mark the educated person'' and that a departure from the ''touchy-feely'' school of thought is long overdue.

Nevertheless, challenging voices are sure to be heard as soon as the report receives wide circulation. For example, Maxine Greene, a professor of education at Columbia Teachers College and philosopher in residence at the Lincoln Center Institute for the Arts and Education, read an advance copy. Professor Greene said today that what is missing from the report is ''the concern for the release of imagination and perception linked to a viewing of the arts as intrinsically meaningful, not primarily contributory to a conservative view of cultural literacy.''

Free copies of ''Toward Civilization'' may be obtained by writing to Public Information Office, National Endowment for the Arts, 1100 Pennsylvania Ave. NW, Washington, D.C. 20506.
