Yale Moves to Make Cum Laude Mean More

By NICK RAVO, Special to the New York Times
Published: May 22, 1988

Twenty years ago, graduating with honors from Yale University was a mark of distinction. Barely a quarter of the graduating seniors qualified for honors. Fewer than 3 percent were awarded diplomas with the highest approbation, summa cum laude. This year, however, with grade inflation, making an A is almost as common as getting a C once was. About half of the university's graduating class of 1,300 will receive honors. About 10 percent are expected to graduate summa cum laude.

The surge in honors has prompted Yale officials to adopt a new program that will make it tougher for future undergraduates to receive honors. No More Than 30 Percent

The program, which was adopted earlier this month and will take effect with the freshman class entering in September, will restrict the number of students receiving honors - cum laude, magna cum laude and summa cum laude - to 30 percent of each graduating class. Grade point averages, which in the past have not been used by the university in compiling students' records, will be calculated to determine whether honors will be awarded.

Honors are currently based on the number of A's and A-minuses a student receives. Cum laude degrees are awarded to students who obtain A's or A-minuses for at least 45 percent of their academic credits. Sixty-five percent is needed to graduate magna cum laude, 80 percent for summa cum laude.

''Students now will be judged versus the kinds of grades their peers get and not on an absolute basis,'' said Sofia Simmonds, an associate dean at Yale and the chairman of the university committee that drafted the new honors program.

''Awarding of a bachelor's degree with honors ought to recognize really superior students,'' she said. ''It is peculiar for a college to say that 50 percent or even 45 percent are so superior that they should receive honors.'' What Other Colleges Do

Most colleges and universities use grade point averages in determining honors. Many schools also limit the number of students who receive honors to a fixed or maximum percentage of those graduating.

At Columbia University, for example, only the top 15 percent of the graduating seniors can receive honors. At Cornell University, the figure is 25 percent; at Dartmouth College, it is 35 percent.

Some schools, however, like New York University and the University of Pennsylvania, do not limit the number of students who can receive honors. Other schools, like Princeton University, allow individual departments to set the standards for awarding honors.

''It does lose some meaning when it's such a large percentage of the class,'' said Elisabeth M. Armstrong, a 20-year-old Yale junior from Baltimore. ''Honors should have more distinction.''

The Yale program will award summa cum laude degrees to the top 5 percent of the graduating class; magna cum laude to the next 10 percent, and cum laude to the next 15 percent. Lengthy Honor Rolls

Last year, 46.4 percent of all seniors graduated from Yale with general honors - 11 percent graduated summa cum laude, 11.5 percent magna cum laude and 23.9 percent cum laude. In 1986, 50.5 percent of the graduating class received honors. By comparison, in 1966, the figure was 26.7 percent. Figures for the class of 1988 will not be available until later this month. Graduation exercises are to be held May 30. Officials at Yale, which has 5,000 undergraduates, said they do not know why the number of students receiving honors has risen so dramatically. They do concede, however, that grade inflation, a practice whereby teachers grant a disproportionate number of high marks, has been a contributing factor.

''It may be because students are smarter or work harder - or because the exams are getting easier,'' Ms. Simmonds said. ''There has been some inflation in grades.''

She also noted, though, that students hoping to attend competitive professional schools and prestigious graduate schools have been under increasing pressure in the last decade to get high marks. 'Hard to Get Below B-Minus'

''There still are plenty of students who take college in stride and don't overdo it,'' Ms. Simmonds said. ''But the pressure to get into some professional and graduate schools is very keen. The pre-med students in particular are known for working very hard.''

Yale students attribute the increase in honors to grade inflation more than any other factor. They also say it is a phenomenon that has occurred at virtually every university.

''It's pretty hard to get below a B-minus in most humanities courses,'' said Elaine M. Pofeldt, 21, a Yale senior from Floral Park, L.I. ''I hear it's a little different in science classes, though.''

''There aren't really F's anymore,'' she said. ''People look at a C and think it's an F.''
