EDUCATION: ABOUT EDUCATION

Fred M. Hechinger
Published: May 25, 1988

''For the first time in history, principals sat down next to teachers so that they could learn together.'' This indictment of aloof school administrators comes from Judith Hodgson, director of a Philadelphia program created to bring principals and teachers together to make schools more effective.

Even if it overstates the case - little in American education is happening ''for the frst time in history'' -the point that there often is a destructive gap between school administrators and teachers is easy to document.

One place where it is fully and persuasively documented is in ''The Empowerment of Teachers: Overcoming the Crisis of Confidence,'' a new book by Gene I. Maeroff (Teachers College Press, $11.95), based on a study commissioned by the Rockefeller Foundation. Mr. Maeroff is a senior fellow of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching and a former education writer for The New York Times.

He found the principal-teacher relationship so paternal and hierarchical that some principals treat teachers ''as though they occupied a niche only slightly above that of the students they teach.''

Any reporter attending staff conferences in schools untouched by some of the recent changes that have swept through the schools will hear personal horror stories. (It might be added that some teachers also treat their students in such a patronizing fashion, but good teachers do not.) The Rockefeller Foundation had a special interest in commissioning this study. The foundation has been financing a program it calls Chart, for Collaboratives for Humanities and Arts Teaching. The program now consists of a network of eight large school systems: Atlanta, Los Angeles, New York, Pittsburgh, St. Louis, St. Paul, Philadelphia and Seattle and all of rural South Carolina.

The programs differ in each district, but all focus on the arts, reading and writing, Western culture and cultures of the rest of the world. All these programs invite private foundations and corporations to support ideas and actions to end the separation between schools and universities.

For example, the Atlanta program concentrated on offering more humanities courses in the schools and having teachers and university professors work together to achieve this.

Just as important has been the attempt to rescue teachers from their isolation and close the gap between teachers and principals.

The Maeroff book is not an exercise in principal-bashing. Alberta Arthurs, the Rockefeller Foundation's director for arts and humanities and creator of Chart, says, ''Without enlightened superintendents and principals, school reform would simply not be possible.''

Nor does Mr. Maeroff assert that school would be miraculously transformed if teachers were only given the power to run them. Many teachers, long used to power being lodged at the top, are suspicious of changes that, they fear, will place on their shoulders even greater blame for students' failure.

The problem was underscored in a Carnegie Foundation study, ''Report Card on School Reform: The Teachers Speak,'' released this week. Dr. Ernest L. Boyer, the foundation's president, said many teachers were upset because they have been ''confronted with working conditions that have left them more responsible, but less empowered.'' He calls for ''a deep commitment to make teachers partners in renewal, at all levels.''

Experiences with imposed changes have made many teachers worry that a supposedly helping hand will strike them again.

Fortunately, many educators who are seeking to improve schools, including those behind Chart, are more sensitive to teachers' roles and feelings. But long-established suspicions are hard to overcome.

As Mr. Maeroff toured the country's schools he found it ''remarkable how distrustful administrators and teachers can be of each other,'' with little or no professional dialogue between them. One teacher who attended a national Chart-sponsored meeting told Mr. Maeroff it was the first time he had had a chance to speak to his own superintendent.

Other teachers who had taken part in Chart projects told him they faced cynicism and resentment when they tried to train their fellow teachers in the new approaches.

Many teachers worried that their principal would object to their giving up time to learn about ideas instead of marking papers or monitoring the lunchroom.

In New York City, for example, Mr. Maeroff writes, ''The principals' union, the Council of Supervisors and Administrators, vigorously objected during the 1986-1987 school year to a proposal to let master teachers train new teachers.'' The union said training was the job of principals and assistant principals, thus shoring up the wall between teachers and supervisors.

That wall, and others between colleges and schools, and between researchers and teachers, have created, in Mr. Maeroff's words, ''a crisis of confidence.'' The drive to improve schools is likely to remain in jeopardy until those walls are dismantled.
