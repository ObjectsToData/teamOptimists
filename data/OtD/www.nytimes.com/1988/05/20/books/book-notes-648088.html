Book Notes

Edwin McDowell
Published: May 20, 1988

Trump Reaches for Pen

With his book currently No. 2 on the nonfiction best-seller list, Donald J. Trump said he was ''thinking about'' writing a new book.

Sounding like someone who has already made up his mind to proceed, Mr. Trump said the success of ''Trump: The Art of the Deal,'' written with Tony Schwartz, had resulted in his being ''inundated by publishing houses offering large sums of money.'' Even major political leaders, he said in a recent telephone interview, have urged him to sign with particular publishing houses where they have friends in high places.

A similar assertion by some authors or their agents would sound suspiciously like a negotiating ploy - a signal to other houses that the author was available, or that he soon would be available if his present publisher did not come up with more money for the next book. What tempers that suspicion in the case of Mr. Trump, who acts as his own literary agent, is that he has earmarked the royalties on his current book to charity. ''To the homeless, to Vietnam veterans, for AIDS, multiple sclerosis,'' he said. ''Originally, I figured they'd get a couple of hundred thousand, but because of the success of 'The Art of the Deal,' they'll get four or five million.''

Mr. Trump agreed to undertake his current book at the urging of his friend, S. I. Newhouse, whose family owns Random House. ''I was looking to get a fair amount of money up front, because it was earmarked for charity,'' he said. ''But I also felt if the book was successful, I would get the money anyhow.'' (''Up front'' money, or the author's advance, is an advance against royalties.) But if money isn't the motivation, is it possible that Mr. Trump is trying to ''lean on'' Random House, some of whose officials he angered last January when he said publicly he was disappointed that his book was unavailable outside of New York for almost two weeks before Christmas? If so, one would not know it from the niceties both parties appear to be observing.

''I had a one-book contract with Random House,'' Mr. Trump said, without elaboration. ''We have a very good relationship.''

Random House, for its part, appears eager to continue publishing Mr. Trump. ''We are thrilled how well his present book has done and we're excited at the prospect of his doing another one,'' said Peter Osnos, who edited the Trump book. With 730,000 copies in print, ''Trump: The Art of the Deal'' has been a best seller for 22 weeks, much of that time as No. 1.

Mr. Trump has not yet settled on the topic of the new book. ''Most publishers would like 'The Art of the Deal, Part 2,' '' he said. ''Since the first book, there has been the purchase of the Plaza Hotel, the fighting back and forth with the Japanese,'' and the boxing bouts in Atlantic City. ''But one publisher,'' he said, sounding slightly amused, ''had a great title - 'Everybody Hates a Winner.' '' 'Kaballah' in English

You just knew that after Harper & Row bid $130,000 at auction for a new translation of ''Tao-te-ching,'' the ancient Chinese classic, agents, publishers and translators would be scouring library shelves and archives for other religious or literary gems. And that is exactly what Glen Hartley did after reading about that auction.

Mr. Hartley, a literary agent, immediately thought of the kabbalah, the mystical Jewish system of interpretation of the Scriptures, one of whose principal written sources probably dates back almost 2,000 years. He quickly lined up both a translator and a scholar to write a commentary, fashioned a three-page proposal for ''The Book of Kabbalah'' and scheduled an auction, which Bantam Books won with a bid of $176,000.

Much of the book's value obviously lies in the reputation of the scholars Mr. Hartley brought into the project. Harold Bloom, Sterling Professor of the Humanities at Yale University, who is the author of ''Kabbalah and Criticism'' (1975), will write the book's 250 pages of commentary. And David Rosenberg, a translator of the Yahwist portions of the Bible, will translate another 250 pages. Even at that, the translation will represent only a fraction of the kabbalah.

While Mr. Hartley makes no claim to originality in dreaming up the idea of translating an important work, he also does not regard his project as a clone. Unlike the ''Tao-te-ching,'' which has been widely translated into English, ''The Book of Kabbalah,'' he said, will be the first publication of a basic text heretofore virtually unavailable in any modern language other than modern Hebrew. 10 Volumes of Nemerov

The first volume of poetry published by the University of Chicago Press, in 1958, was ''Mirrors and Windows'' by Howard Nemerov, who earlier this week was named poet laureate of the United States. In the 30 years since then, the press has published nine other volumes of his poetry, including ''War Stories: Poems About Long Ago and Now'' (1987) and ''The Collected Poems of Howard Nemerov'' (1977), which won the Pulitzer Prize and the National Book Award. All but two of those volumes are still in print.
