Education; Teachers Brushing Up On French Revolution

By JOSEPH BERGER
Published: August 3, 1988

The storming of the Bastille. The rousing music of La Marseillaise. The fanatic Madame Defarge with her sinister knitting needles in ''A Tale of Two Cities.''

The French Revolution, which many scholars call the great turning point of modern civilization, has been a source of cultural images and impressions for generations of American students. But teachers say many students leave their courses with little more than these impressions. Some teachers attribute this void to their own fatigue with lessons repeated year after year or on their failure to catch up on fresh interpretations of the revolution.

One group of teachers might now be able to do something about that. They are the 37 public, parochial and private high school teachers who attended a summer institute to help them recharge their sense of the revolution and reappraise it. Next year is the 200th anniversary of the revolution, and New York University, as part of an 18-month-long bicentennial commemoration, just finished holding the four-week institute. Stirs Interest of Teachers

The university's institute is indicative of the growing interest in programs to help high school teachers recapture the intellectual electricity that led them into their fields.

''We don't have a chance to have these types of discussions in school, so bogged down are we in administration,'' said Vincent Brevetti of the High School for the Humanities in Manhattan. ''This has highlighted what's lacking in our own condition. We're so constricted that it makes us salivate when we hear about summer institutes.''

At the institute, teachers of social studies, English, French and art spent their time with leading scholars who lectured on the order of things in France before the social changes brought about by the revolution, known as the ancien regime, and the revolutionary period, the effect of the revolution on England's Romantic poets, the impact on art and modern political thought, and on its global legacy today.

In the afternoons, the teachers broke up into workshops and discussed how they might construct livelier, more informed lesson plans.

At one workshop last week, Joan Kenyon, a social studies teacher at Hunter College High School in Manhattan, wondered how she might teach some of the prerevolutionary libelles, or underground lampoons, many of them pornographic attacks on Louis XVI's difficulties in producing an heir and on Marie Antoinette's sexual tastes. 'Kid Will Never Forget'

''With these cartoons, the kids will never forget the revolution,'' she told her colleagues. ''These cartoons gave life to the 18th century ideas on dynasty, legitimacy and potency.''

''If you get fired, call me,'' teased Nicholas Licari, a social studies teacher at Norman Thomas High School in Manhattan.

Artemis Stamatopoulos, a teacher at the Buckley School in Manhattan, said she picked up an anecdote from one lecturer that will help her get across more of the sense of abuse by the ancien regime. ''If a lord of the manor could not sleep because of the noises made by the frogs in the moat,'' she said, ''the peasants were rounded up to sweep up the frogs.''

Most important, institute participants said, restudying the French revolution at such length and with so many top scholars, helped them vividly understand the power of the French Revolution.

The revolution, was, as one participant said, ''the Big Bang of revolutions.'' It made palpable the ideas of individual rights, national self-determination, and written constitutions. It hastened the rise of sovereign nations and inspired dozens of revolutions that came after. Paradoxically, as historians like R. R. Palmer have noted, it also set a precedent for revolutionary violence and anchored the powerful modern state.

''The French Revolution bequeathed to us our very political language,'' said Anthony Judt, a Cambridge-trained professor of history who lectured last week about the relationship between the French Revolution and the Russian Revolution. Terms Born of Revolution

''The terms left, right, radical, conservative, reactionary, counterrevolutionary,'' he told the teachers, ''were literally invented by the French Revolution.''

Talking at one workshop about revolutionary heroes and the disillusionment that sometimes follows, Mr. Judt recalled that in the 1960's students idealized revolutionaries like Trotsky, Ernesto Che Guevera, and Rosa Luxemburg, who either never acquired power or never held power for very long. ''Of course, you can makes heroes out of people who never had the chance to be unpleasant,'' he said.

Mr. Judt seemed to enjoy the chance to teach other teachers. ''They're much better informed then you'd expect,'' he said. ''Many of them have very strong views, even dogmatic views. They're much more radical and that makes it interesting.'' But he said he sensed ''a little chip on the shoulder'' of some teacher-students. ''They'd like to be academics,'' he said.

The institute was funded by the National Endowment for the Humanities, and it is directed by Leslie C. Berlowitz, the university's associate vice president for academic affairs. Participants received $900 each. The university's bicentennial commemoration will include lectures by an international roster of scholars, an exhibition of revolutionary caricatures and other art, festivals of films about the revolution and the films of Jean Renoir, special courses and symposia.

John Brademas, president of New York University, said the school decided to devote a year and half to the bicentennial because of it's highly regarded French department.
