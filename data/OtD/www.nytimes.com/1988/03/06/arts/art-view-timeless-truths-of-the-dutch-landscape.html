ART VIEW; Timeless Truths of the Dutch Landscape

By JOHN RUSSELL
Published: March 6, 1988

It is not often in first youth that people fall in love with Dutch 17th-century landscape painting. At 18 or 20, we prefer wilder shores and a more ecstatic mode of expression. Rare is the young person who would pass up a Provencal landscape by van Gogh or a Fauve harbor scene by Matisse or Derain in favor of rutted flatlands, blasted trees and tumbledown cottages by painters whose names we cannot pronounce, let along remember. We think of Dutch landscape painting as amiable, pedestrian and - dare we say it? Yes, of course, we do - just a little bit dull.

Later, and with luck, we see our mistake. Dutch landscape paintings and drawings reveal themselves to us as, on the contrary, rich, various, complex, full of surprises, freighted with deep feeling and inexhaustible. This is borne out by a current exhibition, ''Masters of 17th-Century Dutch Landscape Painting,'' which can be seen at the Boston Museum of Fine Arts through May 1 and will then go to the Philadelphia Museum of Art from June 5 through July 31. Previously seen in that epicenter of Dutch painting, the Rijksmuseum in Amsterdam, it was organized primarily by Peter C. Sutton of the Boston Museum and comes with a 560-page catalogue ($24.95 paperback) with a lengthy essay by Mr. Sutton and contributions by seven other scholars.

A major effort, the show comes at a time when a 20-years' resurgence of interest in Dutch landscape painting has brought it into the forefront of critical and historical debate. Prompted in large part by the appearance in 1966 of the late Wolfgang Stechow's shapely and ever-eloquent book on the subject, that resurgence has been felt in every department of Dutch studies, worldwide.

Thanks to it, we think of the Netherlands in the 17th century as infinitely more bizarre and disconcerting than we had formerly been told. What kind of a country was this, when housemaids were sent to the flogging scaffold for springing their lovers from prison, and grown men shot parrots, and every passenger on a long-distance boat trip was given a song book at the point of embarkation, the better to wile away the time?

In that same fascinating society, Jews were absorbed and made welcome with a fine and rare freedom from prejudice. Art would not be art if it did not react to phenomena of this sort, and one of the most moving of all Dutch 17th-century landscape paintings is the ''Jewish Cemetery'' by Jacob van Ruisdael. (This will be a part of the forthcoming show in Philadelphia only.) No less a connoisseur than Goethe himself prized this painting very highly and said that ''through the wholesomeness of his outer and inner mind, Ruisdael delights, teaches, refreshes and animates us at one and the same time.'' The stately Sephardic tombs - still to be seen at Oudekerk, near Amsterdam -have a grandeur that speaks for itself, but the painting is remarkable for a depth and weight of feeling and a panoramic overview of human life for which pedestrian is the very last word that comes to mind.

It is also pointed out in the catalogue that when Ruisdael painted fields of grain and buckwheat, as he clearly liked to do, it was not simply because he thought that they looked nice. His contemporaries did, indeed, enjoy fields of buckwheat for their particular fragrance, but in a painting like ''The Grain Fields,'' lent to the present show by the Met, the choice of the subject is allied to the choice of place.

It is, in fact, directly related to the revival of wheat-growing in the Netherlands after a long period in which grain was imported, first from Poland and the German Hanseatic States and later from Flanders and England.

Even that staple of Dutch landscape painting, the cow, has taken on a new dignity as a result of recent researches. Looking at the cows in Aelbert Cuyp's monumental ''Cattle by a River,'' lent from Budapest for the present show, we admire what Alan Chong in the catalogue calls their ''architectonic shapes,'' and we also admire the celestial light that is reflected upwards from the wetlands in which they stand.

But should we infer from all this that Cuyp painted those cows ''because they were there?'' Certainly not. Cuyp painted at a time when, in a real sense, the cow was Holland and Holland was the cow. Not only was the Dutch cow the superior of any other cow, but it was the source of prosperity and well-being for the nation as a whole. To quote Mr. Sutton in a recent issue of the Journal of the J. Paul Getty Museum, ''Improved drainage and systematic fertilization of pasturage, as well as better fodder, such as oil cakes made of pressed pulp and cole seed, contributed to the increased weight and size of Dutch cattle.'' The untroubled heavyweights who form the foreground frieze in Cuyp's great painting stood for, in Mr. Sutton's view, ''the Dutchman's sense of the contented cow as a metaphor of freedom, security and the tranquility of living life in accord with nature.''

Perhaps the most beautiful painting in the show, for this visitor's taste, is the view of Nijmegen, also by Cuyp, that has been lent by the Indianapolis Museum. Once again, this was not a motif chosen at random. The fortified city of Nijmegen had been known since Roman and Carolingian times as the locus of Netherlandish patriotic feeling and resistance to oppression.
