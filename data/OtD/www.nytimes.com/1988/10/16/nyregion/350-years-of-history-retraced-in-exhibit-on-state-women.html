350 Years of History Retraced in Exhibit on State Women

By SHARON L. BASS
Published: October 16, 1988

FRAMED petition from 1669 which accused Katherine Harrison of witchcraft; the diary of a 16-year-old Irish girl who came to New Haven in the 1880's, and a 1987 photograph of the female graduating class of the police academy in Windsor Locks are all part of the exhibit ''Coming of Age: Connecticut Women and their Choices'' at the Connecticut Historical Society in Hartford.

The exhibit spans four centuries and consists of more than 150 photographs, pieces of clothing, works of art, journals and documents that trace women's lives over the last 350 years, from the hearth to the assembly line, into the classroom and on to the executive office.

Sponsored by the Connecticut Permanent Commission on the Status of Women and Hartford College of Women, the artifacts, displayed on ash rose colored panels, depict Connecticut women who were famous and wealthy, as well as the struggling, ordinary housewife and factory worker. Helping Women Regain Their History

The purpose of the exhibit, which will remain at the Historical Society through March, is to help ''women in Connecticut regain their history as a process of empowerment,'' said Janet Kniffin, grant coordinator for the Historical Society. ''If we don't understand that we played a significant role in household economics, for example, we really don't have a foundation to continue making choices which will give us direction for the future.''

Ms. Kniffin said she has been particularly taken by the reactions of women who have visited the display and seem ''drawn into it by recognizing themes and lifestyle issues. This goes beyond the stereotypical role of women.''

Dr. Ruth Moynihan, a history professor at the University of Connecticut, was a curator for the project, which was partially financed by an $18,000 grant from the Connecticut Humanities Council. Contributions from corporations, foundations and individuals helped meet the total cost of $85,000.

The exhibit is separated into eight themes: education, marriage, childbirth, motherhood and kinship, employment, professions, wartime and women's rights.

A black-and-white photograph on the education panel shows a female physical education class in 1880 at the Dwight Street School in New Haven. Another photograph is of the 1985 National Champion Women's Soccer Team at the University of Connecticut. Photographs and a Gown

Also on this panel is a silk and linen sampler of the alphabet from 1791; a photograph of the Class of 1868 at the American School for the Deaf, and an 1890 picture of a night class for immigrants at the Booth Preparatory School in New Haven.

A red Laotian wedding gown with gold and silver braiding highlights the section on marriage. Other items include a book from 1843 on how to have a happy marriage which espouses tolerating husbands even if they are ''cruel, incompetent and less intelligent'' than their wives. Near the book is a divorce petition from 1723.

On the employment panel hangs a picture of Indian women planting crops; a 1740 certificate of sale for three black slave women; a photograph of women working at the Colt Firearms Factory in Hartford around the turn of the century; a photograph of a telephone operator in 1883, and a color photograph of a female automobile mechanic taken this year.

Portraits of the writers Ann Petry and Harriet Beecher Stowe and of the actress Katharine Hepburn, as well as other noted women from Connecticut, fill the section labeled professions.

On the wartime panel are dog tags from the Vietnam War, worn by a nurse, Linda Schwartz, a Connecticut resident, in 1969; photographs of nurses tending to wounded soldiers at their bedsides from the two World Wars, plus a recruitment poster for the Waves.

''So little has been done until recently on women's history,'' said Pamela Cartledge, acting registrar for the Commission on Women. ''Women have always thought they were just at home making clothes. We've been so invisible.''
