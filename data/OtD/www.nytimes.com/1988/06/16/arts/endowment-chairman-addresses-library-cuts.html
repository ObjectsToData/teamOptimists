Endowment Chairman Addresses Library Cuts

By HERBERT MITGANG
Published: June 16, 1988

Lynne V. Cheney, chairman of the National Endowment of the Humanities in Washington, took the Government's case to the New York Public Library yesterday, not long after the agency indicated that it would cut the library's funds substantially in 1989.

Former Senator Abraham Ribicoff of Connecticut, a member of the library's board of trustees, told Dr. Cheney during a meeting in the trustees room, ''I want to commend you for coming before this disappointed group. You are the first official from Washington ever to do so and explain your position.''

Speaking in response to criticism of her agency by Dr. Vartan Gregorian, the library's president, Dr. Cheney said: ''I come today to make clear how much we respect the library. The endowment's position about the grant to the research libraries will not reflect negatively on future applications. I'm here as a gesture of respect for the excellence of your great institution.'' Competition for Money

At the same time, Dr. Cheney offered encouragement that the library could receive additional funds by exploring other grant categories. ''We look forward to receiving your applications,'' she said. Specifically, she mentioned the possibility of more Federal money for preservation and also to build up the library's own endowment.

However, Dr. Cheney said that the library would have to compete against other requests by institutions around the country ''out of a sense of fairness and equity.'' The library has argued that it is a national institution, second in importance only to the Library of Congress, and deserved to have a continuing ''special relationship'' because of its use as a resource for scholars all over the country.

''The $1 million grant for one year is the single largest grant that I've given since I've been chairman,'' Dr. Cheney said, during a question period for the trustees. ''I promise to continue to work on behalf of the library, which has undergone a remarkable renaissance in the last seven years.'' Requested a Meeting

When Dr. Cheney heard that the library's board of trustees was meeting, she asked for an invitation to address the board members face-to-face about the conflict that has developed in recent weeks concerning a cut in Federal money for next year and the library's status as a national institution.

Dr. Cheney is a highly respected Republican from Wyoming who was unanimously confirmed by the Senate two years ago to succeed William J. Bennett, now Secretary of Education. An occasional novelist, she holds a doctorate from the University of Wisconsin, where her dissertation was on the poetry of Matthew Arnold.

What had caused the clash between the endowment and the library was a reduction in the contribution to its research libraries from $2 million a year over the past three years - on a matching basis of one Federal dollar for every $3 from the public - to $1 million, for one year only, on a matching basis of one Federal dollar for every $4 from the public.

Dr. Cheney pointed out that the New York Public Library had been receiving what she called ''anomalous'' grants outside the rules and guidelines used for all other institutions applying for endowment funds. ''The library has become a viable, even growing institution,'' she noted, ''one that has had increasing success in recent years achieving both public and and private support.''

''The magnitude of this institution is great in every area except wealth,'' Dr. Gregorian said yesterday. ''We are big, but we are not rich.''
