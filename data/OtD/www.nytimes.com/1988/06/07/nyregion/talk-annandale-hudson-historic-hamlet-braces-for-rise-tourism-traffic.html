The Talk of Annandale-on-Hudson; Historic Hamlet Braces for Rise in Tourism and Traffic

By HAROLD FABER, Special to the New York Times
Published: June 7, 1988

This hamlet on the banks of the Hudson River in northern Dutchess County is braced for an influx of tourists and traffic. The reason: Montgomery Place, a restored estate dating back to 1805, will open to the public on Saturday, bringing in an estimated 200 visitors a day.

That might not seem like much traffic, but any increase in traffic would be a major event in Annandale, which consists of two homes, seven empty buildings and a small hotel closed for renovation, all clustered around a village green.

So several small steps have been taken to control the anticipated traffic. A new sign has gone up on the green, pointing to Bard College to the north and Montgomery Place to the south. A bump in River Road leading to Montgomery Place has been leveled, improving the sight lines for cars.

At Montgomery Place itself, a 23-room mansion set amid 434 acres of orchards and fields, a score of workers are busy completing the parking field, a visitors center and repairs to its exterior.

Montgomery Place's transition from a private home into a museum for the public has produced a generally favorable reception from its neighbors, but also mixed feelings about the impact of tourists and traffic on the quality of life in the quiet rural area.

''I think Montgomery Place will be a valuable addition to the community,'' said the Rev. Dr. Frederick Q. Shafer, the retired chaplain of Bard, and one of the two homeowners in Annandale.

The other homeowner, Robert Kelly, a writer and professor of English at Bard, said, ''Montgomery Place seems to have two contradictory goals, one to bring people to it and the other to preserve the look of the town. I am crossing my fingers and hoping for the best.''

Dr. Shafer and Mr. Kelly are the closest neighbors to Montgomery Place, now owned by Historic Hudson Valley, a nonprofit organization that also operates Sleepy Hollow, the Philipsburg Manor and the Van Cortlandt Manor in Westchester County.

With the financial and personal support of the Rockefeller family, Historic Hudson is expanding its operations north into the mid-Hudson Valley, which it hopes to see develop as one of the nation's major tourist attractions.

As part of that move, two years ago, Historic Hudson bought Montgomery Place as well as the buildings in Annandale - the ones that are now empty - for $3.75 million. It is spending that much more to restore them, according to Kenneth Toole, its director of business operations. An Ancestral Home

The three-story mansion, surrounded by apple, pear and peach orchards, is near the northern end of a 16-mile stretch of historic homes on the east bank of the Hudson River, extending from the Franklin D. Roosevelt home in Hyde Park north to Clermont, the ancestral home of the Livingston family.

Montgomery Place was built in 1804-5 by Janet Livingston Montgomery, widow of Gen. Richard Montgomery, who was killed leading the American forces at the Battle of Quebec in December 1775. Its grounds, with magnificent views of the Hudson River and the Catskill Mountains, were landscaped later by Andrew Jackson Downing, America's first landscape architect.

An outstanding example of Federal architecture, it was occupied by members of the Livingston and Delafield families continuously for almost 200 years. As a result, it contains portraits, silver, glass, furniture and books reflecting changes in American family life from the Revolutionary War to recent years.

''We want people to come and enjoy the historic, scenic and environmental aspects of Montgomery Place,'' said Richard F. Halverson, president of Historic Hudson. ''We hope people will spend the day here using it as a summer retreat, just as it was for the Livingston family.''

He predicted that increased tourism would benefit other nearby historic sites as well as restaurants, hotels, motels and gas stations. In addition, he said, Montgomery Place was creating about 40 new jobs, most of them filled by local people.

In operating Montgomery Place, he added, ''We know there is a delicate balance between visitors and preservation.''

It is that delicate balance between tourists, who will come by car and bus, and the serene bucolic quiet of Annandale that bothers Mr. Kelly and some other residents of the region.

''I see some increased traffic on River Road,'' said J. Winthrop Aldrich, who lives on the road that runs parallel to the river from Rhinecliff to Annandale. ''But I think that Montgomery Place will be good neighbors and good land stewards.'' More Construction Coming

There is more construction in Annandale's future. A few hundred yards north of the green, Bard College, which has 875 students and houses the Annandale post office -zip code 12504 - has started a major expansion program.

Bard recently opened a $4.35 million humanities building. It is renovating Blithewood, a Hudson River mansion on its grounds, at a cost of $2 million, for use as an economics institute. Under construction is a $4 million gymnasium. And, in conjunction with the Hudson Valley Philharmonic, it plans to build a $5 million, 1,800-seat amphitheater for the performing arts. 'Vandals Took the Handles'

So far, the village is quiet except for the bustle of passing construction trucks. But Historic Hudson has plans for revitalizing it as well. It hopes to lease the vacant two-story Annandale Hotel to a restaurant operator, to convert the vacant buildings to bed-and-breakfast places with 30 to 40 rooms and to open a gift shop.

''It's a 19th-century village, and we're going to keep it that way,'' Mr. Halverson said.

Incongruously, one of Annandale's best-known artifacts, a pump that sits under an open shed on the southern edge of the village green, is a symbol of the rebellious 1960's. It was made famous by Bob Dylan, a frequent visitor to friends at Bard, in ''Subterranean Homesick Blues.''

In addition to containing the line that gave the Weathermen, the violent faction of the time, its name -''You don't need a weatherman to tell which way the wind is blowing'' - the song also had this line: ''The pump don't work 'cause the vandals took the handles.''

Historic Hudson has no plans to repair the pump, or to erect a descriptive marker, according to Mr. Toole.
