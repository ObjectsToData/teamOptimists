For News Magazines, Growing Identity Crisis

By ALEX S. JONES
Published: June 29, 1988

Time, Newsweek, and U.S. News & World Report are immersed in an effort to answer a question that all three view as critical: what should a news magazine be in the 1990's?

Some editors and writers at each of the magazines believe their very survival is at stake as they battle such new rivals as cable television news, national newspapers and a growing number of specialty magazines.

Although circulation at all three news magazines has never been higher, the essential problem for each of them is how to retain, or perhaps expand, their audience and hence their advertising base over the next decade.

As some editors see it, the challenge is to strike a balance: to be novel and entertaining enough to attract readers already soaked in information, but to avoid straying so far from the traditional review of the week's events as to become irrelevant, or at least nonessential, as providers of serious news and analysis.

Some regular readers believe this ''softening'' has already happened to some extent. ''They're less compelling and less informative, and you can be very well informed and not read the news magazines these days,'' said Everette E. Dennis, executive director for the Gannett Center for Media Studies at Columbia University. A Sharp Rejoinder

Such sentiments are vigorously challenged by top editors at the three magazines. ''News is more than what Reagan said yesterday,'' said Henry Muller, managing editor of Time. He maintained that it was ''harder to convince people that we've got something to offer'' in an environment of news saturation where ''people think they're informed because they hear things in five-second sound bites.'

The strategy for Time, the nation's first and largest weekly news magazine, with a circulation of 4.7 million, is to maintain its position as the industry leader by keeping most of its focus on the traditional review of the week's news. But while Time has long devoted the back of the magazine to so-called soft articles about life styles, the arts and celebrity profiles, there is also a growing tendency to emphasize such aspects in articles about important news events. An example is the recent cover article on Soviet women during the Moscow summit meeting.

U.S. News has long separated itself from the others by devoting its back-of-the-book pages to service features (''news you can use'') rather than to movie, theater or book reviews. It likes to portray itself as more serious than its rivals, though U.S. News, with a circulation of 2.2 million, is growing softer, too.

''There's now a waterfall of news descending on people all the time,'' said David R. Gergen, editor of U.S. News. Mr. Gergen said the magazine was aimed less at chasing breaking news than ''making sense out of the week's events'' and telling people ''how to get their kids into college.''

At Newsweek, the perennial No. 2 news magazine, with a circulation of 3.2 million, the identity problem is most acute and the solution most radical and risky. Newsweek's vision is to be very broad and unpredictible in its definition of news and reporting.

''The clarity of the vision is knowing you have many weapons in the arsenel,'' said Richard M. Smith, Newsweek's editor in chief. Strong Editorial Views

The result is a magazine whose reporting ranges from dispassionate balance to strong advocacy, with some articles reflecting the point of view of the writers. Some who follow the news media believe, however, that such nontraditional journalism jeopardizes the magazine's overall credibility.

''When you have a Newsweek so taken over by the idea of campaigning, the information value diminishes to the vanishing point,'' said Jude Wanniski, president of Polyconomics Inc., a business and political consulting company. Mr. Wanniski gave as an example Newsweek's coverage of the campaign to oust Ferdinand Marcos in the Philippines, which he said Newsweek, in effect, had joined.

''We don't shy away when we think it's an appropriate response'' to a news event like the Philippine revolution, Mr. Smith said. It is clear to readers, he added, that articles written from a strong point of view are ''reporter-driven, not preconceived'' by the magazine's management.

Newsweek's content is equally unpredictable, ranging in one recent issue from an interview on the eve of the summit meeting with Mikhail S. Gorbachev to an article that quoted some unnamed ''columnists'' as linking New York socialites to characters in a roman a clef. More Space for Soft Sections

The space the three magazines devote to soft sections has been growing.

In 1980, both Time and Newsweek devoted about 30 percent of their space to national affairs, while U.S. News gave such topics about half its space, according to Hall's Editorial Reports, a company that measures the editorial content of leading consumer magazines. Last year, Hall's said, both Time and Newsweek devoted about 25 percent of their space to national affairs, while the percentage at U.S. News dropped to about 28 percent.

In that period, U.S. News has nearly doubled its coverage in the culture and humanities categories, though the percentage is still only about two-thirds what Time and Newsweek devote to them.

For some readers, the soft sections have become the principal appeal. ''It's the respectable way to get material that's gossipy and light,'' said Catherine Gay, executive director of the Center for Communication, a New York-based organization that brings together professional journalists and journalism students.

Looking ahead, media analysts say Time and U.S News appear to be financially secure. But Newsweek, which is owned by The Washington Post Company, has seen its profitability drop in recent years to $15.3 million in 1987, with further declines projected in the years to come, said J. Kendrick Noble Jr., an analyst at Paine Webber Inc.

Although Time's ad pages dropped by over 8 percent in 1987, compared with a 1.3 percent gain for Newsweek and a 2 percent increase for U.S. News, Time's circulation dominance allows it to charge more for advertising. It finished the year with $329 million in advertising revenue, compared with $239 million for Newsweek and $106 million for U.S. News.
