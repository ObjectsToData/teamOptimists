An Abundance of Awards as Doing Good Becomes an Industry

By KATHLEEN TELTSCH, Special to the New York Times
Published: June 19, 1988

Charles Grawemeyer, a retired industrialist who made a fortune in plastics, fervently believes that a few well-conceived and uplifting ideas can make the world a better place. And he is putting his money where his convictions are.

Mr. Grawemeyer has established five annual prizes of $150,000 each, among the highest in a growing number of international competitions that aspire to the luster of the Nobel Prizes.

Since 1985, he has given out three of the awards, all of them in music.

The newest Grawemeyer award, to be given June 28, will honor a concept for improving world order.

''Offering that prize just might turn up ideas that would help avoid friction between nations,'' said the 75-year-old philanthropist, who runs what he calls ''my little one-horse operation'' from a small basement office on Muhammad Ali Boulevard in downtown Louisville. Rewarding Ideas

The Grawemeyer prizes stand out because of Mr. Grawemeyer's determination to award them to people from all over the world and to reward ideas instead of accomplishments. Then there are their impressive price tags.

All in all, they reflect how the prize business is booming.

''Prize-giving is becoming an industry,'' said Richard Magat, who retired recently as president of the Hazen Foundation in New York.

Evidence for this assertion lies in the reference work Awards, Honors & Prizes, published by the Gale Research Company of Detroit. A sixth edition, published in 1985, had 13,000 entries. According to Gita Siegman, the editor, the seventh edition, 18 months later, had more than 17,000.

True, many of the competitions listed in the book offer only citations or trophies, such as the plaque of a mule's head from the American Donkey and Mule Society for riders surviving a 1,000-mile ride. But there are thousands of more substantial prizes, though only a relative handful are in excess of $100,000. And while many big-money prizes continue to go for scientific accomplishment, many of the new awards are in the humanities fields.

Mr. Magat suggested that a key stimulus for the boom, especially in the big-dollar competitions, has been the MacArthur Foundation's no-strings awards to ''exceptionally gifted individuals.'' Over seven years the Chicago-based foundation has spent $40 million honoring 223 people with its awards for ''genius.''

The newer awards, mainly from private sources, are frequently named for individuals, corporations or professional associations, a reflection of the growing trend to use prizes as a public relations technique and as a means of elevating the donor to celebrity status.

''Doing good quietly is less common today,'' Mr. Magat said.

Dr. Harriet Zuckerman, a professor of sociology at Columbia University, has examined the impact of awards and particularly the Nobels.

''I have the feeling donors believe gifts to institutions these days is becoming a little old hat, and they cannot get much attention for less than several millions,'' said Dr. Zuckerman. ''But establishing a prize offers a chance to perpetuate the donor's name and, in the case of a living donor, opportunities to meet and talk with the recipients, and that's a small bonus. Participating in an elaborate or impressive awards ceremony also pleases the donor as much as the recipient.''

She does not see a great risk that proliferation of serious prizes will cheapen the awards practice as long as recipients are chosen by valid criteria such as scholarship. 'Attention is Important'

Neither does Susan Berresford, vice president of the Ford Foundation, who calls herself a ''big fan of awards.'' Arguing that American society has very few ways of publicly honoring distinguished people, Ms. Berresford said, ''There are many fields - teaching is one - in which people toil ceaselessly and facelessly and public attention is important because it indicates what society values and inspires others.''

Among the newer honors is General Foods' World Food Prize, a $200,000 award created in 1986. It was given this spring to Dr. Robert F. Chandler Jr., the American founding director of the International Rice Research Institute in the Philippines, which developed high-yield crop strains.

There is also an annual $100,000 Human Rights Award established this year by the Reebok Foundation, which was set up by the athletic footwear company. The award is for young people here and abroad who have ''significantly raised awareness of human rights.''

The international awards, in some cases better known abroad than in the United States, include the Alexander Onassis prizes. They were established by the will of Aristotle Onassis, the billionaire shipping magnate, in memory of his son, who was killed in a 1973 plane crash. In a ceremony in Athens recently that was capped by a cruise around the Greek islands, the Onassis Foundation presented three $100,000 prizes: one shared by the actress Elizabeth Taylor and the French volunteer organization AIDES, for their work on AIDS, another to former World Bank president Robert S. McNamara for encouraging economic cooperation between rich and poor nations, and a third to China's Bureau of Museums and Archeological Data for protecting ancient treasures.
