MONK MOLLOY'S NOTRE DAME

By Alex Ward; Alex Ward is an editor of this magazine.
Published: June 12, 1988

EDWARD MALLOY'S modest, book-filled suite is only a few steps from the Golden Dome, Notre Dame's main building and the symbol of the nation's - and arguably the world's - most famous Roman Catholic university.

That's handy, because Malloy, a Holy Cross priest, professor of theology and now the president of Notre Dame, is fond of staying up till 3 A.M., writing, reading and catching up with administrative papers. With a habit like that, it helps to have a short trip to work.

Malloy lives in Sorin Hall, the university's oldest dormitory, and when he's in, as he is on this Sunday evening, there is a hand-lettered ''Welcome'' sign on his door. It's about 9:30 - the night is young - and the president is chatting in his quarters with a young woman, now in medical school at Washington University in St. Louis, who is visiting her alma mater. Malloy, dressed in an argyle sweater and gray slacks, leans back in a swivel chair, his arms clasped behind his head - a favorite position. Liz Martucci perches on a couch. For an hour, their conversation ranges from health care for the aging to pediatrics in third world countries to the pressures of medical residencies. Before leaving, Martucci gives her old professor a goodbye hug.

Just over a year ago, the Rev. Edward Aloysius Malloy officially replaced the Rev. Theodore M. Hesburgh, the man who transformed Notre Dame from a respectable university best known for its football teams into a first-class academic institution by upgrading the faculty, curriculum, research facilities and graduate school and opening the doors to women. Brilliant, forceful and charismatic, Hesburgh, who governed the university for three and a half decades, became a legend on campus, where stories of students scampering up the fire escape outside his office for a glimpse of the great man are a part of Notre Dame lore, like winning one for the Gipper.

Malloy's two rooms in Sorin are on the first floor, and the sign on his door means what it says: anyone who wants to come in and talk, can. Since becoming an administrator, his duties and responsibilities have changed considerably from his days as a teacher. But he still takes freshmen out for pizza, still says Mass in the dormitory chapel two nights a week at 11 o'clock, still plays basketball regularly and still likes to be called Monk. The nickname was a schoolboy joke that predated any serious thoughts he had of becoming a cleric, but it suits him. Six feet 4 inches tall, angular and bespectacled, the 47-year-old Malloy has pale skin, gray hair and a voice that seems permanently modulated at medium. There is about him an air of unflappability.

The day after Liz Martucci's visit, he sits under a chandelier in his spacious, high-ceilinged office listening to the university's new administrator for computer operations recite a litany of badly needed improvements and what they will cost - nearly $20 million. The administrator clears his throat. ''I hope you don't mind my being so candid,'' he says, almost apologetically.

''Better you have wisdom to share,'' is the unblinkng response.

A RECENT VISITOR to Notre Dame's verdant, 1,250-acre campus on the outskirts of South Bend, Ind., is struck by its orderliness. The spacious grounds are carefully tended, the students well-scrubbed and friendly. A spring ritual known as ''bookstore basketball'' - played on an outdoor court behind the bookstore -is under way, so the sound of cheering can occasionally be heard. Otherwise, it's tranquil.

Malloy's succession was orderly, too. He had served for five years as associate provost while being groomed, along with several other candidates, for the top job. After Malloy's appointment by the board of trustees, the 71-year-old Hesburgh tactfully left to travel for a year. (He plans to return to work with the university's International Institute for Peace Studies and the Helen Kellogg Institute for International Studies.) ''That was a gift from Ted,'' Malloy says of Hesburgh's excursion. ''It allowed everyone to get used to the new arrangement.''

It's difficult to discuss Hesburgh at Notre Dame without the word visionary being mentioned at least once, but it seems the new arrangement wasn't that hard to get used to. Malloy's personal, hands-on style has been warmly embraced.

''Moses is a tough act to follow,'' says Frederick J. Crosson, a professor of humanities and former dean of the liberal arts college, ''but the feeling about Father Malloy is, this is a good guy we've got here.''

Malloy knows that comparisons with Hesburgh are inevitable, but he deflects them deftly. ''I believe Ted Hesburgh will be seen by historians as the great Catholic educator of the 20th century, and he did a tremendous job of leading this university,'' he says. ''But it's fair to say that after 35 years there were a lot of people ready for a change, including Ted.''

A check of its vital signs indicates that the university Malloy took over is in fine fettle. Applications are up (more than 9,600 this year for a freshman class of 1,800); the endowment is $450 million, and a campaign to raise another $300 million has reached 94 percent of its goal in just one year. The students, who come from all 50 states and over 60 countries, are bright (averaging better than 1,300 on the Scholastic Aptitude Test, out of a possible 1,600); motivated (most were in the top 10 percent of their high school classes), and their graduation rate -93 percent finish in four years - is extraordinary. A faculty that includes the noted biologist George B. Craig Jr., the legal scholar G. Robert Blakey and the philosopher of religion Alvin Plantinga has been strengthened by the recent addition of the bioethicist the Rev. Richard McCormick (another well-known ethicist, Alisdair MacIntyre, will join the philosophy department next fall); and the radiation laboratory, which boasts the largest collection of radiation chemists in the country, has attracted visiting research scientists from more than 50 countries.
