A Black College Gets Cosby Gift Of $20 Million

By LEE A. DANIELS
Published: November 8, 1988

Bill Cosby and his wife, Camille, have donated $20 million to Spelman College, the elite black women's college in Atlanta.

The gift is the largest single contribution ever made to a black college and one of the largest donations in recent years to any school. The gift was announced by Johnetta B. Cole, Spelman's new president, at a dinner Friday that began a three-day celebration of her inauguration.

''Mrs. Cosby and I have been blessed because I found a vein of gold in the side of a mountain,'' Mr. Cosby said to an amazed, applauding audience of 2,000 at the black-tie dinner off campus in Atlanta.

Referring to Dr. Cole, he added, ''Mrs. Cosby and I wanted this woman to know how much we love this school.''

Mr. Cosby said through a spokesman yesterday that he would have no further comment about the gift.

Dr. Cole and others who were at the dinner said Mr. Cosby made it clear that he and his wife considered the donation not only an expression of their support for Spelman, but also a challenge to other black Americans to support black colleges.

''We must claim and therefore support those institutions at the heart of our peoplehood,'' Dr. Cole said yesterday from her office in Atlanta. ''That was his point. If we can tithe for our churches, we can tithe for our schools. This doesn't mean we turn away from the Federal Government or from the private sector, but either we support these institutions or they will die.''

Dr. Cole said most of the huge gift would be used to build a new academic center that would include classrooms for the school's honors program, its women's center, an audio-visual center, faculty offices and possibly a fine-arts gallery. The building is to be named the Camille Olivia Hanks Cosby Academic Center; Dr. Cole said the gift was as much Mrs. Cosby's initiative as her husband's.

Dr. Cole said that the gift would also finance the 107-year-old college's first endowed chairs, most likely in the fine arts, humanities and social sciences. The college's total endowment is $42 million. It has 1,741 students.

At the Cosbys' request, Dr. Cole announced the donation at the dinner. She said her statement was greeted by ''gasps of wonder, foot-stomping, shouts of jubilation,'' and prolonged applause. President Is Jubilant

''I woke up the next morning and pinched myself to see if it had been a dream,'' she added.

The Cosbys have been regular and highly visible supporters of black colleges in recent years.

Mr. Cosby, whose homespun humor and universal appeal have made him extremely wealthy in recent years, has made it a point to mention the names of several historically black colleges on his television series ''The Cosby Show.'' That show's spinoff, ''A Different World,'' is set at a fictitious black college and the outside scenes are filmed on the Spelman campus.

One of his daughters, Erika, recently graduated from Spelman. Mr. Cosby graduated from Temple University, in Philadelphia, and received a doctorate in education from the University of Massachusetts at Amherst. Mrs. Cosby attended the University of Maryland.

The reaction from educators was joyful.

''They've sent out a wonderful message to the country and these colleges' alumni,'' said Joseph Johnson, president of Grambling State University, in Grambling, La.: ''that is, that these schools are important national resources and deserve to be significantly supported.''

Dr. Johnson is the chairman of the National Association for Equal Opportunity in Higher Education, which represents the nation's 117 historically black colleges and universities. 'The Biggest Boost'

Christopher Edley Sr., executive director of the United Negro College Fund, which raises money for historically black colleges and universities, called the donation ''the biggest boost and opportunity we've ever had in terms of funding.''

Mr. Edley said the Cosbys ''know black colleges well, so this is a very informed gift.'' He added that it would set an example and be ''a message that blacks who are wealthy are going to be more significantly supporting these institutions.''

Ira Michael Heyman, chancellor of the University of California at Berkeley, said that gifts of this magnitude ''really put a school on the map,'' adding, ''It's a tremendous affirmation of the institution's worth, and I would think a special vote of confidence in black institutions in general. It's magnificent.''

In the 1960's the Ford Foundation gave a total of $50 million to 26 black colleges during a two-year period. But educators and fund-raisers said the Cosby gift was by far the largest donation any individual had made to a black college.

Nathan Weber, editor of ''Giving, USA,'' a book published by the American Association of Fund Raising Counsel Trust for Philanthropy, which annually tracks all charitable donations of more than $1 million, said that in terms of individual giving to all educational institutions in 1987 and thus far in 1988, the Cosbys' contribution would rank as fourth-highest.

Among other recent big donors to colleges are Leonard N. Stern, chairman of the Hartz Group, who has pledged at least $25 million to New York University; John W. Kluge, former chairman of Metromedia, to who gave $25 million to Columbia University, and Curtis L. Carlson, a Minnesota-based entrepreneur who gave $25 million to the University of Minnesota. John S. R. Shad, former chairman of the Securities and Exchange Commission, donated $23 million to the Harvard Business School, In recent years several foundations and businesses have given up to $40 million in a single gift to colleges and universities, and bequests to colleges from several wills have exceeded the Cosby gift.

These donations have occurred even though the total amount of giving to higher education slumped to $486.1 million last year from $668.3 million in 1986, according to data gathered by the consulting firm of Brakeley, John, Price, Jones in Stamford, Conn, George Brakeley Jr. the firm's founder, said that reflected the negative impact of the Tax Reform Act of 1986, which limited tax deductions for all kinds of philanthropy.

Mr. Brakeley added that whatever its exact rank, the Cosby gift ''is at the very top of the giving structure,'' among that strata of givers able and willing to give $15 million and more with one swipe at the checkbook. Mr. Brakeley said fund-raisers call such contributions ''ultimate gifts.''
