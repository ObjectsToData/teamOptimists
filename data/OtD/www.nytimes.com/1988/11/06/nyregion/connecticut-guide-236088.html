CONNECTICUT GUIDE


Published: November 6, 1988

19TH-CENTURY FUNERALS ''American Funeral Customs'' is the title of a new exhibition of artifacts, clinical equipment and mourning clothing on view until Dec. 2 at the Scott Fanton Museum, 43 Main Street in Danbury. A cooperative effort by the museum and several of the city's funeral directors, the exhibition provides some unique glimpses into the burial customs of the 19th century.

Rodney Bourdeau, director of the Cornell Memorial Home on White Street, is a member of the museum's board of directors. He contributed a substantial portion of the roughly two hundred items on display. ''I am a compulsive collector of antiques,'' he said. Many of the antiques are related to his profession.

Items will be installed in period settings. One vignette will show a Victorian parlor with wicker baskets of flowers, draperies and ''an early reposing couch on which the deceased was laid out,'' said Mr. Bourdeau. ''There were no funeral homes until this century,'' he explained. ''Friends and relatives gathered at home.''

Mourning jewelry in which a lock of the dead person's hair was woven into gold rings, brooches and necklaces, and several antique mourning costumes from the museum's collection will also be on view.

Museum hours are from 2 to 5 P.M. Wednesday to Sunday. Admission is free. For more information call 743-5200. ROMANTICS ON RADIO

David Dubal, music director of New York City's classical music radio station WNCN, will entertain his audience at noon Wednesday with a lecture-recital called ''The 19th Century: The Birth of the Character Piece.''

Presented by the Schubert Club of Fairfield County in Fellowship Hall of the First Presbyterian Church at 1101 Bedford Street, Stamford, the program will cover the Romantic Period in which abstract compositions came pouring out of European composers - Chopin nocturnes, Beethoven sonatas, Schubert impromptus, and an assortment of rondos, moment-musicales and rhapsodies created during the first half of the 19th century.

Mr. Dubal, a noted concert pianist as well as lecturer and author, will illustrate his talk by performing a number of the selections under discussion. His book, ''Reflections From the Keyboard,'' in which he recorded conversations with 35 of the world's leading pianists, is now available in paperback. Admission to the program is $7 at the door. For more information call 322-1292 or 637-3422. AMERICAN FOLK ART

The Museum of Art, Science and Industry in Bridgeport has an exhibition of ''American Folk Art of the 20th Century'' that celebrates untaught but well-known artists. It opens today through December.

Represented in the 100 works on view are memory painters including Grandma Moses, Ralph Fasanella, Mattie Lou O'Kelley and Jane Munro, and a number of black artists like Clementine Hunter, Mose Toliver and Horace Pippin.

Self-taught artists include Manuel Quiles who was born in Puerto Rico and Antonio Esteves who is from Brazil, whose carved portrait of Ella Fitzgerald is titled ''Carnegie Hall.''

Morris Hershfield's stylized patterns, shown in a retrospective at the Museum of Modern Art in New York City in the 1940's, are in the exhibition, along with primitive views of the far West by Bob Carter and the rocky shores of Maine by Sylvia Alberts.

Curator of the show is Jay Johnson, owner of America's Folk Heritage Gallery in Manhattan. The museum is open from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday to Friday and from noon to 5 P.M. weekends. Admission is $3.50 for adults, $2 for students and the elderly. Call 372-3521 for more information. VIEWS ON TRANSLATION

The world of the translator will be explored in a Conference on Translation on the Yale campus. Saturday and next Sunday.It will be open to the public free of charge.

''A Translator's View with Special Reference to Baudelaire and Proust'' will open the conference at 10:30 A.M. Saturday with Richard Howard, a Pulitzer Prize-winning poet and translator. At 1:30 P.M., Edwin McClellan, the Sumimoto Professor of Japanese Studies at Yale, will discuss Japanese translation into English, and at 3:30 P.M., Ruth Hein will head a panel of literary agents, publisher's representatives and a representative of the National Endowment for the Humanities, on translations from German.

Sunday's program includes translations from poetry of various nationalities including the works of the contemporary Greek poet George Seferis. The conference will be held until noon next Sunday at Davies Auditorium, 15 Prospect Street.. Call 624-7079 for more information. TRIBUTE TO TAP

The Dance Theater of Southern Connecticut State University in New Haven will present an original revue called ''Put On Your Dancin' Shoes'' at 8 P.M. Thursday to Saturday in the Lyman Center for the Performing Arts.

A segment of the show will be a tribute to Charles (Honi) Coles, winner of a Tony Award for his dancing in ''My One and Only'' on Broadway. Mr. Coles served as consultant to the 25 student performers and supervising faculty members who put the revue together.

Dance styles ranging from the Keystone Kops number in ''High Button Shoes'' through the Jazz Age and into the Swing Era of the 1940's will be featured. Bill Brown and his jazz orchestra will supply the musical background. Tickets are $5, the elderly $4. Call 397-4435 for reservations. $90ELEANOR CHARLES
