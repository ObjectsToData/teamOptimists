LIKE INTERPRETING THE DREAMS OF PHARAOH

By ANTHONY LEWIS; Anthony Lewis is a columnist for The New York Times.
Published: November 6, 1988

ORIGINAL INTENT AND THE FRAMERS' CONSTITUTION By Leonard W. Levy. 525 pp. New York: Macmillan Publishing Company. $19.95.

On June 1, 1787, the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia took up a proposal that the government it was trying to frame should have ''a national Executive.'' That would be a striking departure from the existing Articles of Confederation, under which the only national political institution was a legislature, the Continental Congress.

Delegates supported the proposal, but one after another said the executive must not make policy on war and peace. Charles Pinckney of South Carolina, as recorded in James Madison's notes, was for ''a vigorous Executive but was afraid the Executive powers . . . might extend to peace & war which would render the Executive a Monarchy of the worst kind.'' James Wilson of Pennsylvania said executive powers should not be defined in terms of ''the Prerogatives of the British Monarch,'' some of which were really ''of a Legislative nature'' such as ''that of war & peace.'' Madison himself was recorded in Rufus King's notes as follows: ''Agrees with Wilson in his definition of executive powers . . . - do not include the Rights of war and peace.''

The intent of the Framers could not be clearer: matters of war and peace were for Congress to decide. What a wonderful irony lies in that fact. For today a school of conservative lawyers and politicians argues that in applying our fundamental law we must follow ''the original intent of the Framers.'' Yet those same conservatives, in the Reagan Administration and out, insist that the inherent powers of the President allow him to use the armed forces and involve this country in hostilities as he wishes - that to subject his military actions to even a modest Congressional check, as in the War Powers Act, is unconstitutional. ''The concept of inherent executive powers was foreign to the Philadelphia Convention,'' Leonard W. Levy writes in ''Original Intent and the Framers' Constitution.'' Those who today argue at the same time for original intent and inherent Presidential powers, he says, display ''either their ignorance or their hypocrisy.''

Mr. Levy, a professor of humanities at the Claremont Graduate School in California and one of our leading constitutional historians, is merciless in exposing the follies of what former Attorney General Edwin Meese 3d called ''a jurisprudence of original intention.'' Merciless and brilliant. In fascinating detail, to our instruction and even more to our pleasure as readers, he demonstrates that there can be no such animal.

Consider the broad subject of foreign affairs. Presidents of both parties have long argued that foreign policy is committed by the Constitution to their exclusive control, and they have had great success in making that claim in the courts. A glazed reverence comes over the eyes of most judges when an official asserts an interest in foreign policy. Judges like to quote a 1936 Supreme Court opinion that spoke of ''the very delicate, plenary and exclusive power of the President as the sole organ of the Federal Government in the field of international relations.''

It is all nonsense as a matter of constitutional history. So Mr. Levy shows. ''The Framers intended the Senate to be the principal architect of foreign policy,'' he says. ''Had original intent prevailed, we would speak of the imperial Senate, not the imperial Presidency.''

In describing the executive in Article II of the Constitution, the convention delegates did not confide in him ''executive power'' or use any such limitless phrase; they said his function was to see that the laws were ''faithfully executed.'' Throughout the convention that was the understood concept of the Presidency: an agent of Congress. So too for foreign affairs. Even Alexander Hamilton, the most executive-minded of them all, wrote in ''The Federalist'' that treaty making partook ''more of the legislative than of the executive character.'' When John Marshall spoke in 1800 of the President as the nation's ''sole organ'' in foreign relations, he meant organ of communication. The Supreme Court in 1936 distorted that description into that of policy maker to whom all must yield.

History shows that Congress had legitimate claims to a role in foreign policy. But Mr. Levy's point is not that we should go back to what was envisaged in 1787, with the President as a mere agent. It is that we cannot be bound by the Framers' expectations because those expectations did not shape our system as it actually developed. Nor could they have, as Justice Holmes eloquently explained long ago: ''When we are dealing with words that also are a constituent act like the Constitution of the United States, we must realize that they have called into life a being the development of which could not have been foreseen completely by the most gifted of its begetters.''

The Framers understood the limits of their vision. That is why their work has lasted. They put their charter of government into spacious words that left room for adaptation to change in human affairs. And they took care not to create a legislative history of their own thinking that would confine the Constitutional text to the literalism of what they expected in 1787.
