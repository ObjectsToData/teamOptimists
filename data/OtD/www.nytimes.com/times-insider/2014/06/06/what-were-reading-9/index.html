    What We’re Reading

    By Insider Staff
    June 6, 2014 6:19 pmJune 13, 2014 12:48 pm

    Recommended reads from New York Times reporters and editors, highlighting great stories from around the web. What We’re Reading is an exclusive benefit for home delivery and digital subscribers that is sent twice a week as an email. Sign up »

    Blank on Blank

    Philip Seymour Hoffman’s Take on Happiness, Life, Childhood, Death

    Was the actor Philip Seymour Hoffman capable of expressing an uninteresting thought? I don’t think so. Check out this animation of an interview he gave in December 2012. “I kill pleasure,” he says. The interview has been whipping around the web this week and for good reason. — Dave Smith

    Vanity Fair

    The Enigmatic Mr. Capa

    Life magazine tapped four photographers to cover the Allied invasion of Normandy in June 1944. Among them was Robert Capa; already famous for his pictures from Spain’s civil war, he was an enigmatic combination of fearless battle correspondent and bon vivant, one who had “just enough time to hurry from his apartment on Belgrave Square to buy a new Burberry coat and a Dunhill silver flask” before shipping out for France. This account of Capa’s brave work during the invasion is gripping, and explains why most of his pictures from the landing will never be seen. — Diantha Parker

    The Washington Post

    The Saint of Latchkey Kids

    Hank Stuever, one of the best writers out there on popular culture, explains why so many people were touched so deeply to hear of the death of Ann B. Davis. For latchkey kids, the cast of shows like “The Brady Bunch” was their after-school bunch. An essay on reruns and lonely childhood. — John Schwartz

    The New Republic

    Technology Invades the English Department. Is That Good?

    A world that is increasingly digital and guided by data is the inevitable drift. But a few caveats are in order. In The New Republic, Adam Kirsch dissects academia’s overeager embrace of the “digital humanities,” in as elegant a critical essay as I’ve read in a while. — Steve Lohr

    The Guardian

    The Surreal Canvas of a Dying Town

    The 700-year-old Belgian village of Doel is largely abandoned, with only 25 remaining residents. But it has become a giant canvas for street artists who use the facades of derelict brick homes to display their art. The photos here are eerie and outstanding. — Samantha Storey
