For a Medical Student, a Shocking Lesson in the Real World

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Print
  • Reprints
  • Save
By LARRY ZAROFF, M.D.
Published: June 13, 2006

Maggots have always been around. Now, along with leeches they have become useful medical devices, fashionable even. Have plastic surgery, have leeches.

Skip to next paragraph
Vivienne Flesher

Both animals have recently been portrayed as beneficial in the popular literature (maggots made a cameo appearance in the film "Gladiator") as well as in medical journals: leeches for the removal of engorged blood in plastic surgery, and maggots to clean up by devouring dead tissue in wounds. (Maggots, the tortured gourmands of the insect world, turn down healthy parts.)

I recall a time when maggots were regarded as anything but acceptable. Phil had a leg full of them when we met. I was a medical student in Washington at City Hospital, then named Gallinger. In those days, the early 1950's, the attitude toward medical students was: "O.K., you got through the cadavers in anatomy class. Now enter into the real world inhabited by the living — 'the horror, the horror' — taking care of the sickest, the poorest, the most miserable patients. Can you deal?"

That was the city hospital, that was Phil, that was me.

I should have known, should have expected the worst, when I was assigned to do the student history and physical examination on Phil, a new admission. The head nurse pointed me toward the only private room on the ward. Who goes to the private? The dying, the patients doctors drift by, pausing to peek in, moving on.

I asked the nurse, "What's the problem?"

I heard clearly, "Only a bad wound," then a murmur, "and a few other problems."

Give her credit: she parted with a sympathetic smile.

I was about to appreciate the consequences of the work of doctors. A price was to be paid to become a real physician. The room — no, the cell, the cave, fit for other species, not human — had gray-green walls, a small dirty window, protected by bars, no escape. The ceiling a vise, squeezing its contents. A single bed, the kind with cranks and metal side rails, a threatening commode, a small cabinet without a drawer to close.

The place smelled empty. Almost. Filled with depression. The figure in the bed, without shape or movement except for the slight bellows of a chest.

I said, "Hello, I'm the medical student assigned to your care."

No answer.

"What brings you to the hospital?"

Still silent, Phil pulled the blanket off his legs. He pointed to one, with a wobbly dirty bandage from groin to knee. He removed it without hesitation, no shame, proud to show off his special reason to be in the hospital, the explanation for his aloneness. Why not reveal his stigma? He was finally with someone, even if only a beginner as doctor. Someone was paying attention.

He came alive, almost smiled at my withdrawal. I stepped away — a reflex — from a wound gaping groin to knee, full of, well, dying flesh infected, engulfed by white, sinister, crawling, tiny maggots, ghosts waiting for reincarnation.

The rest of him eventually registered. The face, covered not with what you would call a beard though hair was present, more like weeds arising from an unplowed garden. The eyes, under the skin, buried, hidden, blind to the world, to himself. The body, less than thin, a reed, a 16th note. The bones about to emerge through the skin.

I was shocked, burned. You don't see this in the middle class. In America.

No story for the chart. Except that he was homeless, alcoholic, eventually found to be a diabetic, without family. His only friend had abandoned him, as Sophocles' Philoctetes was abandoned on a deserted island by the Greeks, because of "his maggot ridden foot."

No memory of how Phil was injured, a fall into glass, a dark interposition with a knife. And the maggots — "Just noticed them yesterday."

The plan: Remove maggots with soap and water, clean, feed, culture wound, antibiotics. Treat diabetes. Insulin.

But how to heal the loneliness engendering despair, the helplessness, the hopelessness? How to make a life of a fragment? The maggots with kin?

I had no clue. I had never thought of alone as a problem. Medical school did not teach how to heal loneliness. Later, I began to reach beyond the knowledge that science imparts to the humanities that bring wisdom.

Finally, now, I understand what John Donne meant: "As sickness is the greatest misery, so the greatest misery of sickness is solitude."
