Rural Manhattan: Travel Back in Time, by Subway

By SETH KUGEL
Published: May 28, 2006
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

BACK in the 1830's, Manhattanites looking to escaping the urban dirt and grime on a summer weekend would head out of the city to recharge in the tranquil countryside. Sounds just like today, except that back then, "out of the city" meant taking a horse and carriage to places like the Mount Vernon Hotel on East 61st Street.

The Dyckman Farmhouse Museum on Broadway at 204th Street.

The countryside is gone, but the hotel is still there (in museum form), just beyond the Bed, Bath and Beyond in the shadows of the Queensboro Bridge. And, tucked among the apartment buildings that hold the island's 1.6 million residents, there are enough vestiges — or reminders — of the island's bucolic days to make for an entire weekend in rural Manhattan. Leave the horse and carriage at home, though; all you need is a Metrocard.

THE RURAL SOUTH

Get in an agricultural state of mind at the Union Square Greenmarket, where regional farmers sell fare like cider, pumpkin spice bread, vegan carrot raisin cookies and the crisp winesap apples that have a crunch that you won't find in a supermarket, and certainly not for a dollar a pound. You can talk a little rural education policy with the maple syrup vendor, too: Andy Van Glad of Wood Homestead, about 25 miles from Oneonta, N.Y., is not just a farmer but president of the Board of Education of the Jefferson Central School District.

Next, you might want to check out the Map Collection at the main New York Public Library building on Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street and pick from the hundreds of old New York City maps in the catalog, including a facsimile of the mid-17th-century Nicholls Map that shows the tree-covered island of Manhados, right across Hudson's River from "Parte of the Continent of America," now known as New Jersey. An 1804 map shows the Bowery not turning into Third Avenue as it does today but becoming the "Road to Boston."

Then it's on to the Mount Vernon Hotel Museum and Garden for a tour of the former carriage house that in 1826 became a place for New Yorkers to spend a relaxing day, separated by gender in the ladies' parlor and a gentlemen's tavern, or watching steamships go up the East River.

At the end of the day, grab a cab and head back down to Greenwich Village, where you can have dinner in a farmhouse. Mas, which means farmhouse in Provençal, has walls decorated with planks of antique wood barns, and serves French dishes of largely organic meat and vegetables. "A meal at Mas has the real possibility of excellence," Frank Bruni wrote in The Times in 2004.

THE NORTHERN HINTERLANDS

Most summer Sundays, there is a nature walk in Inwood Hill Park, at the upper end of Manhattan, given by Mike Feller, naturalist of the Parks Department. Make sure you ask for an early wake-up call; the two-hour tour starts at 8 a.m. and you'll need an up to an hour to get there by subway if you're starting from midtown. (Take the 1 train to 215th street.) Inwood Hill is home to 160 acres of native forest and is where Peter Minuit is said to have bought Manhattan from the Lenape Indians for a pittance. Though it is not as virgin as some would have you believe — many trees were cut down during the Revolutionary War and families later had estates there — you'd never know it from the winding paths inhabited by enormous tulip trees, honeysuckle, orioles and wild turkeys. In June, for the fifth and final year, baby bald eagles will be placed in the park as part of a five year project; until they start flying, you'll be able to watch them via video from the Urban Ecology Center building nearby.

If you're craving brunch after the nature walk, northern Manhattan also has a truly out-of-this-urban-world restaurant, the New Leaf Cafe in nearby Fort Tryon Park. The cafe is housed in a 1930's stone building surrounded by flowering plants and chirping birds. If that's not un-Manhattan-like enough for you, consider the astounding plentiful — and free — parking. (You're still better off taking the A train.) The $16 brunch menu includes tomato and cheddar omelettes and the like, made with organic eggs from upstate farms.

From the New Leaf, you can hike back down the hill and head a few blocks north on Broadway to the Dyckman Farmhouse Museum, built in 1784 by the Dyckman family. They planted cabbage and probably apple and cherry orchards on land worked largely by slaves. They owned several hundred acres of Manhattan property, which at today's real estate prices would be roughly a gajillion dollars. The house, perched on the corner of Broadway and 204th Street, recently reopened after a renovation.

Want to have dinner at another farmhouse? Then take the A train back to midtown — to the stop at 8th Avenue and 42nd Street — and grab a table at La Masseria, an Italian restaurant on 48th Street whose name also translates as farmhouse, and which features scialatielli with eggplant and smoked mozzarella or oven-roasted rabbit.

"La Masseria," Mr. Bruni wrote in The Times last year, "can be diligent and deft" under optimal circumstances.
