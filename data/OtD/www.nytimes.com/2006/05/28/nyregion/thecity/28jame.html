The Old Neighborhood

  • Sign In to E-Mail This

  • Save
By SAM ROBERTS
Published: May 28, 2006

A CENTURY ago, when the novelist Henry James returned to New York after living abroad for two decades, he found himself appalled by how the neighborhood of his youth — the streets around Washington Square Park — had been transformed into a place he pronounced as impermanent, alien, shrill, blighted and a "vast crude democracy of trade."

A NEW WORLD James grew up in a house at 58 West 14th Street; it had already been torn down when he visited New York in 1906.

He recorded his impressions of "New York Revisited" in a three-part series that was excerpted in The New York Times and published in full in Harper's Monthly magazine. The final installment appeared 100 years ago this month, shortly after the novelist's 63rd birthday.

In these articles, he expressed revulsion at the physical transformation of the village of his birth into a great city, likening the skyscrapers to the teeth of a comb or pins protruding from a cushion.

"Crowned not only with no history, but with no credible possibility of time for history, and consecrated by no uses save the commercial at any cost," James wrote, "they are simply the most piercing notes in that concert of the expensively provisional into which your supreme sense of New York resolves itself."

Provisional was the word. Since James had last walked these streets, a parking garage and an apartment building have replaced his grandmother's house at 18 Washington Square North. His family's home on West 14th Street is a Foot Locker.

By 1906, his birthplace on Washington Place, just off the square, had already been demolished to accommodate a 10-story loft building, "a high, square impersonal structure, proclaiming its lack of interest with a crudity all its own." Less than a decade later, 146 employees of the Triangle shirtwaist company would die there in a horrific fire.

Of the disappearance of his birthplace, James wrote, "The effect for me, in Washington Place, was of having been amputated of half my history."

Still, despite the enormous changes that have swept through the neighborhood over the past century, the author would recognize a surprisingly large number of landmarks today.

The loft building still stands, though it is now used for classrooms by New York University. Although Washington Square South is now dominated with imposing new N.Y.U. buildings, the Italianate Judson Memorial Church, built in 1890, remains, as does the park's famous marble arch, which replaced a wooden one in 1892.

The remaining 1830's Greek Revival houses along Washington Square North would look familiar, although the door marked No. 18 is flanked by tasteful plaques that, rather than memorializing the site as the setting of James's novel "Washington Square," advertise the offices of a urologist and a cosmetic surgeon.

In the novel, written in 1881, James wrote of "the Square, containing a considerable quantity of inexpensive vegetation, enclosed by a wooden paling, which increased its rural and accessible appearance; and round the corner was the more august precinct of the Fifth Avenue, taking its origin at this point with a spacious and confident air which already marked it for high destinies."

The destinies would prove higher than he could have imagined. Although Macdougal Alley and Washington Mews retain much of their 19th-century charm, a modern 21-story apartment building, about as high as the tallest skyscraper in 1906, looms over the park, which though still lush is paved with more concrete than in James's day.

Near Eighth Street, the author recorded a Proustian moment outside a bakery — "a blessed repository of doughnuts, cookies, creamcakes and pies." The section of the street between Fifth Avenue and Sixth Avenue — now the Avenue of the Americas — has long since been transformed to a hip emporium, but the smell of baked goods still drifts onto the street from places like Le Pain Quotidien, a waffle shop called Mon Repas, and Choux Factory, which specializes in cream puffs. Smells of a different sort emanate from Gray's Papaya, another occupant of the block.

AT 10th Street, James could still pause today to reflect on John La Farge's painting "The Ascension of Our Lord" inside the Church of the Ascension, an English Gothic edifice built in 1840. But he would have found his house on 14th Street west of Fifth Avenue long since demolished, replaced first by Jacob Rothschild's millinery store (it had branches in Brooklyn and Paris) and early in the 20th century by an unattractive five-story building, soon to be topped by several more floors of apartments. The site is flanked by a Starbucks — James might have appreciated the allusion, at least, to the first mate in "Moby Dick" — and a storefront that sells electronics and luggage.

The city to which James returned in 1904 was reaching both new heights and new depths. The first subway would open a year after his visit, taking theatergoers and other former trolley riders uptown to Times Square and even farther to Central Park, which had opened just a few years before James sailed from New York in 1883.

Despite the author's disdain for impermanence, James scholars suggest that he might have found much to celebrate in 21st-century New York.

"I think that the master would have the power of thought to be delighted by the ongoing exuberance of Lower Manhattan," said Harold Bloom, the literary scholar and Yale humanities professor, who keeps an apartment in Greenwich Village.

And John Auchard, a University of Maryland professor who has written and edited books about James, recalled the novelist's exuberant — if often ambivalent — response to both the Bowery and to immigrant ghettoes, whose tenement fire escapes offered "a little world of bars and perches and swings for human squirrels and monkeys."

"I think above all the high-culture Mandarin Henry James would have regretted the transformation of the Lower East Side, and other parts of the city, into something that begins to approximate a mall," Professor Auchard said. "Today James might wish to chomp on a pastrami sandwich in a remote corner of Katz's Deli on East Houston. But he might do so with unspeakable Jamesian dread of the Starbucks inching up the block."

Katz's opened in 1888. But there is no evidence that James ever ate a pastrami sandwich there.
