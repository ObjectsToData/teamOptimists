Princeton to Receive Record Gift for the Arts

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By ROBIN POGREBIN
Published: January 21, 2006

Peter B. Lewis, the Cleveland philanthropist known for his tough standards, is giving his alma mater, Princeton University, $101 million to expand its creative and performing arts activities, including the creation of an artists-in-residence program. The gift, the largest in the school's history, is to be announced today.

Mr. Lewis, who said he chose the figure of $101 million to top the last large donation to the university ($100 million), called the arts "an important part of life I didn't know when I was at Princeton and didn't know when I was a kid."

Mr. Lewis, 72, resigned from the board of the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum last year - leaving the institution without its biggest benefactor - because of differences over the museum's direction and financial management. In Cleveland, Mr. Lewis withdrew his support from every cultural and charitable organization in the city to pressure Case Western Reserve University's board to exercise greater fiscal restraint or to resign.

But in a telephone interview yesterday, he sounded easygoing about his gift to Princeton, saying he was confident that the university would use his money wisely. Mr. Lewis, chairman of the Progressive Corporation, an automobile insurance company in Cleveland, has been generous to Princeton; this gift brings his total contributions to more than $220 million.

"This will have a transformational effect on Princeton," said Shirley M. Tilghman, the president of the university. "It will bring the creative and performing arts into real focus in a way they haven't been in the history of the university."

The gift will underwrite a new Center for the Creative and Performing Arts at Princeton, featuring a Society of Fellows in the Arts program for about 12 artists in residence. The fellows program will allow emerging young artists to live and work at the school for two years at a time, pursuing their own work and contributing to the school through teaching or extracurricular activities.

Princeton hopes to capitalize on the university's proximity to New York and Philadelphia to attract working artists. The center will also expand the campus's physical space for art, possibly providing a new gallery and additional theater space.

The donation will allow the university to expand its arts faculty and course offerings, as well as performances, Ms. Tilghman said. Currently, for example, Princeton offers courses in lighting design or set design once every two years.

After becoming president in 2001, said Ms. Tilghman, a molecular biologist, she began "trying to identify the things we are doing well while also being particularly alert to ways in which we are failing to meet either student demand or faculty aspirations."

The arts emerged as an area in need of redress. In talking to students and faculty members, Ms. Tilghman heard of student dance groups having to move all the chairs out of a classroom to create rehearsal space; of a student who was turned down eight times for Emmet Gowin's oversubscribed photography class; and of music students who carried their instruments all over campus in rain and snow because there was no storage area.

"It became clear to me that this was one area in which Princeton was just simply underperforming," she said.

Ms. Tilghman said she was particularly impressed with Swarthmore College, where the arts had been integrated into the humanities curriculum. "There was a seamlessness that I liked a lot," she said. "That's one of the things we are trying to promote."

Princeton hoped to attract artistic students who before might not have applied. "It certainly kept us off the list of students whose first priority was to find a place where they could continue to pursue their music, dance, painting," Ms. Tilghman said. "Part of this initiative is to visibly commit Princeton to expanding these programs in the arts."

The faculty task force rejected the idea of creating a graduate school in the arts; Princeton does not have graduate programs in law, business or medicine. Yet the university wants to foster interaction between graduate and undergraduate students. "When you put together a graduate student committed to a field and an undergraduate still trying to figure it out, wonderful things happen," Ms. Tilghman said, explaining how the Society of Fellows idea evolved.

While the arts on campus will intensify, students will not be required to participate. But Ms. Tilghman said she hoped students would experience more, if only as members of an audience. "We're not going to force feed it," she said. "But we're going to make it hard to avoid."
