A Spotlight on the Battles That Set the Stage for Revolution

Published: January 18, 2006

What was George Washington's accent like? "The War That Made America," a four-part edutainment series that begins tonight on PBS, makes some guesses about that and hundreds of other lost historical details, as it chronicles the French and Indian War in high-gloss and ultimately successful re-creations. Possibly Washington sounded very American, with hard R's; possibly he sounded Irish-like; maybe he sounded British or southern or vaguely mid-Atlantic. The actor can't seem to decide. And the dramaturge here - this is television, so let's say that's Laura Fisher, a historian and one of the show's executive producers - has set herself bigger tasks.

She has set out to render in lavish particulars the story of the strange war between the French and British empires for control of the Ohio River Valley in the 1750's and 60's. The war was triangulated: American Indians, for whom the valley was a homeland, played the empires against each other, eventually tipping the balance of power in favor of the British. The Indians' strategy, diplomacy and unorthodox military tactics are the chief focus of this program, which attends closely to their considerable role in the war. (Graham Greene, the actor and Oneida Indian whose ancestors fought in the war, serves as narrator.)

With Deborah Acklin, the film's other executive producer, Ms. Fisher has elected to use voice-over and re-enactments almost exclusively; no eminent talking heads appear to interrupt the war's suspense with interpretation and exposition. In preserving the pace of this bloody, pulse-racing narrative, this choice makes sense. But it also lays the documentary open to the charges that dog every historical re-creation: it's specious, and it looks like a school play.

Consider the first charge: it's specious. The program's promotional materials claim great accuracy, authenticity and fidelity to history - and no doubt the filmmakers used all the artifacts and documentation they had. These include original weaponry, portraits of the principals and battle notes by George Washington, who got his training for the Revolution as a young officer in the French and Indian War. But this film contains much more drama than can be extrapolated from the available materials; the appearances of the various characters - to say nothing of the dialogue, the stage business and the blow-by-blow of the various battles - are often nothing but hypotheses on the part of the filmmakers.

Then there is the grade-school factor. With the various players, including George Washington and others, turning periodically to the audience to give soliloquies culled from historical writings, "The War That Made America" strongly suggests children's drama. The characters here don't quite say, "I'm a sweet potato; I'm loaded with beta carotene," but the hokey pedantry is only slightly less obvious. Moreover, the emphasis on the role of women - both in the Iroquois League and with the British Army - seems overplayed, as if driven by public-school ideology.

But finally, who cares? "The War That Made America" got its financing from various foundations and grant-givers, including the National Endowment for the Humanities. This is the ideal PBS project. Pittsburgh's WQED put up millions of dollars for it, and the program's appearance on PBS roughly marks the 250th anniversary of the war, as well as of the founding of Pittsburgh. This film is, above all, a patriotic teaching tool - a conscientious rendering of American military history, and an effort, using available technology and narrative techniques, to "bring history to life," in the optimistic phrase.

The War That Made America

PBS, tonight and Jan. 25 at 9; check local listings.

Deborah Acklin and Laura Fisher, executive producers; written, directed and produced by Eric Stange of Spy Pond Productions and Ben Loeterman of Ben Loeterman Productions.
