Book Notes

By Edwin McDowell
Published: January 25, 1989

Awaiting the Other Shoe

Many books are published without fanfare, although few of them intentionally so. But the University of Nebraska has asked reviewers to hold off until the spring on a book it published recently. The book is ''Wartime Journalism, 1939-1943'' by Paul de Man, a collection whose contents have already stirred worldwide controversy.

A little over a year ago the academic world was stunned to learn that Professor de Man - who was Sterling Professor of Humanities at Yale at the time of his death in 1983 and who founded the school of literary criticism known as deconstruction - wrote for an anti-Semitic, pro-Nazi newspaper in Belgium in World War II. This collection contains those writings.

''We asked reviewers to hold off because we are doing a companion volume in late March,'' said Dr. Willis Regier, the director of the press. The companion volume, ''Responses,'' will consist of precisely that - responses to the de Man articles from a wide spectrum of international scholars.

''Wartime Journalism,'' a 399-page book, is edited by Werner Hamacher, Neil Hertz and Thomas Keenan. Some articles are in French and some in Flemish, but in the interest of publishing the book quickly, only the Flemish articles have been translated.

After the first de Man articles were discovered by a researcher in Belgium, the Oxford Literary Review planned to publish them last spring, and the University of Nebraska Press arranged to distribute the journal in this country. But as more writings were discovered and the project became more ambitious, the Oxford Literary Review pulled out. Nebraska - whose list is strong in literary theory as well as American history - took on the project.

''One of the major purposes of publishing is to get books out that will make a difference,'' Dr. Regier said. ''In literary studies, very few books we've published have aroused this much interest.'' Villard Best Sellers

It took a while for Villard Books to get its sea legs, but it has been smooth sailing the last year or so for the Random House division established in 1983. Last year it had six books on The New York Times best-seller list and this week it had these three:

''All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kindergarten'' by Robert Fulghum, the No. 1 nonfiction book; ''Don't Bend Over in the Garden, Granny, You Know Them Taters Got Eyes'' by Lewis Grizzard, No. 7 on the nonfiction list, and ''Wilderness'' by Jim Morrison, No. 13 on the fiction list. The first two books are holdovers from the 1988 list.

Villard, a comparative midget in Random House, was started by Marc Jaffe, who moved to Random House from Bantam in 1980 and brought in Peter Gethers. Mr. Jaffe left in 1986, and Mr. Gethers became editorial director.

Two weeks ago, Villard and Ivy Books bought a second collection of Mr. Fulghum's essays for seven figures. That book, as yet untitled, is scheduled for publication next fall. Cheever's Next Books

A few years ago Bantam Books bid for Susan Cheever's novel ''Doctors & Women'' but lost to Clarkson N. Potter, part of Crown Publishers. Miss Cheever also has a novel scheduled for publication by Farrar, Straus & Giroux this September, ''The Education of Elizabeth Cole.'' Now Bantam has bought her next two books for what Steve Rubin, Bantam's publisher, described as ''a very respectable six figures.'' Paying that much for books not yet written, Mr. Rubin said, ''speaks to our commitment to her as a writer.''

One, a nonfiction work, will be titled ''Treetops'' and is an account of the author's mother. Deborah Futter, the Bantam editor who acquired the books, described it as a companion book to ''Home Before Dark,'' Miss Cheever's recent biographical memoir of John Cheever, her father. The other, a novel, is tentatively titled ''The Hour of the Wolf.'' Medal-Winner's Book

In one of his final official acts, Ronald Reagan last week awarded the Presidential Citizens Medal to Dr. Dorothy I. Height, the president of the National Council of Negro Women. Now Warner Books has signed Dr. Height to write her autobiography with Maya Angelou.

The council, which includes professional organizations and church groups, was created in 1935 by the noted educator Mary McLeod Bethune. in New York City. Dr. Height has been its president since 1957.

Publication of Dr. Height's autobiography is planned for early next year. ''While it is a story told from a perspective of enormous historical content and importance,'' said Nansey Neiman, Warner's publisher, ''it is also one of inspiration and achievement by a young girl who was undeterred by the barriers racism put in her way and brought this spirit of excellence to a life of public service.''
