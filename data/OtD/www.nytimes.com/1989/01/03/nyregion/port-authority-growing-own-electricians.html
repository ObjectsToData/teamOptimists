Port Authority 'Growing' Own Electricians

By WINSTON WILLIAMS
Published: January 3, 1989

Hours after the High School for the Humanities has adjourned its regular daily sessions in Manhattan, a band of young Port Authority employees squeezes their young adult frames into the small desk chairs. Clad still in their dusty boots and green work uniforms, they join a larger contingent of union-sponsored apprentices for two hours of lectures, quizzes and the occasional catnap.

Though it resembles other night-school classes, the cluttered classroom is also a laboratory where social engineers are conducting a ground-breaking experiment: the Port Authority is training its first electrical apprentices in 20 years, saying that it is ''growing our own'' in response to its fears of impending labor shortages.

Like other large employers in the region, the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey is concerned about the aging of its work force and the paucity of skills among recent high-school graduates. Shortages of skilled labor could hamper the authority's multibillion-dollar crash program to rebuild the bridges, tunnels, airports and transportation terminals in and around New York.

''There's a mismatch between where the jobs growth is and the areas of high unemployment,'' said Rebecca Andrade, who heads the staff that oversees the program. ''We have to make the link. We literally have to change the complexion of the work force.''

The Port Authority is working with the unions' traditional apprentice programs. For example, the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers, a close-knit union, has relinquished some of its power to restrict its membership, opening its doors to more nonwhite and female trainees.

Dissatisfaction had been expressed, even among union members, with the quality of new electricians. The authority, in training its own apprentices, and in a companion program in which it is training its own heating-plant engineers in conjunction with the operating engineers union, is hoping to develop a program that can serve as a model for rebuilding the region's work force.

Over the next several years the authority faces an almost complete turnover of its skilled workers. The migration of workers from other parts of the country, a traditional cure for regional labor shortages, is not expected to be a major source of replacements, whose flow has been stanched by New York's expensive housing.

To make matters worse, the pool of entry-level industrial workers - those in the 18-to-20-year age bracket - is unusually low, a consequence of the relatively low birth rates of the 1960's.

Retirements already average 9 percent a year at the Port Authority, which has about 200 electricians, and with the age of its skilled trades workers averaging about 49, the authority's retirement rate is expected to accelerate soon. Deluged With Applicants

Within the Port Authority, however, there seems to be an abundance of young people who want to replace them. Last year's pilot electrical training program and the two full-fledged apprenticeship programs that started last summer were deluged with applicants. More than 250 Port Authority employees applied for the 14 electrical apprenticeships.

''This is an opportunity that doesn't come along every day, and when it comes, you jump on it,'' said Kirk Nelson Jr., a toll collector at the George Washington Bridge and a part-time political science student at Columbia University when he was accepted as an apprentice. Mr. Nelson, the son of a Port Authority employee, is now learning the intricacies of runway lighting at Newark International Airport.

''I had always wanted to get into the trades,'' said Donna Brunno, an electrical apprentice who had worked as a secretary for construction-related companies before she joined the authority two years ago as a clerk-typist. 'Never Dawned on Me'

But, she confided ''it never dawned on me'' to seek admission to the electrical union's traditional apprenticeship program, which had been less than hospitable to women. But when she heard of the Port Authority program, she decided to apply.

The program takes four years, during which apprentices work full time and take classes after work. The Port Authority electrical apprentices are paid $7.25 an hour to start, compared with about $20 an hour for a beginning journeyman and $9.75 for ''helpers,'' the traditional nonapprentice entry job in the electrical trades. Apprentices not in the authority's program are paid about 25 percent more, but they have to pay their own tuition and are subject to the seasonal nature of the job.

Excluding administrative overhead, the authority spends about $215,000 a year on the electrical apprentices, including their salaries and tuition payments, and advocates of such training say the money is well spent. They contend that the apprenticeships could become valuable models for rebuilding the region's skilled labor force.

But the Port Authority's austerity measures have brought the apprentice programs under review, possibly jeopardizing plans to expand them.

Still, the authority is expecting to reap benefits beyond cost savings. It hopes to foster more racial integration in the workplace by bringing nonwhites and women into trades that have previously been largely reserved for white men. The current class of 14 electrical apprentices has seven nonwhite men and one woman, who is white. Getting Over the Hurdles

A maze of hurdles has traditionally stood between applicants and the union's apprenticeship rolls. The Port Authority is running interference with the union to remove some of the impediments.
