MUSIC; An Encore for the Cleveland

By ROBERT SHERMAN
Published: January 22, 1989

THE Hartford and Waterbury Symphonies started the new season yesterday evening, and you can catch the repeat of the Waterbury Symphony's Gershwin program at the Kennedy High School today at 3. At least eight other orchestral programs hop on the musical bandwagon this week, beginning with tomorrow's annual visit of the Cleveland Orchestra to Bushnell Hall in Hartford.

The unusual program finds the music director Christoph von Dohnanyi pairing Beethoven's ''Eroica'' Symphony with widely contrasted British scores - the gentle Prelude to ''Irmelin'' by Delius and Sir Michael Tippett's spiky Triple Concerto.

Note another unusual element: the concert by the orchestra, which has been visiting for more than 40 years, begins at 7 P.M., an hour earlier than all others in the Bushnell series. For more information, call 246-6807.

The musicians' strike forced the New Haven Symphony to cancel the first four of its eight scheduled concerts this season and delayed the debut of Michael Palmer as music director. Now that the labor problems have been resolved, Mr. Palmer finally gets to give the downbeat to his orchestra at 8 P.M. Tuesday at Woolsey Hall.

On the program arev an early Mozart symphony, No. 12 in G, the Shostakovich Ninth and Tchaikovsky's ever-popular Violin Concerto, with the superb young British virtuoso Nigel Kennedy as soloist. For more information, call 776-1444.

Skipping ahead to the weekend, we find the Greater Bridgeport, Stamford and Greenwich Symphonies in competing musical action on Saturday.

At Klein Auditorium at 8:30 P.M., the Greater Bridgeport Symphony welcomes as a guest conductor Susan Haig of the Calgary Philharmonic for a program that begins with ''Oiseaux Exotiques,'' a suite by the Canadian composer Harry Freedman.

The program continues in a more romantic vein with Mendelssohn's Piano Concerto No. 1 and the Rachmaninoff Second Symphony. Information is at 576-0263.

Another young pianist, Peter Serkin, plays the Ravel G major with the Greenwich Symphony. The music director David Gilbert surrounds that concerto sparkler with Bach's Brandenburg Concerto No. 4 and the Franck Symphony in D minor. The 8:30 P.M. Saturday program will be repeated at the high school next Sunday at 4. Details are at 869-2664.

The Stamford Symphony outings will be at 8:30 P.M. Saturday and 3 P.M. next Sunday at the Palace Theater. The music director, Roger Nierenberg, will omit the concertos altogether in favor of two expansive orchestral masterworks, Brahms' lovely and under-appreciated Serenade No. 2 and the Schubert Ninth.

Earlier next Sunday at 1:30, children from 8 through 13 years old are invited to an innovative and free concert experience called ''Kids on Stage.'' They will actually sit in the Stamford players' chairs as Mr. Nierenberg and the musicians discuss the music to be performed. For more information, call 325-1407.

The British are coming to Yale in musical force this week, starting Thursday at 8 P.M. when Music Spectrum presents a free program at Sprague Hall tracing English music from the period of the Elizabethan John Dowland through the 20th century with such masters as Britten, Vaughan Williams and Walton.

The distinguished roster of Yale faculty musicians includes Donald Currier, Elizabeth Parisot, Sidney Harth, Benjamin Verdery, Thomas Nyfenger, Ronald Roseman and Jerold Siena. Details are at 432-4158.

Next Sunday at 3, Mr. Siena will perform in another free recital at the Yale Center for British Art. The tenor will be joined by Korby Myrick, a mezzo-soprano, and the pianists William Braun and Melvin Strauss. They will perform English songs by Britten, Walton and their less-celebrated colleague, Gerald Finzi. Call 432-2850 for more information.

You'll also find English folk songs arranged by Haydn at the next New Haven concert of the Wall Street Chamber Players, but otherwise the program is both eclectic and international. Offerings include Schoenberg's Opus 9, ''Kammermusik,'' art songs of Beethoven and Dvorak's glorious A-major Piano Quintet. The guest baritone is Richard Lalli. The concert begins at 8 P.M. Friday in the Whitney Humanities Center. Details are at 773-0885.

On Thursday, only a day after the 230th birthday of Scotland's great bard, they'll be parading the Haggis at the annual Robert Burns Night at the South Congregational Church in Hartford.

Beginning at 7:15, the poet's verses will be read, his tunes played and his ballads sung by members of the Manchester Pipe Band and the South Church Ensemble under Richard Donohue. As Burns himself put it: ''Perhaps it may turn out a sang, perhaps turn out a sermon.'' One way or another, the participants will surely share a cup of tonal kindness for auld lang syne. For more information about the free program, call 249-8627.

Two fascinating concerts for aficionados of guitar music bracket the week in Hartford. Today the Connecticut Classical Guitar Society presents the Japanese virtuoso Masahiro Ojiri playing solo works of Bach and Sor, plus Bach and Giuliani concertos with the Farmington Valley Symphony Orchestra under John Eells. For more information about the 2 P.M. concert at the Connecticut Historical Society, call 249-7041.

Then on Saturday, you can quadruple the pleasure by attending the state debut of the Los Angeles Guitar Quartet. The 8:15 P.M. recital at Trinity College includes a new score by David Leisner, based on one of Kipling's ''Just So'' stories and enticingly titled ''The Cat That Walked by Himself.'' Details are at 297-2199.

A brief word of upcoming visits by two distinguished dance companies that boast very different French accents: Les Ballets Jazz de Montreal brings its ''sleek and sassy'' evocations of popular dance forms to the Garde Theater in New London at 7:30 P.M. Wednesday. Information is at 444-6766.

On Friday and Saturday evenings at 8, it's back to the classics with performances by Rudolf Nureyev and principal dancers of his Paris Opera Ballet at the Bushnell in Hartford. Call 527-0713 for details.
