WESTCHESTER GUIDE


Published: October 15, 1989

SAMURAI EXHIBITION

Private collectors in the New York City area have provided the exotic contents of a new exhibition at the Rye Arts Center called ''Yoroi: The Armor and Art of the Samurai Class of Japan of the Edo Period, 1615-1869.''

Coordinated by Roger Dunn, a collector of Japanese art and restorer of more than 45 suits of Japanese armor, the display features several complete suits of the intricately decorated armor and swords worn by the ruling warrior class.

Also on display is a collection of masks and costumes used in Japanese Noh theater, a ritualized form of storytelling in which exaggerated movement and sound are enhanced by extravagant, voluminous costumes and masks whose facial expressions can range from terrifying to demure.

Authentic Japanese screens and scrolls form a background for the exhibition, which opens today and remains through Nov. 11. Hours are from 9:30 A.M. to 5 P.M. weekdays and 2 to 5 P.M. Saturday. The center is at 51 Milton Road and admission is free. Call 967-0700 for more information. RUSSIAN IN CONCERT

Vladimir Feltsman, the Russian pianist, will perform the works of Bach, Mozart and Liszt in a special recital at 8 P.M. Wednesday at the Performing Arts Center of the State University of New York in Purchase. Mr. Feltsman, who spent eight years obtaining a visa to leave Russia and come to the United States, is being presented by the Jewish Home for the Elderly of Fairfield. Call (203) 374-9461, extension 292 for ticket information. CASE FOR VILLAGES

Densely populated cities were apparently as troublesome in the America of 1939 as they are 50 years later, according to a documentary film made that year by a highly-regarded group of people. ''The City,'' conceived by the historian Lewis Mumford, photographed by Ralph Steiner, Willard Van Dyke and Pare Lorentz and accompanied by the music of Aaron Copland will be shown free at 12:15 P.M. Friday in Room B-04 of Carman Hall, at Lehman College in the Bronx.

Described as grimly realistic and hopelessly idealistic, the 45-minute film will be introduced by David Bady, director of the City and Humanities Program at the college. ''The message was that America needed regionally planned villages rather than high-density cities - a concept that is still relevant today,'' Mr. Bady said.

The college is on Bedford Park Boulevard West at Goulden Avenue. Call (212) 960-8211 for more information and directions. RUSTIC OUTING

For a rustic day in the country, try the 10th Annual New York Sheep and Wool Festival in Rhinebeck from 8:30 A.M. to 4 P.M. Saturday. ''It's a major crafts festival for some and for others it's a day for buying and selling livestock,'' said Patricia Barry, one of the organizers for the Dutchess County Sheep and Wool Growers Association, sponsors of the event. ''There are lots of sheep farmers around here.''

The trip to the Dutchess County Fairgrounds on Route 9 can take between an hour and an hour and a half on the Taconic Parkway, exiting at Route 199. Admission is free, and the attractions are unusual as well as numerous.

First on the agenda is a sheep show and auction featuring bred ewes, meat and wool breeds, white and black. A black and white fleece show and sale, sheep-shearing competitions and demonstrations of sheep dogs at work are other events, plus a series of demonstrations illustrating the progress of wool from the sheep's back to the human back as it is sheared, spun and woven into fabric.

Llamas, sheep, angora goats, rabbits and alpacas will be on view all day and available for petting, and a wide variety of Hudson Valley foods and produce will be for sale, including of course, lamb.

Handcrafters from throughout the Northeast - including several from Westchester - will show an array of items made from wool, and there will be hides, yarns, spinning, knitting and weaving supplies, antique spinning wheels and livestock supplies. Call 877-3466 for more information. AUTUMNAL FAIR

One of the last outdoor antiques and arts and crafts fairs of autumn takes place from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. today in Nyack, with more than 300 booths to explore, rain or shine.

Main Street and South Broadway, the heart of the town's business district, are completely lined with furniture, Tiffany and Depression glass, antique and estate jewelry, porcelain figurines, bronzes, Art Deco accessories, hand-loomed rugs, baskets, dried flowers, oils, watercolors, miniature collectibles and sculpture.

The show is sponsored by the Arts, Crafts and Antique Dealers of the Nyacks. Admission is free. FALL FOLIAGE RUN

There is still time to enter the Scarsdale Historical Society's Ninth Annual Fall Foliage Run beginning at 3 P.M. today. Check-in time from noon to 2:30 covers the 3.1-mile run and the 1.55-mile Walk for Fun beginning at 2:30.

Registration costs $8 for adults and $6 for those younger than 18 at the historical society headquarters, at 937 White Plains Post Road. The step-off location for both races is in the parking lot across the street.

The first three run finishers in each male and female age group will receive awards, and silver trophies will be presented to the man and woman who finish first overall. Volunteers are needed to help out along the course, which runs through Arthur Manor. Call 723-1744 to offer your services or obtain more information. JOB WORKSHOPS
