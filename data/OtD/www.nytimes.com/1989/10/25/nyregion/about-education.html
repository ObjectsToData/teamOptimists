ABOUT EDUCATION

By Fred M. Hechinger
Published: October 25, 1989

Exposing the ignorance of the young is perennially popular with their elders. The latest example is ''50 Hours: A Core Curriculum for College Students'' by Lynne V. Cheney, chairwoman of the National Endowment for the Humanities.

A test of 87 multiple-choice questions was given to 696 seniors from 67 colleges. Among the findings were that 42 percent of the respondents did not know that the Civil War was fought between 1850 and 1900.

Mrs. Cheney recommends a core curriculum for all undergraduates that includes six survey courses on ''cultures and civilizations,'' a one-year course on ''the scope and power of mathematics,'' a one-year science-laboratory course and a course to explore the influence of the social sciences.

The report comes about two years after the publication of ''What Do Our 17-Year-Olds Know?'' by Diane Ravitch and Chester E. Finn Jr. Press reports on that study also concentrated on what young people, that time high school seniors, do not know.

Editorial acclaim of such exposure of youth's ignorance is rarely accompanied by disclosure of how reporters, editors and anchors might have fared on the tests. While wishing that college seniors knew more about American history, some responses were rather encouraging.

Many older people would have struck out identifying Faulkner as the author of ''The Sound and the Fury'' and ''As I Lay Dying,'' but 44 percent of the students got it right. More than two-thirds could identify correctly the themes of ''The Scarlet Letter'' and ''A Raisin in the Sun.'' Sixty-one percent knew that the Scopes trial dealt with the teaching of evolution. Not bad. The intention in questioning the endowment's gloomy conclusions is not to excuse ignorance. Some experts, however, ask whether the proper remedy is to prescribe a rather fixed college curriculum.

For example, Vartan Gregorian, the president of Brown University, said the critical focus should not be on the colleges but on the schools. In a discussion on the ''MacNeil/Lehrer Newshour,'' he said:

''I would urge that we require all our high schools to do all those things that Lynne Cheney recommends. I am not for rendering our institutions of higher learning into becoming remedial institutions in order to do the work that 12 years of education have been unable to do.''

A case in point may be Mrs. Cheney's call for two years of studying a foreign language in college. Many experts believe that languages are best learned in elementary school. Most colleges are ill equipped to deal with languages, except to study literature, which presumes a level of mastery before college.

The president of the College for Human Services in New York, Audrey Cohen, asked ''why we have to go back'' to the kind of core curriculum that was popular 50 years ago and ''assume that an old solution to a problem of a new kind of society is going to work.''

The president of Columbia University, Michael I. Sovern, supports the concept of a core course like his university's longstanding one on Contemporary Civilization; but that, too, seems less than Mrs. Cheney's proposed core curriculum.

Some observers fear that the schools, if asked to provide improved general education, may misread Mrs. Cheney's message and concentrate on dates and facts to improve test scores, to the detriment of real understanding. Knowing when the Civil War was fought is no substitute for knowing why.

On the bright side, some high schools are turning away from coverage by textbook, stressing instead exploring ideas like liberty from the Bill of Rights to the battle for civil rights. That brings history to life, but may not improve students' scores on multiple-choice tests. But Audrey Cohen said, ''To have multiple-choice tests to prove whether, in fact, you were an educated person to me is anathema.''

Still, a strong case can be made for a modest amount of time spent in college on a common core course like Contemporary Civilization at Columbia or western civilization at Stanford, which was recently revised to include non-Western offerings. But many college educators think that the explosion of knowledge makes it difficult to agree on a required core. Harvard struggled through the 70's to create a modern core curriculum, but came up only with a grab bag of courses with which students could satisfy the requirements.

Many colleges rely on ''distribution requirments,'' making students choose courses in the humanities, social sciences and natural sciences. But experience shows that selecting, for example, a general science course or even a basic laboratory course does little for a student who has not become familiar with science and mathematics in the 12 years before college.

Serious deficiencies of American education, many critics think, cannot be remedied at any one level. The problem, they believe, stems from a failure to see learning as a continuum, from kindergarten and even earlier, to graduation from high school or college.
