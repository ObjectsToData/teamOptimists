Reviews/Art; On Long Island: Vienna, Video and Visions of Nature

By ROBERTA SMITH
Published: August 4, 1989

In terms of art, Long Island is not exactly going quietly into the lazy days of late summer. On the contrary, from Roslyn to Riverhead and eastward to the various Hamptons, the current exhibitions in the area's museums and alternative spaces offer pleasures and challenges to many tastes and levels of sophistication.

At the Nassau County Museum of Fine Art in Roslyn, one can become immersed in the languid drawings of the early Viennese modernist Gustav Klimt and works by his contemporaries Egon Schiele and Oscar Kokoschka. At the Heckscher Museum in Huntington, the gently didactic ''Choice Pairs: Contrasts and Affinities in the Heckscher Collection'' offers a quick tutorial in the building blocks of art making and, by extension, art looking.

At the East End Arts Council in Riverhead, the installation art of Curt Royston attempts a truce between video and painting. And painting reigns supreme at the Parrish Art Museum in Southampton - where ''Painting Horizons,'' an exhibition of landscapes by Jane Freilicher, Albert York and April Gornik, has just opened - and also at the Dia Art Foundation in Bridgehampton, where a new cycle of canvases by the Italian Neo-Expressionist Francesco Clemente is on view. Klimt in Roslyn Weighing in at 125 works, the Klimt drawing show at the Nassau County Museum of Art in Roslyn is noteworthy in several respects: its size and scope, the smaller groupings of turn-of-the-century Viennese art and posters clustered around it, and also for a certain peculiarity of circumstance.

The Nassau County Museum, which has just been taken out of the county's jurisdiction and placed under the control of a newly appointed private board of directors, is in the midst of revamping its staff, financing structure and exhibition programs.

The present show has been cobbled together by Serge Sabarsky, a retired Manhattan art dealer who has long specialized in German and Austrian Expressionism and who has now been made director of the museum. Its scope is unusual for a small suburban museum, and not even so usual for Manhattan, where Viennese Modernism is not seen in bulk on an everyday basis.

Spanning the years 1879 to 1918, the Klimt show begins with the artist's skillful academic portraits and figure studies and, concentrating on his images of women, traces his progressive liberation of form, line and female sensuality. Klimt produced thousands of drawings and many of those here are not of the first rank. Still, they convey a strong sense of the radical autonomies - of line on paper and women in the world - that Klimt achieved, confirming the opinion that the artist's drawings are more inventive than his paintings.

The surrounding clusters of art are better still, especially a small selection of paintings by Klimt, Kokoschka and Schiele. This includes ''Water Nymphs,'' a small transitional oil, circa 1899, by Klimt, showing three snakelike women rising in an aqueous green atmosphere, and Kokoschka's 1910 ''Count Verona,'' which portrays an anxious modern personality against a background straight out of Lascaux.

Additional selections of watercolors and drawings by Kokoschka and Schiele also deserve careful consideration. All told, this marvelous, if somewhat haphazard, ramble through one of European Modernism's richest moments should not be missed. Huntington Pairings In contrast to the ambitious goings on at the Nassau County Museum, ''Choice Pairs,'' on view at the Heckscher Museum in Huntington, reflects a small suburban museum making the most of its own resources. The show, which has been organized by a guest curator, Barbara G. Coller, consists of a series of textbooklike yet illuminating juxtapositions drawn from the museum's collection.

Viewers are invited to compare the use of light in Ralph Blakelock's painting ''The Poetry of Moonlight'' with William Pratt's ''Forge,'' both from the late 19th century; the compositional strategies of Stuart Davis's 1924 watercolor ''Rigging'' with Fairfield Porter's 1974 canvas ''Morning From the Porch''; or the attitude toward nature (not to mention vast scale) in Thomas Moran's 1911 painting ''Grand Canyon of the Colorado River'' with Berenice Abbott's 1935 photograph of the Norris Dam under construction.

The other pairs, which number 18 in all, include Gustav Klimt against Audrey Flack, Hiram Powers against Daniel Chester French and Jean Ferdinand Monchablon against Camille Pissarro. Ms. Coller's explanatory wall texts guide the eye while leaving it plenty to discover for itself, adding much to a show that is an excellent introduction for beginning art lovers and a useful refresher course for those who think they've seen everything. (The next show at the Heckscher, opening Aug. 12, is ''Curators' Choice: New Directions,'' an exhibition of eight Long Island artists each selected by a curator from one of eight local museums.) Riverhead Video Art Meanwhile, at the 17-year-old East End Arts Council in Riverhead, the video artist Curt Royston attempts a fusion of painting and video that doesn't quite work but that is nonetheless entertaining. Mr. Royston constructs coarse and sometimes large paintings-cum-stage sets involving a few real objects (here a yellow Macintosh, there a folding beach chair), the occasional trompe l'oeil effect and a lot of bright, energetically applied colors.
