Slow Pace Toward Doctorates Prompts Fear of Unfilled Jobs

By JOSEPH BERGER
Published: May 3, 1989

Students are taking longer than ever to earn doctorates, about seven years compared with five and a half years in the 1960's. The slow pace is causing government officials and educators to fear there will be growing shortages of college teachers, scientists and many other professionals in coming years.

The concern has become so great that the Council of Graduate Schools is forming a special group to examine alternatives to the dissertation, the book-length treatise that is the capstone of the doctorate but that for many students becomes an obstacle of Himalayan proportions. Science departments in several schools are already accepting groups of published research articles in place of the dissertation. Others are setting stricter timetables for completion.

Thousands of Americans spend year after year mired in dissertations, demoralized and unable to start their careers. Campuses brim with legends about scholars who spent years toiling in libraries and at typewriters creating a definitive masterpiece, or worse, simply going through bouts of angst while resisting libraries and typewriters. 'Waste of National Resources'

There are cases of people who handed in their dissertations after retirement, when they finally had time to write them, or of people who work at several careers while their dissertations incubate. For those who do not finish, the dissertation lingers as a lifelong haunting regret.

''It's a high waste of national resources, not just financial but intellectual resources, to have an increasingly discouraged proletariat developing,'' said Dr. Theodor Ziolkowski, dean of Princeton University's graduate school. ''And that's what happens when students go for too long in that terrible state of limbo of aging graduate students.''

Part of the universities' incentive for speeding the process is self-preservation; the time it takes to complete a doctorate may be dissuading people from entering academic life just as a wave of professorial retirements will create 500,000 new college teaching vacancies in the next decade. Shift From 1960's

The doctorate has become a requirement for getting faculty jobs at most accredited colleges as well as professional jobs in many industries and licensed fields.

The National Research Council, an arm of the National Academy of Science, brought new attention to the issue in March when it reported in its annual survey of completed doctorates that the median time spent earning the diploma had risen to 6.9 years in 1987 from 6.1 years in 1977 and 5.4 years in 1967. That is a rise of 1.5 years in just two decades.

It found that the median age of the 32,278 students earning doctorates in 1987 was 33.6 years. Chemistry students were the speediest, with a median age of 29, and education students the tardiest, with a median age of 39.8. Loss of Prime Years

In part, these figures reflect the peculiarities of various professions. Education students, for example, usually work full time as teachers or administrators while working on their doctorates. The age rise also indicates that an increasing number of students had taken more time off after college graduation to raise families or explore other careers.

Still, the council's figures suggest that some of the most productive years of Americans who hold doctorates were spent preparing for their profession rather than fully working at it. Prime earning years were lost. ''Every year the time to doctorate increases, the cost to you goes up and the benefit goes down,'' said Howard B. Tuckman, a professor of economics at Memphis State University who conducted a study on doctorate time for the National Science Foundation, a Federal agency.

Because of such grim statistics, the Council of Graduate Schools, made up of 385 deans of graduate schools, is questioning whether the dissertation is still serving its original purpose of demonstrating the student's ability to do independent scholarship.

''Many scientists would say a book-length work is not the way we demonstrate scholarship,'' said Jules B. LaPidus, the council's president. ''We publish articles in journals.'' Educators also note that more than 70 percent of college teachers never publish another piece of scholarship after their dissertation.

Many reasons are given for the persistent upward slope in completion times for doctorates: the explosion in information that students must consume, cutbacks in Federal financing for the fellowships that once freed students from taking jobs, even a spirit of the times that makes it acceptable to wile years away as professional students.

''There's a fear of completion,'' said Ellen Benkin, director of graduate research at the University of California at Los Angeles. ''People who are in doctoral programs have been in institutions of higher education for 10 or 12 years, and that's a very comfortable environment.'' Security in Student's Life

Dr. Virginia Fromkin, dean of the graduate division of U.C.L.A., attributes the growing time it takes to complete a doctorate to a decline in academic jobs in the 1970's and most of the 1980's. ''Students did not feel compelled to finish early because there were no jobs waiting for them out there, and so there was greater security in being a graduate student.''
