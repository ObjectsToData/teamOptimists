Education; More Minority Foreign Affairs Specialists Is Goal of Lincoln University's New Institute

By LEE A. DANIELS, Special to The New York Times
Published: May 31, 1989

World capitals like Beijing, Moscow and Harare, Zimbabwe, seem far from the verdant rolling countryside surrounding Pennsylvania's Lincoln University, a leisurely hour's drive from Philadelphia.

But Lincoln is starting a new academic program, a Center for Public Policy and Diplomacy, to prepare undergraduates to take their first steps toward aiding economic and social development, doing business or conducting affairs of state in those places and many more.

The planned center will become the first international affairs program among historically black colleges in the country. Lincoln officials and foreign affairs experts say the center could give a boost to the efforts now under way to increase the number of minority group members in the United States Foreign Service.

That goal brought nearly 100 foreign policy specialists, including 11 current and former United States Ambassadors, to Lincoln's picturesque campus last week for two days of discussion with the 60 Lincoln students in a summer institute that is the university's pilot project for the center. Significant Gathering

Most foreign affairs experts present had served in Africa and said the event represented one of the most significant gatherings of American experts on Africa in two decades.

Also present were Terence A. Todman, the current Ambassador to Argentina; Charles William Maynes, editor of Foreign Policy Magazine, and others who spoke about world affairs and the need for greater minority involvement in international activity.

Elliott P. Skinner, the Franz Boas professor of anthropology at Columbia University, reminded the gathering that most of those present who were black Americans had come to the international affairs work ''by fortuitous and circuitous means.''

Now, said Professor Skinner, who served as Ambassador to Burkina Faso when it was called Upper Volta, their task is to develop' programs that do not leave minority involvement in international affairs to happenstance.

His remarks, like those of many participants in the course of the speeches, panel discussions and informal conversation, provided a virtual blueprint for the center's curriculum. Varied Training Urged

Professor Skinner emphasized that the Lincoln Center must enable students to study natural sciences and master specific technical skills as well as require them to study languages, social sciences and the humanities. And, he said, it must offer a curriculum that prepares students for work in the international private sector as well, both in multinational corporations and nonprofit relief and development organizations.

Niara Sudarkasa, Lincoln's new president and the first woman to hold that post in the school's 135-year history, pointed to a reinvigoration of the school's strong international tradition, with the new program joining the university's center for the comparative study of the humanities and academic programs in Russian, Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, Portuguese and Arabic languages.

Lincoln was the first American college to educate African students, and its many African alumni include Kwame Nkrumah, the first Prime Minister and President of Ghana, and Nnamdi Azikiwe, the first President of Nigeria.

Lincoln's program follows by a year a major conference sponsored by Georgetown University's Institute for the Study of Diplomacy on increasing minority participation in international affairs. Officials from Georgetown, Syracuse University's school of public affairs, as well as the State Department, were among those at last week's session. Schools Must Share Effort

''This is something where there needs to be a shared effort,'' said Ms. Sudarkasa. ''There's so much that needs to be done, none of the schools that now have international affairs programs can do it alone.''

The need was further sketched by Horace G. Dawson Jr., a Lincoln alumnus and a former ambassador to Botswana, who pointed out that only about 7 percent of Foreign Service workers are minority group members and that many of those have lower ranking positions.

In addition, Mr. Dawson, now director of the Office of Equal Employment Opportunity and Civil Rights at the United States Information Agency, said that as higher ranking blacks retire from the service, ''fewer and fewer such officers exist in the senior Foreign Service to provide replacements.''

''This is one of the tasks Lincoln must set about correcting,'' he said.

Yet, as Mr. Dawson's remarks indicated, the very scope of the discussion at the conference underscored the substantial challenge the school of 1,100 undergraduate and 200 graduate students is taking on.

As the conference moved toward its end and the suggestions for the Center piled up, participants like Manning Marable, director of the Black Studies program at Ohio State University, cautioned against unrealistic short-term expectations.

Ms. Sudarkasa agreed, saying that officials were planning a ''prudent, incremental expansion.'' She said they would expect to double the 60 students enrolled in the program by next summer, and then build slowly.
