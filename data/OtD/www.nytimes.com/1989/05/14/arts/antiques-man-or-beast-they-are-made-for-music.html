ANTIQUES; Man or Beast, They Are Made For Music

By RITA REIF
Published: May 14, 1989

Musical sculpture - slit gongs, horns, harps and drums in the shape of oxen, buffalo, horses, long-beaked birds or armless men - are silhouetted against the walls of the great hall at the Smithsonian's National Museum of African Art. These figural instruments include several that are monumental in scale and many more that convey movement or the music they were devised to bring alive.

The exhibition ''Sounding Forms: African Musical Instruments,'' is the first major survey of musical instruments as sculpture from sub-Saharan regions. According to Bryna Freyer, an assistant curator of the National Museum of African Art, it is also one of the largest traveling shows of African art ever assembled.

Organized over four years by the guest curator Marie-Therese Brincard of the American Federation of Art, the exhibition of 165 musical instruments and sculpture includes loans from 62 European, American, Australian and African museums, collectors and dealers. The show is to remain here through June 18.

The variety of forms is stunning, especially since most African musical instruments are not figural. ''Compared to masks and figures, sculptural instruments are not really predominant in Africa,'' Ms. Brincard said. ''They were mostly found in areas where there was a proliferation of forms and a strong tradition of music.''

Explanations for the symbolism and the sculptural meanings of some musical instruments are explored in the scholarly paperback catalogue of the exhibition ($35 paper), which Ms. Brincard edited. ''The human body is the ultimate source of musical articulation,'' writes Robert Farris Thompson, a professor of African and Afro-American art history at Yale University. ''Human figures in sculptural form on musical instruments, double the summoning in visual form. They stamp the instrument with social meaning.''

Some of the more memorable instruments come from one or two regions. Two spectacular zoomorphic slit gongs - a life-size ox and an even larger buffalo with swollen bellies and stout legs - were carved deeply enough by Ubangi artisans in Zaire to produce resonating roars. The drums of Zaire and Congo dominate as sculpture among more than 30 examples arranged on the gallery's largest stage. From Zaire's Luba-Shaba people comes a barrel-like drum boldly carved with a haunting masklike face so abstract it is impossible to know for sure whether it is man or beast. Another drum - a soaring six-foot-tall wooden tube from Congo - is ambiguous too. Its abstract form is an elongated human or animal form that seems purposely indeterminant in form and in the figural carving of its handle. From elsewhere - Guinea - comes an immense drum, a Baga carving more than four feet tall of a painted wooden horse bearing a drum on its back. Zaire's anthropomorphic harps are Surrealist conceptions by Mangbetu carvers who depict men with eager faces, long, pipelike bent necks and boldly carved legs. These armless creatures, poised as if about to dance, are akin to but more curious than the long-necked star of Alice's Adventures in Wonderland.

Also from Zaire comes a Tabwa zither topped by a carving of a wide-eyed man with piercing gaze, his arms covering the scarification on his chest and abdomen. The most haunting of these strummable instruments is a Tanzanian troughlike device from which emerges a small round head of a screaming bald man. Cheerier and rarer is an East African zither embellished on top with a pair of love birds, their beaks locked.

Whistles take many forms and are decorated with images of birds, beasts and humans. A fierce long-beaked bird with protruding eyes is one of several memorable Nuna flutes from Burkina Faso. Another is a diamond-shaped whistle, an abstract conception from base to neck - on which sits a grinning Janus head.

Among more than a score of sculptures that depict musicians are five Nigerian Benin bronzes of horn players, a Mali Dogon rendering of two musicians playing a zylophonelike balafon and an Ivory Coast gong mallet of a drummer by a Baule carver. From Zaire come several other arresting carvings of drummers including an exquisitely wrought image of a musician, bent and drumming, a figure that was a Mbala chief's charm.

Money for the exhibition comes from the National Endowment for the arts, the Federal Council on the arts and Humanities, Chevron U.S.A, the Laura Boulton Foundation and the New York branch of the Generale Bank. The show's year-long tour will include Richmond's Virginia Museum Fine Arts, the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art in Kansas City, Mo., and the Museum of African and Oceanic Art in Paris.

Although sculptural African instruments are uncommon in the market and in auctions, Bernard de Grunne, director of tribal art sales at Sotheby's, said that he tries to include one or more in each sale. The more important of two offered in Sotheby's tribal-art auction last Monday was a hollowed and tapered ivory trumpet from Cameroon embellished at its narrow tip with a carved figure of a man with robust features. The horn, which was expected to bring $8,000 to $12,000, did not sell. A crescent-shaped Kongo slit gong carved with human figures and animals, was expected to bring $700 to $1,000 and was sold for $5,225.

Merton D. Simspon, a New York dealer at 1063 Madison Avenue, near 81st Street, has several sculptural musical instruments in stock. A 28-inch- tall Congo drum is priced at $6,000. Also available are two Mangbetu zithers - one with a three-quarter figure costs $14,000 and the other with a long-necked figure is priced at $6,500.
