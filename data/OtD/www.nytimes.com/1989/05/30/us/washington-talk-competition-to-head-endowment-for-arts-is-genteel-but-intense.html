Washington Talk; Competition to Head Endowment for Arts Is Genteel but Intense

By BARBARA GAMAREKIAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: May 30, 1989

Cultural politics being as hot as any other kind here, rumors are flying about who will be named to head the National Endowment for the Arts, and when.

''I keep hearing the decision is going to come within the next few weeks,'' said Livingston Biddle, chairman of the Endowment in the Carter Administration.

''The latest I've been told is that something could happen in early June; I tried to check that out myself yesterday,'' Lois Burke Shepard, an anxious candidate who is winding up her job as director of the Institute of Museum Services here, said last week.

''My hunch is they will probably be doing something about it next week,'' Schuyler G. Chapin, a former dean of the School of the Arts at Columbia University who is another contender, said recently. More Names Solicited

''We've been hearing a lot of things,'' said Anne Murphy of the American Arts Alliance, a Washington lobbying group. ''Chase interviewed two people this week,'' she said Thursday, referring to Charles G. Untermeyer, director of Presidential personnel. ''And then they called this morning and asked us for a list of other potential candidates. My guess is that they are not clear just what sort of person they do want.''

As for the people at the Endowment itself, Hugh Southern, the deputy director who is acting chairman until a successor to Frank Hodsell, who left in February, predicts: ''We'll probably be the last to know.''

Mr. Untermeyer, who says he has been working round the clock on a number of Presidential appointments, confirmed that the White House was closing in on a decision. ''My staff has interviewed all the candidates and I've interviewed several personally and hope to complete those interviews next week,'' he said.

Among other visible contenders for the prized post, many of them mounting intensive campaigns, are Roger Horchow of Dallas, the creator of the Horchow Catalogue, who has served on the board of a number of cultural institutions; Marta Istomin, artistic director of the Kennedy Center; Barnabas McHenry, general counsel to Reader's Digest and co-vice chairman of the Reagan Administration's Committee on the Arts and Humanities, and Milton Rhodes, president of the American Council for the Arts. Lining Up Support

Mr. Horchow and Konrad Matthaei, former head of the American Shakespeare Theater in Stratford, Conn., who is said to have close ties to the President's brother Jonathan Bush, flew in for White House interviews this week.

''I had my first interview about a month ago by phone,'' Mr. Horchow, who calls himself ''a self-proclaimed candidate,'' explained from Dallas. ''I thought when I set out for this job that not too many people would be interested in it, but it's a hotly contested position and, I'm afraid, somewhat political. I read that one candidate had contacted 17 Senators.''

Indeed, supporters have written letters. Phone calls have been made. Mrs. Istomin, it is said, was personally escorted to her White House interview by Mr. Hodsoll. Mr. McHenry has been able to call upon old school ties, having roomed at Princeton with Secretary of State James A. Baker 3d. Mr. Chapin, it is reported, has the enthusiastic support of William F. Buckley, the columnist and a Bush family friend, and Mr. Rhodes has enlisted the support of a number of Senators and officers of corporations with ties to the arts.

''The big institutions like Schuyler very much,'' said one member of the art community here. ''His name seemed to disappear for a while but now I hear he's a contender again.'' May Be a Complete Surprise

Another advocate of the arts community here noted that ''Rhodes is well positioned because he comes out of community arts, he knows both the grassroots groups and the major arts institutions.''

And, of course, there are those who predict the appointee may be a complete surprise, someone whose name has not been bandied about as a top contender.

Capitol Hill is waiting for the phone to ring, too. ''I would think they would check it out with the ranking Republicans, Orrin Hatch in the Senate and Tom Coleman on the House authorizing committee, but Coleman hasn't heard anything,'' observed Ms. Murphy. ''I'm not sure that we will hear anything first,'' said Michael Lance, who works with Representative Thomas E. Coleman, a Missouri Republican, on educational and cultural matters.

Jan Naylor, formerly with the arts endowment, and Anne Gwaltney, formerly with the National Endowment for the Humanities, have conducted most of the interviews for Mr. Untermeyer's office. Prospective candidates report they are asked questions like: What do you see as the future prospects of Endowment? What are some of the main issues it faces? How would you guide the Endowment? What would be your priorities with limited appropriations? Thorny Question

Some luckless candidates have been asked how they would handle a recent controversy that has raised hackles on Capitol Hill. Along with the Rockefeller and Equitable Foundations, the endowment supported the Awards in the Visual Arts Program, administered by the Southeast Center for Contemporary Art, which selected a work of photography by Andreas Serrano that includes an image of Christ that has raised objections.

Mr. Biddle, no novice to arguments over aesthetic control and the politics of Federal money to the arts, has been consulted in the past few months by a number of the candidates.

Meanwhile, speculation continues as word is awaited on just who is to fill this country's most visible and influential cultural job, dispensing $150 million a year in grants to arts organizations and individual artists. The choice will be used as a crystal ball by the arts community as it tries to determine just what the Bush team has in mind for the arts.
