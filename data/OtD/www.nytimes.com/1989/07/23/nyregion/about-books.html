ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: July 23, 1989

WHEN Len Fulton, the editor of The International Directory of Little Magazines & Small Presses, began compiling a list of small publishing houses in 1965, his directory consisted of 40 pages with 250 listings. This year the directory has grown to 900 pages with 4,700 listings, including 156 in New Jersey.

''It's a trend that's continuing to grow, and New Jersey has followed the general profile of small press publishing,'' Mr. Fulton said.

Unlike large publishing houses, a small publishing concern with expertise in specialized fields - poetry, religion, crafts, foreign languages, technical or remedial writing, nutrition, self-help or satire - does not have to publish 100,000 copies of a book to make money. ''You can make money on a short run of even 1,000 or 500,'' Mr. Fulton said.

William J. Higginson and Penny Harter, owners of From Here Press in Fanwood, agree. ''Don't talk about profit,'' Ms. Harter said. ''But by bringing out only about 500 copies of a book of poems at a time, we've managed to break even for 14 years - and introduce new talent as well.''

New Jersey has become an especially attractive base for small publishers for a variety of reasons.

''It's happening in a big way here,'' said Joseph Barbato, who writes about small presses for Publishers Weekly, a magazine about book publishing, ''because you have the advantage of being able to do business in Manhattan - still the publishing capital - while avoiding both its distractions and tremendous overhead.''

The newer computer technology for desktop publishing, in sharply reducing the costs of printing books, has also contributed to the growth in small, independent publishers.

A course in desktop publishing offered at Union County College in Cranford has been ''very much in demand,'' said Mark Bala, a coordinator of management information services at the college.

''The course is advertised not only as a way of preparing newsletters, fliers, quality proposals, but also for making camera-ready copies for printing, and that is often the route for the average person starting a small press,'' Mr. Bala said.

A look at three small presses in New Jersey shows that along with possible financial savings, the reasons for their becoming small publishers are often as diverse as the books they publish.

''Why did I start a press in the wilds of New Jersey? Let's call the step an extraordinary hatching,'' said Robert Wechsler of Highland Park, the founder of the Catbird Press, a small publishing house that he has run out of his home since 1987.

''I'm one of the thousands of people you read about who annually abandon the law,'' he said. In 1983, he quit the Boston law firm where he specialized in bankruptcy law. He said he was unhappy about what he described as ''ethical problems.''

''So I quit law cold turkey,'' he said, ''married Jill Schneider and took a three-month honeymoon to Eastern Europe.''

''I had grown up in Pittsburgh, a city noted for the largest Slovak community outside of Czechoslovakia,'' he said, explaining his choice for the trip. He later ''began to think of writing a novel about a teen-ager and his Slovak-American grandmother that would incorporate folk tales, history and the contemporary scene,'' so he studied Czech.

''Jill was the only one with a job when we returned to America,'' Mr. Wechsler said, explaining how his wife's employment at Johnson & Johnson accounted for their move to central New Jersey. He took on what he calls ''an assortment of endeavors'' - writing the novel, attending a class in translation at Princeton University, writing for The Princeton Packet and then becoming the editor at the Princeton Architectural Press.

''All these roads enabled me to become a publisher of Czech literature in translation,'' he said. This month Catbird brings out a translation of ''Catapult,'' a picaresque novel by Vladimir Paral, one of Czechoslovakia's most popular authors.

''Being liberated enough to be able to learn all the time and to share that pleasure with others,'' Mr. Wechsler said, ''means finally sitting in the catbird seat, which is one way of explaining the name of my press.''

Raymond Smith is the co-publisher of the Ontario Review Press, which he and his wife and associate publisher, the novelist Joyce Carol Oates, founded in 1979 on the outskirts of Princeton.

Asked his response if a gift of unlimited funds was offered to his small press, Mr. Smith answered, ''Thanks, but no thanks.''

''I don't want to become just a businessman, because I love what I'm doing - reading, editing, helping to develop young writers,'' he said. ''If the press expanded, I would have to delegate much of this work to others and relinquish the close ties that I have with our authors.''

The devotion to belles-lettres that the couple espouse has long shaped their lives. Mr. Smith and Ms. Oates met in 1960 at the University of Wisconsin, where he was a doctoral candidate in English literature and she was pursuing a master's in the same field. Their goals realized, they married in 1961 and went on to teach, eventually at the University of Windsor in Ontario. In 1974, the couple founded The Ontario Review: A North American Journal of the Arts, a semiannual literary magazine for aspiring authors.
