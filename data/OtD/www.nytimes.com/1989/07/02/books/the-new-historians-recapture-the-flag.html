THE NEW HISTORIANS RECAPTURE THE FLAG

By MICHAEL KAZIN; Michael Kazin is the author of ''Barons of Labor: The San Francisco Building Trades and Union Power in the Progressive Era.'' He teaches history at American University.
Published: July 2, 1989

A century ago, some Americans celebrated the Fourth of July with gestures more controversial than the lighting of Roman candles. In Denver and San Francisco, radical unionists issued fierce ''declarations of independence'' from monopoly and the two-party system. In Worcester, Mass., working-class men - many of them recent immigrants - thronged downtown streets, passing around strong drink and harassing more privileged citizens with drums and firecrackers. One group of these workers even built a cannon and fired it outside the house of their factory's superintendent.

Americans have always been fond of proclaiming their patriotism, their feeling that the nation was and is a blessed exception in a world full of dictatorship and disorder. But they have clashed repeatedly over exactly what ''Americanism'' means and the political ends it should serve. The Declaration, the Constitution, the flag, and the Founding Fathers have long been employed to render legitimate a variety of opposing manifestoes and movements.

Over the past decade, young historians responsible for pushing social history to the forefront of their discipline in the late 1960's and 1970's have quietly been moving beyond their earlier passion for discovering how the common people lived. While not abandoning their interest in such topics as work and the family, they are increasingly exploring the more political aspects of history ''from the bottom up'' - specifically, the language in which an activist minority of Americans justified strikes, demonstrations and third parties, and how national holidays were used to express class and ethnic hostilities that were normally held in check.

Titles like ''Workingmen's Democracy,'' ''Liberty Under Siege,'' ''Chants Democratic'' and ''Working-Class Americanism'' convey the message. The authors of such works, most of whom belong to the sizable academic left, are staking a claim through their subjects to an alternative form of patriotism, one that uses the national ideology to oppose the wealthy and the powerful.

This shift is ironic. As undergraduates in the 1960's and early 1970's, these scholars were generally repelled by the public's fondness for flags and tales of national triumph. They would have agreed with Jane Fonda when she announced in 1970 that ''anyone who says patriotism is 'my country right or wrong' ought to have his head examined.'' But moving from chants of ''Power to the People'' to studying what the people actually believed compelled a change both sobering and liberating. The historical meaning of ''my country'' came to seem more open to multiple interpretations, less the natural possession of the right than a prize to be fought over.

In fact, historians have established that not until the era of World War I did ''Americanism'' become closely bound up with a fealty to the status quo. Then, prominent politicians and educators, made anxious first by the influx of millions of new immigrants and later by the transnational appeal of the Bolshevik Revolution, promoted the idea of ''citizenship training.'' In 1915, Theodore Roosevelt, a leader of this movement, told the Knights of Columbus that the duties of patriotism required giving up all other loyalties - to old country, ethnic group and class: ''The only man who is a good American is the man who is an American and nothing else.'' In the early 1920's, the American Legion got its start by using the slogan ''100 percent Americanism'' to crusade against unions and the left-wing press.

By 1927, every state in the nation was teaching its schoolchildren the essentials of ''citizenship.'' The current ubiquity of ''The Star-Spangled Banner'' and the Pledge of Allegiance obviously reflect the nationalistic ardor of many citizens, especially those who have fought or fervently supported the country's wars. But the original popularity of these two patriotic affirmations was the result of a conscious project to inoculate Americans against radical ideas and alien loyalties with the help of a few simple, unifying rituals. Today, conservatives seem to have cornered the market on patriotism. Oliver North told an approving television audience in the summer of 1987 that ''we must guard against a rather perverse side of American life, and that is the tendency to launch vicious attacks and criticism against our elected officials.'' And Ronald Reagan's chairwoman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, Lynne V. Cheney, together with former Secretary of Education William J. Bennett, has advocated teaching history by means of an Americanist canon to which, it is assumed, all citizens once subscribed.

To be sure, Mrs. Cheney's golden-age vision is not entirely myopic. In the late 19th century, most native-born Americans accorded the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution almost the same stature as Holy Scripture; and they probably were familiar with at least some of the words and deeds of Washington, Jefferson and Lincoln.

But as the leftist scholars are demonstrating, the sharing of icons did not necessarily represent a harmony of belief. Large numbers of wage-earners and small farmers employed terms like ''the republic'' and ''independence'' in ways that differed from the definitions favored by industrialists and established politicians. Although these dissenters never seriously challenged the rules and rulers of capitalist democracy, it is hard not to agree with the historian Daniel Rodgers that ''political talk in America . . . is not the story of a slowly unfolding tradition but of contention, argument, and power.''
