MARKETS DON'T HAVE TO BE SAVAGE

By PAUL BERMAN; Paul Berman, a fellow at the New York Institute for the Humanities, writes on politics and culture.
Published: July 16, 1989

SOCIALISM PAST AND FUTURE By Michael Harrington. 320 pp. New York: Arcade Publishing/Little, Brown & Company. $19.95.

Michael Harrington's ideas have always contained a bit of what the French call Molletisme, except in reverse. Guy Mollet was France's Socialist Prime Minister who, in the 1950's, spoke in great gobs of revolutionary rhetoric - and acted in little steps of unimpressive reform. Mr. Harrington's proposals enter the room with a polite cough. Yet if they were enacted with any kind of thoroughness, they would, over a period of years, transfer economic control from corporations to state and especially nonstate institutions that reflect the public as a whole. As ''Socialism Past and Future'' shows with splendid clarity, a grand-scale democratization is what he has in mind.

Mr. Harrington argues, along with Marx and Engels, that capitalism is ''a communal product.'' Earlier economic systems didn't require a vast coordinated activity by people and nations. Capitalism does, which is its genius. But who gets to coordinate this communal activity and on what basis?

The answer has so far come in two historic phases. In the phase of laissez-faire, individual capitalists managed their own businesses in their own interests while the ''invisible hand'' of market forces coordinated the different efforts. The result was great wealth, and great poverty, until poverty won and the system met its 1929. The second phase increased the system's coordination and democratized it by means of state regulation, social welfare and new power for nongovernmental forces like unions, in order to keep society stable and insure that workers could buy back the goods they produced. This was the period of ''Keynesian social democracy,'' which, Mr. Harrington observes, worked wonders for 40 years, until it, too, began to fall apart, starting in the 1970's.

Today, a third phase, not yet fully defined, is creeping upon us - a ''slow apocalypse,'' Mr. Harrington calls it. Will this new phase lean toward laissez-faire? In current circumstances that would mean global planning by transnational corporations. Or will there be a new kind of democratic control? Nothing is foreordained. The final shape will turn on political choices.

Mr. Harrington recognizes that in the present mood laissez-faire looms as the popular alternative. Many people have come to regard market savagery as nothing less than human nature. Every sort of social or economic setback is blamed on kindly governments and militant unions - even though, as Mr. Harrington observes, three of the industrial countries that fared best during recent crises were Sweden, Norway and Austria, social democratic homelands every one. Everything good, on the other hand, is credited today to unrestricted markets, for instance prosperity on the Pacific Rim - even though Rim development stems from authoritarian state centralism, and nothing laissez-faire about it.

Likewise the economic growth under President Ronald Reagan and Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher. Anglo-American prosperity flies the flag of freedom from government - yet depends, Mr. Harrington points out, on binge borrowing, military expenditures, government restrictions on unions and every kind of state activity except the sort that benefits the poor. And with what result? The Reagan-Thatcher boom, such as it is, will leave behind a wedding-cake society consisting of an elite on top, highly skilled workers in the middle and a huge underclass. This is a social structure that won't even be stable, let alone desirable.

Mr. Harrington's policy alternatives are, as he says, ''realistic, and even chastened'': state intervention in the form of retraining the work force and improving public education, a pragmatic mix of regulation and markets, restraint on leveraged buyouts and ''paper entrepreneurship,'' a no-delusions Marshall Plan for the third world that will slowly build our own exports and other measures. His view on markets - he is by no means hostile to them - may even lead some readers to surmise that capitalism has conquered Mr. Harrington, instead of the other way around. But markets, as he observes, are a mechanism for offering free choice in goods, jobs and investments. Their effect depends on social conditions. In a society of dreadful inequality, those who have nothing else won't have much in the way of market freedom, either. In a society of democratic planning and social equality, markets will, as Adam Smith hoped, make for freedom as well as for efficiency. All of which is to say that Mr. Harrington's incremental reforms and market theories do, as he has always shouted from the rooftops, add up to democratic socialism.

His argument is socialist in another way, too. Read Mr. Harrington and you find yourself exchanging comments with Karl Marx, Karl Kautsky, Rudolf Hilferding and other masters of classic socialism, with strategists of British Labor from the 1950's and French Socialism from the 1980's, with leaders of the Socialist International like Willy Brandt and Michael Manley, with theorists of Swedish Socialism and with the champions of ecology and feminism. In this way, Mr. Harrington's discussion of phases and alternatives manages also to summarize the long useful history of democratic socialist theory - errors, delusions, revisions, insights, Communist betrayals, practical triumphs and all.

Mr. Harrington's command of this material makes him a national resource. Yet he wears his learning with perfect modesty, turning to discuss Swedish business theories of 1911 with the ease of a man reaching across the table for a salad. ''Socialism Past and Future'' is an ambitious summary of Harringtonian ethics, economics and history, a weighty book, yet never less than lucid, calm, logical. It is a first-rate model for how to write a political essay.
