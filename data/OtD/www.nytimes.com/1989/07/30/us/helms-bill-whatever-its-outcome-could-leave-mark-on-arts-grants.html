Helms Bill, Whatever Its Outcome, Could Leave Mark on Arts Grants

By MICHAEL KIMMELMAN
Published: July 30, 1989

Senator Jesse Helms's move to bar Federal financing of ''obscene and indecent'' art, which sailed through a nearly deserted Senate on Wednesday evening, might not become law, some supporters acknowledge, but could have major influence anyhow.

The amendment by Mr. Helms, a North Carolina Republican, was the most aggressive step to impose restrictions on Government financing of culture since the National Endowment for the Arts was created in 1965. It has united arts advocates across the political spectrum behind the endowment and against the Senator.

But many politicians note that at a time when conservative organizations have been making headway with issues from abortion to flag burning, fallout from Mr. Helms's action and other attacks on the endowment might be felt even if the amendment never becomes law. Keeping Public in Mind

The endowment has been under fierce attack from conservatives for its grants supporting exhibitions of Robert Mapplethorpe's photographs of homosexual and sadomasochistic practices and of Andres Serrano's depiction of a plastic crucifix submerged in the artist's urine. The grants spurred the political reaction that helped lead to the Helms move.

One supporter of that move, Senator James A. McClure, an Idaho Republican who serves on the boards of the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts and Wolf Trap Farm Park for the Performing Arts, said he was ''not sure if the amendment will survive through the entire legislative process.'' The amendment is now attached to the $10.9 billion Interior Department appropriation bill and could be deleted when that bill comes to conference with the House.

But Mr. McClure said he saw the Helms amendment as a signal that ''the arts community has to recognize we work in a political context that has to be acceptable to the public.''

Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, Republican of New York, an early critic of the Mapplethorpe and Serrano exhibits, said he thought the Helms amendment ''went too far'' and would probably be dropped in the conference.

His Democratic colleague, Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan, advocated ''amused contempt'' toward the amendment. ''If we start saying that something horrendous has happened, we'll be giving them power they don't have,'' he said.

But many politicians said it might be difficult to speak out against a bill that opposes obscenity and indecency, since they might be labeled as advocates of ''sadomasochism,'' ''homoeroticism'' and ''the exploitation of children,'' which Mr. Helms condemns in the amendment.

Senator Ted Stevens, Republican of Alaska, said that if a copy of the catalogue for the Mapplethorpe exhibition is distributed to members of Congress ''the amendment will pass unanimously.'' 'Substitutue for Serious Policy'

Like Mr. Stevens, Hilton Kramer, editor of the arts journal The New Criterion, is critical of the endowment. He has been a leading opponent of endowment activities like giving funds to art critics and focusing too much on ''cuting-edge art.'' Nonetheless, he considers the Helms amendment ''undesirable and unworkable.''

''This is a substitute for a serious policy,'' Mr. Kramer said. ''It is not the policy we need. I think the most desirable change would be a policy that was designed to preserve the principal monuments of our civilization.''

John Brademas, the president of New York University, a former 11-term Congressman from Indiana who helped draft the legislation that created the National Endowment, said: ''The climate in Washington after the last 10 years has changed, and that is a large reason this has occurred now. I think many members respond to the pressure by saying, 'Who needs the aggravation?' I understand that, but I don't think it's acceptable under the circumstances.''

Jack G. Duncan, counsel to the American Council for the Arts, is a former staff director and counsel to the House Subcommittee on Postsecondary Education, which approved funds for the endowment. ''The times are much more conservative,'' he said, ''and so opposing these kinds of efforts by Helms and others become more difficult to deal with,''

Representative Pat Williams, a Montana Democrat who is chairman of that subcommittee, expressed worry that Congressional pressure from conservatives ''may be casting a chill of censorship'' over the arts.

When the endowment was founded in 1965, Mr. Williams noted, opposition in Congress came from conservatives who were concerned that Government officials would get involved in deciding which art to finance. ''The people who were originally concerned about Federal influence on the arts were what one might call Eisenhower Republicans,'' he said. ''It is ironic that it is the Reagan Republicans who are practicing this censorship.''

To rally support against the Helms amendment, arts advocates and sympathetic politicians have pointed to the growth of the arts across the country since the inception of the endowment; to the endowment's financing of research, art conservation and many other behind-the-scenes activities, and to its role in fostering private, state and local financing for the arts.
