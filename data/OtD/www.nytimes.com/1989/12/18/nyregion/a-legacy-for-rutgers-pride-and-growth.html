A Legacy for Rutgers: Pride and Growth

By ROBERT HANLEY
Published: December 18, 1989

For five years, Rutgers University has bustled with change as New Jersey has pumped millions of dollars into its state university in a carefully drawn plan to raise its image and the state's.

Construction is filling the woods and fields of once-peripheral campuses. A new thrust into high-technology research has begun with advanced work in fiber optics, ceramics and plant biology in research centers intended to benefit New Jersey's economy and environment. Admissions standards are up, the faculty is stronger, and Rutgers' national prestige is growing.

About the only big disappointment among top administrators is the football team's failure to do well with its strengthened big-college schedule and to earn a major bowl bid. Bloustein 'Left Footprints'

The architect of Rutgers' campaign to shed its second-class image was Dr. Edward J. Bloustein, who became president of the university in 1971 and guided it until his death on Dec. 9. And only last February did he achieve one dream, when Rutgers was invited to join the Association of American Universities, a group of 58 private and public universities that emphasizes high standards in doctoral studies and research.

Since Dr. Bloustein's death, his peers in higher education have praised his efforts to move Rutgers toward the upper echelons of the nation's public universities.

''Ed Bloustein was one of those rare educational leaders who left footprints,'' said Donna E. Shalala, chancellor of the University of Wisconsin. ''He moved Rutgers a giant step toward becoming a major public university in this country. He knocked heads. He pulled people along.

''It is not in the first rank,'' she said, ''but it's moving in that direction and becoming an increasingly important institution.''

Stanley O. Ikenberry, president of the University of Illinois, said, ''Rutgers has made more progress over the last 20 years than any other public institution I can think of.''

Though spending priorities and the new emphasis on research have troubled some professors and students, Rutgers officials say the direction set by Dr. Bloustein is firmly entrenched.

''Everybody is committed to this program,'' said Michael Bongiovanni, chairman of Rutgers' board of governors. ''It's hard for me to see how somebody's going to sidetrack it.''

Like Gov. Thomas H. Kean, Dr. Bloustein chafed at so-called ''Jersey jokes'' and at the state's - and the university's - weak self-image and sense of being overshadowed by New York City and Philadelphia, associates say.

''He hated the syndrome of being willing to settle for second best,'' said Donald B. Edwards, Rutgers' vice president for development and public affairs. ''He felt New Jersey and Rutgers were shortchanging themselves in terms of ambition.''

Change was considerable in the Bloustein years. Growth Across the River

A $550 million building campaign, the biggest in the university's 223-year history, is concentrated heavily in the 1,700 acres of the once-isolated Busch and Kilmer campuses, the sites of Livingston College, the School of Business, and the College of Engineering.

Busy Route 18 and the Raritan River separate both campuses from the compact 64-acre downtown campus where Rutgers was founded in 1766 and administrative offices are now situated.

The expansion across the river has created an atmosphere of sprawl and forced many students to shuttle from dormitory to classroom by bus.

At the heart of the building boom are the new centers for advanced research.

One center is seeking new uses for ceramics - extremely hard, heat-resistant materials - in computers, auto engines and aerospace equipment. Rutgers officials say they believe a $10 billion worldwide market for advanced ceramics will exist by 2000. Fiber Optics for Surgery

Another is exploring applications of fiber optics - the hair-thin glass strands that transmit light at great speed - to manufacturing and medicine, including their use to destroy cancers without surgery and burn plaque from clogged arteries.

A new center in agricultural molecular biology is working on the development of plant varieties less dependent on chemical fertilizers and pesticides. Others are seeking advances in plastics recycling, food technology, biotechnology and medicine, and are analyzing coastal overdevelopment and diseases of salt-water fish and shellfish.

Corporations help underwrite most centers. Their donations will allow them to set some research agendas and later apply the results to new products. Such marketplace research is increasingly common at major universities, education experts say. Tougher Admissions Standards

Under toughened admissions standards, the average Scholastic Aptitude Test score of freshmen at Rutgers College, the university's oldest and most select school, is 1,169. On all campuses here and in Newark and Camden, the average now is 1,098, up 57 points since 1984.

In the same period, the average national S.A.T. score has increased 6 points, to 903 from 897, said Elizabeth Mitchell, director of admissions.

Despite the improved scores and the fact that 88 percent of undergraduates are state residents, Ms. Mitchell said the university remains disturbed by the continued migration of the state's best high school graduates to private colleges outside New Jersey.
