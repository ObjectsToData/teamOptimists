History on the Run

By JAMES MacGREGOR BURNS; JAMES MacGREGOR BURNS, the political historian, is a fellow at the Humanities Center of Williams College.
Published: December 31, 1989

SOMEWHERE between Hyde Park Corner and Buckingham Palace, London, around noon, May 27: I am changing clothes in a London taxi, in mixed company, while my companions politely look aside, and the cabby threads his way through crowds around the palace with a look on his face that says, ''How in Heaven's n-a-i-me did I ever get into this?'' I am trying to run a historic mile in each of four cities from Leningrad to Washington in one day, and London is the second stop.

I concocted this crackbrained idea many months earlier when I read about a man who had managed to swim in the Thames in the morning and the Hudson in the afternoon. Very good, I thought, but why only two cities? If you could fly west with the sun, and during a time of longest daylight, why not three cities in one day? Or even four?

It happened that I was scheduled to be co-chairman of a conference at the Institute of Psychology in Moscow and then meet with historians in Leningrad. I calculated that I would have about 26 hours to fly to London and New York. I could then arrive in Washington before dark.

As my plans grew, so did my motivation. As a historian of freedom in America I was dismayed that so little attention was being paid to the bicentennial of the Bill of Rights (passed by Congress in September 1789; ratified by the states in December 1791).

I feared that American officials were being less than responsive to glasnost and perestroika in the Soviet Union. As a peace activist I saw a chance to do a little bridge-building. So I would carry peace messages from Americans to Russians, to be handed out on the run. I would wear a placard in both languages - ''Peace and Freedom.''

I spawned other reasons. In each city I would run from a place that symbolized an older regime to one that represented a newer regime closer to the needs of the people. And having just turned 70, I didn't mind showing that you can be a septuagenarian and still shake a leg.

Then hard reality intruded, of a kind that reminded me of Herbert Spencer's reference to a beautiful dream murdered by a brutal gang of facts. Among them: airline schedules, volatile spring weather, plane connections. My travel agent, Richard Pierce, bravely tackled the airline schedules on his computer. But even his face paled when he discovered that I had to change planes at Charles de Gaulle Airport. ''Fog,'' he muttered. ''Traffic. Security checks. Strikes.''

The most difficult logistics were not intercity but intracity transportation, delays such as traffic and finding a cab. I would have to get from Heathrow into central London and back in three hours, assuming passport and traffic delays, and then do the same from Kennedy International Airport to lower Manhattan and back to La Guardia before the last shuttle to Washington.

Even so, Leningrad loomed as the biggest hurdle. What would the police think of an American loping along in running garb and handing out private communications? How to forestall an ''incident''? Since my Moscow conference cochairman, Prof. Patricia Hunt-Perry of Ramapo College, was planning to travel to Russia the week before I did, she volunteered, between bursts of laughter, to initiate arrangements in Leningrad. There she turned to my longtime friend, Aleksandr Fursenko, head historian in the Leningrad branch of the Institute of U.S.S.R. History. He agreed to help, obtained the use of a car and then cleared matters with police headquarters. He also suggested a change. Instead of running at 4 A.M., leaving time to catch the 7 A.M. plane to Paris, I should go shortly after midnight. Leningraders would be celebrating the beginning of their famed ''white nights'' by strolling along the Neva River, and I would have recipents for my messages. 12:30 A.M. Leningrad: Patricia, Alex, the driver and I arrive at a corner just north of the Winter Palace shortly after midnight. Two policemen stand there, and a long delay ensues as my Leningrad friends confer with them. Then all break into smiles - yes, word miraculously has come down from headquarters. All is ''O.K.''

Soon I am running down the sidewalk in front of the Winter Palace, with Patricia hooting at me from the car as it drives along next to me, a huge police van following us, and the stolid citizens of Leningrad looking at me with mixed incredulity and amusement. But many accept my messages.

I run almost effortlessly on the uncrowded sidewalk. Then I turn the corner to my destination, the Lenin Museum, where a small crowd has gathered, along with more police. Once again handshakes all around, once again all O.K.

TO bed at 2 A.M. and up two hours later to catch the 5 A.M. bus to the airport. The flight to Paris is uneventful, and then there's a surprise: de Gaulle is quick and quiet.

Until we are moving to our gates. A chorus of whistles fills the large hall, and gendarmes pour in and herd us to the other end. Word circulates that there is an unaccompanied suitcase in the men's lavatory. As a longtime traveler, and skeptical of such rumors, I doubt even more the next information, which is that the police have found the suitcase and are going to blow it up. Ten minutes later, there is a loud ''boom'' close by. 10 A.M. London: Passport control at Heathrow is fairly expeditious. Then the question, long debated - tube (slow), taxi (possibly as slow, and expensive) or airport bus (equally slow)? Decision: tube. We begin to rue it, though, when (1) the queue for tube tickets takes an agonizing 10 minutes or more, and (2) our luggage carrier jams in the turnstile, costing more minutes as London Underground officials fix it. We emerge half an hour later at Hyde Park Corner and flag down the cabby.
