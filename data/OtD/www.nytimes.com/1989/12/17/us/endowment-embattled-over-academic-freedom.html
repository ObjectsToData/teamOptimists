Endowment Embattled Over Academic Freedom

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: December 17, 1989

New rules intended to force stricter supervision of grants by the National Endowment for the Humanities have led to protests about infringement of academic freedom.

One major research center, the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton, has said it will forgo two Federal grants rather than comply with the new rules.

The rules were imposed after Congress voted limitations on the endowment's appropriations because of unhappiness over grants for art works that many lawmakers found offensive. Rule on Subgrants Set Out

Lynne V. Cheney, chairwoman of the Endowment, set out the new rules on Nov. 13, telling recipients of direct grants how they must handle subgrants, or financial awards to people or organizations made by institutions that receive money from the Endowment.

Congress became concerned about subgrants after it was learned that the exhibition of a photograph by Andres Serrano, a plastic crucifix immersed in urine, had been supported by funds from the National Endowment for the Arts that had been channeled through a subgrant. An exhibit by Robert Mapplethorpe including a depiction of homosexual acts, which was financed through a direct grant, also offended many members of Congress.

In October, Congress passed an appropriations bill requiring the National Endowment for the Humanities and the National Endowment for the Arts to ''amend their procedures and guidelines'' so that agency chairmen and advisory councils would be ''as thoroughly informed and responsible for the subgrants as they are for direct grants.''

The new rules, Mrs. Cheney wrote, would require the submission of ''lists of proposed subgrants'' for review by the Endowment, as well as ''visits to selection committee meetings in all subgrant programs'' by staff members of the Endowment.

Some scholars have reacted strongly to Mrs. Cheney's effort to impose this procedure. Rules Are Called 'Intrusion'

''We feel the new rules represent an unwarranted, unreasonable and largely unmanageable intrusion on fundamental matters of academic autonomy and integrity,'' said Marvin L. Goldberger, director of the Institute for Advanced Study.

Mr. Goldberger said that after consulting with faculty members, he wrote to Mrs. Cheney on Dec. 6, asking her to reconsider the procedure and observing, ''It is ironic that as Eastern European societies become more open in all areas of life, the Congress and the N.E.H. tighten and centralize the control of scholarly research.''

He received a reply from Mrs. Cheney a day or two later, he said, in which she said that although she hoped to ''make every effort'' to ''accommodate the circumstances of subgranting organizations,'' the Endowment could not ignore the wishes of Congress.

On Dec. 14, Mr. Goldberger called David Coder, a research-program officer at the Endowment, explaining that the institute would not comply with the new procedure and was therefore prepared to forgo two grants, totaling about $220,000, that were made by the Endowment but not yet received.

The institute, a center for advanced scholarly research in mathematics, the natural sciences, historical studies and the social sciences, has also applied for some $550,000 in future grants. Since decisions about those grants are not due to be made until next December, Mr. Goldberger said, ''We would like to maintain our application in the hope that they will change their rules.''

No other recipient, Mrs. Cheney says, has refused compliance or rejected a grant because of the new rules. But the directors of several scholarly organizations say they are disturbed about them and plan to take up the matter with their trustees in the near future. Nothing 'Pernicious'

In an interview Friday, Mrs. Cheney said: ''Were we not to do this, the whole subgranting procedure is going to go down the tubes. The Congress raised the question of whether we should be doing it at all, and said if it's going to be done it has got to be done this way. On our own we would not have undertaken this increased responsibility, but I don't see anything pernicious in it.''

Asked about the response to her letter of Nov. 13, she said, ''The expressions of concern were much less than the expression of the desire to make the whole thing work.'' She cited as an example a letter from Thomas A. Graves Jr., the director of the Henry Francis du Pont Winterthur Museum in Delaware.

In his letter dated Dec. 1, Mr. Graves said that ''we are not entirely in agreement with this Congressionally mandated procedure,'' but assured Mrs. Chaney that ''we will cooperate fully with your instructions.''

W. Robert Connor, director of the National Humanities Center in Research Triangle Park, N.C., a private, independent institution involved in disciplines like history, philosophy and foreign languages, called the situation ''a bad dream for us.''

He said he had written to Mrs. Cheney on Nov. 29 expressing ''quite serious concerns about the presence of an observer during the selection of our entire list of fellows, most of whom are supported by non-N.E.H. funds.''

''Under other circumstances,'' he wrote, ''under another Administration with different staff or in an inflamed political climate, one could imagine that the presence of government observers in a private institution might produce results which none of us can fully foresee.'' 'There Is No Problem Now'

''My rejoinder to that would be, 'When there's a problem, then solve that problem,' '' Mrs. Cheney said. ''But there is no problem now. So let's work our way through this procedure and keep the subgranting process alive.''

Philip Converse, director of the Center for Advanced Study of the Behavioral Sciences at Stanford, Calif., a private foundation that awards about 50 fellowships a year in the behavioral sciences, said he was ''sufficiently concerned'' about Mrs. Cheney's letter to visit the Endowment on Nov. 30 and request an explanation. Letter of Regret

Stanley N. Katz, president of the American Council of Learned Societies, an organization chartered by Congress in 1982 to represent the humanities nationally and internationally, said he had written to Mrs. Cheney expressing ''regret'' over the new rules.

''We are going to monitor the results of the new procedure carefully,'' he said, ''and if worthy candidates are being turned down we will alert Congress and get the law rewritten.''

Richard H. Brown, academic vice president of the Newberry Library in Chicago, an independent research institution, said that although the desire of Congress to exercise oversight on scholarship had ''ominous implications for the future,'' he could not object to the specific procedures outlined in Mrs. Cheney's letter.

''Given the attitude of Congress,'' Mr. Brown said, ''the N.E.H. handled it as well as it could be handled.'' The Newberry Library receives $140,000, or about one-third of its annual budget for fellowships, from the humanities endowment.
