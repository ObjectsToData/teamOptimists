Review/Art; A Show in Minneapolis Offers History Behind Designs

By MICHAEL KIMMELMAN, Special to The New York Times
Published: December 20, 1989

One of the most ambitious exhibitions in recent months is ''The American Craftsman and the European Tradition'' at the Minneapolis Institute of Arts.

Although ostensibly a display of American and European furniture, silver, glass and ceramics from 1620 to 1820, it is much more. Differences between one chest of drawers and another are not demonstrated simply to document shifts in taste over two centuries, but to support the broader theme that is the real purpose of this exhibition.

Organized by Francis J. Puig, a former curator at the museum, and by Michael Conforti, its chief curator, ''American Craftsman'' aims to show that contrary to what many scholars and other experts in the decorative arts have long maintained, American design did not develop independently from Europe but evolved very much out of European sources.

Whether it was a chest from New Mexico that owed its patterning to Spanish precedents or a commode from the upper Mississippi River Valley that copied French models, American decorative arts depended on styles exported to the colonies by European artisans and their followers.

Which styles were preferred by the colonists, whether they were up to the minute with the latest fashions in London or whether they reflected the tastes of a previous generation, are among the questions the exhibition sets out to address. And the answers, in turn, provide fodder for bigger topics.

Similarities between American and British glassware, for example, become part of a discussion about the political and economic forces brought to bear on the colonists by the Townshend Acts of 1767, under which the British imposed a heavy tax on the importation of such materials as glass, lead and paint. American glass manufacturing thrived as a result of those taxes, with manufacturers like H. W. Stiegel in Pennsylvania campaigning to ''Buy American'' and producing sugar bowls, goblets and other objects in English styles to accommodate the tastes of buyers who would not pay increased taxes for imports.

In the case of cabinetry from Connecticut, research on the economics of that state is used to demonstrate that the rise of a nouveau riche population, which profited from the sale of agricultural and other goods to the Revolutionary Armies, explains a surge in the production of elaborate desks and chairs and other furniture.

''The American Craftsman,'' in other words, concerns itself as much with American social history as with the history of decorative arts. It includes more than 100 objects drawn from public and private collections across the country and in Europe. The works have been divided into 11 sections. There are sections not only on the decorative arts in New Mexico and the Mississippi River Valley but also on silver from Boston, furniture from Philadelphia, Baltimore and Connecticut and porcelain and glassware from Pennsylvania.

Everywhere, American works have been juxtaposed with related objects from France, Spain, Germany, England or the Netherlands, and, because so much of the furniture is unsigned, every effort has been made to identify specific artisans and workshops. Altogether, a great deal of ground is covered by ''American Craftsman,'' and the opinions and research of more than a dozen scholars can be found in the catalogue.

There are, it must be said, parts of the exhibition that suffer from a kind of pedantry characteristic of such social-history shows. A good proportion of the exhibition depends on arguments made in the catalogue that are otherwise impossible to derive from simply looking at the objects. Unusually lengthy wall labels, including an introduction and conclusion, serve at once as an aid and a burden for the viewer.

Still, there are a good number of objects that can be appreciated entirely on their own. It would be difficult to find a more fantastical piece of 18th-century sculpture than the Masonic master's chair from Williamsburg, Va., with its symbol-laden backrest made of masons' tools and decorated with a crescent moon and gilded sun.

From New Mexico there is a rough-hewn chest on four legs, adorned with geometric patterns, that has a monumentality all out of proportion to its modest size. For boldness of design, there is nothing quite like the serpentine chest on cabinet from Baltimore, which mixes diamonds and ovals to create an extraordinary abstract facade. And for sheer elegance of execution, there are few objects to equal a high chest of drawers from Philadelphia topped with an elaborately carved bust of a woman, at one time thought to be Mme. Pompadour.

Scholars will obviously have much to ponder and debate in ''American Craftsman.'' But even those who may not know what distinguishes an armoire from a commode will find something to mull over here.

''The American Craftsman and the European Tradition'' remains at the Minneapolis Institute of Arts through December, and can be seen at the Carnegie Institute in Pittsburgh from April 14 to June 10. The show is supported by the National Endowment for the Humanities.
