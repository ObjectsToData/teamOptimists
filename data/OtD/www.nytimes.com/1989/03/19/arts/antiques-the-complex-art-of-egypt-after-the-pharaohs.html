ANTIQUES; The Complex Art Of Egypt After the Pharaohs

By RITA REIF
Published: March 19, 1989

The imagery of copticart - owl-eyed women, plump birds, exotic beasts, pagan mythological figures and Christian symbols - comes alive in highly expressive tapestries, artifacts and stone sculpture produced in Egypt under the Romans and Byzantines. Long overlooked by scholars and museum curators, this early Christian material is now the subject of an ambitious exhibition here at the Rhode Island School of Design's Museum of Art through April 16.

More than 200 examples of weaving, household objects, gravestones, calligraphy and architectural elements have been assembled in ''Beyond the Pharaohs: Egypt and the Copts in the Second to the Seventh Centuries A.D.,'' described by the show's organizers as the first comprehensive presentation of Coptic artifacts held anywhere during the last 25 years.

''Many people thought of this period as decadent - a low point in the culture,'' said Florence Friedman, the curator of antiquities at the museum, who organized the show. ''I wanted to illustrate that this was a thriving period in which beautiful works of art appeared.''

Mrs. Friedman explained that Copt - a corruption of the Pharaonic name for Egyptian - is the term used to designate a native of the land of the Nile descended from its earliest inhabitants. Most Copts were Christians during the period covered in this exhibition. Their art reflects in its stylistic complexity the rich cultural interplay of Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Byzantine and Christian influences, which were very much alive in this ancient land in the centuries before it was fully occupied by Moslem Arabs in A.D. 642.

Many works in the show depict pre-Christian legends, demonstrating that pagan themes continued to coexist with Christian ones throughout this period. By the fifth century A.D., the ancient Egyptian and Greco-Roman gods and heroes were no longer treated as major themes in Egyptian art but simply used decoratively. This change is seen in several tapestry fragments depicting, with mechanical virtuosity, a familiar labor of Heracles and several horned satyrs at a Dionysiac festival.

The majority of the selections on view, however, incorporate Christian motifs - Virgin and Child, Christ, crosses, haloed saints - or a blend of pagan and Christian images. The popular use of amulets was a polytheistic tradition that lingered and sometimes combined old and new religious themes. A silver armband on exhibit is a case in point. It is embellished with four amulet-medallions: one shows Christ riding on a donkey; another, the Greco-Egyptian lion-headed snake, Chanoubis.

Although Christian monasticism is thought to have begun in Egypt, the art presented here emphasizes naturalism rather than asceticism. Peacocks carved on two sixth-century combs are typically robust and vivid images of this colorfully plumed bird, conveying nature's splendor and, by traditional association, the spiritual meaning of immortality. Boldly carved stonework - architectural reliefs chiseled with geometric motifs, staves and crosses, birds eating grapes and imposing eagles framed in leaves - is displayed in a gallery with a full-size replica of a monastery chapel, an unexpected ersatz touch.

Mrs. Friedman said that she and 10 other writers sought to achieve a scholarly reassessment of Coptic art in their essays for the show's 300-page catalogue ($30 paperback). ''The overall conclusion was that the art of Roman-Byzantine Egypt is expressing the artistic vocabulary of the Roman Empire, rather than any specifically Egyptian style,'' she said.

The objects exhibited - most are decorative - come from 38 museums and private collections, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art, Boston's Museum of Fine Arts, the British Museum, the University of Michigan's collections and the Walters Art Gallery in Baltimore, where the exhibition will be from May 21 to July 16. Money for the show was provided by the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Rhode Island State Council on the Arts.

Coptic textiles, objects and small sculptures appear from time to time in specialty galleries and at auctions. The largest stock in Manhattan is at L'Ibis Gallery, 23 East 67th Street, where Lucien Viola, the owner, offers an imposing fifth-century stone niche carved with angels. The three-foot-square relief is $100,000.

Among the larger textiles at L'Ibis are two brilliantly hued wall hangings woven in the fifth or sixth century, each depicting a nude Dionysiac figure holding an ear of corn. Each panel measures about 29 by 22 inches and is $60,000. A fifth-century red and green tapestry awash with marine images and flowers measures 25 by 20 inches and is $55,000. Smaller textile fragments include a sixth-century, 10-inch-square weaving of a man, possibly a Nubian, in a yellow cape, for $15,000, and a seventh-century, 11-inch roundel depicting a running panther with a flaming tail, for $14,000.

The Merrin Gallery, 724 Fifth Avenue (at 57th Street), has a Coptic fourth-century relief on which are carved two angels and the head of St. John the Baptist on a platter; the wood panel is $12,000. A tapestry almost six feet square, woven sometime between the fifth and seventh centuries, depicts in thick pile and rich colors six bearded men, possibly apostles, wearing robes decorated with crosses. Above and below these stylized figures are animal images; the price is $125,000. Merrin also stocks such smaller fragments as a third- to fifth-century weaving of Dionysus riding an ass, at $3,500.

Among the Coptic offerings handled at Sotheby's in recent years, textile fragments are the most numerous and least costly, ranging from about $425 to $4,000. The limestone sculptures auctioned there have included a comic mask, which brought $1,320, the head of a smiling youth, which was $605, and an architectural fragment carved with acanthus leaves and bellflowers, which went for $4,400.
