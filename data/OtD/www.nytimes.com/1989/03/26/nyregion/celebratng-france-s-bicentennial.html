Celebratng France's Bicentennial

By CAROLYN BATTISTA
Published: March 26, 1989

MUSICAL performances, talks by scholars and French citizens, an exhibit of quilts and other programs in Connecticut will celebrate the 200th anniversary this year of the French Revolution.

The programs, which are free and open to the public, are being sponsored by the Connecticut Committee for the Commemoration of the Bicentennial of the French Revolution. As part of the celebration in France, the Wadsworth Atheneum in Hartford has sent more than 50 works of American art dating to the 18th century to be exhibited at the Galeries Lafayette in Paris from Wednesday through April 29.

The Atheneum collection includes works by Winslow Homer, Ralph Earl and Georgia O'Keeffe. Seven quilts, including an Amish one that was recently donated to the museum, will also be on display At the West Hartford campus of the University of Connecticut on Friday, the bicentennial committee and the Connecticut Humanities Council will sponsor a symposium on ''The Impact of the French Revolution on the Modern World.'' A conference there on April 28 will be devoted to ''The French Revolution in the Arts and in Contemporary Life.'' Both will include varied viewpoints and feature the rousing music of the revolution.

At the symposium, scholars and teachers will focus on a ''schizoid revolution,'' said Marvin R. Cox, chairman of the organizing committee and an associate professor of history at the University of Connecticut at Storrs.

Professor Cox, whose specialty is the French Revolution, said it was often viewed as the precursor of totalitarian revolutions like those of China and Russia. But, he said, the French Revolution played a major role in bringing democracy to the Western world, even though it brought the infamous Reign of Terror and a dictatorship to France.

''We planted the idea, but we were in the boonies,'' Professor Cox said. ''The French made democracy a practical idea for Western civilization.''

To integrate high school and college teaching, the symposium will include presentations by scholars and commentary by Connecticut high school teachers. ''The concepts of the French Revolution - its liberal ideas - had a great influence,'' said Stephen Armstrong, a history teacher at Manchester High School, who believes that American high school programs should focus more on the French Revolution.

Professor Cox will discuss the work of the historian R. R. Palmer. ''Palmer says the French Revolution 'established liberal democracy at the heart of Western civilization','' he said. Women's Rights

Harriet Applewhite, a political science professor at Southern Connecticut State University, and Darline Levy, a history professor at New York University, will talk about the revolution and women's rights. Although the first French constitutions did not recognize women's rights, Professor Applewhite noted, ordinary working women demanded the right to bear arms in 1792. The women became involved in militant ceremonies and actions and insisted on their right to defend themselves and their country.

The impact of the French Revolution on the Middle East will be covered by Dr. Mounir Farah, head of the social studies department at Masuk High School in Monroe and a lecturer at Western Connecticut State University. ''The revolution was a factor in the rise of nationalism in that area,'' Dr. Farah said.

Prof. Robert LeBrun of St. Paul's College at the University of Manitoba will discuss the influence of the revolution in Canada. ''The traditional belief is that there wasn't much influence in Canada, but more recent scholarship shows that there was,'' he said. Other speakers are also scheduled. Songs of Revolution

The symposium and the conference will include musical programs prepared by Marsha Hogan of the Hartford Camerata Conservatory and will feature the Renaissance Revival Singers of Storrs. Mrs. Hogan said that she found ''lots of music'' representing the turbulent years of the revolution. Much of it ''is very raucous, with ribald lyrics,'' she said.

The singers will perform the ''Marseillaise'' and the ''Ca Ira,'' songs that spurred the revolutionaries, as well as a tune from a 1780's opera that was popular with royalists. ''They would sing it whenever they heard ''Ca Ira,'' Mrs. Hogan said.

She also plans to include music of the court of Louis XVI. ''Mozart was very popular,'' she said. And, while Louis's wife, Marie Antoinette, was highly unpopular, Mrs. Hogan intends to present a work by a fan of the queen.

''We found a piece called 'The Suffering of the Queen of France.' It was written after she was beheaded,'' she said. Written by a Czech composer, the piece has many stanzas, with such titles as ''The Queen Says Goodbye to Her Children,'' ''The Queen Mounts the Scaffold'' and ''The Guillotine Drops.''

Besides the music, the April 28 conference will include a presentation on ''Paintings and Politics: the Art of the French Revolution'' by Rhea Higgins, a lecturer at Wesleyan University. ''It's so revolutionary to overturn a class system entrenched for hundreds of years,'' Mrs. Higgins said. ''The images have to be radical, and they really are. It's a new iconography for the new religion of republican ideology.''

Mrs. Higgins said she would present both fine art and political caricatures of the revolutionary period. She plans to focus on Jacques-Louis David, the leading artist of the revolution who was commissioned to do paintings of major figures and events. His ''Death of Marat,'' for instance, depicts Jean-Paul Marat, considered a martyr of the revolution after he was murdered in his bathtub.

The conference will also feature two French visitors - Michele Le Menestrel, who will describe what the revolution means to women, and Claude Braillard, who will discuss what it means to men. ''I want to hear what they think, what people really know, compared to what professors tell people,'' Professor Cox said.

On April 25, the Alliance Francaise of Hartford will sponsor ''Chansons Revolutions ou l'Esprit de 1789,'' featuring the French folk singer Serge Kerval. Mr. Kerval is to perform revolutionary and counter-revolutionary music at the 7:30 p.m. concert in Auerbach Auditorium at the Hartford College for Women.

The bicentennial commemoration will also include an Exhibition of Contemporary French and American Quilts from June 19 to Aug. 18 at the Homer Babbidge Library at the UConn campus in Storrs. For more information on the bicentennial, call 486-2253 or 486-3722.
