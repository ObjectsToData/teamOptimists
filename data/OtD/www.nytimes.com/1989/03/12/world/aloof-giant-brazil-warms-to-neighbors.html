Aloof Giant, Brazil Warms To Neighbors

By ALAN RIDING, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 12, 1989

Displaying the daring shapes of the country's most famous architect and the idealistic vision of a well-known anthropologist, a $50 million monument has been built to a subject that interests few Brazilians: Latin America.

But the purpose of Sao Paulo's new Latin American Center, with its library, museum, auditorium, conference rooms and restaurant, is to change just that - to make Brazilians turn their eyes away from the United States and Europe and take notice of their immediate neighbors.

''Over the years, we have been very separate,'' said Orestes Quercia, the governor of Sao Paulo State who ordered construction of the project. ''Above all, we Brazilians have been separate from the others. I think the center can awaken curiosity and help us understand the benefits of closer ties.'' Center Faces Big Challenge

The cultural complex, which will be inaugurated March 18, nonetheless faces a heady challenge. For the best part of five centuries, after a brief period of competing for territory, the relationship between Brazil and the rest of Latin America has been marked largely by indifference, as if their common Iberian heritage were purely accidental.

For example, to this day, no educated Brazilian would dream of learning Spanish before English and French or visiting Buenos Aires before New York or Paris. Music, art and movies from Spanish America are rarely available here. Even news from the continent is given minimal display by local newspapers.

The reverse is less true. While the Portuguese language and the vast Amazon rain forest are barriers to closer ties, the 10 nations bordering on Brazil have a greater need to know what the largest, most populous and economically most powerful nation in the region is up to. But they too view this country as strangely isolated. 'A Cruel Mistake'

''Latin America is the most homogeneous area on earth, the only truly homogeneous bloc,'' noted Darcy Ribeiro, the anthropologist who developed the concept of the Latin American Center. ''Yet Brazil and Spanish America have finished up divided into two worlds, back to back to each other. This is the story of a cruel mistake.''

In reality, thanks to their common difficulties in consolidating democracy and servicing huge foreign debts, Brazil and its neighbors have in recent years moved slowly toward each other. Brazil's President, Jose Sarney, for example, has tried to visit all civilian-ruled countries of the region, while Brazil and Argentina are inching toward economic integration.

Closer ties between the United States and Canada and the prospect of a united Europe after 1992 have also made Latin American nations aware of their own differences. ''If the Europeans, which have stronger economies than ours, can join forces in a community, we have all the more reason to do the same thing,'' Governor Quercia argued. Role of Center Unclear

How the Latin American Center fits in, however, is less immediately clear. Sao Paulo newspapers, themselves little interested in the third world, have questioned the enormous cost of Oscar Niemeyer's sculptural architecture. Some intellectuals have also criticized the center as a political stepping stone for Mr. Quercia, who is known to harbor presidential ambitions.

Yet the Governor has pointed to his introduction of Spanish into the high school curriculum in Sao Paulo as evidence of his genuine interest in Latin America.

Certainly, the center has been situated next to a major subway and bus terminal in such a way that it is easily reachable. Its library aims to contain 30,000 volumes as well as copies of Latin film and music classics. Its museum will display a permanent collection of regional folk-art, while its 1,800-seat auditorium will feature Latin American music, dance and theater. Four Annual Prizes Planned

With the idea of stirring interest elsewhere in the region, the center plans four annual prizes worth $100,000 each for prominent Latin contributions to the arts, science and technology, literature and the humanities. To stimulate research on Latin American subjects, 40 scholarships will also be granted every year - all funded by the Sao Paulo State government.

Today, the only similar experiment is the Casa de las Americas in Havana, which serves as a focalpoint for Latin American literature. While it is clearly in Cuba's political interest to court the region's writers and intellectuals, Brazil still seems to be feeling its way, as if unsure how Spanish America will react.

''The real significance of the Latin American Center is that it is being built in Brazil,'' said Eric Nepomuceno, a Brazilian writer who is involved in the project. ''If it were being built in Mexico City or Caracas, it would not have the same weight.''
