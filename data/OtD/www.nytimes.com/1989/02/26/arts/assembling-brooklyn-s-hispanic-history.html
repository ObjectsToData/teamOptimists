Assembling Brooklyn's Hispanic History

By RICHARD F. SHEPARD
Published: February 26, 1989

The Brooklyn Historical Society, which adopted its new name and changed its focus to emphasize the people who live in the borough now rather than those who originally peopled it, has embarked on an ambitious project to present the first full picture of Brooklynites with Latin roots.

More than a sixth of Brooklyn's population is Hispanic, and this large and growing segment consists of a half-dozen or more diverse communities, each with its own folkways, background and dialect. The society's program, when fully established, will assemble oral histories of early Hispanic settlers, photographs, documents and artifacts into a major exhibition, probably early next year. A Cornerstone, of Sorts

One of the objects in the exhibition-to-be is an adoquin, a paving block that came to Brooklyn as ship's ballast in 1905 and that until recently was part of 37th Street, between Fourth and Fifth Avenues, in Brooklyn's Sunset Park district.

''A Dr. Hahn gave it to me, and it was made of iron slag in Spain in 1600,'' said Dr. Morton A. Marks, director of the society's Hispanic Communities of Brooklyn Project. ''That is our first object. We hope to get costumes, cooking utensils, anything that will tell us about the Hispanic people here.''

David M. Kahn, the society's executive director, put the project into the context of change that has overtaken the 125-year-old organization. Only four years ago the society announced a shift of direction when it changed its name from the Long Island Historical Society.

''What we are doing reflects what's been going on in historical museums,'' Mr. Kahn said. ''Formerly we concentrated on the white Anglo-Saxon past. Now we are looking on more recent history that has an impact on the population of today, the ethnic and social history over the colonial history of early representatives.

''The demography in Brooklyn shows that the population is 34 percent black, 16 to 18 percent Hispanic. There are Italians, Jews and other groups. We want them all to know that we are their historical society. This project is a great luxury for us. We've never taken as much time. It doesn't focus on the exhibition, as many other projects do. Our aim is to create a great collection that will be preserved.''

Dr. Marks, who is from New Jersey, is an anthropologist, and he is nothing if not an enthusiast. He has immersed himself in Brooklyn's Hispanic lore and custom with the zeal of an explorer who has just discovered a new people in New Guinea. Starting With Interviews

''It's more complex than I ever imagined,'' he said. ''You can't speak of one Hispanic. To start, we have done interviews with individuals in five communities in Sunset Park, where there is a concentration of Brooklyn's half-million Hispanics.'' The interviews, he added, were done by the project's two ''assets,'' Marcelo Herman, who is from Buenos Aires, and Lucia Rodriguez. who was born in Cuba.

''In Sunset Park, there are Puerto Ricans, mostly, and Dominicans, Mexicans and Central Americans -from Nicaragua and El Salvador. The Puerto Ricans came in the 1950's, mostly from two towns, Hatillo and Moca. Along Third Avenue you can see signs like 'Moca Taxi Service.' An important thing about Sunset Park is that it's one of a few examples of Hispanic residents participating in the rehabilitation of a neighborhood. It used to be in decay, and now there's a rising homeowning class.''

''That's starting to show in Williamsburg, too,'' Dr. Marks said. ''We saw something interesting there in All Saints Church on Throop Avenue. They have an Ecuadorean congregation, but in December, Mexicans from Puebla had their festival of the Virgin of Guadalupe in the church. The people wore costumes and there were what they call Los Tecuanes, dramatic dances with Indian and Spanish elements. But the music is medieval Spanish, with no Indian influence.'' Broad Range of Influences

Dr. Marks showed several pictures of the brightly caparisoned Mexican procession winding through the streets of Williamsburg. They were made by Tony Velez, the project photographer, and will probably be included in the exhibition.

''The exhibition will show the range of what's included in the term Hispanic,'' Dr. Marks said. As examples of the wide variety of Hispanic elements ''all feeding into Brooklyn,'' he cited Panamanians dancing a quadrille of British origin, of Puerto Ricans and Cubans who have embraced the religion of the Yoruba people in Africa and of the celebration on Jan. 6 of Three Kings Day, which is more important to many Hispanic people than Christmas.

Last March, the project started with $20,000 from the New York State Council on the Arts. The Ford Foundation has expressed interest in supporting the documentation with a $50,000 grant, and the society has applied to the National Endowment for the Humanities for help in paying for the exhibition. It is now planned for early 1990, and will be held in the society's new Shellens Gallery of Brooklyn History, which is to open this spring.
