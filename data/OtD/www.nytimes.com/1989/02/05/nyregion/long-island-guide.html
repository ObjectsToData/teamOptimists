LONG ISLAND GUIDE


Published: February 5, 1989

SOVIET PIANIST On her third private visit to the United States, Valentina Schubinskaya, a noted Russian pianist and composer, decided that it was time to play for the American public. And since it's Long Island she visits - her sister, Tamara Jouraweff, lives in Shirley - East Hampton is where she will make her American debut this afternoon. A graduate of the Moscow Conservatory of Music, where she and pianist Bella Davidovich studied together (Ms. Davidovich has written a program note for the concert), Ms. Schubinskaya has an international reputation as a recording artist with the Grand Symphonic Orchestra of the U.S.S.R. and tours extensively throughout her homeland and Eastern Europe. She's also a faculty member of the Gnessin Institute of Music in Moscow. In her appearance at 2 P.M., at Guild Hall's John Drew Theater on Main Street, she'll perform Mozart, Liszt, Debussy, Metner and one of her own compositions, ''Russian Pictures.'' For ticket information call 324-4051. PLAYERS PLAY ON

What's better than one performance of the Sea Cliff Chamber Players? Would you believe three in one week, and all on the Island? Believe it, for this is the week the Players really get to play around. Today it's via the Town of Hempstead's ''Great Performer Series,'' when at 7:30 P.M. at Freeport High School, the ensemble, joined by Ida Kavafian, the acclaimed violinist, offer Dohnanyi, Ravel and Brahms. The school is on Sunrise Highway and tickets at the door are $6. On Friday the group, without Ms. Kavafian, moves to Northport for the first of two subscription concerts; the second is Saturday in Sea Cliff. While Dohnanyi's ''Serenade for String Trio in C, Op. 10'' and Brahms's ''Piano Quartet in A, Op. 26'' will be repeated, the Ravel will not. It will be replaced by Debussy's ''Sonata for Cello and Piano'' featuring the cellist Ko Iwasaki. Both performances, at Northport High School and the Sea Cliff School, respectively, are at 8 P.M. For ticket information call 671-6263. 'COLORED ORPHANAGE'

More than a century ago, thanks to the Long Island Rail Road's trek eastward, Smithtown became a mecca for experimentation in rustic (and social) living. St. Johnland, a utopian community where young and old were to live together in harmony, was among the more notable examples of this movement. A more obscure effort was the Howard Colored Orphanage. Founded in Brooklyn in 1866, in 1906 the home-school brought 30 boys to St. James to teach them how to become farmers.

Spurred on by an initial success that saw the farm produce 900 bushels of potatoes its first year, the Orphanage in 1910 purchased 500 acres in Kings Park from the Jewish Agricultural and Industrial Society, yet another failed social experiment. The Howard Orphanage and Industrial School, as it became known, taught youngsters basic skills and then, from the age of 12 through 21, bound them out to local families to learn a trade or to work. In 1918, however, the school went into bankruptcy - and obscurity.

Until now, that is, for the people at the Smithtown Historical Society discovered the program in the archives and with the financial help of the New York State Council on the Arts, have mounted ''To Give a Child a Chance.'' It's an exhibition that, with photos and documents, tells the school's sad story. Whether the boys' descendants still live on the Island, however, remains a mystery, said Louise Hall, the society's executive director. ''The boys' names were never mentioned, just as their faces were never shown on photographs,'' she said.

Honoring Black History Month, the show will run through March at the society's headquarters in the Caleb Smith House, off Route 25A in Smithtown. It can be seen from 9 A.M. to 4 P.M. weekdays and from from 1 to 4 P.M. Saturdays. Admission by donation. ON ICE

For its latest edition, ''Return To Romance,'' the Ice Capades naturally has signed the Really Big Names from the last Winter Olympics. This time out the production went for the bronze: Jill Watson and Peter Oppegard, the bronze medalists at Calgary and three time-United States Pair Skating Champions. The two head a cast that also includes Robert Wagenhoffer, an innovative athlete on skates; Simone Grigorescu, Rumania's young ice artist; Terry Head, the ''Clown Prince of Skating Comedy''; Gerald Soules and his Poodles of Paris; Kristan Lowery and Chip Rossbach, 1988 World Ice Dance Silver Medalists, and the California Raisins of television fame. They all skate into the Nassau Coliseum in Uniondale Tuesday for a 12-performance stand through Feb. 13. For ticket information and schedule call 587-9222. CENTER STAGE

Words, some sung, but mostly spoken, commemorate Black History Month in three special performances this week. On Wednesday, Bernice Johnson Reagon, a poet who is curator of the Smithsonian Institution's Museum of American History, Community Life Division, and the founder of Sweet Honey in the Rock, the a cappella sextet, will be at the State University at Stony Brook. She'll perform her one-woman show at 7:30 P.M. at the Poetry Center, Room 239 of the Humanities Building. The program is free.
