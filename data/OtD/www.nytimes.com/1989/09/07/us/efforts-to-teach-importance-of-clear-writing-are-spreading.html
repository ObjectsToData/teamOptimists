Efforts to Teach Importance of Clear Writing Are Spreading

By KATHERINE BISHOP, Special to The New York Times
Published: September 7, 1989

After 15 years, the instructors in the Bay Area Writing Project, which recently completed its summer session, have reason to believe that the rest of the country is catching up to their concern for the importance of teaching writing.

Their optimism lies in that last spring the Association of American Medical Colleges announced plans to include in its medical school admission exam a test of writing skills.

''There's an increasing call for whole faculties - not just English teachers, but also science and math teachers - to use writing as a way for kids to not just retain material, but to understand it and make it their own,'' said Mary Ann Smith, a junior high school English teacher who for five years has worked full time as the director of the writing project.

The Bay Area Writing Project, which is run from the University of California's Berkeley campus, was begun in the 1973-74 school year with a $13,000 grant from the university in response to the weak composition skills of entering freshmen. Founded to teach teachers to teach writing, it has became the model for the National Writing Project, also based at the Berkeley campus, which was begun with a grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities. The National Writing Project now has a network of 165 sites training more than 80,000 teachers yearly. Explaining to Novices

Ms. Smith, who was one of the original 25 teachers in the project's first summer session, recalls a typical day of work: A physics teacher picked a topic most of the nonscience teachers did not really understand, like how people see color, and had them discuss it in small groups and then write a paper about it as though they were explaining it to someone else who did not understand.

In another session, a social science teacher demonstrated how students could gain a deeper understanding of a broad topic by writing about one moment in its history as though they had been there. Instead of trying to encapsulate the entire civil rights movement, one participating teacher wrote about the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr.'s ''I Have a Dream'' speech, interspersing portions of the speech in the essay's text, thereby learning about the event as well as the actual words of the speech. Other Model Programs

While the Bay Area Writing Project is widely replicated throughout the nation, it is not the only model for improving writing skills. In New York City, the Teachers & Writers Collaborative has been bringing professional writers into the classroom since 1967 to work directly with students assisted by teachers.

''Some kids are more inspired meeting a writer,'' said Nancy Shapiro, director of the program. ''That may be a catalyst for some students more than a teacher who's interested in writing.''

Financed by state, Federal and corporate grants and school fees, the collaborative employs about 50 writers and serves approximately 8,000 children, from kindergarten through eighth grade, each year.

Ms. Smith said the teachers of the Bay Area Writing Project rejected the notion that they should not require their students to read difficult works. Susan Reed, a teacher at De Anza High School in nearby Richmond, whose students are predominantly from low-income and minority families, has her pupils keep logs of questions and observations as they read ''Macbeth'' and then write essays on topics suggested by those logs. The result is writing on themes like the pitfalls of ambition and about things they were ashamed of having done.

Ms. Smith said a recent evaluation demonstrated that the program is working. At Bret Harte Junior High School in Oakland, participating teachers had students write on one topic early in the fall and then write again on a similar topic in the spring. They found that the second group of papers showed substantial improvement in organization, fluency, specificity and grammar.
