THE NOVEMBER REPORT; A Hard Look At Software

By Peter H. Lewis
Published: November 5, 1989

A RECENT Gallup Poll found that one in four American college seniors - seniors, mind you - did not know that Columbus's voyage to the New World was made before the year 1500. Nearly half could not place the American Civil War in the period 1850-1900.

One wonders, then, how many students would be able to answer questions such as ''Who were the contemporaries of Christopher Columbus in politics, art, science and religion?'' or ''While Americans prepared for their Civil War, what global revolution was beginning that would change society into the 21st century?''

Walter Reinhold, who has been enlightening and entertaining students of music, history and the arts at New York University for more than two decades, believes that history must be viewed not as a series of discrete dates and events but in context, as an interaction of various events, personalities and humanist disciplines.

To that end, Professor Reinhold and his associates have created a computer program called Culture 1.0, The Hypermedia Guide to Western Civilization ($175 from Cultural Resources Inc. of Scotch Plains, N.J., 201-709-1574). This set of seven diskettes for the Apple Macintosh represents one of the best educational uses of computer technology. A version for I.B.M. and compatible computers is expected by year's end and will cost about $190. There's also a workbook for both versions that costs $35 plus $3 for shipping.

Culture 1.0 uses the Hypercard system, which is included with each Macintosh sold. The Hypercard software enables the user to create interactive links among related sets of information, using a ''stack of notecards'' metaphor. Each card, or screen, may contain a variety of features, including text, pictures and graphic symbols. Some symbols might be buttons or arrows that connect the currently displayed card with others in the stack, or to sound effects. For example, a card on Beethoven might have several buttons that, when clicked with the mouse pointing device, play short selections of the composer's works.

Students or teachers are free to explore those stacks, following their interests rather than any rigid path. Users tend to spend more time with the material and to get more deeply involved than they would with conventional textbook-and-lecture classes.

For example, searching for ''Columbus'' might lead the user to explore the Mature Renaissance period. Shortly after Columbus's death, Niccolo Machiavelli wrote ''The Prince.'' In the arts, Leonardo da Vinci was in his prime. Michelangelo, or maybe even Martin Luther, might have been among the young men inspired by news of Columbus's discoveries. Elsewhere, King Henry VIII of England was composing music, perhaps for one of his weddings. And so the student goes forth across the rich tapestry of history and the humanities.

One generation of history links to the next, just as one aspect of the arts links to another. The effect is a multidimensional view of history that can be examined from a number of refreshing perspectives.

Culture 1.0 comprises stacks on Biblical History, Greco-Roman culture, the Middle Ages, Renaissance and the Baroque, Classic and Romantic periods. Another program, Culture 1.1, scheduled for delivery next spring, will complete the 20th century to date and offer other features. One of them may be a link to a video laser disk of the National Gallery of Art, in Washington, D.C., which would enable users to see color images of the paintings and art objects mentioned in the software.

The I.B.M. version will be particularly interesting, since the Macintosh Hypercard has no equal in the I.B.M. PC world, although I.B.M.'s Linkway and other programs are beginning to show promise. Cultural Resources has chosen to built the PC version around a new software system called HyperWriter, which is being developed by a company in Connecticut.

For an extra $35, Cultural Resources offers a workbook to accompany the software. The title is ''Interdisciplinary Lessons Covering Various and Sundry Things Every Secondary School Student Should Learn and Every College Student and Graduate Should Know.'' That describes it well; teachers will find it especially useful as a planning aid.

Culture 1.0, which comes on seven diskettes, comprising five megabytes of data, is quite an achievement. But it cries out to be translated to CD-ROM or other laser technologies that can store vast amounts of information, including digital sound and photographic-quality art. A laser-based Culture 1.0 could become the model for educational software in the 1990's.

Younger students can also explore history and the humanities with an old friend, Carmen San Diego. ''Where in Time Is Carmen San Diego?'' ($44.95, or $54.95 for the school edition for Apple II and I.B.M. computers, which includes a backup disk and a teacher's guide, from Broderbund Software Inc. of San Rafael, Calif., 1-800-521-6263) is the latest version of one of the most popular educational programs for grades 6 to 12.

The player once again assumes the role of detective, tracking the nefarious Carmen and her gang of oddball villains from crime to crime across the globe. As with past Carmen games, the clues left by the bandits gently impart a knowledge of geography.

The new wrinkle is that Carmen has stolen a time machine, and now can hide in the Middle Ages, the Renaissance, the Industrial Revolution or the modern age. Witnesses to her perfidy might include famous historical figures, and clues involve famous events and inventions.

The program comes with a reference book, The New American Desk Encyclopedia.

Lower prices are always good news, and a $100 price cut on a good program is welcome indeed. Michael Newman's Verse Perfect Poetry Processor for the I.B.M. PC, which was recommended in this column recently, is now available until Christmas for $89.95 plus $4 shipping and handling from Mr. Newman, in care of The Paris Review, 541 East 72d Street, New York, N.Y. 10023; telephone 201-525-2122.
