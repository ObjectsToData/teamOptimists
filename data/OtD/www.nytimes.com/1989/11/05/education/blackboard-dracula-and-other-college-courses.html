BLACKBOARD; Dracula and Other College Courses

By John Arundel
Published: November 5, 1989

A RECENT report by the National Endowment for the Humanities expressed concern that more colleges do not have core curriculums that are heavy on history and languages.

But judging from an informal survey of colleges around the nation, students this fall are seeking - and finding - a bounty of classes to take them far away from anything even resembling a corpus of scholarly knowledge.

Witchcraft, New England Basketry, the History of Horror, and Does God Exist? are among the courses that students don't necessarily boast to their parents about.

Under the category of ''if you can't beat 'em, join 'em,'' even soap operas, which professors blame in part for a drop in attendence in afternoon classes, are now the subject of academic discussion. So the University of Colorado at Boulder offers the Soaps Course: Popular Culture, Popular Television, which allows students to watch the daytime dramas while analyzing them for their popularity and content.

As do most instructors of offbeat courses, Prof. Michael Tracy, who teaches the soap-opera class, defends it as one with a serious intent.

''This is an introduction for students to think more critically of TV, to see TV as a social reality and to look at how it fits into their lives,'' Professor Tracy said. ''It's not a flippant course.''

Courses on other American pastimes are finding their way into college curriculums. Students taking Wine in Western Culture at Ohio State University learn to make wine and taste it properly, while students at Essex Community College in Baltimore learn about coffee roasting, grinding and brewing in the Essence of Coffee.

A Food and Culture class at the Johns Hopkins University examines how Americans eat, while History of the American Automobile is about how Americans drive.

If expanding into the outside world gets weary, college students can take courses on college students. At Harvard College, Prof. David Rudner is teaching an anthropology course, Romance, Consumerism and Alienation: Explorations in American College Culture, in which students examine their own habits, how much they study, how much they party and how they choose companions. They also analyze how they establish personal identities through consumer goods, like beer and clothing.

''I thought it might be interesting and fun to have the class do a reflexive self-study,'' Professor Rudner said. ''It's like a traditional study of anthropology in Africa or New Guinea, but here a lot of things aren't applicable. We don't have to study lineage systems, witchcraft or sorcery.''

Freshmen at Long Island University are receiving survival tips in the form of a seminar, College 101. The required course teaches freshmen how to take notes in class, how to balance work and play, and how to use the library.

The more seriously offbeat course offerings, especially those focusing on the macabre, are swelling, with upperclassmen eager for lively readings and spirited debate - and in many cases an easy A. At the University of Delaware, Prof. John Kelly teaches the Kennedy Assassination and the Study of Murder. ''Students' minds are exposed to such a Disney World type of murder,'' he said. ''So I try to make them a part of the victim's family. Their whole perspective switches around.''

Death-Related Issues, another course at Delaware, teaches the value of funerals and rituals, the grieving process and how death is perceived in the news media. The course includes a computer simulation of the student's death, including a tombstone with the student's name. ''It puts you in touch with your own feelings,'' said the instructor, Madeline E. Lambrecht.

Arizona State University has a course devoted to the study of Dracula and includes a detailed account of blood types.

Some professors use devices like counterculture songs, avant-garde films and even comic books to get their points across. At Duke University one instructor uses Batman comic books and beatnik poetry to teach modern literary movements in a freshman writing seminar.

Another Duke professor, Ariel Dorfman, a Chilean author and teacher who has been exiled for his writing, has students watch cartoons like ''He-Man'' and the movie ''Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom'' to discern messages and themes of Western culture.

Students seeking to break from the career pack will find courses on how to become a psychic in Boston or a comedian in Ohio.

At Boston University, Stalking the Wild Mind teaches tarot-card reading, dowsing, divining and tea-leaf reading. ''It's one of those classic, eccentric courses where you learn something about yourself in an unconventional way,'' said Angelica Brisk, a student.

At Ohio State University, Mel Helitzer, a former Madison Avenue advertising executive, teaches Humor Writing for Fun and Profit, offering aspiring comics the theory and technique of humor. Despite a 15-page term paper and stiff grading based on the success of a peer-judged standup comedy routine, the course has a one-year waiting list, he said.

For the final examination, students do a five-minute standup routine before hundreds of Ohio State students. The grading scale, as Mr. Helitzer explains it, is simple: ''A standing ovation, that's an A. Enthusiastic applause is a B and polite applause is a C. If the audience throws fruit, you get an F. But if the fruit's edible, your grade is bumped to a D.''
