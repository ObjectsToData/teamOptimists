University of California Faulted on Hiring of Women

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Save Article
By TAMAR LEWIN
Published: May 18, 2005

A new report by four professors at the University of California, Davis, has found that despite an unusual hiring wave and a steady increase in the number of women in the Ph.D. applicant pool, the University of California still lags in hiring women.

Indeed, the study found that the public university system, the nation's largest, had yet to match the proportion of women it hired in the mid-1990's, before the 1996 adoption of Proposition 209, which made it illegal for public universities to consider sex or race in admissions or hiring.

From a high of 37 percent in 1993-94, the proportion of women hired for the faculty dropped to 25 percent systemwide in 1999. In 2003-4, the latest year for which figures have been released, the study said, women made up about 36 percent of the University of California faculty hiring.

The report comes at a time when universities nationwide are rethinking their efforts to increase faculty diversity.

After a fracas this spring about his suggestion that "intrinsic aptitude" might be a factor behind the low number of women in science and engineering, Harvard's president, Lawrence H. Summers, announced on Monday that the university would spend at least $50 million in the next decade to recruit, support and promote women and members of underrepresented minority groups on its faculty.

In California, Gyöngy Laky, one of the authors of the new report, cited the impact of Proposition 209.

"I think after Prop. 209 people thought they didn't need to think about it anymore, they didn't need to bother, it wasn't an issue," she said. "We're all fallible, we're all most comfortable hanging out with the people we're used to hanging out with, and so white men tend to hire white men."

Sheila O'Rourke, executive director of academic placement in the University of California's office of the president, did not dispute the numbers in the report. Nor did she challenge the sense that the hiring of women had dropped in the late 1990's or that further attention was needed to increase the hiring of women. But she said that the situation was far more complicated than the report indicated and that aggregating the numbers systemwide distorted the picture.

"The patterns are very field-specific," she said. "In the humanities and social sciences, we're pretty close to parity. And in math and science, as we see at universities everywhere, there are disparities that begin in high school and undergraduate years.

"With such big gender gaps from field to field, if you define the applicant pool as all Ph.D.'s, it's misleading. For example, there are a large number of Ph.D.'s in education, a woman-dominated field, but that doesn't mean there are a lot women to hire for the engineering department."

In the last three decades, as the nation has debated the merits of diversity and affirmative action, the issue has been particularly contentious in California, and the university's hiring practices have drawn much attention. State Senator Jackie Speier held three hearings on gender equity from 2001 to 2003, and the university itself had a summit meeting on the question three years ago.

But Martha West, the lead author of the new report, who has worked on the issue for years, said that as the number of women earning Ph.D.'s increased, the rate of female faculty hiring should be growing much faster than currently.

"Women are earning Ph.D.'s in record numbers, particularly in the biological sciences," Professor West said. "Given these numbers, we are concerned that the Larry Summers view of the world may still be standard operating procedure at U.C. when it comes to faculty hiring decisions. The urgent tone of our report reflects concern over a downturn in hiring women at a time of unprecedented increases in the numbers of faculty hired."

The report points out that different University of California campuses show a wide disparity in hiring women. In 2003-4, women were 48 percent of the faculty hiring at U.C., Berkeley, but just 22 percent of the total at U.C., Riverside. Over all, though, every school except Berkeley had hiring percentages below the percentage of women earning Ph.D.'s nationally.

"U.C. should not be satisfied unless faculty hires contain at least 40 percent women, which would represent only a 4 percent increase over the 2003-4 hires," Professor West said. "As long as the percentage of women hires remains significantly below women's proportion of the Ph.D. pool, the data indicates that women continue to experience discrimination in the U.C. hiring process."

Ms. O'Rourke takes a more positive view. "The good news is that since this came to light in the late 1990's, the University of California has witnessed substantial progress, with the proportion of women faculty hired increased from 25 percent to 36 percent," she said.

"I'm actually not worried. I think the awareness is high, and the pool is getting stronger, and I think President Summers's comments have led to national awareness of these issues."

The University of California, Ms. O'Rourke said, is gathering information for a summit meeting on diversity that will concentrate mostly on minority hiring but will also include a look at gender issues.

In the wake of Proposition 209, she said, minority faculty hiring did not drop as sharply as the hiring of women. She said that perhaps this was because the increases in numbers of minorities leading up to Proposition 209 had been less noteworthy.
