Books of the Times | 'Melville'

A 'Shadow' Who Left Indelible Works

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Save Article
By WILLIAM GRIMES
Published: October 1, 2005

Hart Crane once called Herman Melville a "fabulous shadow." The characterization is apt. As the author of "Moby-Dick," quite possibly the greatest American novel, and slippery protomodern works like "Bartleby the Scrivener," "Benito Cereno" and "Billy Budd," Melville is a towering presence. His knots and perplexities and preoccupations remain shockingly contemporary.

MELVILLE
His World and Work
By Andrew Delbanco
415 pages. Alfred A. Knopf. $30.

The man himself, however, keeps disappearing just around the next corner. Andrew Delbanco rightly points out in his preface to "Melville: His World and Work" that "any conventional biography of Melville is a business bound to fail" for sheer lack of material. There are some surviving letters, most of them brief, and a few perfunctory journals. Long stretches of Melville's life are almost completely undocumented, including the early years at sea in the South Pacific. When he died in obscurity in 1891, at 72, the few Americans who noticed must have been surprised to find that the author of "Moby-Dick" had not died decades earlier. Despite the obstacles, Mr. Delbanco, a humanities professor at Columbia and the author of many books on American literature, has managed to deliver a first-rate biography, shrewd and engaging, that ingeniously tacks back and forth from fiction to fact. He adds nothing to the modest pile of biographical data about Melville. Instead, he concentrates on placing the fiction in historical and political context, and, through a series of imaginative leaps, on making connections between Melville's works and the circumstances surrounding their creation.

A striking example of this approach can be found in Mr. Delbanco's colorful evocation of New York in the 1840's, where Melville made the artistic transition from his semidocumentary tales of the South Seas to the grand, go-for-broke "Moby-Dick." Swept up in the visual chaos, the assaultive odors, the sheer human crush of the city, Mr. Delbanco argues, Melville developed a voracious appetite for the democratic spectacle of American life.

His style changed. Abandoning convention, "his books became eclectic miscellanies, with innumerable tangents spoking out from the spine of the story, each one reaching for some new analogy that diverts our attention to some novel sensation, or topic, or fact." Melville's fiction - digressive, exclamatory and wide open to the play of free association - suggested the influence of newspapers or advertisements as much as conventional novels.

Mr. Delbanco does not document this thesis in a conventional way. Rather he dramatizes and suggests, by evoking the rude, seething life of Broadway, and the city's crowded visual field of placards, tabloid newspapers, sandwich men and advertising signs attached to passing carriages. Melville may not be an urban writer in the ordinary sense; yet, Mr. Delbanco insists, there has never been anyone in American literature "more deeply affected, indeed infected, by the tone and rhythm of the city." New York, by example, pushed Melville toward formal experiment with the possibilities of formlessness. It inspired him to take risks, to "accede to his reckless moods," as Mr. Delbanco puts it. "Better to sink in boundless deeps, than float on vulgar shoals; and give me, ye Gods, an utter wreck, if wreck I do," Melville wrote at the time. The high-stakes gamble of "Moby-Dick" paid off, in artistic terms, but thereafter Melville floundered. Mr. Delbanco ruefully follows the trail of financial desperation and repeated artistic failure, punctuated by unexpected comebacks, none of them remunerative. After the messy, incomprehensible "Pierre" ("Herman Melville Crazy," read the headline over one newspaper's review), Melville rebounded with the sublime "Bartleby" and "Benito Cereno," but then subsided into pathetic hackwork and a long, sad career as deputy inspector No. 75 for the United States Custom Service.

Mr. Delbanco makes this more than a Grubstreet tragedy. His concise, illuminating discussion of publishing economics in the United States underscores the heroism of Melville's struggles. The absence of international copyright law stacked the deck against American writers, who, in trying to establish both a native voice and a domestic audience, encountered the entrenched resistance of their publishers. Why risk money on new American writing when it cost a mere pittance to print the work of tried-and-true English authors?

Melville found solace in his friendship with Nathaniel Hawthorne, a relationship whose ups and downs Mr. Delbanco traces with a sensitive hand. Melville, open and impulsive, poured his heart out to Hawthorne and, one suspects, tried his patience with high-flying philosophical orations that lasted late into the night. Hawthorne, cool and composed, took it all in but returned little of the heat. Melville was a "blurter-outer," Mr. Delbanco puts it, and Hawthorne "a man of circumspection." Mr. Delbanco, with a searching eye, finds Hawthorne's "resistant presence in the many scenes of confrontation that recur in Melville's fiction between men of ardor and men of reserve," like Ahab and Starbuck.

The friendship fizzled, for reasons that Mr. Delbanco cannot resist speculating about. At some point between completing "Moby-Dick" and starting "Pierre," he theorizes, "Melville may have gotten close to some explosive secret in his friend's private life, and Hawthorne may have pushed him away." Here, as he does from time to time, Mr. Delbanco goes too far. What is the evidence? If there isn't any, this is a small step removed from gossip.

One is tempted to pose the same question when Mr. Delbanco zeroes in on Melville's penchant for obsessive characters like Ahab, or Mr. Claggart in "Billy Budd," and proposes that "one deep purpose of his writing was to save himself from becoming too much like them." This is an intriguing notion, but ultimately fruitless. Perhaps it was. And then again, perhaps it wasn't.

It's hard to blame Mr. Delbanco for taking a shot in the dark, when there is so much darkness to confront. In the end, Melville remains a man of shadows, but his character and achievement as a writer have never been clearer.
