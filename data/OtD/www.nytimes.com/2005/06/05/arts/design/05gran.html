How to Install a 48-Foot, 12-Ton Orange Steel Giant

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Single-Page
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By ANNETTE GRANT
Published: June 5, 2005

MOUNTAINVILLE, N.Y.

"I CAN'T talk," Mark di Suvero said to an importunate visitor. "I have to get to work." His face crinkled into a smile of apology.

"Work," on a recent blustery afternoon at the 500-acre Storm King Art Center here, was installing "Ad Astra," a new monumental sculpture, in the middle of what was once a hay field. Mr. di Suvero, who is 71, was operating the crane that lifted each section of a 48-foot-tall, 12-ton orange steel behemoth into place. He fidgeted with his hard hat, eager to get going.

This is Mr. di Suvero's third major exhibition at Storm King, more than any other artist has had there. He has been a star for 40 years, installing his monumental, architectural steel works all around the world - even in the Tuileries in Paris in its first solo show devoted to a living artist.

But there was something he did want to talk about: Richard Bellamy, for 40 years his dealer, friend and adviser, who died in 1998. "Dick was a soul mate," Mr. di Suvero said. "He was a little nutty, but he knew what I was thinking before I was thinking it. We were a tandem pair, out of the usual modalities. Which is not to say we always got along perfectly."

Bellamy had started the Green Gallery at 15 West 57th Street and given first shows to avant-garde artists, including Claes Oldenberg, Donald Judd, James Rosenquist, Lucas Samaras and Richard Serra. The gallery's inaugural exhibition, in 1960, was wood constructions by Mark di Suvero. The art historian Sidney Geist called it "one of those moments of which one can say that 'From now on nothing will be the same.' " It was the same year that Storm King opened as an outdoor museum and that sculpture went on steroids, growing larger and larger, finally bursting out of gallery walls.

Bellamy closed the Green Gallery in 1965 and became a private dealer, by 1983 concentrating almost exclusively on Mr. di Suvero. In 1985 Bellamy moved to a pier in Long Island City, Queens, near Mr. di Suvero's studio. Meanwhile, he applied his brilliant eye to photography and took thousands of pictures of Mr. di Suvero's works in every state of completion. Some 83 of these will be on view in the Storm King museum building, along with 7 of Mr. di Suvero's preliminary drawings, starting on Wednesday through the fall of 2006, along with Mr. di Suvero's concurrent show of 25 indoor and outdoor sculptural works.

Beyond art, the two men shared a love of literature, especially poetry. Mr. di Suvero reeled off a long list of favorite poets and has named works for some of them: Yeats, Marianne Moore, Gerard Manley Hopkins, Rumi. Sometimes poetry even inspired a name for a piece of equipment. "I called my first crane 'For Love,' after Robert Creeley's first book," he said.

Continuing to search for ways to describe all the ways Bellamy helped artists, and particularly him, he started to say, "You know, we artists are fed by something else besides just food or money, a different kind of necessity ..."

"Oh, good, Nate!," he called out suddenly to a worker who had lobbed a block of wood up to a cherry picker where a colleague caught it at its exact apogee. The small crowd of workers and assistants inhaled, "ohhhhhh." So, today we wouldn't find out what nurtures artists. But the interruption reminded Mr. di Suvero that he was neglecting work.

"Why don't you go play 'Beethoven's Quartet,' " he suggested, referring to another of his new sculptures, on view outdoors for the first time. "Lowell can drive you over, though I really need him here." He turned briskly and hurried back to his rig with the support of the crutches he has used since suffering a near-fatal accident on a construction site 45 years ago. "And take a hunk of wood, a 4-by-4," he called back over his shoulder. "Really batter it."

Lowell McKegney has worked with Mr. di Suvero since his 1975 show at the Whitney Museum, which placed sculptures in all five New York boroughs, and these formed the core of the di Suvero permanent collection here. Mr. McKegney is one of eight permanent staff members and he oversees the sculptor's studio in Petaluma, Calif., where "Ad Astra" was made, loaded on a truck and sent to upstate New York. "Beethoven's Quartet," which was made in Long Island City and shipped here along with Mr. di Suvero's battery of cranes and lifts, sits in front of his "Pyramidian," a towering (65 foot) open-frame pyramid, as imposing as any in Egypt.
