From Pieter Claesz to Pixar

By KEN JOHNSON
Published: September 11, 2005

Correction Appended

Dates are subject to change.

September

CHUCK CLOSE, 'SELF-PORTRAITS, 1967-2005' Beginning with Mr. Close's first giant, black-and-white Photorealist self-portrait from 1967-68, this exhibition will explore the career of an artist who has brought an extraordinary spirit of invention to the task of copying photographic portraits. Walker Art Center, Minneapolis, through Oct. 16. (612) 375-7656. www.walkerart.org. Travels to the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, Nov. 19-Feb. 28, and the High Museum of Art, Atlanta, March 25-June 18.

'ACTING OUT: THE INVENTED MELODRAMA IN CONTEMPORARY PHOTOGRAPHY' In recent years many photographers have staged dramatic narratives for the camera following the leads of movies, television and advertising. This show will present 13 of the most influential of those artists, including Tina Barney, Gregory Crewdson, Philip-Lorca diCorcia and Anna Gaskel. Neuberger Museum of Art, Purchase, N.Y. Through December. (914) 251-6100. www.neuberger.org.

'MOVING PICTURES: AMERICAN ART AND EARLY FILM, 1880-1910' The intriguing idea of this show is to compare paintings depicting movement with movies made during the early years of cinema. It will include 100 paintings by artists like George Bellows, Thomas Eakins and John Singer Sargent and 50 films made by Edison, Lumière and the American Mutoscope & Biograph Company. Williams College Museum of Art, Williamstown, Mass. Through Dec. 11. (413) 597-2429. www.wcma.org. Travels to Reynolda House Museum of American Art, Winston-Salem, N.C., March 24-July 16; the Grey Art Gallery, New York, Sept. 13-Dec. 9, 2006; and the Phillips Collection, Washington, Feb. 17-May 20, 2007.

'THE (S) FILES: THE SELECTED FILES 2005' El Museo del Barrio's fourth biennial exhibition of up and coming Latino and Latin-American artists from the New York City area presents approximately 35 artists, selected from the museum's files, who work in painting, photography, sculpture, installation, video, sound, performance and other art forms. El Museo del Barrio. Through Jan. 29. (212) 831-7272. www.elmuseo.org.

'DIALOGUES: DUCHAMP, CORNELL, JOHNS, RAUSCHENBERG' Themes and relationships shared by four of the 20th century's most influential artists will be explored by this potentially revelatory exhibition. Dallas Museum of Art. Through Jan. 8. (214) 922-1200. www.dallasmuseumofart.org.

'LOOKING AT ATGET' This exhibition will present about 120 pictures from a recently acquired collection of pictures by the great French photographer Eugène Atget while telling the story of how the photographer Berenice Abbott and the gallery director Julien Levy collaborated in introducing and promoting Atget in America after his death in 1927. Philadelphia Museum of Art. Through Nov. 27. (215) 763-8100. www.philamuseum.org.

'LORDS OF CREATION: THE ORIGINS OF SACRED MAYA KINGSHIP' Exploring the emergence 2,000 years ago of sacred kings in the Maya region, this show will present 150 objects borrowed from national museums in Mexico, Guatemala, Honduras, Belize and Costa Rica. Los Angeles County Museum of Art. Through Jan. 2. (323) 857-6000. www.lacma.org. Travels to the Dallas Museum of Art, Feb. 12-May 7, and the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, June 11-Sept. 10.

'PAPER MUSEUMS: THE REPRODUCTIVE PRINT IN EUROPE, 1500-1800' Before the invention of photography, great artworks were reproduced and disseminated in the form of prints that were often the works of great artists and craftsmen. This scholarly exhibition will explore the history of the reproductive print from its beginnings in Germany to its peak of popularity in the Netherlands, France and England. Grey Art Gallery, New York University. Tuesday through Dec. 3. (212) 998-6780. www.nyu.edu/greyart.

'OBSESSIVE DRAWING' Self-taught contemporary artists who pursue extremist forms of drawing will be featured, including the Japanese conceptualist Hiroyuki Doi, the compulsive stippler Charles Benefiel of Pittsburgh, the British visionary Chris Hipkiss and the New Zealand modular draftsman Marin Thompson. American Folk Art Museum, New York. Wednesday through March 9. (212) 265-1040. www.folkartmuseum.org.

'GENERAL IDEAS: RETHINKING CONCEPTUAL ART 1987-2005' Contemporary conceptualists who were born during the first wave of conceptualism - 1965 to 1975 - will be studied with an eye for work that deals with everyday forms of behavior and with an emphasis on emotion and humor. Francis Alÿs, Liam Gillick, Andrea Fraser, Emily Jacir, Kay Rosen and Rirkrit Tiravanija will be among the participants. C.C.A. Wattis Institute for Contemporary Arts, San Francisco. Friday through Nov. 13. (415) 551-9210. www.wattis.org.

MAX LIEBERMANN, 'FROM REALISM TO IMPRESSIONISM' From the mid-1880's to the Nazi takeover in 1933, Liebermann was one of the most celebrated painters in Germany. More than 70 works ranging from early realist interpretations of Dutch peasant life to late Impressionist-style works will be included in the first major museum exhibition of his art in the United States. Skirball Cultural Center, Los Angeles. Thursday through Jan. 29. (310) 440-4500. www.skirball.org. Travels to the Jewish Museum, New York, March 10-July 9.

'POPULENCE' The title says as much about the dazzling excessiveness of today's popular culture as it does about the work of some contemporary artists responding to it. Organized by the Los Angeles art critic and curator David Pagel for the Blaffer Gallery at the University of Houston, where it closed Aug. 27, the show will feature Polly Apfelbaum, Mary Heilmann, Jeff Koons, Takashi Murakami and 15 others who react to our media-saturated world through works of opulent presence. Museum of Contemporary Art, Cleveland. Sept. 16-Dec. 30. (216) 421-8671. www.mocacleveland.org .

'RUSSIA!' Billed as "the most comprehensive and spectacular showing of Russian art ever sent to the United States," this big show will cover not only Russian art of the last nine centuries but also Russian collections of Western art from the last 400 years. Guggenheim Museum, New York. Friday through Jan. 11. (212) 423-3500. www.guggenheim.org. Click here for a related article.

ROBERT SMITHSON, 'FLOATING ISLAND' In conjunction with the seminal earth artist's retrospective on view through Oct. 23 at the Whitney Museum, the Whitney will create one of Smithson's heretofore unrealized proposals, using the artist's 1970 drawing called "Floating Island to Travel Around Manhattan Island." A tugboat will haul a large barge planted with vegetation around Manhattan. Both will be visible from shore at a number of publicized sites on the Hudson, Harlem and East Rivers. The island will sail daily from Saturday through Sept. 25. Whitney Museum of American Art. (877) 944-8639. www.whitney.org.

'THE GREAT AMERICAN THING: MODERN ART AND NATIONAL IDENTITY, 1915-1935' During the years between World Wars I and II many American artists thought deeply about the meaning of America and of being American. The first special exhibition in this museum's brand new David Chipperfield-designed building will feature more than 120 works by American modernists like Stuart Davis, Charles Demuth, Georgia O'Keeffe, Charles Sheeler and Alfred Stieglitz. Figge Art Museum, Davenport, Iowa. Saturday through Jan. 1. (563) 326-7804. www.figgeartmuseum.org.

'SEMINA CULTURE: WALLACE BERMAN & HIS CIRCLE' Semina was an art and poetry journal published just nine times between 1955 and 1964 by Berman, the mercurial and influential beatnik-era artist whose collages and assemblages combined the sensibilities of a Pop artist and a Hebrew mystic. Along with his works, the exhibition will feature works by 48 artists who contributed to Semina, including Jay DeFeo, Bruce Conner, Joan Brown, George Herms and Jess as well as poetry by Robert Duncan, David Meltzer and Philip Lamantia. Santa Monica Museum of Art. Saturday through Nov. 26. (310) 586-6488. www.smmoa.org.

PIETER CLAESZ, 'MASTER OF HAARLEM STILL LIFE' Twenty-five works by this 17th-century Dutch painter who specialized in monochrome table-top still lifes. He rendered pies, breads, lemons, olives, nuts, fruits and wine with intense realism, acute attention to the effects of light and symbolic suggestiveness. National Gallery of Art, Washington. Sept. 18 through December. (202) 737-4215. www.nga.gov.

'PRAGUE, THE CROWN OF BOHEMIA, 1347-1437' During the 14th and early 15th centuries, Prague rivaled Paris and Rome as a center of European culture. This exhibition assembles about 200 examples of painting, sculpture, goldsmithing, illuminated manuscripts, silk embroidery and stained glass drawn from collections in the Czech Republic and other European and American collections. Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York. Sept. 20-Jan. 3. (212) 535-7710. www.metmuseum.org.

THOMAS HIRSCHHORN, 'UTOPIA, UTOPIA = ONE WORLD, ONE WAR, ONE ARMY, ONE DRESS' Mr. Hirschhorn is known for all-enveloping installations made of cardboard, packing tape and found materials alluding to sociological and political ideas. Mimicking a museum of natural history, this show will focus on the military use of camouflage. Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston. Sept. 22 through December. (617) 266-5152. www.icaboston.org. Travels to the C.C.A. Wattis Institute for Contemporary Arts, San Francisco, March 15-May 13.

'RIGHT UNDER THE SUN: LANDSCAPE IN PROVENCE FROM CLASSICISM TO MODERNISM' This survey of landscape painting in southern France will include about 200 works representing 18th-century Romanticism, 19th-century Impressionism and 20th- century Cubism by artists like Vernet, Monet, Gauguin, van Gogh, Cézanne and Braque. Montreal Museum of Fine Arts. Sept. 22- Jan. 8. (514) 285-1600. www.mbam.qc.ca.

'THE JEWISH IDENTITY PROJECT: NEW AMERICAN PHOTOGRAPHY' The Jewish Museum commissioned contemporary photographers and video artists to work with individuals, families and organizations across the United States to produce works that reflect on what it means to be Jewish and American. Jewish Museum, New York. Sept. 23-Jan. 29. (212) 423-3200. www.jewishmuseum.org.

HIROSHI SUGIMOTO, 'HISTORY OF HISTORY' The acclaimed Japanese photographer will present artworks and religious artifacts from his own collection along with his haunting minimalist images of the ocean, vacant movie theaters, museum dioramas and wax museums. Japan Society, New York. Sept. 23-Feb. 19. (212) 832-1155. www.japansociety.org.

KATE ERICSON AND MEL ZIEGLER, 'AMERICA STARTS HERE' From 1978 to 1995, Ericson and Mr. Ziegler, a married couple, collaborated on elegantly wry conceptual projects that explored politics, philosophy and everyday life. The exhibition, organized with the List Visual Art Center at M.I.T., will feature sculptures, installations, models and video documents of site-specific projects. Tang Teaching Museum and Art Gallery, Saratoga Springs, N.Y. Oct. 1-Dec. 30. (518) 580-8080. www.skidmore.edu/tang.

ADOLPH GOTTLIEB, '1956' In 1956 the Abstract Expressionist Adolph Gottlieb shifted from the busy pictographic designs he had been making to the open, so-called "burst" compositions that pointed the way toward 60's-style Color Field painting. This exhibition will present 23 paintings from that year. Hyde Collection, Glens Falls, N.Y., Sept. 25-Dec. 11. (518) 792-1761. www.hydecollection.org.

ANSELM KIEFER, 'HEAVEN AND EARTH' In the 1980's this German artist's huge, viscerally physical, Neo-Expressionist paintings exploring German history and various world mythologies riveted the art world in Europe and the United States. His first museum survey in America in almost 20 years will include more than 60 paintings, books and sculptures dating from 1969 to the present. Modern Art Museum of Fort Worth. Sept. 25-Jan. 8. (817) 738-9215. www.themodern.org. Travels to the Musée d'Art Contemporain de Montréal, Feb. 11-April 30; the Hirshhorn Museum, Washington, June 26-Sept. 10; and the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, Oct. 15, 2006, to Jan. 14, 2007.

'THE PERFECT MEDIUM: PHOTOGRAPHY AND THE OCCULT' This exhibition will present more than 60 photographic works made by people attempting to capture energies ordinarily thought to be more or less supernatural: thoughts, dreams, auras, hallucinations and spirits of the dead. Metropolitan Museum, New York, Sept. 27-Dec. 31. (212) 535-7710. www.metmuseum.org.

'THE SURREAL CALDER' Though not exactly a secret, the sculptor Alexander Calder's close ties to French Surrealism are not commonly noted. This exhibition will present nearly 60 objects made between 1932 and 1947 in which qualities of chance, disjunction, biomorphism and cosmic imagery reveal the influence of the movement on Calder's work. Menil Collection, Houston. Sept. 30-Jan. 15. (713) 525-9400. www.menil.org.

October

CANDIDA HÖFER, 'ARCHITECTURE OF ABSENCE' The German photographer known for her sumptuous pictures of institutional spaces - train stations, libraries, museums and more - has her first North American museum survey. Norton Museum of Art, West Palm Beach, Fla. Oct. 1-Jan. 1. (561) 832-5196. www.norton.org.

'STAR STAR: TOWARD THE CENTER OF ATTENTION' The desire to become famous is studied in sculptures, film, photography, drawings and installations by Daniel Bozhkov, Slater Bradley, Brice Dellsberger, Matt Marello and Jim Torok among others. Contemporary Arts Center, Cincinnati. Oct. 1-Jan. 1. (513) 345-8400. www.contemporaryartscenter.org.

'CATHERINE THE GREAT: ARTS FOR THE EMPIRE - MASTERPIECES FROM THE STATE HERMITAGE MUSEUM' Drawn from collections created by the Russian empress Catherine the Great (1729-96), this blockbuster will present hundreds of works, most never seen in North American, including paintings, jewelry, furniture, architectural models and even a complete, wildly ornate coach. Art Gallery of Ontario, Toronto. Oct. 1-Jan. 1. (416) 979-6648. www.ago.net.

ANDREA ZITTEL, 'CRITICAL SPACE' Over the last 15 years Ms. Zittel has explored relations between art, life, design, consumerism and the landscape in all kinds of inventive, witty and conceptually provocative ways. This is her first comprehensive museum survey. Contemporary Arts Museum, Houston. Oct.-Jan. 1. (713) 284-8250. www.camh.org.

'BLUR OF THE OTHERWORLDLY: CONTEMPORARY ART, TECHNOLOGY AND THE PARANORMAL' A show about artists who use modern communications technologies like photography, film, video, radio, the Internet and digital media to explore things commonly deemed supernatural. Center for Art and Visual Culture, University of Maryland, Baltimore County. Oct. 6 to Dec. 17. (410) 455-3188. www.umbc.edu/cavc.

OSCAR BLUEMNER, 'A PASSION FOR COLOR' Though not among the most celebrated, Bluemner (1867-1938) remains one of the most appealing of the American Modernists to emerge from the circle of Alfred Stieglitz and his 291 Gallery. With a remarkable personal warmth, he painted geometrically simplified American land- and townscapes in jewel-like hues. This exhibition will survey his entire career. Whitney Museum. Oct. 7-Feb. 12. (877) 944-8639. www.whitney.org.

ROY LICHTENSTEIN, 'AMERICAN INDIAN ENCOUNTERS' When the Pop artist Roy Lichtenstein was a student at Ohio State University from 1949 to 1951, he made paintings reflecting his interests in European Modernists and American Indian art. This exhibition will present a selection of those little-known paintings as well as works from 1979 also based on American Indian themes. Montclair Art Museum, Montclair, N.J. Oct. 16-Jan. 8. (973) 746-5555. www.montclairartmuseum.org.

'REMBRANDT AND HIS TIME' Exclusively for the Milwaukee Art Museum, the Albertina in Vienna has organized an exhibition that focuses on Rembrandt's pivotal role in the development of 17th-century Dutch art. It will present 27 works by the great draftsman and humanist, and more that 80 by predecessors, contemporaries and followers. Milwaukee Art Museum, Oct. 8-Jan. 8. (414) 224-3200. www.mam.org.

'CAMILLE CLAUDEL AND RODIN: FATEFUL ENCOUNTER' Meeting when she was 18 and he 42, Camille Claudel and Rodin became artistic collaborators and lovers, and she became a widely recognized sculptor in her own right. This exhibition presents more than 130 works by the two artists along with personal memorabilia. Detroit Institute of Arts. Oct. 9-Feb. 5. (313) 833-7900. www.dia.org.

'ECSTASY: IN AND ABOUT ALTERED STATES' Reflecting the heightened interest in nonordinary states of consciousness animating art today, this show will include paintings, sculpture, videos, installations, photographs and works in other media by 30 internationally known artists. Participants will include Eija-Liisa Ahtila, Olafur Eliasson, Pierre Huyghe, Takashi Murakami, Charles Ray and Pipilotti Rist. Museum of Contemporary Art, Los Angeles. Oct. 9-Feb. 20. (213) 626-6222. www.moca.org. Click here for a related article.

'THE LEGACY OF HOMER' This big, two-museum exhibition will present 133 works of 17th-, 18th- and 19th-century art based on Homeric themes from the collection of the École Nationale Supérieure des Beaux-Arts, Paris. Major French artists will be represented, including David and Ingres, along with many forgotten but still interesting academic painters and sculptors. Dahesh Museum of Art, New York. Oct. 11-Jan. 22. (212) 759-0606. www.daheshmuseum.org. Princeton University Art Museum, Oct. 8-Jan. 15. (609) 258-3788. www.princetonartmuseum.org.

'MEMLING'S PORTRAITS' The great Flemish painter Hans Memling (1435-94) is the subject of an exhibition of approximately 20 portraits by the artist and his school organized in collaboration with the Museo de Arte Thyssen-Bornemisza, Madrid, and the Groeningemuseum, Bruges, Belgium, two sites where it appeared before its final stop in New York. Frick Collection. Oct. 12-December. (212) 288-0700. www.frick.org.

LOUIS COMFORT TIFFANY, 'ARTIST FOR THE AGES' The career of one of America's greatest designers will be examined in an exhibition of approximately 120 objects that concentrate on works in glass. Seattle Art Museum. Oct. 13-Jan. 4. (206) 654-3100. www.seattleartmuseum.org.

'HATSHEPSUT: FROM QUEEN TO PHAROAH' An exhibition of ancient artifacts will inaugurate the de Young's brand new building designed by the Pritzker Prize-winning architects Herzog & de Meuron. The show will present almost 300 objects made under the reigns of the New Kingdom rulers Tuthmosis I, Hatshepsut and Tuthmosis III (1504-1425 B.C.). De Young Museum, San Francisco. Oct. 15-Feb. 5. (415) 863-3330. www.thinker.org. Travels to the Metropolitan Museum, March 28-July 9.

SAM GILLIAM, 'A RETROSPECTIVE' The Washington Color School painter known for his adventurously hedonistic ways with paint, color and support structures has a 40-year retrospective. Corcoran Gallery of Art, Washington. Oct. 15-Jan. 22. (202) 639-1700. www.corcoran.org. Travels to the Speed Art Museum, Louisville, Ky., June 6-Sept. 3; the Telfair Museum of Art, Savannah, Ga., Oct. 11-December 2006; and the Contemporary Arts Museum, Houston, Feb. 10-May 6, 2007.

'SAFE: DESIGN TAKES ON RISK' Exploring all kinds of products designed to protect body and mind, this exhibition will present more than 300 objects, including pacemakers, shelters for the homeless, baby strollers and nutritional information labels. Museum of Modern Art, New York. Oct. 16-Jan. 2. (212) 708-9400. www.moma.org.

'HOUSE OF ORACLES: A HUANG YONG PING RETROSPECTIVE' The contemporary Chinese artist Huang Yong Ping was one of the leading members of the Xiamen Dada movement, which sought to combine Western Modernism with Zen and Taoism. This first retrospective will present works in many different mediums made under the influences of Joseph Beuys, Marcel Duchamp and John Cage as well as traditional Chinese art and philosophy. Walker Art Center, Minneapolis. Oct. 16-Jan. 15. (612) 375-7600. www.walkerart.org.

VINCENT VAN GOGH, 'THE DRAWINGS' This presentation of about 110 works in ink, graphite, chalk, charcoal and watercolor as well as some related paintings will be the first major American retrospective to focus on van Gogh's drawing, which, it can be reasonably argued, was better than his painting. Metropolitan Museum. Oct. 18-Dec. 13. (212) 535-7710. www.metmuseum.org.

'SPLENDOR OF THE WORD: MEDIEVAL AND RENAISSANCE ILLUMINATED MANUSCRIPTS AT THE NEW YORK PUBLIC LIBRARY' The New York Public Library owns one of the best collections of medieval and Renaissance illuminated manuscripts in North America and will put 100 examples on view. New York Public Library at 42nd Street, Humanities and Social Sciences Library. Oct. 21-Feb. 12. (212) 869-8089. www.nypl.org.

'THE IMAGERY OF CHESS REVISITED' In 1944 an exhibition organized by Marcel Duchamp and Max Ernst called "The Imagery of Chess" opened at the Julien Levy Gallery in New York. The Noguchi Museum will recreate that exhibition, including as many of the original chess sets or works relating to chess by Calder, Gorky, Man Ray, Yves Tanguy and Isamu Noguchi, among others, as could be found. Noguchi Museum, Long Island City, N.Y. Oct. 20-March 6. (718) 204-7088. www.noguchi.org.

EGON SCHIELE More than 150 paintings and drawings by the great and perennially popular Viennese expressionist drawn from the collections of Neue Gallerie founders Ronald Lauder and Serge Sabarsky will occupy all exhibition spaces. Neue Galerie, New York. Oct. 21-Feb. 20. (212) 628-6200. www.neuegalerie.org.

'THE WALL: RESHAPING CONTEMPORARY CHINESE ART' The largest exhibition of contemporary Chinese art ever to travel outside China will feature paintings, sculptures, videos, installations, performances and films by more than 50 artists. Albright-Knox Art Gallery. Oct. 21-Jan. 29. Buffalo. (716) 882-8700. www.albrightknox.org.

'TROPICÁLIA' This exhibition examines one of the most influential movements to come out of South America during the last 50 years through artworks by Lygia Clark, Lygia Pape, Hélio Oiticica and others as well as through film, music, advertising, fashion, television and theater. Contemporary artists affected by tropicalismo, like Arto Lindsay, Marepe and Ernesto Neto, will also be included. Museum of Contemporary Art, Chicago. Oct. 22-Jan. 8. (312) 280-2660. www.mcachicago.org.

ELIZABETH MURRAY The career of the influential and beloved painter who exuberantly finessed the gap between Cubist formalism and funky symbolism and shattered the traditional rectangle will be surveyed through about 75 paintings and works on paper dating from the late 1960's to her most recent works. The former MoMA curator Robert Storr is the organizer. An exhibition of Ms. Murray's prints will also be on view at the museum Oct. 23-Jan. 9. Museum of Modern Art, New York. Oct. 23-Jan. 9. (212) 708-9400. www.moma.org.

FRA ANGELICO The first exhibition of the much loved 15th-century Italian to appear in the United States will include approximately 75 paintings, drawings and manuscript illuminations as well as 45 works by assistants and followers. Metropolitan Museum. Oct. 26-Jan. 29. (212) 535-7710. www.metmuseum.org. Click here for a related article.

'IRREDUCIBLE COMPLEXITY: CONTEMPORARY SHORT FORM VIDEO' This exhibition presents 24 videos by an international array of artists. Each is structured around a particular situation, event or performance, some are humorous, some critical and most shot in public spaces. Bronx Museum of the Arts, New York. Oct. 26-Feb. (718) 681-6000. www.bxma.org.

'STYLE AND STATUS: IMPERIAL COSTUMES FROM OTTOMAN TURKEY' Approximately 66 examples of world's finest Ottoman royal textiles were borrowed from the Topkapi Palace Museum, Istanbul, the Kremlin Armory, Moscow, and other important institutions for this exhibition. Freer Gallery of Art, Washington. Oct. 29-Jan. 22. (202) 633-4880. www.asia.si.edu.

'BEYOND THE VISIBLE: THE ART OF ODILON REDON' When it comes to combining beauty and mystery, few artists are in Redon's league. This exhibition will highlight a 2000 donation from the Ian Woodner Family Collection of more than 100 paintings, pastels, watercolors, drawings, prints and illustrated books by the extraordinarily imaginative symbolist and sensualist French artist. Museum of Modern Art, New York. Oct. 30-Jan. 23. (212) 708-9400. www.moma.org.

'PART OBJECT PART SCULPTURE' Reopening after a $15.8 million renovation of its Peter Eisenman-designed building, the Wexner Center will present an ambitious exhibition exploring the 20th-century tradition of artworks that blur the line between sculpture and everyday objects. Among nearly 100 pieces will be works by Marcel Duchamp, Eva Hesse, Jasper Johns, Marcel Broodthaers, Rachel Whiteread and Robert Gober. Wexner Center for the Arts, Columbus, Ohio. Oct. 30-Feb. 26. (614) 292-3535. www.wexarts.org.

November

'WEST AFRICAN GOLD: AKAN REGALIA FROM THE GLASSELL COLLECTION' This exhibition of 130 examples of fine gold work - including jewelry, crowns, sandals, swords, figurative sculptures and fabrics - is drawn from a collection of African gold owned by the Museum of Fine Arts, Houston, that is considered one of the most important in the world. Museum of Fine Art, Boston. Nov. 2-March 26. (617) 267-9300. www.mfa.org.

'BORN OF CLAY' Beginning with an example of one of the earliest known Western Hemisphere ceramics - a female figure from Valdivia, Ecuador, from 3000 to 1500 B.C. - and ending with works from the late 20th century, this exhibition of 300 objects from the collection of the National Museum of the American Indian will survey ceramic craftsmanship from the Andes, Meso America and the eastern and southwestern regions of the United States. National Museum of the American Indian, New York. Nov. 3-May 2006. (212) 514-3700. www.americanindian.si.edu.

'PERFORMA 05' The most ephemeral and provocative of art forms gets its own biennial. The first biennial of new visual art performance will take place at various sites in New York including Anthology Film Archives, Artists Space and Art in General. The performance art historian and critic RoseLee Goldberg is chief curator, and participants will include the Danish video artist Jesper Just, the Belgian Francis Alÿs and the American Laurie Simmons, who will present a musical puppet show. Nov. 3-21. (212) 533-5720. http://performa-arts.org.

MARINA ABRAMOVIC, 'SEVEN EASY PIECES' On each of five consecutive nights, the artist known for her physically and psychologically challenging solo performances will recreate a famous performance work from the 60's or 70's by Vito Acconci, Joseph Beuys, Valie Export, Bruce Nauman and Gina Pane. On the sixth night she will recreate one of her own past works and on the seventh she will present the premier of a new piece. Guggenheim Museum, New York. Nov. 9-Nov. 15. (212) 423-3500. www.guggenheim.org.

'FREQUENCY' An exhibition of works by 30 of the most promising emerging black artists of 2005. Paintings, drawings, sculpture, photography, videos and digital works will reveal influences ranging from Abstract Expressionism to hip-hop and sports culture. Studio Museum in Harlem, New York. Nov. 9-12. (212) 864-4500. www.studiomuseum.org.

ANDREW WYETH, 'MEMORY AND MAGIC' The mythic realism of one of America's most popular painters is explored in an eight-decade retrospective of approximately 100 tempera paintings, watercolors and drawings, many from the personal collection of the artist and his wife. High Museum of Art, Atlanta. Nov. 12-Feb. 26. (404) 733-4400. www.high.org. Travels to the Philadelphia Museum of Art, March 29-July 16.
