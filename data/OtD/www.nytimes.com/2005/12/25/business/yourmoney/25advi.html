How to Shush the Office Magpie

  • Sign In to E-Mail This
  • Printer-Friendly
  • Reprints
  • Save Article
By MATT VILLANO
Published: December 25, 2005

Q. One of your co-workers loves to chat, and whenever he isn't dealing with a pressing deadline, he barges into your work space to shoot the breeze. How do you restore peace and quiet?

Skip to next paragraph
Sean Kelly

A. As tempted as you may be to tell the office chatterbox to scram, it's best to approach this grating situation with tact, said Gini Graham Scott, president of Changemakers, a consulting firm in Oakland, Calif. "Be as diplomatic as possible," said Ms. Scott, the author of "A Survival Guide for Working with Bad Bosses" (Amacom, 2005). "Try to show respect and compassion for the other person when you do this, but if the person doesn't get the hint, then you have to be firm."

Q. What drives certain co-workers to talk excessively?

A. Whether the topic is "Desperate Housewives" or the conflict in Iraq, everybody likes to chat at work from time to time. But some people simply do not know when to stop.

According to Cheryl A. Dellasega, professor of humanities at the Pennsylvania State University College of Medicine in Hershey, many chatterboxes are procrastinators who use banter to avoid the tasks at hand. Sometimes anxiety, nervousness or loneliness can make a person prone to chattering. In rare cases, she said, excessive talking can be a form of hostility.

"Dominating the conversation is a form of aggressive behavior in which you become the victim," she said. "This behavior is not only annoying, but it's also a subtle form of harassment."

Q. Are there nonverbal clues that will help tell a garrulous colleague to leave you alone?

A. If you continue staring at the computer screen in front of you and refuse to make eye contact, the talker may take the hint. Another approach is to check your watch repeatedly. Dr. Cal LeMon, president of Executive Enrichment, a management consulting firm in Springfield, Mo., said that more overt gestures include packing your briefcase and putting your hand up like a policeman stopping traffic. "Open-palmed gestures say to the person that you're trying to wrap things up," Dr. LeMon said.

Using technology is another option. Alisa Cohn, president of AC & Associates, an executive coaching company in Brookline, Mass., said she once advised a client to set her Microsoft Outlook calendar program to chime every 10 minutes. When the office chatterbox heard the chime, he assumed that Ms. Cohn's client had an appointment. "The chatterbox didn't respond to physical clues, but when the Outlook went off, he respected it and told my client, 'It seems you have a meeting,' " Ms. Cohn said. "The little buzz ended up giving her a graceful way out."

Q. What do you do if your colleague will not take a hint?

A. Then it's time to be more direct. Start with the facts: tally the number of times this person interrupts you throughout the day, note the length of each visit, and then use these statistics in a confrontation about the behavior.

Simply outlining the problem may not solve it. Larina Kase, president of Performance and Success Coaching, a consulting firm in Philadelphia, said it was also important to have a plan for further corrective action. Ask the talker if you can catch up over lunch. Let him know how to tell whether you have time to talk (for example, if your door is open - if you're lucky enough to have a door).

Once you have established some ground rules, "it's a good idea to have a more general discussion about how you can't afford to chat during work," Ms. Kase said. "Explain to him that it's hard for you to regain focus after interruptions."

Q. Is it ever acceptable to lash out at the gabber?

A. Generally, yelling in the office is not a great idea. You never know who else may be listening. And shouting is likely to make an unpleasant situation worse. "The chatterbox is annoying, but he might be someone who can help you down the road," said Don Gabor, president of Conversation Arts Media, a consulting company in Brooklyn. "People spend 8 to 10 hours a day in the workplace, so it pays to do your best not to alienate anyone."

Q. At what point do you take the situation to your boss?

A. Nobody likes to be a tattletale, but if your chatty co-worker demonstrates that he simply is not going to let you work, you may need to take the problem to a higher authority.

The last thing you want is for your colleague to hinder your own ability to meet deadlines. Besides, at some point, your disruptive office mate has to stop talking and listen to reason.

Jonathan Jordan, account manager at 919 Marketing, a marketing firm in Holly Hills, N.C., learned this the hard way. Earlier in his career, Mr. Jordan encountered what he calls "the world's most long-winded man." After trying unsuccessfully to silence this co-worker, Mr. Jordan turned to his boss for help.

Sensing Mr. Jordan's frustration, the boss agreed to summon Mr. Jordan for a meeting every time the talker approached. Finally, even the boss became aggravated and complained to his superiors, who disciplined the bloviator and corrected his behavioral problem.

"I don't see how it ever would have stopped without help from my boss and his boss," Mr. Jordan said. "Without formal intervention, I don't think there was any other way we actually could have gotten back to work."
