    A Man in Constant Motion

    Lil Buck Expands Jookin’s World

    By MELENA RYZIKJUNE 26, 2014

    Inside
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      BOSTON — The dancer known as Lil Buck was walking across a stage in slow motion, four beautifully exaggerated steps, like a moment out of “The Matrix.” It was the springtime benefit for the Institute of Contemporary Art here, and he was improvising to a string quartet.

      The audience, well-heeled local arts patrons, had probably come for Yo-Yo Ma, also on the evening’s program, not the lithe young man with the mien and facial hair of a teenager, in high-tops and baseball cap. They gasped when Lil Buck accomplished a signature move, gliding smoothly across the floor as if levitating. He moved so that the notes seemed to vibrate up his body, his sneakers squeaking as he pirouetted.

      “I think he’s a genius,” Mr. Ma said after the show. A video of their duet to Camille Saint-Saëns’s “The Swan” went viral in 2011; they have since performed it around the world — “one of the greatest experiences of my life,” Mr. Ma said.

      At 26, Lil Buck, born Charles Riley, has already carved out a niche that almost no other dancer can fill, bouncing from music videos (that’s him, slo-mo spinning through Janelle Monáe’s “Tightrope”) to a Super Bowl halftime show (2012, with Madonna) to Lincoln Center, where in April he was a star soloist in the debut of a ballet by the French artist JR. He introduced mainstream audiences to jookin, a style of street dance born in his hometown, Memphis, whose intricate freestyle footwork has captivated critics.

      All this barely five years after Lil Buck moved to Los Angeles, where he began his career busking on the Santa Monica Pier. Now he’s an artist-in-residence at the Aspen Institute, speaking this weekend at its Aspen Ideas Festival, where the lineup includes Robert De Niro and Al Gore. In the fall he will perform new works choreographed for him by Damian Woetzel at City Center in New York and the Works & Process series at the Guggenheim Museum. This month, you might have caught him as a guest judge on Fox’s “So You Think You Can Dance.”

      But even as his schedule has grown fuller, his celebrity showier, Lil Buck has kept reaching back to dance at benefits, work with children and teach classes. “If you have the gift to affect people in a good way and in an artistic way and in an inspiring way, then I believe that’s a responsibility,” he said.

      He’s part of a recent wave of young, iconoclastic artists who started on the streets, rose to acclaim in the worlds of performance and fine art, and ever mindful of the distances they’ve traveled, are trying to pull others up with them. “It’s just like any other superhero,” Lil Buck said, grinning. They can use their superpowers “for good or bad. And I’m using mine for good.”

      Here’s a glimpse into his high-flying life.

      April 25, 4:15 p.m.

      New York City Ballet rehearsal room, Lincoln Center

      Lil Buck and JR, the street artist known for his large-scale photographs, have been tight since they met at a TED talk in 2011, and their unlikely project with Lincoln Center quickly piqued attention. In one of the final rehearsals before opening night Lil Buck strolled in long after the corps de ballet began practicing, trailed by a French documentary crew. He warmed up, headphones on, torquing his body in seemingly impossible angles before joining the rehearsal and commanding the floor.

      Continue reading the main story

      “Remember, when you’re onstage, there should never be a smile,” JR instructed. His ballet, “Les Bosquets,” was based on the 2005 riots in the Parisian suburb of the same name. Lil Buck and his pas de deux partner, the City Ballet soloist Lauren Lovette, portrayed people caught up in the action, he mirroring her moves on point in his high-tops. He swept across the floor, doing his version of balletic swan arms — shoulders forward, muscles visible — improvising spins on his knees and bowing his legs, his ankles swiveling.

      “He has something that you really can’t teach,” Ms. Lovette said later. “He has this connection with his body — he understands it so well, he can really get it to do anything he wants it to do. Most people don’t have that kind of mind-body control, that coordination.”

      “I only know this because I’ve tried to do a lot of what he does,” she added. “It’s not just the moves that he’s doing, it’s his musicality. He’s hearing stuff that not everybody can hear, and then he translates it so quickly into movement so it looks like you’re watching music.”

      Ms. Monáe and Madonna attended the Lincoln Center shows. “JR invited Robert De Niro so I had to, you know, come with something,” Lil Buck said gleefully.

      The eight-minute piece, which opened City Ballet’s spring season, was included in its best-selling week of repertory programming for 2013-14, a spokesman said. Critics were not wowed, sniffing at JR’s lack of choreographic expertise, but it delivered on the company’s goal, attracting younger audiences, who cheered for Ms. Lovette and Lil Buck.

      “The way he manipulates his body never seems real, yet it is,” said Ms. Monáe, who called him her favorite living dancer. “I love the fact that he has the Memphis streets running through his veins as an artist.”

      May 5, 6 p.m.

      “Dance Against Cancer” benefit, AXA Equitable Center

      The night after he wrapped “Les Bosquets,” Lil Buck was back in a Midtown Manhattan theater, rehearsing a jazzy number with a group of children. He could’ve been forgiven for taking a break, but the annual benefit, which he’d joined before, was meaningful. “I dedicate most of my shows to my grandfather,” who had Parkinson’s and cancer, Lil Buck said. “He believed in me a lot.”

      Onstage, he clowned with the kids, some in school uniforms, giving an impromptu lesson in flexing, a street style he’s perfected, like a goofball kid himself.

      Growing up in Memphis, where his mother, Sabrina Moore, worked at a clothing store and his father, Charles Riley Jr., drove a truck, Lil Buck — he’s been called that since childhood — picked up jookin from his older sister, Stephanie, when he was about 12. The style, which grew out of a move called the Gangsta Walk, was gaining ground in Memphis; when he encountered a local expert nicknamed BoBo gliding in a dance circle at the Crystal Palace roller rink, he was hooked.

      “I was a huge fan of Michael Jackson, but to me this guy was moving even crazier than Michael,” Lil Buck said. “That’s when I knew this is it, this is what I’m going to do.”

      Continue reading the main story

      He had moves and flexibility but, he said, “I wasn’t naturally good at it.” He enrolled in dance classes and transferred to an arts high school, studied hip-hop and ballet, developing his own style.

      Continue reading the main story

      “This was the only kid that danced as much as he breathed, and I mean literally,” said Tarrik Moore, the owner of U-Dig Dance Academy in Memphis, where Lil Buck studied. “This dude would get a pair of shoes and rip ’em up in like two days.”

      Lil Buck was an unfocused student, except when it came to dance. “My mom doesn’t know this, but I used to stay up all night practicing, until sunup,” he said. “And then it’s time to go to school, and I’m knocked out at school.”

      But being at an institution where his talent was considered a calling changed his life. “I didn’t really see myself as having this big role in the arts, and having this thing about me that really inspires a mass amount of people,” he said. “Being at that school really brought that out.”

      At 19, with Ron Myles, another jooker, he moved to Los Angeles. It was the first time he’d been on a plane. “Everybody was worried, because I was so young, but I was so driven that nothing could stop me,” he recalled.

      Now Lil Buck is an advocate for arts education, his subject at the Aspen Institute. Though he hopes to elevate jookin — “I see jookin as this dance that can be put in the same category as hip-hop, ballet, jazz, modern,” he said — his ultimate goal is to start a foundation promoting the arts for children. “The whole mission is way bigger than jookin now, it’s all about making an impact in this world,” he said.

      Mr. Woetzel, a former principal dancer with City Ballet, an appointee to the President’s Committee on the Arts and the Humanities and the director of arts programs for the Aspen Institute, said: “Buck is definitely a citizen-artist. His path has come through dance but also through education. He has a great ability to connect.”

      It was Mr. Woetzel’s wife, the former ballerina Heather Watts, who spotted one of Lil Buck’s early videos on YouTube, after a Memphis ballet teacher, Katie Smythe, had him dance to “The Swan.” “It was instantly clear that this was a dancer who was bridging different worlds,” said Mr. Woetzel, who programmed Lil Buck’s first “Swan” with Mr. Ma, and is working on a full-length dance for him.

      After he did the opening number at the benefit, Lil Buck hung around with other dancers, demonstrating hip-hop versions of their pliés and jetés. “One time I had these low-top Pumas, and I spin so hard in them, that they started smoking,” he said backstage. “I’m dead serious. I spinned a huge hole in the side of them.”

      A ballerina, stretching, looked up. “If my toe shoes ever start smoking,” she said, “I’ll know I’ve arrived.”

      May 7, noon

      Home, Las Vegas

      Lil Buck lives in Las Vegas, but only barely — he often touches down at his apartment just long enough to appear in Cirque du Soleil’s “Michael Jackson: One,” where he is in rotation as a dancer. He went to Los Angeles for business meetings — he is appearing in ads for Nissan — before returning to New York to pose in front of paintings at the Frieze Art Fair for an Instagram feature.

      Continue reading the main story

      “I’m used to insane whirlwinds,” he said of his schedule. When he has downtime, “I become a spectator instead of a performer,” he added. “I just chill.”

      But he wasn’t low-key for long. The French filmmakers who had followed him at Lincoln Center had aired their documentary on Canal Plus, and suddenly Lil Buck was being courted by European audiences. “My social media’s been going crazy,” he said, pleased. With the French crew filming, he had stopped over in Memphis, the first time he had been back in months — an absence that left some other Memphis jookers grumbling.

      May 16, 3:30 p.m.

      Rehearsal and benefit show at the Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston

      “That’s the jam,” Lil Buck said as James Brown’s “Please, Please, Please” streamed from his phone during a break. He intently watched a video of Brown dancing, and got up on his own toes instantly, mimicking. “I can’t mash it with sneakers,” he said. “You need loafers to mash it like he’s doing.”

      Lil Buck has worked with classically trained choreographers like Benjamin Millepied and borrowed b-boy techniques. But most of his moves are improvised; he dreams in dance moves. “I battle myself in my head a lot,” he said. “I have an alter ego, when I go to sleep. And he always kicks my butt, because he’s doing some impossible stuff.”

      In the green room after his performance, he hung out in his socks, talking about his travels and veganism. His joy in his ability to embody music is infectious: When Lil Buck finally made it to the after-party, through a gauntlet of fans requesting selfies with him (all were obliged), he joined but somehow did not intimidate the guests dancing to tunes D.J.’ed by Questlove. Instead, his presence seemed to embolden them. He danced with young and old, male and female, to Nirvana and Bell Biv DeVoe.

      His crossover popularity is another sign of the slackening cultural boundaries between the street and the academy  — even if the high art world was quicker to accept him with a Saint-Saëns soundtrack than a hip-hop beat. His virtuosity and authenticity shone through, Mr. Ma said. 

      Lil Buck is nimble in any context. At a dinner after the program, he was introduced to Gov. Deval Patrick of Massachusetts, and they chatted easily about music — Mr. Patrick’s father was a saxophonist who played with Sun Ra. Mr. Patrick had missed the show, so standing beside their table, Mr. Ma clapped a beat and Lil Buck did an impromptu routine.

      The governor was impressed by how smooth Lil Buck was.

      “Yeah,” Lil Buck said, chuckling, “I get that a lot.”
