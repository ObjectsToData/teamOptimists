    Spare Times for Feb. 7-13

    By ANNE MANCUSO and PHILIP RICHARDSONFEB. 6, 2014

    Inside

      ATHENA FILM FESTIVAL (Friday through Sunday) This annual festival celebrating strong women in film started on Thursday and continues with screenings, discussions and other events at Barnard College, 3009 Broadway, between 116th and 120th Streets, Morningside Heights.

      The centerpiece is “Decoding Annie Parker,” Steven Bernstein’s 2013 film starring Helen Hunt, who portrays a geneticist. It is based on the true story of two women whose lives were affected by breast cancer, and will be shown on Friday at 8 p.m. Also on Friday night at 8: “Reaching for the Moon,” Bruno Barreto’s 2013 feature about the relationship between the American poet Elizabeth Bishop, played by Miranda Otto, and the Brazilian architect Lota de Macedo Soares, played by Glória Pires.

      A foster-care center for at-risk teenagers is the setting of “Short Term 12,” with Brie Larson and Keith Stanfield, a 2013 feature written and directed by Destin Daniel Cretton. It will be shown on Saturday at 6 p.m. The writer Alice Walker is the subject of “Alice Walker: Beauty in Truth,” a 2013 documentary by Pratibha Parmar. It will be screened on Saturday at 9 p.m.

      Callie Khouri, who won an Oscar in 1992 for her original screenplay for “Thelma and Louise,” will talk on Saturday at 3 p.m. Screenings and events are $12 each, $5 for students; an all-events pass is $65, $20 for students. Details: athenafilmfestival.com. (Anne Mancuso)

       

      Museums and Sites

      Metropolitan Museum of Art: ‘Gridiron Greats: Vintage Football Cards in the Collection of Jefferson R. Burdick’ (through Monday) This is the last weekend to see these vintage football cards that are part of this exhibition of football-related items from the museum’s collection. Also in the display are photographs and a collegiate football sweater dating to the early 1900s. Friday and Saturday from 10 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Sunday and Monday from 10 a.m. to 5:30 p.m.; 212-535-7710, metmuseum.org; suggested admission: $25, $17 for 65+, $12 for students, free for those 12 and younger.

      American Museum of Natural History: ‘The Power of Poison’ (through Aug. 10) The good and bad uses of poison are covered in this interactive exhibition, which includes animation and a bit of theater. In his review for The New York Times, Edward Rothstein said that this is one of the museum’s “most theatrical exhibitions” and that the curating team has “combined elements with deft curatorial alchemy, touching on every taxonomical category.” Daily from 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m., Central Park West and 79th Street, 212-769-5200, amnh.org; $22, $17 for students and 60+, $12.50 for children 2 to 12.

      Brooklyn Historical Society: ‘Brooklyn Abolitionists/In Pursuit of Freedom’ (Tuesday) This interactive exhibition of manuscripts, paintings and maps tells the story of the Brooklynites who were instrumental in the abolition of slavery. On Tuesday at 6:30 p.m., “The Abolitionists,” a film by Rob Rapley, will be screened, and it will be followed by a discussion with Prithi Kanakamedala, a historian at the society, and Manisha Sinha, a professor of Afro-American studies at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst. The event is free with admission. The exhibition can be seen Wednesdays through Sundays from noon to 5 p.m., 128 Pierrepont Street, near Clinton Street, Brooklyn Heights, 718-222-4111, brooklynhistory.org; suggested admission: $10, $6 for students 12 and older and those 62+, free for children under 12.

      Metropolitan Museum of Art: ‘Early American Guitars: The Instruments of C.F. Martin’ (through Dec. 7) More than 20 guitars made by the 19th-century German-born craftsman Christian Frederick Martin are included in this display of 35 vintage instruments drawn from the museum’s permanent collection as well as the Martin Guitar Museum in Nazareth, Pa., and private collections. Among the Martin guitars on view: a 1939 model made by the company he founded, C.F. Martin & Co, that was played by Eric Clapton. Special events are planned in conjunction with the exhibition, including a performance later this month by Rosanne Cash, who will play songs of her father’s — Johnny Cash — on Martin guitars that he owned and played. Fridays and Saturdays from 10 a.m. to 9 p.m., Sundays through Thursdays from 10 a.m. to 5:30 p.m., 212-535-7710, metmuseum.org; suggested admission: $25, $17 for 65+, $12 for student, free for members and children under 12.

      Museum of Chinese in America: Chinatown Exhibitions (through April 13) The photographer Annie Ling and the artist Tomie Arai present images of Chinatown from two different points of view and through different media. Ms. Ling’s “A Floating Population” exhibition captures the neighborhood in nearly 100 photographs, while Ms. Arai’s “Portraits of New York Chinatown” tells tales of gentrification and displacement through oral history, screen printing and personal artifacts. Museum hours daily, except Monday, from 11 a.m. to 6 p.m., Thursdays until 9 p.m., 215 Centre Street, between Howard and Grand Streets, Lower Manhattan, 212-619-4785, mocanyc.org. Museum admission: $10; $5 for students and 65+; free for children under 12; free for members. Walking tour: $15, $12 for students and 65+, $8 for members, free for children under 5.

      Museum of the City of New York: ‘Unfinished Work: Views of Superstorm Sandy From the Ground Up’ (through March 2) When Hurricane Sandy pounded the area last year, amateur and professional photographers took to the streets to document the storm’s devastation and the recovery efforts that followed. More than 200 of those color and black-and-white images are included in “Rising Waters: Photographs of Sandy,” organized with the help of the International Center of Photography. The display is grouped into six categories — storm, destruction, coping, home, relief and not over. The display can be seen daily from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m., Fifth Avenue at 103rd Street, 212-534-1672, mcny.org; $10, $6 for students, free for members and children 12 and younger; $20 for families.

      Museum of the City of New York: ‘Graffiti 101: New York Subway Art of the 1970s and ’80s’ (Wednesday) Chris Pape, a painter and graffiti artist who started painting murals at 14, will talk about his experiences. The talk, at 6:30 p.m., is presented in conjunction with “City as Canvas: New York City Graffiti From the Martin Wong Collection,” an exhibition of artists’ sketchbooks, artwork and photographs on view through Aug. 24. Fifth Avenue, at 103rd Street, 212-534-1672, mcny.org; $16, $12 for students.

      Museum of the Moving Image: ‘Indie Essentials: 25 Must-Play Video Games’ (through March 2) Visitors can dive into the world of independent video games at this exhibition, presented by the Museum of the Moving Image and IndieCade: the International Festival of Independent Games. A selection of games that have affected game design and culture in the last decade can be played. Saturdays and Sundays from 11:30 a.m. to 7 p.m.; Wednesdays and Thursdays from 10:30 a.m. to 5 p.m., Fridays till 8 p.m.; 35th Avenue at 37th Street, Astoria, Queens, 718-784-0077, movingimage.us; $12; $9 for students and 65+; $6 for ages 3 to 12; free for under 3.

      New York Public Library: AIDS Exhibition (through April 4) The exhibition “Why We Fight: Remembering AIDS Activism” uses manuscripts, videos and other items from the Library’s collections to tell the story of the grass-roots groups and individuals that were at the forefront of efforts to inform the public about the disease and help stop its spread. Fridays, Saturdays, Mondays and Thursdays from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m., Tuesdays and Wednesdays from 10 a.m. to 8 p.m. Stephen A. Schwarzman Building, New York Public Library, 917-275-6975, nypl.org/events/exhibitions/why-we-fight; free.

      New York Public Library for the Performing Arts: ‘Ladies and Gentlemen...the Beatles!’ (Monday) Drawn from the Library’s collection, as well as that of the Grammy Museum at L.A. Live and private collectors, this exhibition of more than 400 items examines the cultural, social and musical impact of the band on American life after its first performance here in 1964. Included are clothing, lyric sheets and the re-creation of a teenager’s bedroom filled with Beatles memorabilia. On Monday at 6 p.m., Robert Santelli, executive director of the Grammy Museum, will discuss the influence of America on the Fab Four before they made their first trip here. Also on hand for the talk: Mark Lewisohn, author of “Tune In: The Beatles All These Years,” the first in a trilogy that was released in October. Free tickets to the talk will be distributed first come first served, beginning at 5 p.m. The exhibition, on view through May 10, can be seen Tuesdays, Wednesdays, Fridays and Saturday from noon to 6 p.m. and Mondays and Thursdays from noon to 8 p.m., 111 Amsterdam Avenue, at 65th Street, Lincoln Center, 917-275-6975, tinyurl.com/kledtsf; free.

      New York Society Library: ‘From the Western Front and Beyond: The Writings of World War I’ (through Nov. 15) Written accounts of the war, from the letters of soldiers to the prose of those not on the front lines, are included in this display commemorating the centenary of the First World War, which began in 1914. Drawn from this library’s collection, the exhibition, on view through Nov. 15, includes pieces by writers like Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, George Bernard Shaw, Rudyard Kipling and H.G. Wells as well as writings by ambulance drivers, clergy members and others. There are also works by the Scottish etcher Muirhead Bone. Mondays, Wednesdays, Fridays and Saturdays from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Tuesdays and Thursdays from 9 a.m. to 7 p.m.; Sundays from 1 to 5 p.m., 53 East 79th Street, Manhattan, 212-288-6900, nysoclib.org; free.

      New York Transit Museum: ‘ElectriCity: Powering New York’s Rails’ Ever wonder how the third rail works? Visitors to “ElectriCity: Powering New York’s Rails” can view a display of items from the museum collection — including switches and circuit breakers — that illustrate how electricity powers the subway system. Tuesdays through Fridays from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m., Saturdays and Sundays from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m., Boerum Place and Schermerhorn Street, Brooklyn Heights, 718-694-1600, mta.info/mta/museum; $7, $5 for 2 to 17 and 62+, free for members and on Wednesdays for those 62+.

      Paley Center for Media: The Olympic Games and a Beatles Fest (Saturday, Sunday, Wednesday and Thursday) On select days from Saturday through Feb. 23, visitors can watch daytime coverage of the Olympics on the center’s big screen. The free programming — a celebration of the center’s United States Olympic Archive, featuring televised Olympic competitions dating to 1960 — will begin with figure skating on Saturday at noon (and continue on Sunday, Wednesday and Thursday). The 50th anniversary of the Beatles’s arrival in the United States will be commemorated with screenings of two programs on Saturday and Sunday at 1 p.m.: “The Ed Sullivan Show” episode from Feb. 9, 1964, with the group’s performance, and “What’s Happening! The Beatles in the U.S.A.,” a 1964 documentary about the band’s tour that was made by David and Albert Maysles for Granada Television. 25 West 52nd Street, Manhattan, 212-621-6800, paleycenter.org; screenings included in admission: $10, $8 for students and 65+, $5 for 13 and younger.

      Queens Botanical Garden: Lunar New Year Celebration (Saturday) Accompanied by musicians on traditional instruments, the New York Chinese Chorus will perform folk and popular songs at this free celebration of the Lunar New Year. The event, from 1 to 4 p.m., will also feature workshops in mixing teas and demonstrations of traditional tea ceremonies. 43-50 Main Street, Flushing, Queens, 718-886-3800, Ext. 204, queensbotanical.org; free, but workshops are $2 to $5.

      Yeshiva University Museum at the Center for Jewish History: ‘Light and Shadows: The Story of Iranian Jews’ (through April 27) This exhibition tells the story of Jewish settlement in Persia nearly 3,000 years ago, with manuscripts, artwork and Judaica. Fridays from 11 a.m. to 2:30 p.m.; Sundays, Tuesdays and Thursdays from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Mondays from 5 to 8 p.m.; Wednesdays from 11 a.m. to 8 p.m., 15 West 16th Street, 212-294-8330, yumuseum.org; $8, $6 for students, free for children under 5, museum members and students, faculty and administration members of Yeshiva University, free on Monday and Wednesday from 5 to 8 p.m., and Friday.

      Gardens

      New York Botanical Garden: ‘Tropical Paradise’ (through Feb. 23) Permanent residents of the garden that thrive in warm weather, like the African bush lily and the Caribbean passion flower, are on center stage for this display in the Enid A. Haupt Conservatory. There are also related activities for visitors, including weekly photography competitions for best close-up and wide-range images of the flowers; winning photographers will receive a spot in one of the photography classes at the Garden. Tuesdays through Sundays from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., Bronx River Parkway (Exit 7W) and Fordham Road, the Bronx, 718-817-8777, nybg.org; $20, $18 for students and 65+, $10 for children 2 to 12, free for those under 2.

      Black History Month

      American Museum of Natural History: ‘Give Your Voice: Honor Black History’ (Saturday) “Dream on Robben,” a new work composed by the jazz trombonist Delfeayo Marsalis and based on the life of Nelson Mandela, will be among the music at this celebration. Mr. Marsalis and his sextet will perform the piece with the Young People’s Chorus of New York City and the hip-hop artist Darryl McDaniels of Run-DMC. Camille A. Brown & Dancers, the New Orleans Swamp Donkeys Traditional Jass Band and the pianist Samora Abayomi Pinderhughes are also scheduled to perform. From noon to 5 p.m., Central Park West and 79th Street, 212-769-5200, amnh.org; suggested admission: $22, $12.50 for those 2 to 12, $17 for students and 60+.

      A Changing Harlem (Tuesday) The shift in the social and economic fabric of Harlem will be illustrated in words and photographs at 6:30 p.m. at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York, Elebash Recital Hall, 365 Fifth Avenue, at 34th Street. Camilo José Vergara, a photographer whose most recent book is “Harlem: The Unmaking of a Ghetto,” will lead the discussion. Also taking part: Eric K. Washington, author of “Manhattanville: Old Heart of West Harlem”; Phil Bicker, a senior photo editor at Time; and Sharon Zukin, a sociology professor. This free event is also sponsored by the Gotham Center for New York City History. Attendance is full, but standby tickets may be available starting at 5:30 p.m. 212-817-8215, tinyurl.com/pltkk3m.

      Morris Jumel Mansion: ‘Anne Northup: Slavery and the Birth of American Cuisine’ (Saturday) The life of Anne Northup, whose husband, Solomon, is the subject of the film “12 Years a Slave,” is the focus of this day of activities and dinner. A professional cook, Ms. Northup once worked for Eliza Jumel, former mistress of the mansion. A discussion about their relationship will begin at 2 p.m., and will be followed by a tour of the mansion and neighborhood at 3 p.m. The dinner menu (drawn from records of Ms. Northup’s recipes and the food bills) will include pepper pot soup, Madeira ham, roast chicken and dandelion salad. It will be served at 5 p.m. The event is sponsored by the New York Council for the Humanities. Reservations are required. 65 Jumel Terrace, at Sylvan Terrace, Washington Heights, 212-923-8008, morrisjumel.org; $65 for dinner, talks and tour; $10 for talks and tour only.

      Open House Weekend at the Apollo Theater (Saturday and Sunday) This weekend of free music and discussion will celebrate black history month and the 80th anniversary of the theater. An afternoon of jazz and gospel on Saturday will feature the singer Nicole Vanessa Ortiz; the singer and guitarist Keith “the Captain” Gamble; the flutist Claudia Hayden; and cast members of the Broadway show “Motown.” Sunday consists of the communal singing of African-American spirituals with Imani Uzuri and a performance, at 5 p.m., by the gospel singer Shirley Caesar. Saturday from 1 to 5 p.m.; Sunday from 2 to 4 p.m., Apollo Theater, 253 West 125th Street, Harlem, 800-745-3000, apollotheater.org.

      Voices of Freedom: Jazz Celebration of Black History Month (Wednesday) This monthlong series in celebration of black history month and the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. continues with a performance by the jazz pianist George Cables and the trombonist Steve Turre. At 12:30 p.m., Winter Garden, World Financial Center, West Street, south of Vesey Street, Lower Manhattan, 212-417-2414, brookfieldplaceny.com/voicesoffreedom.

      Events

      Athena Film Festival (through Sunday) This annual festival honoring the role of women in film and life — the fourth — continues with screenings, panel discussions and other events at various locations at Barnard College, 3009 Broadway, between 116th and 120th Streets, Morningside Heights. Films this weekend include “Reaching for the Moon” (Friday at 9:30 p.m.), a depiction by Bruno Barreto of the romantic relationship between the American poet Elizabeth Bishop and the Brazilian architect Lota de Macedo Soares; the documentary “Alice Walker: Beauty in Truth” by Pratibha Parmar on Saturday at 9 p.m.; and “Geraldine Ferraro: Paving the Way,” a documentary by Donna Zaccaro about her mother, a former member of Congress and the Democratic candidate for vice president in 1984, which closes the festival on Sunday at 6 p.m. Screenings are $12, $5 for students; an all-events pass is $65, $20 for students. A schedule is at athenafilmfestival.com.

      Holiday Train Show at Grand Central Terminal (through Feb. 23) This 12th annual show, featuring a 34-foot-long Lionel model train layout, is on display at the New York Transit Museum Gallery Annex and Store near the Station Master’s Office. Weekdays from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturdays from 10 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Sundays from 10 a.m. to 7 p.m.; mta.info/mta/museum; New York Transit Museum Gallery Annex and Store, Grand Central Terminal, 212-878-0106.

      Thunderbird American Indian Dancers Annual Dance Concert (Friday through Sunday) Members of the Thunderbird American Indian Dancers will share the traditional dances, stories and music of the Iroquois and other tribes from the Southwest and Great Plains. Friday at 8 p.m., Saturday at 3 and 8 p.m., Sunday at 3 p.m., Theater for the New City, 155 First Avenue, at Ninth Street, East Village, 212-254-1109, theaterforthenewcity.net; $10, $1 for those under 12 at the matinees.

      Spoken Word

      Conversation with Meredith Monk (Wednesday) The composer, singer and choreographer Meredith Monk will talk about her career with Adam Gopnik, a writer for The New Yorker. The discussion is part of the “Conversations With Chevaliers” series, which features those who have been awarded this honor by the French government. The event is also sponsored by the Cultural Services division of the French Embassy. At 8:15 p.m., 92nd Street Y, 1395 Lexington Avenue, Manhattan, 212-415-5500, 92y.org; $29 to $36, $15 for those 35 and younger.

      Hemingways on Hemingway (Tuesday) The literary and familial history of the Hemingways will be discussed by two of Ernest Hemingway’s descendants: Cristen Hemingway Jaynes, a great-granddaughter and author of “The Smallest of Entryways,” a 2012 collection of short stories, and John Hemingway, a grandson whose 2007 memoir, “Strange Tribe,” chronicled the lives of his father and grandfather. At 7 p.m., Strand Book Store, 828 Broadway, at 12th Street, 212-473-1452, strandbooks.com; purchase of a $15 book or gift card.

      Readings by A.M. Homes and Dorthe Nors (Tuesday) The authors will read from their latest books. Ms. Homes’s newest novel, “May We Be Forgiven,” was released in 2012, and Ms. Nors’s “Karate Chop: Stories,” with a translation by Martin Aitken, was released this month. Brigid Hughes, founding editor of the literary magazine A Public Space is the moderator. At 7 p.m., Center for Fiction, 17 East 47th Street, Manhattan, 212-755-6710, centerforfiction.org; free.

      Tuesday Talks: The Presidents (Tuesday) Just in time for Presidents’ Day, this series at the Asphalt Green at Battery Park City features a look at our greatest and worst presidents. Leading the discussion will be Dennis Gaffney, a journalist and co-author, with his brother Peter, of “The Seven Day Scholar: The Presidents.” At noon, Asphalt Green Battery Park City, 212 North End Avenue, Manhattan, 212-298-2930, asphaltgreenbpc.org; $22, $18 for members.

      Walking Tours

      Art and History of the Metropolitan Museum (Friday) Doctoral students in art history lead this Big Onion Tour of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, surveying the art and providing historical facts about the museum. It meets at 1 p.m. in front of the group admission door, Fifth Avenue, at 81st Street, Manhattan, 212-439-1090, bigonion.com; $40, $34 for 65+, $28 for students; price includes museum admission.

      Beatles Walking Tour (Saturday and Sunday) Manhattan sites associated with the group, including the Ed Sullivan Theater, where the Beatles performed 50 years ago, will be covered on this walk, starting at 2 p.m., led by NYC Discovery Tours. Other stops include the Plaza Hotel, Carnegie Hall and the Dakota. The meeting place will be given with reservations. 212-465-3331; $20.

      Flatiron District Walking Tour (Sunday) This free tour of the Flatiron district meets at 11 a.m. on the southwest corner of Madison Square Park, at 23rd Street and Broadway, in front of the William Seward statue. Sponsored by the Flatiron/23rd Street Partnership; flatironbid.org/tour.php.

      Spanish Harlem Salsa Tour (Friday through Thursday) This tour, which meets at 110th Street and Fifth Avenue, includes a lecture on the music and a walk through the neighborhood, visiting social clubs and homes of famous salsa musicians. Sponsored by the Lubona Corporation. Daily at 10 a.m. and 6 p.m., with an additional evening tour Saturdays through Thursdays at 9 p.m. Reservations can be made at lubonacorp@yahoo.com; $25.

      ‘Union Square: Crossroads of New York’ (Saturday) Sponsored by the Union Square Partnership, this free year-round tour focuses on the area’s political and social histories. This walk, led by guides from Big Onion Walking Tours, meets at 2 p.m. by the statue of Lincoln in Union Square Park, near the 16th Street transverse. 212-517-1826, unionsquarenyc.org; free.

      ‘Where Brooklyn Meets the Middle East’ (Saturday) The roots of the Arab-American enclave along Atlantic Avenue is the focus of this tour, starting at 2 p.m., sponsored by the Municipal Art Society of New York. The meeting place will be given with reservations. 212-935-2075, mas.org; $20, $15 for members. ANNE MANCUSO and PHILIP RICHARDSON
