DANCE VIEW; BIRTHDAY CANDLES IN NORTH CAROLINA ARE SEEN AFAR

By Anna Kisselgoff
Published: June 7, 1987

EVERYONE LIKES TO CELE-brate anniversaries, and the American Dance Festival is no exception. In 1984, the festival staged a wide-ranging international season to celebrate its 50th anniversary, harking back to its pioneering summer sessions at Bennington College in Vermont and, later, at Connecticut College. Resident at Duke University in Durham, N. C., since 1978, it is now content to mark its new season as a ''10th birthday'' in North Carolina. A giant cakewalk contest, organized by the choreographer Daniel Nagrin, will help wind up the festivities.

The festival, which is still considered the foremost center of modern-dance schooling and creativity outside New York City, has changed markedly and yet remained true to its founding principles during its decade in Durham. Two examples suffice. Creativity is honored tonight as the '87 performance season opens with a bang when Alvin Ailey receives - from Harry Belafonte - the $25,000 Samuel H. Scripps-American Dance Festival Award for lifetime achievement in modern dance. Friday marks the beginning of the festival's first official alumni reunion. Madonna, not always a pop star and once a scholarship modern-dance student (she attended the festival's first session in Durham), has already sent a contribution to the scholarship fund.

Neither the Scripps Award presentation nor an alumni gathering that spans half a century existed at Bennington or at Connecticut, to which the festival moved in 1948. It may be hard to believe, but modern dance has only recently attained the maturity and recognition that have made both events possible. No patron before Mr. Scripps, a member of the newspaper-publishing family, had deemed modern dance's creative figures worthy enough to establish a prize specifically to honor their field.

An overview of the past decade in Durham and the festival's future projects show it to have moved swiftly and innovatively to acknowledge modern dance's coming of age. Under Charles Reinhart, who became the festival's director in 1969 in Connecticut and who transferred it to a more supportive setting in North Carolina, the ''mecca'' of modern dance has struck out in new directions and continues to do so.

As the 10th-birthday season, which continues through July 18, gets underway, it is time to bring into relief the remarkable ventures Mr. Reinhart has initiated during the last few years. They give the festival the long-term vision no other dance organizations have matched.

The major new items on the agenda testify to the festival's increasingly activist role on the international and national levels. For starters, the festival is helping to found what is to be the first official modern-dance company in China. A group of high-ranking Chinese administrators and teachers from the Peking Dance Academy visited the festival in 1979, a year before Mr. Reinhart paid the first of his two visits to China. Chinese choreographers subsequently attended the festival in 1984, '85 and '86. One of these was Yang Meiqui from Guangdong Dance College in Canton. Ms. Yang has now been authorized to establish a modern-dance department in her college under a three-year project that will lead to the formation of the dance troupe.

The festival will send two teacher-choreographers a year to China. The first pair are Sarah Stackhouse, once with Jose Limon's company, and Ruby Shang - who, incidentally, will present another of her internationally known outdoor pieces as the festival's ''10th Birthday Event'' on July 13 and 14. Ms. Yang, in turn, will send a Chinese teacher to study in the United States under festival auspices each year. The project, funded by the Rockefeller Foundation and the Asian Cultural Council, is designed to ''build a solid base of modern-dance techniques,'' Mr. Reinhart says. In a recent interview, Mr. Reinhart said that modern dance, as a form of individual expression, is by no means antithetical to a traditional culture. Indeed, the festival's International Choreographers Workshop, co-sponsored by the United States Information Agency since 1984, continues to bring foreign choreographers to attend classes and create works at the festival. A piece by a choreographer from India might look more like Indian dance than like American modern dance. Yet, the choreographer is working from the same creative base as his American counterpart, although often incorporating elements from his own culture.

''When ballet was introduced to China, it remained ballet, what it was,'' Mr. Reinhart says. The interesting aspect of the Chinese project is that it points toward a distinctly Chinese version of modern dance (with no handed-down tradition). The choreographers from 16 countries at the festival this year are exposed to American dance techniques, ''but their work should grow out of their own image,'' Mr. Reinhart says. American cultural imperialism is not an issue, he asserts. In fact, four choreographers (from Argentina, Indonesia, India and Spain) who were in the program in the past are returning this summer in a new ''graduate level'' project to create works to be presented by the festival on July 12.
