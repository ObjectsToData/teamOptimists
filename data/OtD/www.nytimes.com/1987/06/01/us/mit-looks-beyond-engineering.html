M.I.T. LOOKS BEYOND ENGINEERING

By EDWARD B. FISKE, Special to the New York Times
Published: June 1, 1987

As the first step in a long-term effort to broaden the education of engineering students, undergraduates at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology will be required to pursue more systematic study of the arts, humanities and social sciences.

Starting with the freshman class in 1988, undergraduates will be required to take at least three courses that stress such fundamental academic themes as literary traditions and the origins of political institutions. And for the first time they will be allowed to choose nontechnical disciplines, ranging from philosophy to the role of women in society, as minor fields of study. No 'Technological Mercenaries'

The changes were approved by a faculty vote May 20 after vigorous debate and student opposition. They are part of what Paul E. Gray, president of the institute, termed a ''searching assessment and reformulation'' of what engineers do and how they should be trained.

''A professional engineer can no longer be narrowly focused on technical interests,'' he said. ''He lives and operates in a social system, and he needs to understand cultural and human values. Humanities courses cannot be viewed merely as frosting on the cake.''

The changes are also meant, M.I.T. officials say, to dispel the notion that engineers are ''technological mercenaries'' who solve other people's problems. Another aim is to prepare more graduates for policy-making posts in politics, business and other fields.

''Too many M.I.T. graduates end up working for too many Princeton and Harvard graduates,'' said Ann F. Friedlaender, dean of M.I.T.'s School of Humanities and Social Science. Bridging Two Worlds

As the next step in the effort, courses are being planned in which students would study the social, political, economic and other consequences of technological developments. Prospective topics include economic competitiveness with Japan and the politics of the development of the hydrogen bomb.

''The idea is to bridge the two worlds, of the humanities, arts and social sciences on the one hand and engineering and society on the other,'' Margaret L. A. MacVicar, dean for undergraduate education, said of the next phase of the program. ''The hope is that this will become a requirement.''

Officials say that if such courses become mandatory, the 125-year old institute may have to abandon its longstanding assumption that engineers can get enough training in four years to go directly to work after graduating.

The Massachusetts Institute of Technology has about 4,500 undergraduates. Two-thirds of them, more than ever before, are enrolled in the School of Engineering and Computer Science. Science and mathematics majors account for nearly a quarter of the students, and the rest, about 10 percent, pursue majors in the arts, architecture, humanities or social sciences. Effort to Reverse a Pattern

The school's administration is seeking to reverse this pattern by recruiting more students who, while strong in mathematics and science, have other professional interests. The number of sophomores selecting a major in electrical engineering and computer science, the largest department at M.I.T., was 400 in 1983 but is projected to be 260 for the current freshman class.

''We are finding that a wider range of employers are coming to recruit at M.I.T. - like banks and corporations that want people with quantitative skills,'' said Michael C. Behnke, director of admissions. ''We also want a student body the reflects the breadth of the faculty.''

Currently, science and engineering students take most of their courses in technical fields, but they must also take at least eight courses outside these areas. Three must be chosen from a list of 156 offered by the School of Humanities and Social Sciences. Students Express Opposition

The eight courses must also include three related courses in a field such as history or psychology. Nontechnical majors, in turn, must take at least nine courses in math, science and engineering.

The requirements are not popular among M.I.T. undergraduates, especially the scientists and engineers. ''Humanities courses are seen as unnecessary evils by most students,'' said Brecht Isbell, a junior. ''Many students try to avoid breadth, taking only subjects that require little work or are scientific and related to their major.''

Faculty dissatisfaction with the system has grown in recent years, in part because the wide range of optional courses, many of them low-level survey courses, does not seem to be serving any overall concept of ''general education.''

''As we evolve toward a more technologically oriented society, engineers will have to be better prepared to take a role in making decisions,'' said Jack L. Kerrebrock, associate dean of the School of Engineering. ''How often do you see an engineer running for political office?''

Dean MacVicar criticized the prevailing perception that engineers are solvers of technical problems. She spoke of an engineer who, on the eve of the January 1986 launching of the space shuttle Challenger, tried to get his superiors to delay the launching because of doubts about seals in the rocket boosters. ''My guess is that a doctor in a similar situation would have had more influence,'' she said. The Challenger's crew of seven were killed in the explosion. 'Our Arrogance as Engineers'
