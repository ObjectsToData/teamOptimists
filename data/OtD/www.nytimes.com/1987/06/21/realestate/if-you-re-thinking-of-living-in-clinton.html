IF YOU'RE THINKING OF LIVING IN:; Clinton

By LISA W. FODERARO
Published: June 21, 1987

THE Film Center Cafe on Ninth Avenue near 45th Street was for years a down-to-earth bar without a menu. But today, although it still uses its old handle, it serves soft-shelled crabs and steak tartare and the stereo plays Vivaldi and Billie Holiday.

The cafe's metamorphosis illustrates the shifting personality of Clinton as well as the divergent reactions to that change within the neighborhood.

''I used to live here 10 years ago when it was really bad and dangerous,'' said Georges Guenencia, the co-owner of the cafe, which was renovated in March. ''Now the streets are clean, the bars with prostitutes have closed and a new, young crowd is appearing. From the outside, our restaurant still looks the same. But when people come in, they're surprised.''

But some are dismayed by the bar's transformation.

''It went from serving 40-cent drafts to $2.75 imported beers,'' lamented Peter Schleissner, a 10-year Clinton resident who will take over in July as chairman of Community Board 4. ''This is changing the neighborhood's character. Many people like myself want to hang on to the cheap places.''

So goes the old gentrification saga, one that has unfolded in many neighborhoods throughout New York City in recent years. In Clinton, however, where the city's major developers are poised along the periphery, the plot is particularly turbulent.

Because of its convenient location, increasingly called Midtown West, the neighborhood has been the target of development pressures in recent years. Apartment rents are rising, rental buildings are converting to co-ops, other buildings are being renovated, new structures are under construction and long-time commercial tenants are rent increases they cannot afford - all signs of a market approaching the boiling point.

''It's a battleground because families being displaced are fighting back, and it's become a cause celebre for community and political groups,'' said Peter D. Salins, chairman of the department of urban affairs and planning at Hunter College. ''Newcomers might find themselves in the midst of some political turmoil.''

Like any neighborhood in a socio-economic transition, Clinton has a number of faces. At one extreme are the prostitutes, the crack pushers and a troubled youth population - many of them junior high school dropouts and runaways - suffering from a 75 percent unemployment rate.

But the bulk of the population is made up of blue-collar and middle-class residents who came to Clinton in search of affordable housing and proximity to work.

IN recent years, young urban professionals have been drawn to the area's reasonable rents and the melting-pot environment.

''The beauty of Clinton is that it's so ethnically diverse, and that's what attracted me in the first place,'' said Judith Ellen Cook, the 27-year-old marketing and promotion manager for The Economist. Ms. Cook pays $623 a month for a tiny studio in a rent-stabilized building on West 55th Street between Eighth and Ninth Avenues.

From West 42d to West 59th Streets and from Eighth Avenue to the river - the only area for which statistics are available, but one that takes in 86 percent of the population - 28 percent of the residents are Hispanic, 4 percent are Asian, 9 percent are black and the remainder are white.

''But the problem is,'' Ms. Cook said, referring to her neighborhood, ''that it could all turn into a Columbus Avenue -homogenous and unaffordable.''

In the mid-19th century, when shipping and manufacturing concerns followed in the wake of the new Hudson River Railroad, the area became a respectable, but modest neighborhood for workers. Later, exhaust fumes, the rattling of the Ninth Avenue el, the smoky trains and gang warfare gave the neighborhood its Hell's Kitchen sobriquet.

A special preservation area, set up in 1974 by the city, continues to restrict building heights to seven stories, from West 42d to West 56th Streets, between Eighth and 10th Avenues. The tight zoning has largely succeeded in limiting new construction in the residential part of Clinton, containing low-rise brownstones and tenements.

But renovations are rampant and after it is completed rents typically rise from $200 to $300 a month to the market level of $1,200 to $1,500 for a one- or two-bedroom apartment.

A few developers, banking on Clinton's continued vitality, have built new and like-new apartment houses within the height limitations, such as Front Row, a rental on West 43d Street east of Ninth Avenue, and the Clinton Club, a condominium on 45th Street near 10th Avenue.

In the meantime, building values continue to advance. The Whitby, a 218-unit, rent-regulated apartment building, on West 45th Street between Eighth and Ninth Avenues, was sold for $1 million in 1976. In 1985, it was sold again - this time for $7.5 million. And recently, a co-op offering plan with a total purchase price of $25 million was issued to tenants.

One apparent problem for Clinton is the quality and safety of the public schools, which make it an acceptable place for ''young, single people who just want crash pads, but not really appropriate for families,'' said Mr. Salins of Hunter College. Public schools in the area include P.S. 51, P.S. 111, J.H.S. 17, I.S. 70 and Park West High School.
