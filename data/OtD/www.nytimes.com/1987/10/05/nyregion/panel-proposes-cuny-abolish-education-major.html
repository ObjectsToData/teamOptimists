Panel Proposes CUNY Abolish Education Major

By SAMUEL WEISS
Published: October 5, 1987

A task force of the City University of New York has proposed that all students being trained as teachers be required to major in the liberal arts and sciences instead of education.

The recommendation is intended to turn out teachers of broader educational backgrounds. It was made in response to growing public concern over the quality of teacher training.

Under the proposal, a student preparing to become an elementary school teacher could not accumulate 40 to 50 credits in education courses, as is now possible, but instead would be required to major in a discipline like history or mathematics. Limit on Education Credits Asked

In addition to fulfilling the requirements of the major, the student would have to take the minimum of 24 education credits needed to fulfill the state's requirement for provisional certification as a teacher. However, under the university proposal, no student could earn more than 30 such credits.

The recommendations of the task force are in keeping with recent proposals made nationally to upgrade the quality of teacher training. If adopted by the City University's board of trustees, they could have a significant impact on teaching in New York City, because it is estimated that more than half of the 60,000 teachers in the public school system have graduated from the university.

''I sincerely believe that implementation of these proposals could thrust the City University into a role of national leadership in the area of teacher preparation,'' said Dr. Robert L. Hess, president of Brooklyn College and a member of the task force.

''I also think it would show we in the university are really committed to doing something to improve the quality of elementary and secondary education in this city,'' he said.

Other recommendations of the task force included these:

* The university's nine four-year colleges should strengthen the general liberal-arts requirements for education students to insure that they receive a well-rounded background in the humanities, the sciences and social sciences.

* The colleges should establish minimum standards of academic progress for students before they could begin their education course work.

* The colleges should establish combined undergraduate and graduate degrees in which bright students could earn a bachelor's and master's degree in education in accelerated time. Students in these programs would be eligible for tuition waivers.

* The university should establish, in conjunction with the city's Board of Education, a consortium of model schools to serve as clinical settings for the preparation of student teachers.

* The colleges should review the coherence of the education courses they offer and insure that the courses reflect the most current research concerning effective teaching practices. Field experience, or working in a school, should occur no later than during the third course in education. A Draft Report Made

The task force - consisting of seven trustees, five college presidents, two faculty members and a student - was created in February by James P. Murphy, chairman of the board of trustees, to review teacher education programs.

Its recommendations are contained in a draft report that is expected to be circulated within the university in the next few weeks. A copy was obtained by The New York Times.

Edith B. Everett, vice chairwoman of the board of trustees and chairwoman of the task force, said in an interview Friday that she expected the board to consider the proposals early next year. The curriculum revisions could be in place in September 1989, she said.

Critics have long maintained that the quality of teaching in the nation has been eroding, in part because new teachers have not been receiving well-rounded educations themselves but have wasted too much time as undergraduates taking trivial education courses. Earlier Proposals Noted

Last year the Carnegie Corporation of New York issued a report calling for the elimination of undergraduate degrees in education. The report echoed an earlier report by a national group of education school deans known as the Holmes Group, which recommended that the training of teachers be concentrated in a student's fifth year of graduate studies.

The group also urged the creation of ''professional development schools'' to act as the educational counterpart of teaching hospitals.

A member of the City University task force said that consideration had been given to eliminating all undergraduate courses in education but that the proposal had been rejected in part because of the teacher shortage in New York City.

In New York State a new teacher must complete a master's degree in education within five years to become permanently certified. Officials Are Enthusiastic

Schools Chancellor Nathan Quinones of New York City, informed Friday of the contents of the task-force report, said the proposals sounded ''terrific'' and went beyond the revisions proposed by the Carnegie Corporation and the Holmes Group.

The president of the Board of Education, Robert F. Wagner Jr., called the proposals ''splendid'' and said that, together with the new contract raising teachers' salaries, ''they could have a profound effect on the quality of teaching'' in New York.

Sandra Feldman, president of the United Federation of Teachers, said that the proposals sounded ''very exciting'' and that the teachers' union would be ''very happy'' to work with the university in implementing such recommendations as the creation of model schools.

Anticipating adoption, the university will ask the state for $5 million in its budget for 1988-89 to put the recommendations into effect.

Nonetheless university officials expect opposition from some faculty members in the school of education who might fear retrenchment and loss of prestige resulting from the abolition of the undergraduate major in education.
