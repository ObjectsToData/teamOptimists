After Nobel Prize, Caviar for Russian Poet

By RICHARD BERNSTEIN
Published: October 31, 1987

Joseph Brodsky returned to New York University yesterday, greeting friends and reading some poems as he has done many times before. But this time, the mood was a good deal more jocular than reverential, with hugs and congratulations and excitement, not to mention caviar and champagne instead of the salami sandwiches served on other such occasions.

''Nothing has changed; life is the same as before,'' Mr. Brodsky said in his first appearance at the university and his first poetry reading in New York since the Swedish Academy awarded him the Nobel Prize for Literature earlier this month.

Mr. Brodsky was at the N.Y.U. Institute for the Humanities, which often schedules lunches with artists, writers, and other cultural figures. He lives in New York City and has been a regular member of the Institute for about 10 years. His reading yesterday had been scheduled before he won the prize. 'Justice Somewhere in the World'

Richard Sennett, a poet and literary critic who teaches at the university, summed up what seemed to be the common conviction when he recalled learning of Mr. Brodsky's prize. ''When I saw it,'' Mr. Sennett said, ''I said, 'Justice somewhere in the world.' ''

Mr. Sennet invited Mr. Brodsky for dinner at home Sunday night, telling him: ''It's so great. I just can't believe it. It's fantastic.''

And so, it was not exactly like old times for Mr. Brodsky, even though a few in the crowd of 70 or so people said a regular salami-sandwich gathering of the traditional sort would have been preferable to the Nobel festivity that did take place. Not sharing this feeling, John Brademas, the former Indiana Representative who is the university's current president, offered a bottle of champagne and told Mr. Brodshy to ''enjoy.''

Mr. Brodsky, who once spent 18 months in a Soviet labor camp and was exiled from the Soviet Union in 1972 on accusations of ''social parasitism,'' said, ''That's one of the better aspects of the establishment, enjoyment.''

A voice from the crowd asked why the news of his Nobel Prize had not yet been announced in the Soviet press, and in reply Mr. Brodsky contended that the Soviet leaders make concerted efforts to persuade the Nobel Prize Committee in Stockholm to give the award to approved Soviet writers. Thus, he said, they must have been disappointed when a dissident poet received it.

Mr. Brodsky admitted being ''a little distressed'' by the Soviet reaction, but he said, ''What's good about it is that it is not only the news for them up to Wednesday or Thursday but it's going to remain a fact for them for a long time, and that pleases me no end.''

When the toasts were finished, the poetry began, with Mr. Brodsky promising to read some new works that were funny. ''You shouldn't take seriously the whole thing,'' he said. ''It's simply fun.'' This is ''the light stuff.''

The poem was a kind of comic epic that tells the history of the 20th century, year by year, from 1900 until the present, in rhyming verse that seemed to owe about as much to Ogden Nash as to Russian tradition.

History! History holds the clue to your taxes and to your flu, to what comes out of the blue.''

Various historical figures emerge in the work, which will be published early next year in Mr. Brodsky's original English. We are Orville and Wilbur Wright. Our names simply rhyme with flight. This may partially explain

Why we decided to build a plane.

Stalin makes an appearance recalling his early days when he robbed banks for the Bolshevik revolutionaries, and Mr. Brodsky has him say:

Some call me eager; some call me zealous.

I just like big figures with their crowd of zeros.

Reading his new work, Mr. Brodsky seemed attentive to the audience, wanting to know what the crowd found funny, apologizing for his accent, which in no way conceals his thorough command of spoken English, and assuring everyone that the portions being read would soon be over.

But, after a request from the crowd, he read a poem in Russian that was decidedly not ''the light stuff.'' The mood and Mr. Brodsky himself seemed to change, so that, for a few minutes at the end, the poet was no longer an amiable, self-effacing old friend of the university family, but a figure from a recent tragic history, a man who had seen and suffered what might be incomprehensible to a non-Russian. His voice deepened and became more hoarse and he did not so much read or recite the poem as he intoned it.

The poem, as roughly translated, began:

I have braved, for want of wild beats, steel cages Carved my term and nickname on bunks and rafters lived by the sea, flashed aces in an oasis. And it ended: What should I say about life? That it's long and abhors transparence.

Broken eggs make me grieve; the omelette makes me vomit.

Yet until brown clay has been crammed down my larynx, Only gratitude will be gushing from it.
