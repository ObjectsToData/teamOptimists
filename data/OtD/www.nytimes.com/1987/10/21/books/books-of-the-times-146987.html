BOOKS OF THE TIMES

By Michiko Kakutani
Published: October 21, 1987

IN her previous novel, ''The Middle Ground'' (1980), Margaret Drabble noted that her heroine ''can never decide whether she is a special case, and as such of little general relevance, or whether she is on the contrary an almost abnormally normal woman, a typical woman of our time, and as such of little particular interest.'' It's a question that also seems to have preoccupied Ms. Drabble in her recent fiction, and her latest novel, ''The Radiant Way,'' is no exception.

Although the novel ostensibly offers us portraits of three former Cambridge classmates as they attempt to come to terms with middle age, it aspires, as well, to give us a portrait of contemporary Britain -its distribution of money, jobs and fortune and its uneasy accommodation with the violence and disappointments of the 80's.

The three heroines of ''The Radiant Way'' are ''not princesses,'' Ms. Drabble observes. Rather, they are a modern sort of heroine: bright, spirited women who've left behind provincial pasts and joined ''the creme de la creme of their generation'' - via scholarships to Cambridge. ''At eighteen,'' Ms. Drabble writes, ''the world opened for them and displayed its riches, the brave new world of Welfare State and County Scholarships, of equality for women; they were the elite, the chosen, the garlanded of the great social dream.''

Liz, ''pale and fair and thin,'' leaves behind an unhappy childhood in the north to become a successful psychotherapist. She marries a wealthy television executive, brings up five children, and appears to become the very model of the modern, liberated woman. In time, though, her marriage will fall apart, and she will also be forced to deal with a disturbing family secret.

Alix, ''mousy, square faced, healthy of complexion'' and ''extraordinarily pleasant of expression,'' ends up marrying the head of humanities of a poorly financed adult-education institute. She begins teaching young women in prison part time, while presiding over a modest, middle-class household.

Esther, ''subversive, aggressive, commanding, Napoleonic of manner,'' transfers her bohemian existence at Cambridge to London intact, taking odd jobs lecturing on art. Instead of marrying, she will carry on a series of furtive affairs with married men, most notably a demonic Italian anthropologist.

In the course of ''The Radiant Way,'' Ms. Drabble shows us how the lives of these three friends crisscross back and forth through time; and in doing so, she also attempts to show us how a generation managed (or mismanaged) its hopes and dreams. If this methodology sounds vaguely reminiscent of that employed by Mary McCarthy in ''The Group'' (which traced the lives of eight former Vassar students), so, too, are the book's problems. There's a schematic quality to both novels, a tendency to substitute exposition for storytelling, sociological observation for the development of character and drama.

While Ms. Drabble gives us heaps of Balzacian details about her heroines' daily routines (the foundation makeup that Liz applies before a party merits two lengthy paragraphs), we are accorded few insights into their hearts or minds. Instead, we're given lists of their house furnishings, lists of their party guests, even lists of what those party guests wore (''dark suits, pale blue suits, Indian kurtahs worn not exclusively by Indians, Viyella shirts, striped mother-knitted pullovers,'' etc.).

As she has demonstrated in some of her earlier novels, Ms. Drabble does have a gift for creating characters of psychological density when she puts her mind to it, but for some reason, she seems determined, here, to turn all her heroines into symbols. Liz comes to stand for all those women who have difficulty balancing a marriage and career; Alix, for those women who have channeled their intellect into a worthwhile but not entirely fulfilling avocation, and Esther, for those women unable to make a lasting relationship with a suitable man.

Nor is Ms. Drabble content to make her heroines into representative women - she also wants to make them and assorted members of their families into mouthpieces for a spectrum of political and social views. At one point, she even has Alix engage in a self-administered question-and-answer session in which she asks herself such questions as ''Did she blame the Tory government for this deterioration in law and order?''

One of the problems with trying to create a composite portrait of contemporary Britain in this manner is simply that too much ground is covered in too little space; Anthony Powell's ''Dance to the Music of Time,'' after all, took 12 volumes to chronicle life in postwar England. As a result, Ms. Drabble is forced to summarize masses of information instead of dramatizing her material. Conversation devolves into a perfunctory litany of topics (''London districts, property prices, houses, the police, no-go areas, rape, violence, murder, robbery, Tennyson and Arthur Hallam, Leslie Stephen and Virginia Woolf''), and toward the end of the novel, whole years flash by in a sentence or two while deaths (including a grisly beheading) proliferate as an expedient means of tying up loose ends.

''We are not ourselves,'' says one of the heroines of ''The Radiant Way.'' ''We are crossroads, meeting places, points on a curve, we cannot exist independently for we are nothing but signs, conjunctions, aggregations.'' Certainly, that seems to sum up Ms. Drabble's attitudes toward her creations, but it makes for a book that lacks the bright specificity of her finest fiction. Indeed, one tends to finish ''The Radiant Way'' feeling one has just read a dry, unilluminating survey about women and contemporary England, rather than a novel by one of England's most prominent writers.
