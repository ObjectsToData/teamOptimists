Rising Art Prices Worry Museums

By GRACE GLUECK
Published: October 17, 1987

The hottest name in the art world today is Vincent van Gogh, whose works, almost a century after his suicide, are turning the market upside down.

The astronomical figures brought at auction by two van Gogh paintings this year, $39.9 million for ''Sunflowers'' and $20 million for ''The Bridge at Trinquetaille,'' underscore for professionals in the field the runaway state of art prices and their devastating effects - particularly on nonprofit institutions.

Even as the stock market declines, the long shadow of the van Gogh sales loomed behind two recent panel discussions on the problem. One, ''Effects of the Art Market on Institutions,'' sponsored by the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities, took place Thursday at the Metropolitan Museum. The other, ''The Price Explosion and the Future of the Art Market,'' was held under auspices of the Art Dealers Association of America at the Guggenheim Museum on Oct. 6.

Each touched on a wide range of relevant issues, among them the effect of rising prices on living artists, the increasing perception of art as a vehicle for investment and how museums can go on collecting as they are priced out of the market. Good News and Bad

On the Metropolitan panel, Kirk Varnedoe, a professor at New York University's Institute of Fine Arts and adjunct curator in the department of paintings and sculpture at the Museum of Modern Art, described the situation as ''a sort of good news-bad news joke.''

''The good news is that modern art has succeeded beyond our wildest dreams, and van Gogh symbolizes the thirst for innovation, for the odd and challenging, that has become a commonplace,'' he said. ''The bad news is that museums, who did so much to create these conditions, are now suffering under their success.''

In full concurrence, Philippe de Montebello, director of the Metropolitan, said, ''If museums can't enlarge and improve their collections, they're in trouble.'' He noted that for major works of art sold at auction, the Metropolitan was ''not in the game at all,'' while at the same time the tax reform legislation of 1986, with its curtailment of benefits for donors, had taken away a key incentive to make gifts. He pointed out that the higher prices also increased insurance rates for exhibitions. But the critical issue, in his view, was that high art market prices ''skewed and distorted fundamental values'' because in making major works unavailable they encouraged the elevation of lesser ones.

Besides Mr. Varnedoe and Mr. de Montebello, the panel, moderated by C. Douglas Dillon, the Metropolitan's former chairman, included Hilton Kramer, art critic and editor of The New Criterion; Donald B. Marron, chairman, president and chief executive officer of Paine Webber Group Inc. and president of the Museum of Modern Art; John L. Marion, auctioneer and chairman of Sotheby's North America, and Eugene V. Thaw, a leading art dealer. About 70 invited guests sat around an immense table in the Met's new Douglas Dillon Board Room. The board room and a lavishly appointed Patron's Lounge nearby, on the fourth floor of the new Lila Acheson Wallace Wing, were paid for by Mr. and Mrs. Dillon.

The panel was convened, according to Diane Paton, executive director of the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities, to examine what it felt to be a priority issue: how the art market boom is affecting institutions. The committee, which meets several times a year for ''useful discussion on matters of vital cultural concern,'' was set up in 1982 by executive order of President Reagan to help increase private support for the arts and the humanities. Members, all selected by the President, include influential private citizens, the chairmen of the National Endowments and the heads of some other Federal agencies. The committee's chairman is Andrew Heiskell, former chairman of Time Inc.

The group was slightly jolted by a suggestion from Mr. Kramer that the market boom could be a ''wonderful benefaction'' to museums if it prevented them from buying contemporary art. ''The new market has created such an immense quantity of bad art,'' he said to audience laughter, ''that museums should stop buying it and let the collectors who have created this boom write the checks.'' Noting that ''worldwide wealth is pursuing far fewer available high-quality objects than ever before,'' Mr. Thaw criticized the proposed sale of van Gogh's ''Irises'' by its owner, John Whitney Payson, who cited the rise in art prices and the changes in Federal tax law as his reasons. The painting had been on long-term loan to Westbrook College in Maine, and, according to Mr. Thaw, was a ''promised gift.'' Such situations, said Mr. Thaw, ''call into question the whole apparatus of art donations and indicate how much superprices have infected art institutions as well as hampering their acquisitions.''

Taking a more positive view, Mr. Marron, who appeared on both the Metropolitan and the Guggenheim panels, conceded that the fast pace of today's art market created more pressure for museums but saw the market as, ''apart from its economics, an open form of communication for people that broadened out to meet new constituencies.'' The real victims of the boom might be artists, he suggested, who feared experimenting with new approaches lest they lose an established clientele. Origins of the Boom

Refusing to apologize for current prices, Mr. Marion, the auctioneer, suggested that the art market boom really went back to the sale, in 1961, of Rembrandt's ''Aristotle Contemplating the Bust of Homer'' to the Metropolitan Museum for $2.3 million, then a record price. The van Gogh prices might be just aberrations, he said.

The earlier panel at the Guggenheim Museum, moderated by Richard Feigen, a well-known art dealer, included four prominent businessmen and art collectors besides Mr. Marron: Wendell Cherry, president of Humana, the hospital chain based in Louisville, Ky.; Mark Goodson, the television game show producer and newspaper owner, and Raymond Nasher, a Dallas real estate developer whose collection of 20th-century sculpture is on view at the National Gallery in Washington.

Among the reasons they assigned for the art market boom were the ''globalization of financial resources,'' the scarcity of high quality works and the glamour that goes with ownership of major works. But the general view was positive. Mr. Cherry saw the $39.9 million price for van Gogh's ''Sunflowers'' as a celebration and said, ''It's a great quality of our civilization that people are willing to pay such a compliment to great works that come from the hand of man.''
