BOOKS OF THE TIMES

By John Gross
Published: October 2, 1987

WHAT DO OUR 17-YEAR-OLDS KNOW?

A Report on the First National Assessment of History and Literature. By Diane Ravitch and Chester E. Finn Jr. Foreword by Lynne V. Cheney. 293 pages. Harper & Row. $15.95.

COLUMBUS discovered the New World some time after 1750. Watergate occurred some time before 1950, quite possibly before 1900. The Jim Crow laws were intended to improve the lot of black Americans. ''Tender Is the Night'' is a novel about Heathcliff's passion for Catherine, and so is ''The Hunchback of Notre Dame.'' The first president of the American Federation of Labor was - wait for it - J. P. Morgan.

These are just a few of the beliefs held by large numbers of young Americans, or at any rate beliefs they are prepared to subscribe to in a multiple-choice test. The evidence can be found in ''What Do Our 17-Year-Olds Know?,'' a report by Diane Ravitch and Chester E. Finn Jr. based on the First National Assessment of History and Literature; and the kind of misconceptions cited are far from being limited to a small minority of the students who were tested.

More than 30 percent of them, for example, didn't know that Columbus reached the New World before 1750; more than 35 percent didn't know that Watergate occurred after 1950. And the dismal statistics could easily be multiplied. Nearly half the students voted for ''Nineteen Eighty-Four'' being a novel about the destruction of the human race in a nuclear war; more than a third didn't know that Aesop wrote fables; no less than 30.1 percent thought that one consequence of the Spanish-American War was the destruction of the Spanish Armada.

We have all heard horror stories like these by now. The great thing about the National Assessment is that it moves beyond the anecdotal or the fragmentary to hard representative evidence. Financed by the National Endowment for the Humanities, the test - carried out in 1986 - was the first of its kind; approximately 8,000 17-year-olds took part, carefully chosen to reflect the makeup of the population as a whole in terms of region, gender, race, type of community and type of school.

Mrs. Ravitch, who teaches at Columbia University Teachers College, and Mr. Finn, who teaches at Vanderbilt University in Tennessee, were two of the moving spirits behind the entire project, but they make it clear that they are not altogether happy with the decision, taken on expert advice, to limit the testing to multiple-choice questions (and to rule out an honest ''I don't know'' as a potential answer).

They have a variety of misgivings about such a procedure, but none of their doubts hold out much comfort in terms of the overall picture. On the contrary, everything suggests that guesswork, whether smart or random, inflated the number of correct replies.

It is a sobering thought, too, that the history questions were largely confined to the central issues of United States history, and that nearly 80 percent of the students were enrolled in United States history at the time. If there had been an equal number of questions on world history, the outcome would no doubt have been more sobering still.

The average score in history was 54.5 percent, and it was no better for post-World War II history than for earlier periods. Only 42.6 percent of the students, for example, knew (or guessed) that Senator Joseph R. McCarthy had something to do with anti-Communist investigations.

The literature score was even less impressive: 51.8 percent. The only remotely bright spots were respectable scores - respectable by the prevailing standards, that is - for the Bible and Shakespeare. On the other hand, fewer than one student in seven managed to identify what was once one of the most widely read books in the English language, ''The Pilgrim's Progress.''

After analyzing the results in detail, Mrs. Ravitch and Mr. Finn relate them to the students' family backgrounds, schooling, general reading and out-of-school activities. None of their conclusions are particularly earthshaking; children from disadvantaged homes naturally tended to get below-average scores, but the problem of poor standards is a pervasive one.

The National Assessment report was in the news earlier this year when E. D. Hirsch Jr. published ''Cultural Literacy,'' a book based on its preliminary findings, in which he argued that all American children should be made to study an agreed body of general knowledge - names, concepts, catchphrases, technical terms. Mrs. Ravitch and Mr. Finn don't have a comparable nostrum to offer, but they do make a lengthy series of recommendations on how to improve things.

These tend to be sensible if unsensational. A few are fairly vague, but others could in principle be acted on quite quickly - an overhaul of textbooks, for instance, with publishers soliciting advice from teachers and scholars rather than reading experts and educationists.

Still, the chief value of ''What Do Our 17-Year-Olds Know?'' lies in its documentation of existing conditions. In particular, the authors have done an important job in concentrating on history and literature, subjects that have been neglected in most recent calls for educational reform. As they point out, for example, none of the major commission reports of the 1980's have called for even a year of world history to be taught in American schools.

One final thought. Lamentations about American cultural illiteracy commonly treat it as a uniquely American problem. An analogous situation exists in Western Europe and elsewhere, however - or so the evidence suggests - and a comparative study would be possibly useful, and certainly interesting.

If 70 percent of American 17-year-olds can't identify Magna Carta, for instance, you wonder how a similar group of English students would make out with the Declaration of Independence. But then more than a third of the students in the National Assessment sample couldn't give the source of ''Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness.''
