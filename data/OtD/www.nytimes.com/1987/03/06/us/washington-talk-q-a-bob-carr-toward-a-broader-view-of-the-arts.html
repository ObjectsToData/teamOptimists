WASHINGTON TALK: Q&A: BOB CARR; Toward a Broader View of the Arts

Special to the New York TimesBy IRVIN MOLOTSKY
Published: March 6, 1987

Representative Bob Carr, Democrat of Michigan, was recently elected chairman of the Congressional Arts Caucus, becoming the first non-New Yorker to head it.

The caucus, now with more than 200 members, was started in 1981 by Representative Frederick W. Richmond of Brooklyn, who resigned from Congress in 1982. The organization was expanded by his successor as chairman, Thomas J. Downey of Suffolk.

A caucus is established to further a particular cause -the arts, Hispanic rights, Irish affairs, women's issues, liberalism, conservatism and the like - and is financed by assessments against the members' office allowances.

Mr. Carr recently discussed his new chairmanship in an interview. The following are excerpts of that conversation:

Q. The Representative you defeated for the chairmanship of the Arts Caucus, Ted Weiss, likes to say there are more artists in his Manhattan district than in the rest of the country put together. Can a Midwesterner have the same awareness of artists' problems or is that a parochial view?

A. I wouldn't sell New Yorkers short and I'm not sure I would agree with the characterization that is implied about the New York arts community. Just a very few New Yorkers can't see beyond the Hudson. They are more cosmopolitan than that. They are aware that people all over the United States go to movies, buy books and visit museums.

Michigan State University recently had the Juilliard Quartet in residence on campus and most leading musicians have performed at Interlochen. There's lots going on across the country. I went to an Arts Caucus luncheon two years ago and the guest was James Coco, the late actor. I said my district included Lansing, and he said, ''Lansing? It has a wonderful theater there called the Boar's Head Theater.'' * * *

Q. What is the role of the Congressional Arts Caucus?

A. The chief role is to support appropriations for the National Endowment for the Arts. It has expanded its legislative role, but support for the endowment is the one issue where everyone agrees. * * *

Q. This raises the question whether the Federal Government ought to be involved in the arts in the first place.

A. The Government does have a role in the arts, and appropriations for the arts and humanities are needed. This doesn't mean that there aren't differences on what the endowment does with the money it gets. * * *

Q. How did you become interested in the arts?

A. My mother made me take piano lessons.

Q. Are you any good?

A. I am terrible.

Q. Do you play Chopin?

A. No.

Q. Mozart?

A. No. I play popular music. * * *

Q. What other arts interests do you have?

A. Photography.

Q. Are you as good as Howard Baker, the new White House chief of staff?

A. I think he's been overpraised as a photographer. Maybe he'll do better with the subject matter at the White House. It's hard to take photographs in Washington artistically and not have them turn out to be cliches, to be postcards. I find greater photo opportunities in the countryside of West Virginia. * * *

Q. How big is the Congressional Arts Caucus?

A. It is clearly one of the biggest three or four. Members used to be thrilled with caucuses, but I think the caucus movement is waning. Now a lot of work is being done by temporary task forces. In that way, it becomes a no-money deal. To belong to the Arts Caucus, a member has to commit $300 a year from office expenses. Tom Downey saw the Arts Caucus beginning to mature. My challenge is to deliver services to the members and, to determine what their needs are, we are going to put out our first questionnaire. I would like to be able to say at the end of my term that the money spent was well worth it. Fred Richmond started it with 30 members and took it to 130 in a few months. Tom took it from 160 to 200, then saw a little dropoff to 185. Now it's at 209 members. The larger you get, the more services you have to render. * * *

Q. Why do members of Congress join the Arts Caucus?

A. A lot of members do join just to be on the invitation list because we have some neat functions, like the James Coco lunch. Some belong because of their interests in the arts. Some belong because of a particular agenda, such as copyright laws. We are going to go beyond the growth phase and into the quality phase. I held the first statewide arts conference in Michigan in 1984 on the reauthorization of the National Endowment for the Arts. I am trying to update the model for the conference so I can offer it to members of the caucus, to get the connection between arts, law and politics back to the American people. We might want to expand our newsletter and we might provide models for local efforts that members can use. * * *

Q. Should there be a Federal role in the arts?

A. Contrary to what many in Washington think, we really write the story of our culture in our art. The National Endowment for the Arts insures the entry of new artists, insures that there is support for people at a time when they might not otherwise get a chance. It is like the basic research that we support for science. The endowment is an investment in basic research for the arts.

It assures that some of this goes on across the country, that a person in Iowa would not have to move to New York to make it in the arts. The culture that gets written down is not just in New York or California. With today's technology you don't have to be there. You could be a professor in a small town in the Midwest and write a script. * * *

Q. What are your thoughts on Frank Hodsoll, chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts?

A. I have some problems with his emphasis on challenge grants, which give organizations money on the condition that they raise two or three times the amount locally. That is hard in some areas of America with high unemployment. It could work in big cities like Cleveland and Milwaukee, but if you are in some backwater of Mississippi, I don't know if a challenge grant is going to accomplish anything.
