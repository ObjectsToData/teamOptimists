ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: March 22, 1987

ON Dec. 18, 1787, New Jersey became the third state to ratify the Constitution, and there are myriad opportunities this bicentennial year to learn about its role in the birth and growth of constitutional government.

An extraordinary recent volume, ''The Illustrated History of the Supreme Court,'' by Robert Shnayerson (Abrams, New York, $60), is well worth its price, for 377 illustations accompany a vivid account of how the Court has held America's dual claims of freedom and equality ''in delicate balance'' for more than 200 years.

One informative section describes the case of Gibbons v. Ogden (1824), ''the explosive steamboat case,'' and ''the Court's first interpretation of the Commerce Clause,'' which, by its result, ''vastly enhanced the national government's power to make laws broadly regulating national affairs''

''The Hudson River became a battleground between licensed New Yorkers, who in 1811 had 'exclusive privilege to run steamboats in state waters,' and unlicensed New Jerseyans,'' writes Mr. Shnayerson. ''Each state arrested the other's crews when caught on the wrong side of the river. Rival crews raided one another's docks, causing waterfront riots.''

The chaos worsened when Thomas Gibbons, an unlicensed New Jerseyan, ''recruited a ruthless captain, the already infamous Cornelius Vanderbilt,'' to run the competition's boats into New York and steal their passengers.

When the New York courts enjoined Gibbons, he ''became obsessed'' with forcing the case into the Supreme Court, hiring Daniel Webster and even providing for a $40,000 fund in his will ''to keep the case going should he die before it ended.''

From its outcome - the destruction of the New York monopoly -came, the author writes, ''something more profound, if not yet visible,'' for ''in the distant future Congress would apply the Commerce Clause to everything from air travel [ and ] television transmission to racial discrimination.''

An assessment of New Jersey's contribution to constitutional jurisprudence is accessible in ''New Jersey and the Constitution: The Four Supreme Court Justices From Our State,'' a 12-page booklet available free from the New Jersey Committee for the Humanities, 73 Easton Avenue, New Brunswick, N.J. 08901.

Previously distributed as a newspaper supplement, this publication evolved from a series of 1985 symposiums sponsored by the Seton Hall University School of Law and financed by the state's Committee for the Humanities and the New Jersey State Bar Foundation.

The four New Jersey justices are William Paterson, Joseph Bradley, Mahlon Pitney and William J. Brennan Jr. The initial essay, ''The Four Justices From New Jersey: An Overview,'' is by Judge John J. Gibbons, Chief Judge of the Third Circuit Court of Appeals and an adjunct professor of law at the Seton Hall.

Judge Gibbons points out that the four subjects - beginning with Paterson, who was appointed at the start of Washington's second term in 1793, and ending with Justice Brennan, appointed by President Dwight D. Eisenhower in 1956 - ''span the entire history of the Court and the Constitution.''

The essays, illustrated by archival photographs and drawings, describe each justice's contributions, together with an account of the politics of his day, and are enlivened by personal insights and provocative evaluations.

The essay on Justice Paterson is by the Rev. Daniel A. Degnan, also a Seton Hall law professor. It spiritedly relates how, during the Constitutional Convention of 1787, he opposed the Virginia Plan for proportional representation of the states.

''His New Jersey roots explain his action,'' writes Father Degnan about Paterson's New Jersey Plan for recognizing ''relatively small'' states. A result, the Great Compromise, provided equal representation of states in the Senate in exchange for House representation based on population.

The three other essays are ''Justice Joseph Bradley: A Reassessment,'' by Jonathan Lurie, chairman of the history department at Rutgers-Newark; ''Pitney and Progressivism,'' by Michal R. Belknap, assistant professor of history at the University of Georgia, and ''Justice William Brennan Jr.: An Appraisal,'' by Elizabeth F. Defeis, a professor of law at Seton Hall University.

For those whose appetites are whetted, there is the original source: ''Constitutional Anniversary Symposium: New Jersey Justices on the Supreme Court,'' printed in the ''Seton Hall Law Review, 1986,'' Vol. 16, No. 2 (Seton Hall University School of Law, 1095 Raymond Boulevard, Newark, N.J. 07102, $4.50.) The Law Review issue includes ''Conclusion: Marking the Bicentennial of the United States Constitution,'' by former Chief Justice Warren E. Burger. It relates how this country's early leaders helped to create ''a national consciousness.''

Although New Jersey was the third state to ratify the Constitution, Justice Burger writes that ''the response that New Jersey troops gave before, when General Washington attempted to gain their sworn allegiance to the United States, is particularly noteworthy.''

''You will recall,'' he continues, ''that with less than all due respect, they refused, crying, 'New Jersey is our country!' ''
