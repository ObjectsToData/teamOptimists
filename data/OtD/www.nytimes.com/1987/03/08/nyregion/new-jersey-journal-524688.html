NEW JERSEY JOURNAL

By Leo H. Carney
Published: March 8, 1987

AGRICULTURE MUSEUM ALTHOUGH it has no building to call home, the Agricultural Museum of the State of New Jersey does not exist on paper only.

With a $2,000 grant from the New Jersey Committee for the Humanities, for example, it has commissioned a script for a film on the state's agricultural history.

Veronica Malone, a member of the museum's board of trustees and an assistant to Stephen J. Kleinschuster, dean of Rutgers University's Cook College, said that requests for grants of $3,000 to actually make the film had been presented to the state's Historical Commission and the National Endowment for the Humanities.

The film, she said, will be made available to educators and serve as an introduction to exhibits once the museum building is completed.

The museum, which was established in 1929, is said to have one of the most valuable collections of artifacts in the country, including 10,000 photographs, plates and slides dating to the 1880's. Without its own building, it sends exhibits, including some of the 3,000 or so farm and domestic artifacts, out in traveling shows or lends them to other institutions.

Viewing of the artifacts and photographs is by appointment only because they are stored in several buildings on Rutgers's New Brunswick and Piscataway campuses. A total of $3 million is being sought to build a modern two-story structure on a 2.65-acre tract in New Brunswick that has been allocated by the university's board of governors.

Mrs. Malone said it was hoped that construction would start before the end of the year. In an interview last week, she said that, with $600,000 in state grants, museum officials had hired a Princeton-based architectural concern to work on plans for a U-shaped building along College Farm Road. Part of it - an exhibit hall in the curve of the U - may be ready this year, she said.

What makes the museum's photographic images unusual, Mrs. Malone said, is that many are examples of the first scientific use of photography to document agricultural research, sometimes in an amusing way.

For example, one early 20th-century photograph - an attempt to document the success of efforts to strengthen peach trees - shows a fully dressed man perching on the limb of a peach tree to demonstrate its strength.

Actually, Mrs. Malone recalled, peach tree research in New Jersey was significant. The work of Maurice Blake in the 1930's contributed much to the success of peach crops in the East.

Another bit of agricultural history is to be had in the family of Stoney Harris, who lives in the Salem County community of Cowtown. Mrs. Malone said that Mr. Harris's relatives were well-known breeders who supplied the cattle used by Washington at Valley Forge.

The reason for New Jersey's prominence in agricultural history, Mrs. Malone said, is that it ''has served as an agricultural pilot plant for the nation since Colonial times.''

Among the exhibits from the agricultural museum is one that opened last weekend at Wheaton Village in Millville and runs through May 22. Financed by the New Jersey Historical Commission, it encompasses rural toys, tools and art from Colonial times to the 20th century.

Another exhibition, ''The Horse, Man's Noble Companion,'' opened last Friday at Monmouth Museum on the campus of Brookdale Community College in Lincroft, where it will continue through June 29. It displays farm implements and pleasure vehicles from the agricultural museum.

Between April 11 and June 21, the State Museum in Trenton will have an exhibition on the design, ornamentation and social use of Victorian gardens. Also on April 11, Allaire Village in Allaire State Park in Farmingdale will sponsor a blacksmithing workshop, for which registration is required. FUNDS FOR COLLEGES

Governor Kean has signed a bill appropriating $22.8 million in bond money that will be shared by more than a dozen colleges and vocational schools for technological engineering and related projects. The bill was introduced last July by State Senator John A. Lynch, Republican of the 17th District (parts of Middlesex and Union Counties).

The money is from the $90 million bond issue approved by voters in 1984.

In an interview last week, Mr. Lynch, the Senate majority leader, said that the $22.8 million would reach ''virtually every level of higher education in the state'' because it was being distributed to state, private and community colleges - 14 in all - and because it also would help county vocational programs operated in conjunction with these colleges.

In most cases, Senator Lynch said, the money will qualify the institutions for matching grants from the state's Board of Higher Education, which will distribute the funds.

The largest appropriation, said Gloria H. Gordon, a spokesman for the Senator, is $3.4 million for the Stevens Institute of Technology in Hoboken for a project that will utilize computers for the school's science and engineering curriculum.

Middlesex County College and Stockton State College will get $2 million each for a technical services center and science and technology projects, respectively.

Many of the other sums were awarded on a shared basis. For example, Rutgers-Newark received $2 million, which will be used in a cooperative biotechnology program with Union and Morris County Colleges.
