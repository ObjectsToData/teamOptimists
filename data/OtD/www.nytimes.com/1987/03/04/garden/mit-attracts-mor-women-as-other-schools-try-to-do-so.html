M.I.T. ATTRACTS MOR WOMEN, AS OTHER SCHOOLS TRY TO DO SO

By NADINE BROZAN
Published: March 4, 1987

THE first woman to be admitted to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Ellen Swallow Richards, enrolled in 1889. Within seven years, there were 76 women at M.I.T.

Despite being coeducational for almost a century, M.I.T. has been slow to shed its image as a male bastion. Now that image may be fading. The class of 1990, which entered this year, is 37.9 percent women - the largest percentage ever at M.I.T. Next year's prospects appear equally bright: 1,589 women have applied, as against 1,361 last year.

Yet the trend at M.I.T. - one that the institution has worked for since the early 1970's - is not now reflected at engineering schools elsewhere.

''There has been a plateau or in some cases a decline in women's enrollment at engineering schools, so M.I.T. must be doing something right,'' said Susan Staffin Metz, director of special programs for women at the Stevens Institute of Technology in Hoboken, N.J., and a co-author of a new report on women in engineering education. The study surveyed enrollment patterns from 1981 to 1985 at 180 schools granting engineering degrees.

''We wish we had 38 percent women,'' said Stirling Huntley, director of admissions at the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena. Like M.I.T., the school makes special efforts to attract women. Unlike M.I.T., which offers majors in such fields as liberal arts, it requires students to major in either engineering or science. The two schools plan to cooperate in a campaign to familiarize women with career opportunities in science and engineering.

At Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute in Troy, N.Y., where women constitute about 20 percent of the freshman class, ''We have seen a pretty flat record in women's applications in the last five years,'' said Brian Spittle, dean of admissions. ''We are very concerned, and an effort to raise the proportion of women to 25 percent is part of a strategic plan for the next five years. We are planning to expand such areas as management and humanities as well as recruitment in the sciences.''

At M.I.T., where efforts are also being made to enroll more minority students, ''No one is talking in terms of absolute 50-50 parity for women,'' said Michael Behnke, director of admissions since May 1985.

''But,'' he said, ''it is assumed that, in the best of all possible worlds, that is where we would like to be. When I came here I was told that 25 to 30 percent was not enough, that it didn't create a fully coeducational atmosphere.''

As a result, Mr. Behnke and his staff began broadening and intensifying efforts. Strategies include expansion of the pool of women receiving recruitment literature, new audio-visual presentations, new publications, recruitment at high schools where many women are enrolled and invitations to women who have been accepted to spend three days on campus before making their decision.

Much of M.I.T.'s effort is aimed at spreading the word about the diversity of its programs, an advantage not shared by strictly technological schools. ''Not only are we strong in science and engineering, but also in such fields as music and economics,'' Mr. Behnke said. ''We have more intercollegiate athletic teams than any other college, 36, and have 13 varsity women's teams. We have a very strong faculty in the arts, and those offerings are particularly attractive to women who tend to have broader interests than men who come here.''

''Women tend to distribute themselves more widely through the academic curriculum than do men,'' he added. Women tend to major in biology, chemistry, aeronautics and social sciences, along with the two most popular majors, electrical engineering and computer science, he said.

The women who aspire to M.I.T. do not have an easy path, according to Marilee Jones, associate director of admissions, who coordinates the recruitment of women. ''The women who apply here are generallly better than the men,'' Ms. Jones explained. ''They have had to come through so many more hoops. They have been so discouraged.

''If they are good in math, they are still told to be math teachers. They are also told that the values of technology are not human, and girls generally do not want to enter fields not seen as human. And guidance counselors still tell them, 'You don't want to go there, it's too hard, intense, nerdy.' ''
