ROCKY MOUNTAIN JOURNAL; BAD IDEA, BAD ART, BAD DOG

By THOMAS J. KNUDSON, Special to the New York Times
Published: March 2, 1987

No one knows why the 20 or so bald eagles decided to roost this winter at the Rocky Mountain Arsenal northeast of here. But whatever the reason, they probably should have reconsidered.

The 27-square-mile arsenal complex, once used to manufacture chemical weapons and pesticides, contains some of the most polluted soil and water in the United States, according to the Environmental Protection Agency.

So far, though, the eagles seem unfazed, feeding on the prairie dogs that thrive in the area. But scientists, taking no chances, are trying to trap the birds for testing.

''We're concerned they might be picking up some contamination from the prairie dogs,'' said Dale Hoffman, a biologist with the United States Fish and Wildlife Service. ''But so far, we haven't caught any, so we don't know.''

The eagles will likely migrate northward in a few weeks. But if they are threatened with contamination, the Government will try to convince them to make an early departure. ''We would probably try to haze them out of there, with noisemakers or flashing lights,'' Mr. Hoffman said. Death and Taxes

About 5 P.M. Feb. 19, tribal police on the Navajo Indian Reservation in Window Rock, Ariz., shut down the nation's only daily Indian newspaper, The Navajo Times.

A spokesman for newly elected tribal chairman, Peter MacDonald, denied allegations that the closing was politically motivated. Last fall, The Times endorsed Mr. MacDonald's opponent, Peterson Zah, in the general election.

''I think they did it because they knew they could never control it,'' Mark Trahant, publisher of The Times, told The Arizona Republic. ''The free press is dead for the Navajo nation.''

The Navajo Times employed 48 people and had a circulation of about 7,000. Loyce Phoenix, chief of staff for Chairman MacDonald, said the administration closed the paper for business reasons.

''We were informed that the Internal Revenue Service had placed a tax lien on the paper for failure to pay past employee taxes,'' she said at a press conference. Singing Auctioneer

The dismal state of Denver's downtown real estate market will come into sharper focus on Friday when a guitar-strumming, singing auctioneer will try to dispose of nearly 200,000 square feet of office space, much of it in the city's most fashionable new high rises.

''No one has ever tried it before,'' said Charles Segal, field representative for Kaufman Lasman Associates, the Chicago-based firm conducting the auction. ''Hopefully, this will fill up some offices and help the market come back.'

Denver has one of the nation's highest office vacancy rates, between 25 percent and 30 percent, largely because of overbuilding and the collapse of the energy market in the early 1980's.

Some people worry that the auction will hurt the city's image. ''It's just the idea of an auction that's negative,'' said Bonnie Emge, chairman of the Building Oners and Managers Association. ''If they can prove me wrong, more power to them. Most brokers in the area have worked every angle they can.'' Arbiters of Taste

The kind of artwork on display in public buildings in Colorado will receive closer scrutiny if a bill passed by the Colorado Legislature is signed by Gov. Roy Romer.

Several legislators were disturbed by a $79,500 sculpture at the state penitentiary in Canon City that features gargoyles spewing streams of water into pools and silhouettes of dancing women, one of whom is pregnant.

''I don't know whether it's artistically good, but it's a psychological downer,'' said Dottie Wham, a Republican member of the Colorado House of Representatives who sponsored the bill to seat two legislators on a jury that selects art for public buildings.

Lucien Wulsin, chairman of the Colorado Council on the Arts and Humanities, which selects the juries, said the bill would be helpful because legislators ''will have to face up to the question of what is art.'' Others are concerned about how lawmakers will make their decisions. ''If they end up trying to politicize the process, I'm not sure they know what they're in for,'' said Gail Goldman, director of individual art programs for the council. Keep On Trucking

And finally, Colorado lawmakers have taken a stand in favor of preserving one of the West's most enduring images: a rancher roaring down the highway in his pickup truck with a dog in the back sniffing at the wind.

Kathi Williams, a Republican member of the Colorado House, thought such a practice was too dangerous and introduced a bill to eliminate it in urban areas.

One Colorado resident who opposed the idea was Will McBride. He told lawmakers that he has hauled dogs in the back of his pickup truck for years. Mr. McBride called the bill ''the silliest thing I've seen in years.''
