LESSONS; Education Chief Finds A School to His Liking

By IRVIN MOLOTSKY, Special to the New York Times
Published: December 16, 1987

William J. Bennett, the Secretary of Education, is a severe critic of many American schools, but he has finally found one that he really likes. It is James Madison High School and it exists entirely in his mind.

James Madison, which does not have a football team despite Mr. Bennett's fondness for school athletics, embodies what the Secretary regards as the model curriculum for a secondary school.

Mr. Bennett will release a report on this model curriculum later this month, a little more than a year after he issued a similar report on elementary school curriculums. He issued similar reports covering colleges and universities while he was chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities.

In a recent interview in his office, Mr. Bennett did not discuss the content of the high school report. He spoke rather of his philosophy about a proposed curriculum and the appropriateness of the Federal Government's highest education official's even pushing for a curriculum, which has long been a matter of local control. 'I Will Be Criticized'

After all, some conservatives opposed forming the Department of Education on the ground that it could lead to an attempt at Federal control over the schools.

''I will be criticized by some conservatives for doing this and by some liberals for what I actually say,'' said Mr. Bennett, a conservative. ''I cannot dictate. This is America. People just don't fall over and quote the Secretary of Education.''

Mr. Bennett, who has not hesitated to use his position as a pulpit, wants to provoke discussion and debate. He said, ''Someone should come forward and say, 'Here is the curriculum I'd like my son to have.' ''

What qualifies a Secretary of Education to offer such suggestions?

''I've been to 87 elementary and secondary schools and 40 colleges and universities,'' he answered. ''I'm an experienced school visitor.''

What can a Federal official learn from dropping by 127 institutions other than a surface view? ''In most places, you don't get more than a snapshot,'' Mr. Bennett conceded, ''but I read up on them well in advance. There is a pattern. They tend to be more alike than unalike.

''I have not encountered anyone in the field who either spends more time in the schools or knows more about what goes on in the schools than I do. I know my material pretty well. I think I'm now school smart.''

His ideal high school would emphasize education in the humanities, and Mr. Bennett chose the school's name carefully. He has long been a scholar and admirer of James Madison, one of the principal authors of the Constitution. School Is Public and Private

''I had thought about calling it Publius High School,'' Mr. Bennett said, but he decided against naming it for the pseudonymous author of the Federalist Papers (actually Madison, Alexander Hamilton and John Jay) on the ground that students not versed in the humanities would not understand its meaning.

The school could be found in the suburbs and the inner city, Mr. Bennett said. It is East and West, public and private. But no football team? No, Mr. Bennett said: ''No junior prom, no football team. It is not an entire high school curriculum.''

The main question the recommendations will address, Mr. Bennett said, is, ''What should our kids know.''

His views on the subject are well known in education. He has found American education lacking when it comes to science and the humanities.

The recommendations will come in a booklet titled ''James Madison High School: A Curriculum for American Students.'' It will have an initial press run of 50,000 and will be available free to anyone who wants it, the primary audience being principals, school superintendents, governors, legislators, parents and school board members.

When asked why his agency was undertaking the project, Mr. Bennett said: ''Why not? It's a good idea. There's nothing wrong with it. We know that things are wrong. What do you do to fix them? The Secretary can help engage in debate.''
