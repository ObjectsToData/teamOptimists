SENATE APPROVES SCHOOL PROGRAMS

By JULIE JOHNSON, Special to the New York Times
Published: December 2, 1987

The Senate voted today overwhelmingly approved elementary and secondary education programs that could cost up to $7.4 billion, including programs for disadvantaged children and grants to states.

The measure, approved 97 to 1, sets a spending ceiling of $169 million in the 1989 fiscal year for bilingual education.

The Reagan Administration supports the legislation, which needs to be reconciled with a similar measure passed in May by the House. That bill, passed 401 to 1, sets a $4.5 billion ceiling on spending, including $770 million in new programs next year.

Senator Claiborne Pell, Democrat of Rhode Island, who is chairman of the Education, Arts and Humanities Subcommittee, managed the floor debate. He said the bill ''reaffirms the role of the Federal Government in education.''

The bill, which would expand many existing education programs, covers remedial education programs established for economically and educationally deprived children through the fiscal year 1993. More for Students in Poverty

It would set a ceiling of $4 billion for school districts with high concentrations of students who live in poverty. According to Congressional estimates, a fourth of the 3.6 million children who enrolled in kindergarten in September are from poor families.

In addition to $580 million for state education block grants, the measure authorizes new programs for next year that could cost up to $1.4 billion. Among them is the a new $400 million program to increase basic skills instruction for disadvantaged junior and senior high school students.

It also sets a ceiling of more than $150 million in spending for programs for gifted and talented students, school dropouts, early childhood education for disadvantaged children and adult education for their parents and programs to increase overall achievement levels of school children.

William J. Bennett, the Secretary of Education, called the legislation a ''step forward for education reform.''

Mr. Bennett expressed approval for provisions to improve the programs for the education of disadvantaged children. He said provisions that call for retooling these programs, for example to expand parental involvement, parallel ''improvements we proposed to Congress in our reauthorization proposal.'' Named for Stafford

In a tribute to the former chairman of the Education subcommittee, the bill was titled the Robert T. Stafford Elementary and Secondary Education Improvement Act of 1987. Mr. Stafford, a Vermont Republican now the ranking minority member of the panel, has been in the Senate since 1971. He is retiring next year.

Senator Stafford said the legislation is important not only because it reauthorizes Federal education programs but because it reaffirms a Federal commitment to equal educational opportunities for America's underpriviledged school children.

''The degree of affluence of your family has absolutely nothing to do with what your I.Q. and brainpower is,'' Mr. Stafford said.

Several amendments to the bill were approved by voice vote. Generally they concerned such education-related issues as the creation of a $100 million program to provide local and state grants for education programs to be transmitted by satellite to school classrooms.

One amendment went further afield. Senator Jesse Helms, Republican of North Carolina, tacked on an amendment that he said would ''remove a gaping loophole'' in Federal communications law and ''eliminate completely the so-called Dial-a-Porn'' telephone operations.

Mr. Helms asserted that the Federal Communications Act of 1934, which does not prohibit sexual communication by telephone between consenting adults, has fostered the profligation of pornographic messages on interstate telephone lines.

''It's time for the Senate to say, forcefully, emphatically that enough is enough,'' Mr. Helms said. He cited a case of a 9-year-old boy encouraged to call a pornographic message line thinking he would reach Teddy Ruxpin, the talking bear.

Mr. Helms used parliamentary maneuvers to force a voice vote on the amendment, which passed 98 to 0. Even so, he did not support the education authorization bill.

Reacting to the Helms amendment, the American Civil Liberties Union called it ''patently unconstitutional and ultimately useless.''

Senator Stafford said that the amendment faces an uncertain future in the conference committee early next year when the House and Senate education bills are reconciled.
