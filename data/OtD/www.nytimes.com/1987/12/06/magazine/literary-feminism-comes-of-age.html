LITERARY FEMINISM COMES OF AGE

By Elizabeth Kolbert; Elizabeth Kolbert is a reporter on the metropolitan desk of The New York Times.
Published: December 6, 1987

OUTSIDE THE LEC-ture hall, sunshine falls in dusty beams through the trees. It is a scene suffused with tradition, a mixture that is two parts dry leaves to one part poetry. Students lounge on the steps, books propped before them, just as Princeton students have sat on the same steps, reading the same books, for generations.

But inside McCosh Hall, Elaine Showalter is trying to wreck this peace. She begins by telling the 100 students who have gathered to hear her lecture in the course ''Literature of the Fin de Siecle'' that she hopes by the end of class ''all of you will have been offended in some way.''

Professor Showalter moves on to outline the various strains of British feminism at the turn of the century, explaining that some feminists on the radical fringe labeled sperm a ''virulent poison'' and advocated sexual abstinence as a political goal. Finally, she points to some dates she has chalked on the blackboard. Instead of the usual battles and political upheavals, they mark watersheds in the struggle for sexual equality.

All this by way of introduction to a Victorian novel by George Gissing, ''The Odd Women.''

One of the country's most renowned feminist critics, Professor Showalter does seem capable of offending some of her students. After class, one young woman, an English major, protests privately that the teacher has managed to make an issue of feminist concerns even in the case of books that lack significant female characters. But to most of her colleagues, Professor Showalter's ideas, while still controversial, have lost their ability to shock. For feminist literary criticism, once a sort of illicit half sister in the academic world, has assumed a respectable place in the family order.

''Anyone worth his salt in literary criticism today has to become something of a feminist,'' says Peter Brooks, a professor of French and comparative literature and director of the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale University. ''The profession is becoming feminized.'' Feminist criticism has become so popular that Peggy Kamuf, a French professor at Ohio's Miami University, has coined a new term, ''femmenism,'' to denote male practitioners.

Like most literary phenomena, the so-called ''feminization'' of literature has more than a single meaning. On one level, the profession is simply becoming more female-dominated. Last year - five years after women first edged by men to earn the majority of doctorates in English and American literature - women earned 59 percent of the Ph.D.'s in the field, according to the National Research Council, and this trend shows no sign of abating.

Despite studies showing that female Ph.D.'s continue to receive full-time teaching positions at a lower rate than their male colleagues, the latest figures available from the Modern Language Association indicate that in tenure-track appointments in literature departments, women are roughly on a par with men.

But the feminization of literary criticism is not just a question of numbers. Fundamentally, it is a matter of asking questions, among them why so few works by women have survived to become part of our literary canon, and what the depiction of women in literature tells us about our culture and ourselves.

In the abstract, these questions may not appear particularly bold. But when posed in the context of contemporary scholarship, they can prove - and have proved - explosive. For they are intended to suggest that the literary tradition we have inherited has as much to do with sexual politics as it does with poetry and prose. ''One of the things we've learned is that you can go back and read everything in a different way,'' says Sandra M. Gilbert, a well-known feminist who is on leave this year from Princeton. Feminist critics are looking to reshape the tradition according to their own politics. ''Everything,'' says Professor Gilbert, ''has to be done over.''

In many ways, feminists have already succeeded in redoing the literary tradition, but not without dissent. Detractors see the changes feminists have made in college reading lists, for example, as a sign that political pressure has won out over more sober academic judgment. Leslie Brisman, a professor of English at Yale, says he is disturbed by some of the ''dubious additions'' that have been made to the Yale English curriculum. ''The politics or sex of the author should play no role'' in what is read, says Professor Brisman. ''Quality alone should determine what's in the curriculum.''

But here the argument seems to come full circle. For most feminists claim that in a world dominated by sexual politics, ''quality'' is a concept employed to keep the male literary tradition intact.

THE CURRENT POPularity of feminist criticism seems to spring precisely from its insistence that scholarship for too long has minimized the connections among literature, politics and sex.

This conviction is, in many ways, a throwback to the kind of political criticism that came of age in this country in the 1960's and 70's, when radical thought found a haven in the academy. At that time, the idea that literature cannot be viewed as distinct from the social forces that prompted its production and perpetuation transformed many literature departments into factionalized camps, with T-shirted Marxists on the left arrayed against tweedy traditionalists on the right.
