THE MANY FACETS OF PAUL KLEE, AT THE MODERN

By MICHAEL BRENSON
Published: February 13, 1987

PAUL KLEE'S inventiveness, imagination and wit have made him a popular, if not beloved, figure. Of all the 20th-century artists, perhaps only Picasso and Matisse have clearly been more influential. Klee's ability to work figuratively and abstractly, to make a few lines read like a dramatic script and to make the same color freeze, throb and glow inspired Joan Miro, Andre Masson, Saul Steinberg, Mark Rothko, Adolph Gottlieb, Robert Motherwell and many others.

But there have been doubts. Although Klee's work lends itself to thematic approaches - Klee and Music, Klee and Nature, Klee and Drawing, Klee and Abstraction, Klee and the Human Figure - small Klee shows have been disappointing. Recollected in tranquillity, the whole seemed greater than the sum of its parts; seen in fragments, however, it seemed less. It has been difficult to know just how limited or substantial Klee was, and how much his intimate, restrained, reflective approach was capable of achieving.

The Klee who emerges from the retrospective at the Museum of Modern Art is a major artist. Although it is dangerous to declare any exhibition definitive, this one feels definitive; it is certainly the best Klee show we are likely to see in this century. It contains around 300 paintings, watercolors, prints and drawings, selected by Carolyn Lanchner, a curator in the museum's department of painting and sculpture, and Jurgen Glaesemer, the curator of the Paul Klee Foundation at the Kunstmuseum Bern, which lent 100 works.

If the idea of 300 works seems oppressive, even destructive for an artist as private and concentrated as Klee, the large number is in fact necessary. Klee does not reveal himself all at once, the way Picasso or Pollock seem to do. It is only in familiar company that individual Klees seem fully at ease to stare, chug, dance, tumble about and sing. The artist's humor, skepticism, unpredictability and conviction are underlined at the beginning and then carefully developed so that the show unfolds with something of the discretion and profusion of Klee's career.

The work is remarkably diverse. Klee painted seasick checkerboard squares in which colors jump around as they did in the later striped paintings of Gene Davis. He painted cats whose eyes seem propped open and whose faces are pressed so close to us that it seems as if we are their prey. He painted quilted landscapes that look like rice paddies seen on a reconnaissance flight. He worked with oil, tempera and paste, on burlap, glass, cardboard and newspaper. No matter what Klee did, however, it was always Klee. He cast his spell over everything, and there is nothing by any other artist quite like it.

The spell has something to do with Klee's complex and particularly modern understanding of time. It is doubtful that any other century has experienced the same absolute difference between time as it is lived separately by each individual, time as it unfolds in the earth and stars, and time as it is measured by clocks. In her catalogue essay on ''Klee and the Avant-Garde 1912-1940,'' the curatorial assistant Ann Temkin suggests Klee's awareness of this division and conflict. By inspiring a quick take, a slow take and a sustained analysis, Klee managed to weave distinct experiences of time into almost every work.

Klee was from Switzerland, a country of mountains and clocks, and it is surely a sign of his distaste for his homeland that his clocks do not work. When he was 5 years old he made a drawing of a clock in which time is scrambled. In his world, measured time is inevitable, arbitrary and false. In the 1925 ''Fish Magic,'' clock time is illogical alongside the unhurried movements of fish, the bloom of flowers and the stillness of night. It is the welcome and dizzying fate of the human figure in the painting to carry inside him human, natural and cosmic rhythms.

Klee could build these rhythms into line, which he described as his ''most personal possession.'' His line can seem impulsive and automatic, the breath of the present, and at the same time frozen and absolute, the stamp of eternity. He needed the big, noisy rhythms of Western cities like Munich, as well as the more suspended rhythms of the Orient. Klee and Egypt is an intriguing subject. The links go well beyond the hieroglyphic quality of many of Klee's signs. His feeling for birds recalls the importance of birds in ancient Egypt. There are traces of Egypt in his ritual feeling for the sun and the moon, in his occasional feeling for boats as ritual barks and in his attraction to the Sphinx.

In the great 1932 painting ''Ad Parnassum,'' Klee was able to make himself into something like a ''cosmic point of reference.'' There are shafts pointing in four different directions. If the pyramid in the center seems eternal, the painting is constructed with divisionist daubs that seem glittering and transparent. If the landscape seems unchanging, there is also a sense of something behind the surface, pressing against it, like a modern city pushing against the past. In a work like this, everything, including past and future, seems present.
