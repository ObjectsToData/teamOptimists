PERFORMING-ARTS CENTER IS PLANNED FOR BOSTON

Special to the New York Times
Published: February 8, 1987

Boston's depressed theater district, where pornographic movie houses outnumber cultural outlets and show tickets are sold from a dilapidated trailer, will be transformed into a busy center of dance, music and theater within the next five years, city officials say.

The Downtown Cultural District, as the renovated area will be known, is intended to provide a focus for the city's sprawling network of performing arts troupes and growing-room for the crowded financial district.

The specifics of the plan were scheduled to be disclosed last Thursday, but changes in parking arrangements and traffic flows in the area will delay the announcement for two to three weeks, said Bruce P. Rossley, commissioner of the Mayor's Office of the Arts and Humanities.

According to the city officials, the plan calls for the development of a mixed-use neighborhood, with new housing units and office space and at least nine new theaters on the butterfly-shaped parcel of land that abuts both Chinatown and the area known locally as the Combat Zone, where prostitutes, drug pushers and pornographic movie houses abound. A Festival Atmosphere

Other features will be art galleries, a writers' workshop, satellite exhibitions from museums such as the Boston Museum of Fine Arts and the Institute of Contemporary Art, and three outdoor spaces for sundry performing artists.

''What I envision is something like a festival,'' said Larry Murray, director of ARTS/Boston, a cultural advocacy group. ''There will be leisure-time activities, a mix of theaters, boutiques, restaurants, health clubs.''

He added: ''There's a little bit of selfishness here. The residents want to get rid of the pimps and drug pushers; the restaurants want the business.''

City officials estimate that the district's cultural components will cost $10 million to $15 million a year to administer and operate. Those costs, they say, will easily be offset by an increase in revenue from the performing and visual arts, which, according to Mr. Rossley, generated $168 million for the Boston economy last year.

As the number of larger commercial productions has dropped, Mr. Murray said, the ranks of smaller local theatrical groups, dance companies and musical ensembles have grown. He said 23 shows played in Boston's five existing large commercial theaters for a record low of 35 weeks last year. ''It wasn't even one full theater for one full year,'' he said. Fewer 'Tryout' Shows

Until about two decades ago, the theater district was a prodigious forum for fledgling shows. Elliot Norton, a freelance theater critic, said that Boston used to attract 30 to 40 ''tryout'' shows - productions being tested for Broadway - to its 23 theaters annually, and that last year the city was host to just two. He added that most of the shows that come to Boston are touring shows and revivals.

According to Mr. Murray, the renovated culture district will end the theater's present reliance on ''blockbuster musicals'' by showcasing more performers and making performances more affordable. Under the plan, all culturally oriented groups using space in the district will be required to offer 10 percent of available tickets to school groups and the elderly at reduced rates.

The plan to revitalize the theater district originated with a group of 70 ''nomadic'' performance groups that were seeking permanent rehearsal and presentation spaces. Under the plan, one of the nine new theaters will be designated for dance groups, one for theater groups and another for music. The six others will be for mixed use.

''Right now, there is no single dance performance space,'' said Jeremy Alliger, director of the production group Dance Umbrella. He said that Dance Umbrella staged performances in seven different theaters this fall and that confusion over performance locations was testing audience loyalty. New Subway Stations Planned

According to arts organizers, the difficulty of finding performance space has forced many groups to relocate to the suburbs and out of state, where rents are more affordable. In addition, they say, the availabiity of performances outside Boston has prompted audiences to shun the theater district, which is seen as an unsafe neighborhood. To lure both performance groups and audiences to the cultural district, the plans include the construction of two subway stations and extensive parking facilities.

Planners say the centerpiece of the development, a retail and cultural center to be called CityPlace, will be housed in the first two floors of the new state transportation building. According to Ron P. Rothman, the planner of CityPlace and executive director of the Artists Foundation, a cultural sponsorship organization, the area will include exhibition space, an art gallery, a stage for evening jazz performances and a specially designated room for writers.

Mr. Rothman said that the 2,000 people who work in the transportation building would provide an instant audience for the exhibits there, which are scheduled to begin in the fall.

Planners said their proposal was drawn largely from the concept behind Quincy Market, an enormously successful downtown development featuring rows of restaurants and boutiques, but they pointed out that the cultural components would insure that the district draws more Bostonians than tourists.

''I think this is going to be a lot more fun than Quincy Market,'' Mr. Rothman said.
