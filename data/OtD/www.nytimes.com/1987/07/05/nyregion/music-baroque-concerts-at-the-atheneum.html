MUSIC; BAROQUE CONCERTS AT THE ATHENEUM

By VALERIE CRUICE
Published: July 5, 1987

THE Aston Magna Festival, the oldest Baroque performance summer music festival in the United States, will give three Sunday afternoon concerts on instruments of the period, at the Wadsworth Atheneum in Hartford on July 12, 19 and 26 at 3 P.M.

''Aston Magna is one of the best-kept secrets in the music world,'' said Ronnie Boriskin, executive director of the 15-year-old festival. ''We do more than just perform 17th- and 18th-century music. We're really devoted not only to performance of music on period instruments, we study intensively the period in which the music was written. We have a whole host of educational programs that support this.''

In 1977, members of Aston Magna made the first American recording for the Smithsonian Collection of Recordings of all six of Bach's ''Brandenburg'' Concertos on authentic instruments. The organization was also the first in this country to perform Mozart symphonies in concert on period instruments in 1982. The Aston Magna Festival was founded by Lee Elman and Albert Fuller in 1972, and took its name from Mr. Elman's estate in Great Barrington, Mass., which was the first site of the Aston Magna Foundation's activities.

On alternate summers for three weeks, the Aston Magna Academy selects a time and place in history, and brings scholars and experts together for lectures, workshops, master classes and concerts. In 1985, ''Bach and His World'' was the topic. More recently, about a hundred participants explored ''The Culture of Restoration England'' at Rutgers University in New Brunswick, N.J., where the academy was held.

An outgrowth of the three-week academy is the Aston Magna Public Outreach Program, in which the work of the academy is brought to the public throughout the United States. A day consists of three lectures followed by a concert. The program is underwritten by the National Endowment for the Humanities.

The three Hartford concert programs, which are also being given on Saturdays in Great Barrington and Fridays at Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, N.Y., each have a particular reason behind them, according to festival director and viola da gamba virtuoso, John Hsu.

''This first program accentuates the difference esthetically between French and Italian expression in a musical setting,'' he said. ''The viola de gamba was the most respected solo instrument in France during the Baroque era, while the violin was popular in Italy.''

The program features works by Couperin, Vivaldi, Marais, Castello, Frescobaldi and Marini.

Mr. Hsu, the Old Dominion Foundation Professor of Humanities at Cornell University, has been described as a phenomenal gamba player. He will be joined by three acknowledged experts in Baroque performance. Stanley Ritchie, a professor of violin at Indiana University, is, according to Mrs. Boriskin, one of the finest Baroque violinists playing today. Elisabeth Wright, harpsichordist, is on the faculty of the Early Music Institute at Indiana University. Catherine Liddell will play the theorbo, a plucked instrument similar to the lute.

The second concert honors the anniversaries of two Baroque composers: the first marks 300 years after the death of Jean Baptiste Lully's, the second, 350 years after the birth of Dietrich Buxtehude. The program will open with music from Lully's ''Bourgeois Gentilhomme,'' in string quartet version. While orchestral versions are well known and played, this version will receive its American premiere with the festival. Works by Byrd and Mozart will be performed as well.

In addition to Mr. Hsu, Anthony Martin, co-director of the Stanford Baroque Orchestra at Stanford University, will play the violin and viola. Charles Sherman, keyboardist for the Aulos Ensemble, will play harpsichord. David Miller will play viola; Loretta O'Sullivan, cello; Linda Quan, violin, and Nancy Wilson, violin and viola, are the members of the Classical Quartet, which is the first of its kind formed with the intent of playing Baroque and early classical music on period instruments. They came together as a result of Aston Magna.

The third concert features Bach's motet ''Lobet den Herrn'' and his Cantata No. 106, ''Gottes Zeit Ist die Allerbeste Zeit.''

Three fantasias for three viols by Henry Purcell will demonstrate the 17th-century equivalent to the string quartet.

In England the viol was probably the most popular instrument of its time and came in different sizes -treble, tenor, bass and so on. ''When you put a group of these together you get a consort,'' said Mr. Hsu.

Works by Byrd, Ferrabosco and an anonymous composer are included.

For more concert information, call the Wadsworth Atheneum at 278-2670 extension 322.
