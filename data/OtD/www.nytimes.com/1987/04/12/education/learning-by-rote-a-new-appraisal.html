LEARNING BY ROTE: A NEW APPRAISAL

By SANDRA SALMANS
Published: April 12, 1987

SOLVE the following problems in American education:

A recent survey of the nation's 17-year-olds found that two-thirds of them did not know that the Civil War occurred between 1850 and 1900; one-third did not know that Columbus sailed for the New World before 1700.

In another survey in which 17-year-olds were asked to multiply two decimals together, nearly three-quarters of the students got the correct answer. But when they were asked in a multiple-choice question to most closely approximate the product of 3.04 x 5.3 - an exercise that tested their basic understanding rather than their grasp of the multiplication tables - only one-third chose 16; many guessed 1.6 or 160.

The two examples, drawn from surveys by the federally funded National Assessment for Educational Progress, do more than illustrate the level of ignorance among young people today; they also highlight one of the disturbing contradictions of American education. The first example shows that students are not committing to memory some of the most elementary facts in a number of areas such as American history. But the second indicates that many students are memorizing basic math facts but have not been trained to apply them to problem-solving. The dismal test scores are the latest of a series of results that have periodically generated concern among educators and parents about the way youngsters learn - or fail to learn - in school.

What has raised that concern recently to the level of a national debate is the comparison of the United States education system with that of Japan, whose students consistently outperform Americans on international math and science tests. The Japanese system is famous for relying heavily on rote or mechanical, unthinking memorization, drilling students on factual material until they can recite it, word for word. A basic characteristic of Japanese secondary education is ''adherence to the view that there is only one right answer,'' according to a report last January on Japanese education by the United States Department of Education.

The evident success of the Japanese approach has led some educators, notably in the Government, to call for the return of methods of teaching, such as rote, that were discredited years ago. ''There are some things to be said for rote and memorization and getting the facts and getting the answer right,'' said William J. Bennett, the Secretary of Education.

Underlying this suggestion, in part, is the growing concern that this nation's educational system is producing a generation without an awareness of a shared cultural heritage and a common pool of knowledge. ''You need to retain the fact that 1776 is the date for the Declaration of Independence, just as you need to know that 2 x 7 is 14,'' said Chester E. Finn Jr., Assistant Secretary for Education in charge of the Office of Educational Research and Improvement. ''You need to retain facts, and that's another word for memorization.''

A number of educators, however, disagree that rote is the remedy for what ails American education. Some say that the rigidity of the Japanese system - which, for all its success, has recently come under attack in its own country - stifles the sort of creativity that makes a nation such as the United States win Nobel prizes. Educators also maintain that the Japanese performance has more to do with that nation's homogeneous culture and its reverence for education than with its use of rote - and they even dispute the extent to which rote is used in Japan. ''If anything, there is less rote in Japan than in the U.S.,'' said Herbert Walberg, a research professor of education at the University of Illinois in Chicago who has visited Japanese classrooms extensively. ''They do memorize,'' he added, ''but there's more emphasis on comprehension.'' In elementary school science classes, for example, children design experiments to prove a phenomenon. Whether the approach is rote or higher understanding, Mr. Walberg added, ''They do both better.''

The reappraisal of the merits of rote learning marks an about-face from the direction taken by educators in the 1960's. In that era of reform, if not revolution, educators swept aside the emphasis on fact acquisition that had characterized the educational systems in the United States and many other nations. In history and literature, the byword became ''relevance''; history courses were folded into social studies, which stressed topical issues, and English students stopped memorizing and reciting ''The Rime of the Ancient Mariner'' or Marc Antony's funeral address from ''Julius Caesar.''

''LINGUISTICS PROFESSORS said, 'We want people to love poetry, and that doesn't happen when they live in fear and trepidation of forgetting it,' '' said Rosemary Winkel Johann, director of the graduate reading program at Millersville University in Pennsylvania and chairman of the elementary school section of the National Council of Teachers of English. ''The thing became to read it to kids.'' In science and math - the New Math of set theory - students were liberated from learning facts or formulas; with open-book exams and hand-held calculators, they could always look it up.
