CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By Eleanor Charles
Published: April 12, 1987

BUXTEHUDE'S BIRTHDAY Legend has it that Johann Sebastian Bach walked 200 miles from Arnstadt to Lubeck to hear Dietrich Buxtehude play, so strong was Buxtehude's influence on young Johann. Yale University School of Music will celebrate the 350th birthday of the Danish composer and church organist with a symposium of concerts and other events today through Tuesday.

The Yale Camerata, directed by Marguerite Brooks, and the Yale Collegium Musicum, Eva Linfield, conductor, will perform at 8 tonight in Battell Chapel. Richard Rephann is to give a recital at 4 P.M. Monday at the Yale Collection of Musical Instruments, 15 Hillhouse Avenue, performing works of Buxtehude and his contemporaries on a restored two-manual harpsichord built in Hamburg in the 1750's.

Charles Krigbaum will play an all-Buxtehude program at 8 P.M. Monday on the von Beckerath organ in Dwight Memorial Chapel, and the final concert at 8 P.M. Tuesday features chamber music for voices and instruments with Laurence Dreyfus, viola da gamba; Drew Minter, countertenor; Sally Sanford, soprano, and Jaap Schroeder, violin.

An exhibition of source materials may be seen in the Yale University Music Library at 11 A.M. Monday, with comments to be made by Harold Samuels, music librarian. A discussion about performing Buxtehude's music will take place at 2 P.M. Monday in the Whitney Humanities lecture room. Participants are Eva Lindfield, Sally Sanford, Jaap Schroeder, and Kerala Snyder and Christopher Wolff, lecturers. Four lectures on various aspects of Buxtehude's music are scheduled Monday and Tuesday mornings by these and other faculty members.

Registration for the entire symposium costs $25. Admission to individual concerts is $8, lectures $5. Overnight accommodations are available nearby. Call 432-2300 for a complete schedule or more information. JAPANESE PRINTS

Fifty-five prints by one of Japan's premier 19th-century woodblock artists will be on exhibition at the Lyman Allyn Museum, 625 Williams Street, New London through May 27. ''The 53 Stages of the Tokaido Road,'' as the series is called, traces the biennial pilgrimage from Kyoto to Edo made by nobles of the Shogun Government to reaffirm their loyalty to the emperor. Along the route were 53 way stations at which the barons and their retinues could enjoy almost all of the comforts of home.

Ando Hiroshige, a member of a firefighter brigade, made the trip in 1832 as part of the entourage, sketching the scenery and his traveling companions from start to finish. A year later, they were published as woodblock prints, enjoying instantaneous success with the public.

Events planned in conjunction with the exhibition include a lecture on Hiroshige and his work at 2 P.M. today by Edith G. Gipstein, a museum docent. Admission is $3. On April 24 and 25, area garden clubs will present an Oriental flower show, culminating in an Oriental food festival from 6 to 10 P.M. Saturday, April 25. Tickets to the event will cost $40, benefiting the museum.

A Japanese Family Festival Day is scheduled from noon to 5 P.M. Sunday, April 26, when visitors may make and fly paper kites, see a display of traditional Japanese dolls, watch demonstrations of calligraphy and origami (the art of paper-folding) and listen to music played on the koto, a 13-stringed Japanese psaltery. Admission is $1. Admission to the exhibition is free from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Saturday, and from 1 to 5 P.M. Sunday. The phone number for more information is 443-2545. WOMAN OF EGYPT

Jihan Sadat, widow of the slain Egyptian president, Anwar Sadat, will speak on the subject of world peace at 8 P.M. Wednesday in the Lyman Center for the Performing Arts at Southern Connecticut State University in New Haven. Her autobiography, titled ''A Woman of Egypt'' will be published this summer, detailing in part her commitment to women's rights. Mrs. Sadat represented the Arab-African nations at the international Women's Conference in Mexico City. During the past two years she was visiting professor at American University in Washington and at the University of South Carolina. Tickets to the lecture cost $5. Call 397-4435 for reservations. CARTOON ART

The Museum of Cartoon Art, which originated in Greenwich in 1974 and since 1977 has been in Rye Brook, N.Y., has sent an exhibition to the Museum of Art, Science and Industry in Bridgeport, on view until June 14. Founded by the cartoonist Mort Walker, the Museum of Cartoon Art is reputedly the only one of its kind in the world.

The Bridgeport show covers cartooning from Honore Daumier's 19th-century political satires to Garry Trudeau's ''Doonesbury.'' More than 40 original works include ''Moon Mullins,'' 1930, by Frank Willard; ''Hogan's Alley,'' 1896, by Richard Outcault; ''The Katzenjammer Kids;'' ''Dick Tracy;'' ''Flash Gordon;'' ''Gasoline Alley'' and many other classic strips from the 1930's through the 1960's. A number of Fairfield County cartoonists have lent their work, among them Stan Drake, Frank Johnson and Dick Hodgins, who do ''Blondie,'' ''Bringing Up Baby'' and ''Henry,'' respectively.

A series of nine programs has been scheduled throughout the exhibition. Some are lectures by artists, others are cartoon film festivals, still others are demonstrations by exhibiting cartoonists. The programs begin today with a talk by Randall Enos, a Westport cartoonist and illustrator, talking about linocut collage, his favorite medium.

Show hours are 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. Tuesday through Friday, noon to 5 P.M. weekends. Admission, including special programs or lectures, is $3.50 for adults, $2 for students and the elderly. The museum is at 4450 Park Avenue. Phone 372-3521 for more information. CHAMBER MUSIC SERIES

Handel's Sonata in G minor (Op.1), transcribed for soprano saxophone, will be performed at 4 P.M. today by Albert Regni, saxophonist, and the Harvey String Quartet. The last of this season's chamber music series at St. John's Church in Salisbury also features the Quintet for Soprano Saxophone and Strings by Seymour Barab, dedicated by the composer to Mr. Regni. Beethoven's Quartet Opus 59, No. 3 completes the program. Tickets are $8 at the door.
