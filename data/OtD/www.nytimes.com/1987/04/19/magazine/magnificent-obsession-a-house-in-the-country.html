MAGNIFICENT OBSESSION: A HOUSE IN THE COUNTRY

By Erica Abeel; Erica Abeel is a writer and a professor at John Jay College of the City University of New York.
Published: April 19, 1987

STANLEY GULKIN, A TRIM, FIERCELY energetic man in his late 40's, is a tax and corporate lawyer who has, he says, ''worked very hard and done very well.'' At his office in Livingston, N.J., he gets 50 phone calls a day. ''You get pulled in all directions - this is success! It makes me feel important.'' But a couple of years back, he felt he was ''on a merry-go-round. My daughters were growing up, I started noticing my gray hair, started worrying about mortality. You ask yourself, 'What's it all for?' So instead of having a mid-life crisis, I bought a house in the country.''

Gulkin is among the growing number of middle- and upper-middle class Americans whose pursuit of happiness has led them to the purchase of a weekend retreat. For the obsession with having a country house is upon us. If every age has fads, foibles and conventions which reveal its collective psyche, one of ours is to acquire a second home in the mountains, in the woods or at the beach - in any rustic area, in fact, within three hours of the city.

At his house in the Northwest Woods section of East Hampton, L.I., a three-bedroom contemporary opening on a deck and pool, Stanley Gulkin is ''at peace. I lie on the lounge in the water, look up at the trees, listen to mellow music.'' But like many country-house owners, he doesn't think of his second home as a luxury. ''It's now experienced as part of the standard package necessary to the good life,'' says Paul Wachtel, distinguished professor of psychology at City University Graduate Center. ''Society ups the ante from generation to generation in what we view as standard requirements for comfort.''

The second-home vogue is reflected in a fever of buying and building. East Hampton Town Supervisor Judith Hope reports a 30 percent increase between 1984 and 1986 in the number of building permits issued. Over the same period, northwestern Connecticut has seen an enormous surge in land buying, reports realtor Joan Titus, while in Columbia County, N.Y., two hours up the Hudson from the city, realtor Grace Post says house sales have tripled. Today's buyers tend to be the omnipresent dual-career couples and an increasing number of single people who no longer consider a mate a requisite for acquiring a cottage of their own. And the boom is scarcely confined to the environs of New York. John A. Tuccillo, chief economist for the National Association of Realtors, believes ''we are witnessing a national phenomenon that is tied to major metropolitan areas.''

What's behind the boom? Why aren't people spending their disposable income on vacations abroad, or, like previous generations, salting it away? To find out, over a period of several months, I stalked New York City's weekend playgrounds, interviewing second-home owners, prospective buyers, realtors and ''year-rounders.'' The reasons that emerged were an ironic, often paradoxical mix.

Financial benefits were cited as a powerful inducement, of course. Mortgage interest rates have plummeted; under the new tax laws, the second-home owner can continue to deduct the interest on the mortgage, and as a result of the Federal tax revisions, owning a second home has become one of the few remaining tax shelters available to middle-income people. At the same time, property values have escalated: ''In the last 10 years, there's been an appreciation of 15 percent a year in vacation houses in desirable locations,'' according to Melvin M. Kazdin, a real-estate lawyer in New York City.

Yet most second-home owners insisted the financial investment was not a prime motivation. They dilated on the joys of nature. They spoke of a rural retreat as the grand urban remedy, an antidote to the city disease. And too, I found another reason, half-alluded to, unarticulated or - most telling - vociferously denied: it's the latest fashion imperative.

Now that ''the jammed appointment book'' is in style, observes the writer and social commentator Barbara Ehrenreich, the second house is ''a form of conspicuous leisure to go with conspicuous busyness.'' Social critic Paul Fussell dubs the second-home fad ''conspicuous duplication: owning two houses is a way of revealing you have a handsome income.'' Both of them are echoing the economist Thorstein Veblen, who, in 1899, in ''The Theory of the Leisure Class,'' noted the American tendency toward ''conspicuous consumption'' and ''invidious display.'' Has there ever been a better vehicle for showing off in America than a place in the country?

IF A MERE 10 YEARS BACK, STATUS WAS DEFINED by your address, job and where your kids went to school, this generation has added to the list: country-house location. According to the mostly unspoken code, if it's in northwestern Connecticut, you've scored high in the status sweepstakes, displaying both a handsome income and genteel style. By the same snobbish standards, the upper Catskills confer negative status (''We'd never consider that area!'' a new owner in Connecticut told me) - unless, like Laurance Rockefeller, you've gobbled up thousands of acres in that area's lush Beaverkill Valley, part of which he is making available for purchase. (Dan Rather has already bought the first house.) Dutchess County, N.Y., covers the spectrum, from the horsiness of socially registered Millbrook to the blue-collar hue of Wassaic. Ulster County, N.Y., as one devotee put it, offers ''reverse snob appeal,'' for those secure enough to pursue the unfashionable.
