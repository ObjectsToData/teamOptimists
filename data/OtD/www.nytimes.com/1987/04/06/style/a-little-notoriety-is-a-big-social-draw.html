A LITTLE NOTORIETY IS A BIG SOCIAL DRAW

By GEORGIA DULLEA
Published: April 6, 1987

A dream dinner party, in some strata of New York society, would include Ivan Boesky and Jean Harris, if she happens to be free. The notoriety achieved by both is said to qualify them for the A list among hosts like Carolina and Reinaldo Herrera.

''They would add spice to the evening,'' Mr. Herrera speculated, ''because she was convicted of murder and he pleaded guilty to robbing nearly the world. But most normal houses don't have these great names at their fingertips.''

Mr. Herrera, an editor at Vanity Fair magazine, and his wife, the fashion designer, rely on controversial friends like Claus von Bulow to create dinner party chemistry. ''Claus is a great catalyst,'' he said. ''People instantly loathe him or like him.''

This comes as no surprise to the Popular Culture Association, whose 3,000 academicians scrutinize best-selling books, movies and television shows as well as newspaper gossip columns in search of cultural trends. Celebrity Gap

One clear trend is that the lines between fame and notoriety are becoming more and more blurry. The academicians attribute this to a decline in ethical standards and to the so-called celebrity gap. The great maw of television has been so quick to devour movie stars and other conventional celebrities, they say, that it must invent new kinds to feed on.

''Never before have Americans been so desirous of brushing up against the notorious and the wealthy,'' said Ray Browne, head of the popular culture department at Bowling Green State University in Ohio. ''These people are a force in television, magazines, books, every medium. We're mad to be in the same room with them, to let a little of the danger they engaged in rub off on us. If they're well-born like von Bulow or the Mayflower Madam, well, that makes it even more wonderful because we're trading up.'' 'Guess Who's Here'

Nowhere is it more wonderful than in New York, where people whose lives have been touched by scandal -Mr. von Bulow, Sydney Biddle Barrows (popularly known as the Mayflower Madam), and Steve Rubell among them - have emerged from their ordeals to become social lightning rods. Their appearance at otherwise dull gatherings prompts whispers of ''Guess who's here.'' Their names pop up in society columns, on guest lists for charity events and on place cards at fashionable dinners. It helps that they already know how to hold a cocktail glass and a meaningless conversation, hostesses say.

Marshall Fishwick, a humanities professor at Virginia Polytechnic Institute in Blacksburg, Va., was struck by the parallel to the 60's.

''In those days,'' he recalled, ''the rage was to have a radical in your living room - Rap Brown or Angela Davis.'' The phenomenon was chronicled by one of his former students, the writer Tom Wolfe, who coined the term ''radical chic.''

Yet as Professor Fishwick noted, the celebrities of the 60's were worlds apart from those of the 80's. Their backgrounds differed sharply from those in the social set that courted them. Their causes were political and even revolutionary.

By contrast, said Neil Postman, a communications professor at New York University and an authority on television's cultural impact, ''Today we have white-collar characters doing things we can identify with -cheating on taxes, manipulating stocks, prostitution.'' He was referring respectively to Mr. Rubell, Mr. Boesky and Miss Barrows.

''In an age when political interests are dulled, these people are acting in their own self-interests,'' he added. ''It's a new version of the old entrepreneurial impulse that Ronald Reagan says made this country great.''

In ''redefining crime and the meaning of crime'' Professor Fishwick said, ''we've let a lot of people into the house who would formerly have been dismissed. In fact, we half envy them.'' Lunch With a Madam

Dropping names as she talked, Miss Barrows said: ''People I've always wanted to meet at parties now want to meet me. I had lunch with Malcolm Forbes.'' So she did, said Mr. Forbes, adding in jest that the occasion was ''the first time I dined with a madam without paying.''

Even before his acquittal on charges of attempting to murder his wife, Sunny, Mr. von Bulow continued to lunch at Mortimer's with his companion, Andrea Reynolds. But now Glenn Bernbaum, owner of the clubby restaurant, no longer jokes about seating Mr. von Bulow and Imelda Marcos at the same table.

Instead, Mr. von Bulow now jokes about his courtroom travails. At the recent opening night party for ''Les Miserables,'' he told a reporter he enjoyed the show ''because I feel so much like Jean Valjean.''

As with the Watergate figures, the popular-culture experts note, there are often books in the new celebrities' stories. Both Miss Barrows and R.Foster Winans - the former Wall Street Journal columnist convicted of securities fraud who is now making the social scene - have written books. Mr. von Bulow's lawyer, Alan M. Dershowitz, has written a book about the trial, and Miss Reynolds is writing a book. Barbara (Basia) Johnson, the stepmother in the Johnson v. Johnson trial, has emerged from the social shadows to promote Barbara Goldsmith's new book on the case.
