WATERBURY PROJECT LOOKS FOR A WAY TO PRESERVE ETHNIC MUSIC

By Sharon L. Bass
Published: January 11, 1987

MARY EURIST'S father was a tenor in a church in Moscow. Her mother was also a choir singer in Russia.

''That's how they met,'' recalled the 53-year-old Naugatuck woman who likes to smile - and sing - a lot. ''So naturally there was always a lot of singing in the house.''

She and her husband, Stephen Eurist, also of Russian descent, say they have kept their family's heritage alive by singing native folk ballads at church and at family gatherings.

''When I was young, my mother would rock me to sleep and sing French songs,'' said a Waterbury resident, Robert Bifaillon, 55, whose parents are from Quebec.

Looking for a way to preserve their music, Mrs. Eurist, Mr. Bifaillon and other Naugatuck Valley residents stumbled across Jeremy Brecher, a community historian and author from West Cornwall who they heard was trying to recruit people from different ethnic backgrounds in Waterbury for a music project.

That was the start of the Waterbury Ethnic Music Project. Mr. Brecher has since collected more than 1,000 musical performances and interviews from 20 ethnic groups, primarily in Waterbury - an industrial city of 100,000 in the heart of the Naugatuck Valley - and has made a collection of audio and video tapes. He said this was the first project of its kind ever done in the state.

The participants sang and played all the music themselves at their homes, churches, clubs and parties.

The tapes have now become part of the permanent archives at the city's Mattatuck Historical Society. Copies are to be made for the Waterbury Public Library and the Library of Folk Culture at the Library of Congress in Washington, D.C.

Mr. Brecher and his co-workers, Sandy and Caroline Paton, who run Folk Legacy Records in Sharon, and Martin Bresnick, professor of composition at Yale University's School of Music, have also created 13 radio programs of music and interviews for the project to be broadcast early this year on Connecticut Public Radio. They are now working on a documentary based on the project for Connecticut Public Television.

They will continue to collect more music, Mr. Brecher said, noting that there were a number of ethnic communities not well represented in the project so far, such as Portuguese and Caribbean.

A festival was held last November at the historical society featuring live music from the project's participants. Mr. Brecher said another festival was planned for this year.

Mrs. Eurist said it was about time ''someone did something about preserving'' Waterbury's rich ethnic diversity.

''I figured the United States is made up of different ethnic backgrounds,'' she said. ''If we all did just what the Yankees do it would be very dull.

Financing for the project has come from the National Endowment for Humanities, the Connecticut Humanities Council and the Waterbury Security Savings and Loan. Some of the pieces Mr. Brecher has taped and stored include Polish polkas, Yankee fiddle tunes, Albanian wedding music, Afro-American gospel hymns, a 92-year-old Italian woman who sang religious songs from her native village and a 5-year-old fourth generation Irish girl who sang native tunes with an elderly relative.

Mr. Bresnick, who served as a consultant to the project, said he got involved because he wanted to learn more about the richness of ethnic music.

''A lot of people think the only music they hear in American cities comes from popular music on the radio,'' he said. ''It was interesting to see that homemade music was still a part of their culture.''

Mr. Brecher, who has written four books on labor history including ''Brass Valley'' - about people who worked in the Naugatuck Valley brass industry - said he found himself welcomed everywhere as he worked on the music project. ''In every community I worked with I got an enormous amount of help,'' he said. ''People said to me, 'this should have been done a long time ago.' ''

He attributed the existence and endurance of Waterbury's multi-ethnicity to the brass industry, which drew people from all over the world, Europe especially.

While gathering participants for the festival, Mr. Brecher said he did encounter some tension among some of the ethnic groups he worked with, but most of the participants ''told me they thought it was important to preserve their heritage through music, not because they think their group is better than any other group, but because they believe it's valuable to the community.''

Mrs. Eurist said she also came across some ethnic bias. But, ''after these people worked with all the different groups, bigotry melted as they all became friends.

''I was out there dancing the Irish jig, dancing to the French fiddlers and Puerto Rican singers,'' she said. ''As artists, everybody enjoyed everybody's company.''

Mr. Brecher said he was impressed by the ''pure motivation'' of the people he worked with. ''They aren't doing it for money or for fame,'' he said. ''They're not competitive or selfish.''

When he first began the project, he said, he was not trying to make a statement. ''But after doing it I learned the importance of valuing your own heritage at the same time valuing others.''

''I'm proud to be a Russian,'' said Mrs. Eurist, as her husband quietly sang one of his favorite Russian ballads, ''Farewell My Little Gypsy Sweetheart.''

''We do anything we can to preserve our ethnic music,'' Mrs. Eurist added. ''I just want to make sure people know the Russians are still around.''
