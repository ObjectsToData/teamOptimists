AFTER CHURCH: PORTRAITS OF A PROUD BLACK COMMUNITY

By STEWART KAMPEL
Published: January 11, 1987

THE photographer stood in the midday summer sun. Her subjects posed in the shade, standing or sitting in the doorway of the First Baptist Church in Riverhead, before a blank, black paper screen that sometimes wafted and billowed in a breeze.

What emerged were more than three dozen portraits of a slice of Long Island life, black worshipers in their Sunday summer finery, projecting, in the words of the photographer, Toba Tucker, ''a pride, an interest in the church and a lot of love.''

The portraits, titled ''Yesterday's Memories, Today's History,'' are part of the Riverhead Black Folklife and Oral History Project, an ongoing effort to preserve the history and cultural heritage of the Riverhead black community. It is sponsored by the East End Arts and Humanities Council and is aided by state, county and private financing.

''I photograph my subjects directly, on line,'' said Ms. Tucker, a largely self-taught photographer. ''My camera is always level. I know because I always check the grid in my screen. I suppose the distance makes the difference in the portraits.''

Ms. Tucker, who works only with natural light, uses a single-lens reflex camera, a Hasselblad, with a 120-millimeter portrait lens that she bought used about 10 years ago. The black-and-white film has a speed of 400 A.S.A. and she develops her own work on 16-by-20-inch exhibition-size paper.

For Ms. Tucker, who currently occupies a house on the Shinnecock Canal, the exhibition at the Riverhead church marks a new presentation of her striking portraits, some of which, notably her continuing series on the Shinnecock Indians, have been seen in recent months on the South Fork.

Originally, Ms. Tucker set out to photograph the subjects in the Riverhead show in their homes, but she could not get the right light and was unhappy with the results. She decided to attend church services and, she said, was ''very touched by the devotion - I found it very uplifting.''

Explaining her results, she said: ''Taking pictures after church may have created a certain atmosphere, a cooperation amongst them. Maybe that's what I caught.''

Ms. Tucker, who generally works alone with a tripod, methodically tries to know her subjects before asking them to pose. She has been working for several years on her portraits of the Shinnecocks, devoting endless hours to gaining their confidence and trust.

The Riverhead project, which was supported by a grant from the New York State Council of the Arts, was completed in several weeks, and there were some Sundays in which she could not work because of weather conditions. ''The people were rather shy,'' Ms. Tucker remarked, ''and I sometimes had to cajole them and convince them to pose for me. I selected the individuals intuitively and by their availability.''

There are 30 portraits on view out of the 45 to 50 she composed. She supplied each of the subjects with copies of their portraits and also gave them copies of her work-prints.

Her favorite portrait in the show is that of Gracie Goodridge and her grandchildren. ''There are certain elements in the portrait that for me represent all the people,'' Ms. Tucker said. ''There's love, a certain refinement and a communication with each other. There's an attitude there, a very proud attitude.''

Ms. Tucker has earned a living with her camera for 10 years, having left the Whitney Museum of American Art, where she served for six years as administrator of its Downtown Branch Museum and coordinator of education programs.

After deciding to make the career change, she set out to photograph people and became involved in a three-year project at Daytop Village, the drug rehabilitation center, with the aid of a Creative Artists Public Service grant.

Her full-face portraits of former drug addicts helped her win a National Endowment for the Arts fellowship in 1981, which she used to photograph Navajo Indians in the Southwest.

That work has been exhibited at the Museum of the American Indian in New York and is in its permanent collection and in the collection of several other museums in the United States. Her photographs of the Shinnecocks are expected to be on view in Guild Hall in East Hampton, and her seascapes of the East End, done in color, will be shown at the Marine Museum in Amagansett in the summer.

Ms. Tucker recently received another State Council on the Arts grant, a Sponsored Project Grant through the Catskill Center for Photography, which will be used to complete the Shinnecock project.

The Riverhead portraits will be on view through January. The First Baptist Church of Riverhead, on the Northville Turnpike, is open daily from 10 A.M. to 1 P.M.
