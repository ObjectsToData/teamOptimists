SHE HAS HAMPSHIRE FEELING FRISKY AGAIN

By Fox Butterfield
Published: January 4, 1987

ADELE Smith Simmons is on the phone again. This time it's an official of the MacArthur Foundation. He wants some advice from Mrs. Simmons, the peripatetic president of Hampshire College, a small, nontraditional liberal arts school in the western Massachusetts town of Amherst.

Mrs. Simmons, the daughter of an affluent businessman who served as a trustee of the University of Chicago, chats easily. Then she casually mentions that Hampshire, after several years of sharp decline in its enrollment, has just had its largest applicant pool in a decade. ''We seem to be on our way to being a 'hot' school,'' says the 45-year-old Mrs. Simmons, her pale blue eyes sparkling. Her claim might seem a pleasant bit of hyperbole. But in an anxious era in which debt-ridden students are increasingly intent on high-paying professional careers, government aid to education is shrinking and small liberal arts schools are becoming an endangered species, Hampshire's turnaround is an unusual success story. And most professors, students and administrators here attribute the school's rebound largely to the energy, enthusiasm and ability of Mrs. Simmons, who became Hampshire's president nine years ago and who remains one of the few women heading a coeducational college.

Hampshire's troubles stemmed from its heretical approach to education. There are no grades, no traditional academic departments, no grouping of students into freshmen, sophomores, juniors and seniors and no tenure for professors.

Instead, to encourage what Mrs. Simmons terms ''active learning'' and ''creative inquiry,'' the 1,020 students design their own courses, studying independently and then meeting with professors to present papers or original projects for which they are given written evaluations. Students and faculty members at Hampshire like to compare this approach to that of a graduate school. Mrs. Simmons, who graduated from Harvard College and who earned her doctorate from Oxford, prefers to characterize Hampshire's philosophy as ''distinctive'' rather than unorthodox.

But because Hampshire was started in 1970, at the height of student rebelliousness, the image of a hippie college has stuck with it. ''It is an unfortunate misperception,'' says Peter Gluckler, executive assistant to Mrs. Simmons. He pointed out that the original idea for Hampshire arose from a plan devised in the 1950's by the neighboring four-college consortium of Amherst, Smith, Mount Holyoke and the University of Massachusetts, which wanted to establish an alternative to the typical American undergraduate experience. The venture was to demonstrate a way in which traditional institutions could lend their prestige to bold experimentation.

But Hampshire's image wasn't enhanced when one student, according to local legend, used the study of a Frisbee to satisfy all his requirements. ''When you're riding on the bus and pass Hampshire, people look out and say 'Isn't that the Frisbee college?' '' says David Smathers, a second-year student from Los Altos, Calif., who is the student representative on the college's board of trustees.

Even well-intentioned projects sometimes seemed to go astray. An innovative farm center, intended to devise new ways to help small New England farmers, has left a flock of sheep at the entrance to the campus. And a successful experiment to breed dogs to guard livestock has populated Hampshire's lawns with stray dogs that nuzzle students wherever they rest.

There's nothing wrong with the sheep and dogs, of course. But with the back-to-basics movement in education in the 1980's and the new stress on preprofessionalism among students choosing colleges, Hampshire came to seem too permissive. Like another maverick school not far to the north across the Vermont border, Bennington College, Hampshire fell into disfavor.

From a high of 1,218 students in the fall of 1980 the school's enrollment began to drop - down to 1,115 in 1982 and then to 965 last year. The number of applicants also dwindled sharply, from a high of 1,290 in 1975 to 863 in 1982.

The decline in applicants could have proved fatal. As a very young school without a large endowment and with only a small number of alumni who were barely into their 30's, Hampshire is heavily dependent on tuition charges for its budget. The pressure was on Mrs. Simmons, who became president in 1977, to keep enrollment high.

But Mrs. Simmons took a radical step. ''We took a careful look at our applicants and saw that both the number and quality was declining,'' she recalls in her spartan office in the Science Center, the walls made of cinderblock and the windows set too high to see out. ''There were too many people coming here who were mismatched, who didn't understand that Hampshire is a challenging intellectual place that demands rigorous self-discipline and hard work. I wanted to send out a different message about our quality.'' SO INSTEAD of panicking and accepting virtually everyone who applied, Mrs. Simmons decided to cut enrollment to try to keep up the standards of the student body. A policy was formulated to eliminate the bottom 20 percent of students who would have previously been accepted.
