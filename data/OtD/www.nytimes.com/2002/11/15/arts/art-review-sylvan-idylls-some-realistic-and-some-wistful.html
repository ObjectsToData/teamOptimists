ART REVIEW; Sylvan Idylls, Some Realistic and Some Wistful

By GRACE GLUECK
Published: November 15, 2002

Would landscape really be there without artists to record it? They have ways of manipulating nature into memorable moments, editing it for beauty, milking it for pleasure and nostalgia, recording its buffeting by technology, even inventing fantasy scenes. At their best, artists can brew something from the essence of particular landscapes that infiltrates the soul.

Small wonder that landscape is a popular genre, in prints as well as other mediums. Nor is it surprising to find simultaneous landscape print shows at two august institutions, the New York Public Library and the Yale Center for British Art, although they needn't be seen together to be appreciated.

In New York, ''A Legacy in Landscapes'' displays more than 100 new additions to the library's voluminous print collection by nearly 60 artists, ranging from Fragonard and Pissarro to present-day practitioners like Alex Katz, Roxy Paine and Terry Winters. All the new works were bought with the aid of a fund established by the bequest of Mary W. Covington in honor of Elizabeth E. Ross, curator of prints at the library from 1968 to 1981.

In New Haven, ''The Romantic Landscape Print: 'The Chiaroscuro of Nature' '' is from Yale's vast collection, although the prints are not necessarily recent acquisitions. The artists are British, and the focus is on the late 18th and early 19th centuries (with a few 20th-century exceptions, like the printmaker Peter Doig, who happens to appear in both the library's and the Yale Center's shows). J. M. W. Turner, John Constable, Thomas Girtin, William Blake, Samuel Palmer and John Sell Cotman are some of the star attractions here, along with more recent names like Richard Long and Hamish Fulton.

The display of earlier works in context with those by present-day artists gives each print show a pleasurable continuity. In terms of technique, both institutions have made pretty conservative choices at the contemporary end; still, the prints can't help reflecting trends and concepts of their day.

At the library, at Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street, Sylvia Plimack Mangold's drypoint and etching, ''Maple Tree'' (1998), a close-up rendering of intersecting bare tree branches, keeps good company with William Delamotte's etching of almost 200 years earlier, ''Beech in Windsor Forest'' (1805-6), a stagey old monster whose thick, knotty ramifications are examined in minute detail.

And Edvard Munch's stark, wintry etching and drypoint ''The Garden'' (1902), a mysterious cone-shaped bush of dense black that looms in a winter-white setting, is by no means at odds with Markus Lupertz's ''Landscape No. 3'' (1998) in drypoint, lithograph and screenprint, a powerfully stroked, almost abstract sketch of a tree-lined stream that leads the eye to a conic shape in the distance.

A standout is Salvador Dalí's etching ''Untitled (Fantastic Beach Scene)'' (1935), a gruesome rendition of body parts and menacing chairs that projects a disturbing Surrealist vision close to the approach of World War II. Other treasures range from Pieter van der Heyden's ''Blind Leading the Blind'' (circa 1561), a wonderfully satirical engraving of two roguish mendicants after a print by Hieronymus Bosch, to Kim Wescott's arresting drypoint ''Re-echo'' (2000), an ingenious abstraction that mingles black, silver and red marks in a dense evocation of the landscape of her native Australia. In short, the library has chosen well.

By comparison, ''The Romantic Landscape Print'' at the Yale Center is a rather dry show, mounted as part of a larger celebration of British Romanticism there this fall. In more than 100 examples -- etchings, aquatints, mezzotints, line engravings and lithographs -- the exhibition tracks the development of Romantic interest in the natural and built worlds, from its late 18th-century origins to its impact on our own time.

The great Turner is well represented, in particular by plates from his ''Liber Studiorum,'' or ''Book of Studies,'' a series of engravings in etching and mezzotint from his own paintings, begun in 1807. Turner made several of the engravings himself, but the majority were done by professionals. One of the more impressive is ''Norham Castle on the Tweed'' (1816), a dark-toned print of a dramatically sited castle in northern England, a site that Turner found so riveting that he periodically revisited it, producing a number of paintings.

John Constable, too, produced a series of prints from his own paintings. An actual oil is shown here, ''Hadleigh Castle, the Mouth of the Thames -- Morning After a Stormy Night'' (1829), along with two wonderful mezzotints, a medium chosen for its effectiveness in conveying lights and darks, made from it by David Lucas, Constable's devoted but difficult engraver.

A wonderful print by Richard Wilson, an artist influential on later Romantics like Turner and Constable, deserves mention here: ''The Summit of Cader-Iris Mountain'' (1775). One of a set of six prints Wilson designed and distributed privately, it may be the first to depict this remote mountain in Wales, seen here as a dramatic volcanic upthrust in a stark and rocky setting. The print established the mountain as a stop on the itinerary of tourists in search of the picturesque.

Among the more unusual offerings is a small group of wood engravings by William Blake that are rare examples of landscapes by him. Tiny but lively depictions of pastoral scenes, made in 1821 (reprints from a 1977 Blake book are shown here), they were done as illustrations for a poem written by Ambrose Phillips based on Virgil's first ''Eclogue.''

Two later artists, friends who admired Blake, also make a strong impression: Samuel Palmer and Edward Calvert. Palmer's dark, brooding etching ''The Lonely Tower'' (1879), depicting two shepherds at night gazing up at a tower silhouetted against a starry sky, is a nostalgic paean to the English landscape. Calvert's ''Bride'' (1828) is memorable for its vision of a standing female nude in a wonderfully composed Edenic setting as she watches a male hunter (perhaps) go off to provide.

But with due respect to the brilliance and finesse of some of the work in this show, I came away with the feeling of having also seen more than the usual share of boring scenery by less-than-lustrous talents. As for the contemporary section, with unadventurous works by John Piper, Mr. Fulton, Mr. Long and Mr. Doig, among others, it needs a bit of bolstering.

''A Legacy in Landscapes'' remains on view at the Humanities and Social Sciences Library, New York Public Library, Fifth Avenue at 42nd Street, (212) 869-8089, through Jan. 4. ''The Romantic Landscape Print: 'The Chiaroscuro of Nature'' remains at the Yale Center for British Art, 1080 Chapel Street, New Haven, (203) 432-2800, through Dec. 29.
