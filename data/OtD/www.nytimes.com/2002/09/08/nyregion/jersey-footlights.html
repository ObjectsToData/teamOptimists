JERSEY FOOTLIGHTS

By Margo Nash
Published: September 8, 2002

Old Magic, New Spin

Louis Prima, the baritone with big hits like ''That Old Black Magic'' and ''Just a Gigolo,'' sang them around the country, the world and at the Hoboken Italian Festival years ago. Tonight his daughter Lena Prima will reprise some of her father's greatest hits in a tribute to her father at the Hoboken Italian Festival in Sinatra Park. Ms. Prima lives in Las Vegas; her mother, Gia Maione, who sang with Prima, grew up in central New Jersey and still lives there. The festival begins at noon. The Jazz Lobsters and other groups will perform during the day. (201) 216-0252.

A Hip (Very) Young Audience

Remember ROY G. BIV? The acronym for the colors of the spectrum is also the title of a children's song written by the performer known as Mr. Ray. His real names is Ray Andersen, and until recently, he played in Meat Loaf's band. Now he tours the country playing for children. ''It is not just a roly-poly type of sound,'' he said. ''It's totally sophisticated pop rock. My fan base? It's the fan base I always wanted, but I just didn't think they would be under 7.'' Mr. Ray performs at the Forum Theatre Company in Metuchen on Saturday at 2 p.m. (732) 548-0582

A Voice for Children

A birdlike figure looms over a crevice in Jo-Anne Echevarria-Myers's painting ''Another Classic Example.'' It is about child abuse, one of several Ms. Echevarria-Myers has done on that subject on display at the Atlantic City Arts Center. She is one of four artists with Hispanic roots whose work is being exhibited in celebration of Hispanic Heritage Month. ''The bird symbolizes an ever-present hurt in children,'' said Ms. Echevarria-Myers, a Cape May resident. ''There is more rage in the world, and children are becoming the victims.'' Through Sept. 30. (609) 347-5837.

Mastodons' Moving Day

It won't be long before two Bergen County mastodon skeletons arrive at the Bergen Mall. One is the Hackensack mastodon, discovered in 1962 when Route 80 was built; the other is the Dwarskill mastodon, found in 1973. Both are in the collection of the Bergen Museum of Art and Science, which moves to the lower level of the mall in a month or so. Next Saturday and Sunday, the museum heralds its arrival with a music and art festival on the north side of the mall. The festival features local and regional musicians and artists, a jazz program and children's activities. Peter Knipe, president of the museum, said the festival ''launches our new beginning.'' (201) 291-8848.

Pageant for Workers

Back in 1913, many newspapers would not cover the Paterson silk strike. So a group of New York intellectuals, artists and activists, including John Reed and Mabel Dodge, decided to tell the story at Madison Square Garden in a show to raise money for the workers. They called it the Paterson Pageant, and it starred strikers. In the process, a new type of political theater was born. ''They pulled a Mickey Rooney 'Let's put on a show,' '' said Rosemary McLaughlin, a playwright who has been commissioned by Playwrights Theater of New Jersey to write a play about the pageant. Ms. McLaughlin, who is on the faculty of the Drew University theater department, said she learned about the episode when the American Labor Museum asked her to create a pageant for East Side High School in Paterson.

What They Were Reading

A Drew University history professor has received the New Jersey Council for the Humanities 2002 Humanities Book Award. Jonathan Rose was honored for ''The Intellectual Life of the British Working Class'' (Yale University Press, 2001). For his analysis, he devised a way to study 200 years of British working-class reading and cultural tastes, from the Bible to the Beatles. ''From 'Hamlet' to 'Uncle Tom's Cabin,' we have tried to imagine how a reader would have responded,'' Mr. Rose said. ''What we are doing is recovering actual reader history, going back to memoirs, marginalia, library records, oral histories and articles in working newspapers.'' Among his findings: British working-class readers seemed to prefer Sir Walter Scott to Marx. Margo Nash
