MY MANHATTAN; Not Just a Library, an Oasis of Civilization

By SUSAN JACOBY
Published: April 12, 2002

Early on the morning of Sept. 11, I was reading a biography of Lincoln and relishing the cloistered atmosphere of the New York Public Library's Center for Scholars and Writers, tucked away on the second floor of the main research building at Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street. Around 8:50, an emergency phone call shattered the calm, and the library, like all of New York, soon closed for the rest of that horrific day.

Two days later, the noble Beaux-Arts structure, compared upon its dedication in 1911 to the great cathedrals of European cities, reopened to the public and stayed open in spite of occasional bomb scares in nearby Grand Central Terminal.

''It feels weird to be away from the TV set,'' one young woman said to a friend as they entered the library's third-floor reading room on the morning of the 13th. Her companion, sporting turquoise hair and triple-studded earlobes, replied: ''Weird but good. Like, all of these books make you feel, like, safe.''

I loved the library long before I first set foot inside it at age 26, but I never loved it more than I did during the days when the wind from Lower Manhattan was still wafting the smell of death throughout the city. Spending hours in an oasis of civilization somehow made it easier to endure the heightened awareness of just how fragile civilization can be.

It is natural, I suppose, that a great library should be a more important presence in the life of a writer than it is for those whose work does not involve constant contact with the written word. My old calendars tell me that I have certainly spent more time in this building during the last three decades than anywhere but in my own apartment.

I have relied on the library to provide the research materials for five books and uncountable magazine articles. But what has always impressed me most forcefully is the institution's connection to the everyday life of New Yorkers, from giggling teenagers to the very old.

My father wrote his high school term papers in Room 315, the quarter-acre reading hall that has been the undisputed glory of the building since its opening. As a child, I was enthralled by Dad's story of having hoisted himself triumphantly (and illicitly) atop one of Edward Clark Potter's famous stone lions on the night of his high school graduation in 1931.

In 1971, when I took my own seat in Room 315 and began working on my first book, I wondered whether my father might have occupied the same chair. I adored the place even though by the time I'd made my way there, the reading room (along with the entire building) had taken on the aura of an aging, impecunious beauty who, like the city, had seen better days.

No one has captured the enduring democratic hospitality of 315 better than Alfred Kazin in his 1978 autobiography, ''New York Jew.'' Kazin spent five years there writing ''On Native Grounds,'' which instantly, upon its publication in 1942, established him as a rising star in New York's intellectual firmament.

He described the reading hall as ''that asylum and church of the unemployed; of crazy ideologists and equally crazy Bible students doggedly writing 'YOU LIE!' in the reference books on the open shelves; of puzzle fans searching every encyclopedia; of commission salesmen secretly tearing address lists out of city directories.''

This struggling cast of New York characters, pursuing various dreams in a city just emerging from the Depression, provided a perfect backdrop for Kazin's creative labors. ''I liked reading and working out my ideas in the midst of that endless crowd walking in and out of 315 looking for something,'' he recalled. ''I, too, was seeking fame and fortune by sitting at the end of a long golden table next to the sets of American authors on the open shelves.''

Today's equivalent of salesmen tearing out lists of addresses are the people who spend their hour of allotted time at computer work stations, e-mailing business contacts and friends.

From its beginnings, this building -- on the site of what had become an obsolete reservoir by the last decade of the 19th century -- has served both as an instigator and a mirror of social change in New York.

The name New York Public Library is somewhat misleading, at least as far as the financing of the four research libraries is concerned. While the neighborhood branch libraries, with circulating collections, are publicly financed, the great research collections are largely supported by private philanthropy. Less than one-third of the budget for the research centers comes from public sources.

''That word 'public' poses a real problem in raising money,'' said Rodney Phillips, director of the Fifth Avenue collection (officially called the Humanities and Social Sciences Library). ''Most New Yorkers have no idea that the research collections depend largely on private generosity, a situation that doesn't exist in any other city. The one thing that is public -- and free -- is the service we give.''

The present library was founded upon the 19th-century fortunes of three New York moguls: John Jacob Astor, James Lenox and Samuel J. Tilden, a former governor of New York and loser in the hotly contested 1876 presidential election. In 1895 three private libraries were consolidated to create the New York Public Library.

The Fifth Avenue building, designed by the architectural firm of Carrère & Hastings and constructed for $9 million, then a munificent sum, opened to the public on May 24, 1911.
