The Soul of a New Machine

By Scott McLemee
Published: August 18, 2002

The Egg Code

By Mike Heppner.

462 pp. New York:

Alfred A. Knopf. $25.95.

The minimalist fiction once fostered by creative-writing programs -- with its miniature profiles in anemic anomie -- is dead, perhaps of exhaustion. The future belongs to M.F.A. maximalism: fiction that sprawls, with narratives as complex as the page can bear, its story lines branching out across whole continents or eons (preferably both). The cast of characters is huge and densely interconnected; as many as possible will be allegorical figures, often bearing funny names. The saga is to be rendered in prose having the texture and intricacy of a circuit board, with all metaphors ultimately deriving from esoteric fields of knowledge. (The maximalist author may have studied quantum mechanics or Renaissance poetry in graduate school. Failing that, there's always Google.) No writer of ambition can be satisfied with quiet epiphanies now, when the soundtrack of our lives throbs with so many layers of noise. Where Raymond Carver was, David Foster Wallace shall be.

The title of Mike Heppner's first novel, ''The Egg Code,'' is a bit of jargon from the earth sciences: it refers to a matrix of numerical data used to record the size, concentration and stage of development of ice floes. (The mother of one of the characters is a glaciologist.) But the title has another layer of meaning, more genomic than geological: the packets of information transferred across generations from parent to child. And finally, there is a Web site (in the novel though not actually online) called www.eggcode.com, which a hacker uses to nearly shut down the Internet by posting bogus information. That almost-apocalypse ties together (insofar as is possible) the many strands of the book's decidedly nonlinear narrative.

The characters here come in squadrons. One set is committed to the printed word: a typesetter, a German émigré publisher, a novelist who wants to create modernist fiction of high seriousness but pays the bills by writing advertising copy. Another wing is ''new media'' -- associated with a murky entity called the Gloria Corporation, which maintains routers (the technology that governs traffic flow on the Internet). Individuals from the print and digital clans meet, marry, have children and do cruel things to one another; the plot lines zigzag across the decades as the information technosphere develops, bit by bit, from its origins as part of the American defense program to its late-1990's ubiquity.

All the while, a rogue router called Gloria 21169 has somehow taken command of the Net. This is either the deepest layer of the book's narrative logic (the portion of the iceberg hidden from view) or a complete red herring. Possibly both. The frequent jumps between story lines are, presumably, the surface effect of Gloria's command of the message pathways -- scrambling, as it were, the novel's egg code. (My hunch is that Gloria's foremother was the enigmatic, unlocatable yet pervasive ''V'' of Thomas Pynchon's first novel.)

For the first, say, 100 pages, it is possible to chloroform one's disbelief in the hope that the parts will start to add up to something. Characters with names like Scarlet Blessing and Simon Tree-Mould soon leave the reader feeling imposed upon. The author makes efforts to suggest a deeper pattern of meaning amid all this joyless wackiness -- some vaguely McLuhanesque point about the sterility of the individualism fostered by Gutenberg, with maybe a dash of Tabasco from Ortega y Gasset's ''Revolt of the Masses,'' reflecting indignation at how dumb and gullible everybody (else) now is. But the passages in which ''The Egg Code'' tries to get serious are its weakest moments. There are no ideas here, only gestures -- like something a real estate agent would do in an empty house to show where the furniture might go.

This is frustrating, because the author has a modest though real gift for satire. It comes through especially in the chapters involving Derek Skye, a successful author of inspirational literature -- the sort of guru found leading seminars on PBS during pledge week. In midlife, he experiences an overdue reckoning with the vacuousness of his own advice and writes an insanely candid book that provides a glimpse into the nihilism his motivational workshops had sought to paper over: ''Death is the goal. Death is

the right thing at the end of everything. Imagine your life as a fast trajectory moving toward a large, unavoidable target. You're not going to miss it. There's no skill involved.'' (See also Don DeLillo, ''White Noise'': ''All plots move deathwards.'')

Meanwhile, Derek's wife churns out a manuscript of her own during their separation -- Many Voices, One Vision,'' a multicultural children's book loaded with warm treacle, even though her tales of ancient wisdom (completely fabricated of course) are all about the treacheries of romantic love.

Somewhere inside ''The Egg Code'' (concealed beneath layers of irony, pastiche, inside joking and all the other lard of maximalist metafiction), there may be a perfectly good novella about unhappy families and the strange alliance of self-help and self-loathing. But it is not struggling nearly hard enough to get out.

Like a Seal

1.

How little we need

to be happy:

a half-kilo increase in weight,

two circuits of the corridors

at Sloan-Kettering

in bedroom slippers

a morning without aspirin

silence gentle as a pit,

a distant

sand dune

behind the green bridge

a patch of lawn

and you beside me beginning

to knit a new sweater.

2.

The rhythmic movement

of the needles between your fingers

has something of the beat -- ai-li

lu-lu-li+-

sounds that live within a man

with all the other unrealities

not wiped out

in the massacres.

Abba Kovner

Translated from the Hebrew

by Eddie Levenston
