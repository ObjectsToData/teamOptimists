Full Lives, Captured In Memory And Words

By Dick Ahles
Published: December 29, 2002

IN times past, tombstones often told the story of a life.

This example comes from the book ''Churchyard Literature,'' published in 1877:

''Samuel Bishop, town clerk of New Haven, 54 years; its representative at 54 sessions of the general assembly, judge of the county and probate courts; died mayor of the city, and collector of the port, 7 August, 1803, aged 80.''

And from a Litchfield cemetery come these words:

''Here lies the body of Mrs. Mary, wife of Deacon John Buel, Esq. She died Nov. 4, 1768, aged 90 -- having had 13 Children, 101 Grand-Children, 247 Great-Grand-Children, and 49 Great-Great-Grand-Children; total 410.

Three Hundred and Thirty-six survived her.''

But fashions change in death, as in life, and the inscriptions on gravestones today are much less likely to venture beyond the basics: name, date of birth, date of death.

Yet for each life, the stories remain to be told, the memories recounted. At the end of each year, the Connecticut section tries to do just that, publishing a collection of obituaries about people who made a difference in the state. These vignettes, of those who died in the year 2002, were written by Dick Ahles.

A Familiar Voice

Bob Steele was on the radio for 66 years. He was hired by station WTIC in Hartford in 1936, shortly after coming to the city from California to announce motorcycle races at Bulkeley Stadium.

When the racing season ended, he applied for a job at the radio station, went through a rigorous audition and was hired for $35 a week.

Mr. Steele retired from daily broadcasting 55 years later, but continued to do his morning show on the first Saturday of each month in the spring, summer and fall. He broadcast his final program on Nov 2.

Mr. Steele's morning show, which had a 48-year run on WTIC, was unlike any other. It was a leisurely paced, tightly formatted program with every segment and even the most minor features broadcast precisely at the same moment every day.

''When people listened to Bob, with the same things happening at the same time every morning, they got a sense that their morning was under control,'' said Arnold Dean, an associate of Mr. Steele for 37 years.

For many years, Mr. Steele had higher ratings than any other local broadcast in the United States. His plaque in the Radio Hall of Fame in Chicago says he broadcast to half a million people every day.

Mr. Steele sprinkled the show with corny jokes, the pronunciation of a word for the day, birthdays of listeners 80 and older, temperatures from other places, little stories of little significance he called ''tiddlywinks'' and music mostly from the 1920's to the big band era of the 1930's and 1940's. Listeners might hear Sir Harry Lauder singing ''Roamin' in the Gloamin'' from time to time and a march on Saturdays, but they would never hear rock 'n' roll.

On Fridays, Mr. Steele would announce his weight, in pounds and ounces and hardly a week went by without him reminding listeners of the proper way to prepare scrambled eggs. (You had to add a teaspoon of cottage cheese.)

He was known for predicting the outcome of sporting events, usually incorrectly. In the 1960's, when Mr. Steele dropped his evening sportscast, WTIC announced that he would continue to make sports predictions ''as a public service.''

As the host of the most listened to morning radio show in southern New England, Mr. Steele was the person families turned to during snowstorms to hear if the schools were being closed.

Mr. Steele died in his Wethersfield home on Dec. 6. He was 91.

For several days after his death, WTIC received calls from hundreds of listeners who wanted to reminisce. One caller, after waiting on hold for an hour, said, ''I was afraid I'd have to shave again.''

That was Bob Steele's kind of joke.

A Major Contributor

For Samuel Birnbaum, the honorary degree he received from the University of Connecticut at age 84 was especially meaningful because his formal education had ended when he was 7 years old.

Sy Birnbaum left school in 1916 to help out in his father's little clothing store in New Britain.

He and his sister Ida took over the store when their father died in 1932 and did extremely well, so well that the Birnbaum family became major benefactors of New Britain General Hospital and the University of Connecticut's Health Center.

Mr. Birnbaum was awarded an honorary doctorate of humane letters in 1993 in appreciation for his family's million-dollar contribution to advance treatment and research in the health center's psychiatry department.

He was also generous to New Britain General Hospital, establishing several scholarships at its school of nursing. In 1987, the hospital's emergency room was dedicated to him.

Mr. Birnbaum also supported the Central Connecticut Association for Retarded Children, New Britain Memorial Hospital, Resurrection House and the Jewish Association for Community Living in Hartford.

In 1991, Town and Country magazine included Mr. Birnbaum in its annual listing of major contributors to art, science, scholarship and the humanities.

Mr. Birnbaum died March 21. He was 93.
