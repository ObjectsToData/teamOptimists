Learning Politics In Class

By Gao Zhan
Published: December 7, 2002

Whenever a survey comes out showing American students doing poorly on math and science tests, comparisons are always made with other countries where students do better. China is invariably on that list. Americans are amazed when they hear of Chinese elementary-school students who handle high-school math easily or when they see new Chinese immigrants to the United States who race ahead in class even while their English is lagging. Why does the Chinese education system produce such strong performers, and will that system enable China to challenge American supremacy in science and technology?

I am a product of that system, and while it can produce students with super scores, I don't believe it poses any threat. I don't have too rosy a view of American education, either, but the Chinese education system, tightly controlled by the government, is by no means a success.

It is, above all, hopelessly politicized, a vehicle of propaganda. ''Political studies'' is mandatory from first grade through college. I still shiver from the memory of having to recite the ''old three pieces,'' three Communist essays written by Mao Zedong, every morning before class started. Now students in China recite Jiang Zemin's ''three represents,'' a theory recently enshrined in the party charter. If you see a six-year-old cooing political jargon on Chinese TV, don't be surprised.

The system places much more weight on producing docile ''revolutionary successors'' than on imparting knowledge. In elementary school, all pupils are encouraged to join the Communist Juvenile Pioneer Group. In middle and high school, good students are supposed to be members of the Communist Youth League. This system doesn't produce engaged citizens, but cogs for the decaying Communist system.

Perhaps the biggest problem is that the system discourages intellectual inquiry, especially in the humanities. A friend told me recently that in one political studies class, her son questioned the teacher's claim that ''capitalism is dying out.'' The student gathered his courage, raised his hand and said, ''It doesn't seem to me capitalism is dying out.'' To this the teacher responded, ''Capitalism will die out.'' The student again raised his hand and said, ''That doesn't seem to me the case, either.'' The embarrassed teacher burst into a rage and accused him of trying to wreck the class.

The incident ended with the student's mother, my friend, being summoned to the school and forced to apologize to the teacher for her son's ''disturbing order in class.'' Kids who raise questions and demand satisfying answers are usually branded as problems. By squashing questions and stigmatizing curiosity, Chinese schools suffocate independent thinkers.

The pressure on children can be cruel. Studying 15 hours or more a day is commonplace. Little attention is paid to children's normal physical and psychological development. Students are intentionally embarrassed daily by having their names posted on classroom walls to remind each youngster of his or her academic standing in class. A slip down the list would not only cause shame but could also lead to harsh discipline from parents. The competition in big city schools is intense, and it is not rare to read of students committing suicide on the eve of college-entrance tests or over low scores. Students are poisoned by cutthroat contests for academic success. Often, desperation is a given.

Lest you think the pressure is only on the children, parents are not exempt. A friend of mine was summoned by his son's teacher, a summons that produced the same sense of dread Americans feel when called by the Internal Revenue Service. Before he was even through the door, the teacher declared, in front of her colleague: ''Your son is a waste! He failed two tests today and he is not interested in improving his score.'' Americans might think this unacceptable behavior, but in China, a teacher can act this way with impunity.

Chinese students may indeed score high on some tests. But an inhumane education system, kidnapped by the totalitarian government and producing hotheaded nationalists and submissive cogs, leaves China in no position to lead the world.
