Prespectives; Myths in the Anti-Liberal-Arts Kingdom

By ARTHUR LEVINE; Arthur Levine is president of Bradford College in Massachusetts.
Published: August 21, 1983

LATELY I've been made to feel like a snake-oil salesman.I haven't committed any crime to speak of. But I can see the disapproval in the headshaking, the doubting looks and the skeptical questions I am asked. You see, I am in the suspect profession of being a liberal-arts college president.

I meet parents who rail that the liberal arts are too esoteric, or put more simply, "a waste of time and money." They want their children to get jobs after college. I hear from legislators who complain that the liberal arts are out of step with the times: a luxury education appropriate to periods of prosperity. The future of the nation rests with the new technologies. I speak with students who ask if it wouldn't be wiser to study engineering, medical technology or welding. But my favorite is the fellow who sat next to me on a plane recently and inquired confidentially whether more liberal-arts graduates were unemployed or driving cabs.

There is a growing sense in America that the liberal arts have become anachronistic, perhaps even irrelevant. Enrollments at liberal-arts colleges are on the decline. It's a tragic situation. An the saddest part is that it is rooted in three myths about the liberal arts.

Myth 1: The liberal arts are inherently impractical, esoteric or unworldly.

This widely shared belief is a historic misreading. The fact is that the liberal arts have always been practical. Throughout the history of higher education, there has been an intimate bond between the liberal arts and the demands of the job market. Students at the earliest colleges -- Bologna, Paris and Oxford -- nearly a millennium ago, chose this curriculum not for the marvelous preparation it offered for counting angels on the head of a pin but because it was the path to jobs and professional school. Logic and Latin were highly marketable skills, and colleges were the only place to learn them.

The American college, a descendant of this medieval university, followed in the same tradition. It offered a classical curriculum consisting of just 12 subjects, ranging from catechism and Aramaic to rhetoric and logic. What could be more esoteric? But the fact is that these schools were vocational-training institutes. They were established explicitly to educate a learned clergy, the people who would lead the colonical communities.

Since that time, the program of the liberal-arts colleges has undergone many and varied permutations, but the commitment to useful education has remained a persistent and consistent theme. If the history of higher education teaches us anything, it is that a liberal-arts education has always been practical. Preparation for life was, is and always will be the mission of the liberal-arts college.

Myth 2: A liberal-arts degree is a poor credential in the job market.

All evidence suggests exactly the opposite. A liberalarts degree appears to be one of the best work credentials. Consider a recent study by the American Telephone and Telegraph Company comparing the earcer success of liberal-arts and technical students. The company found that liberal-arts graduates were almost twice as likely to achieve managerial positions as holders of business or engineering degrees. The study concluded: "There is no need for liberal-arts majors to lack confidence in approaching business careers. The humanities and socialsciences majors in particular continue to make a strong showing in managerial skills and have experienced considerable business success. We hope and expect this to continue."

Study after study at corporations and colleges all over this nation have produced the same conclusion. A liberalarts education spells the difference between a job and the succession of positions and richness of life we call a career. Narrow technical training does get students a first job, with perhaps greater ease and benefits than for many liberal-arts graduates, but it offers little mobility. In the long run, a liberal-arts degree is an excellent investment.

During the last decade, engineers and business graduats have enjoyed a short-run advantage. They have got more and better entry-level offers than liberal-arts students, but this appears to be changing. As reported in the June 28, 1983, edition of The Wall Street Journal, and many other sources, opportunities for liberal-arts graduates are expanding while technical offers are dropping. In short, the liberal-arts graduate, particularly the student who includes experiences like internships and practical minors in his education, has splendid short-term as well as long-term prospects.

Myth 3: The liberal arts are outdated and out of step with the times.

This may be the greatest misconception. If anthing, the liberal arts are more critical today than perhaps at any other time in human history: We live in an era when the ability of young people in basic skills -- reading, writing and arithmetic -- has plummeted. We live in a time when our values and beliefs are confused and uncertain. We live in an age when the job market is hard and ceaselessly changing. And we live in a world where the margin for misunderstanding and error is paper-thin.

TODAY'S young people are more cynical and less optimistic than their predecessors. They are educationally geared more toward job training and socially more concerned with life's material rewards. While students are optimistic about their personal futures, they are pessimitic about the future of the nation and the world. Consequently, college students are more committed to their personal futures than to the future we face together.

It is a situation that brings to mind an interview with one of the scientists who worked on the Manhattan Project, which led to creation of the atomic bomb. He was asked whether he felt guilty for having helped create the bomb. The scientist said he felt as much guilt as a tin-can maker feels when a can is thrown through a window. His answer is horrifying Looming larger than the question of right or wrong -- whether it is good or bad to make atomic bombs -- was the man's isolation to such a degree that he was unable to see beyond his job to the world outside his laboratory.

As a global society we cannot afford a generation that fails to care or think about such connections. Yet if young people are allowed to be educated solely in a profession or a technology, this is precisely the risk we face. The future demands a generation that is broadly educated, not narrowly focused. It requires people who are ethically rooted, understand human and global connections and have the ability to ask and answer the tough questions ahead. This is what the liberal arts have always sought to teach. And this is the purpose and the urgency of the liberal arts today.
