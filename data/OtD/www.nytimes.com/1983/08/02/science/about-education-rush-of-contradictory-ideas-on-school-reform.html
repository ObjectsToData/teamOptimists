ABOUT EDUCATION; RUSH OF CONTRADICTORY IDEAS ON SCHOOL REFORM

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: August 2, 1983

CRITICISM of the schools and proposals on how to reform them are rushing in from all directions. This outpouring of concern is a welcome sign of public awareness after years of indifference. But the mass of proposals and the contradictory remedies could well neutralize each other. A confused public may grow impatient with all the talk and countertalk, and tune out again. Then, nothing would happen beyond some minor cosmetic readjustments. It may therefore help to lay out some of the options of what is being proposed.

Curriculum: There is virtually universal agreement on the need for early emphasis on the basic skills and on specific academic requirements in the high schools, including English, social studies, mathematics, science and computer science. But there is much disagreement on how to achieve this. The debate is between those who want a more rigid study plan and those who believe that true reform calls for more creative and exciting schools.

The National Commission on Excellence in Education tends to push in the direction of rigid prescriptions; reformers such as Theodore R. Sizer, the former dean of the Graduate School of Education at Harvard, and John I. Goodlad, dean of the Graduate School of Education at the University of California at Los Angeles, also want more emphasis on academic subjects, but they would like the reforms to come from teachers and principals working together with less rather than more standardization of the curriculum. They fear the present reform movement will fail if the politicians persuade the public that all that is needed is a tightening of the screws.

There is much agreement on the need for an earlier start in kindergarten, but agreement ends with the discussion of what should be done with 4- or 5-year-olds. Should they be pushed to master the basic skills earlier, or be treated like children in need of warmth, play, and an opportunity to glide naturally into reading and writing?

There has been an understandable revulsion against past policies of promoting children from grade to grade, regardless of their achievements, until they are ultimately consigned to remedial instruction in high school or even college. Some reformers demand tough promotion standards. They would keep pupils back until they master the required skills. Others agree with Mr. Sizer, who would respond to youngsters deficient in the basics by concentrating exclusively on those skills until they are mastered, postponing all other studies. Still others lean toward the recommendations of the Twentieth Century Fund that, rather than holding pupils back year after year, they should be placed in ''small, individualized programs staffed by certified teachers and run as small-scale academies.''

The science problem: Most of the reform proposals call for more science and mathematics, and more teaching attuned to the demands of a technology-dominated economy. But some reformers worry that this could distort the curriculum and focus too narrowly on immediate needs. They urge instead something the Sloan Foundation has labeled ''the new liberal arts,'' a combination of the humanities, the social sciences and an understanding of science and technology. They warn that the only way to make the schools better is to reform the teaching of all subjects.

Time in school: While there is much agreement that many students have not learned enough by the time they leave school, there is less agreement on the remedy. The commission on excellence and other critics would lengthen the school day and year, in addition to requiring more homework. This is also what the New York State Board of Regents proposed last week. Others feel this would encourage school authorities to fill up the added time with more of the same content. They propose instead a revamping of the school structure and better use of teachers' and students' time. For example, Mr. Sizer wants the curriculum to be less ''frenetic'' and chopped up. He wants larger blocks of time to work with, even if it means reducing the number of subjects. For example, chemistry might have to be sacrificed to make more time for biology and physics.

Teachers: There is agreement that better teachers are essential. But how to get them is the question. There are advocates of merit pay for a few outstanding teachers, more pay for all teachers, or a new career ladder that, for example, would create the position of ''master teacher'' for those who assume new responsibilities, such as the training of teachers, planning the curriculum or presiding over teams of teachers.

There is also the question of whether mathematics and science teachers should get better pay to alleviate the current shortage. The commission on excellence thinks so, calling teaching ''market-sensitive.'' Others warn that this would be divisive and undermine overall reforms.

Policy and financing: There is agreement on the need for better planning and more money. But who should plan and who should pay? The commission on excellence would leave most of it to the states and local school boards. The Twentieth Century Fund wants the Federal Government to assume a more active role, even though it says the schools ''must primarily remain a responsibility of state and local governments.'' It calls for the continued Federal role because the Federal Government must help to meet the needs of poor and minority students and show the way to educational quality. The fund also wants federally financed rewards for master teachers.

Given all these proposals, what are the options? Where are they likely to lead?

There is much popular and political support for ''get tough'' remedies, a return to the old ways of a prescribed curriculum and longer hours. This would probably pay off in better test scores in the short run; but if past experience is any indication, such reforms would be quickly followed by an educational and social counterrevolution. There would be protests against the falling by the wayside of less able youngsters, particularly those from deprived homes.

Related to the ''get-tough'' approach is concentration on immediate problems, producing short-lived reforms like those that followed Sputnik. At the same time, there is little prospect of a reform movement based on a ''new permissiveness.'' Public sentiment is clearly opposed, and recent experiences with such an approach have been dismal.

The compromise to emerge from the welter of proposals could be a determined setting of educational goals, but accompanied by modern (to avoid the loaded term ''progressive'') and humane methods to bring about real change in the way children are taught and schools managed. This could include a new way of selecting and training teachers, with reliance on medical-type internships. Better qualified teachers could play a key part in the search for better education; experience shows that, without teachers in the lead, reforms are doomed.

On one more point there is much agreement: The time for change is right, but time is short. It may not last beyond the 1984 elections.
