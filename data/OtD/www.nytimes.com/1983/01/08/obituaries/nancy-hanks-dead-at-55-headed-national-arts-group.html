NANCY HANKS DEAD AT 55

NANCY HANKS DEAD AT 55; HEADED NATIONAL ARTS GROUP

By WOLFGANG SAXON
Published: January 8, 1983

Nancy Hanks, who headed the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Council on the Arts under the Nixon, Ford and Carter Administrations, died last night of cancer at Presbyterian Hospital. She was 55 years old, and lived in Washington.

Holding the highest arts post in Government, Miss Hanks and her agency distributed Federal funds to a wide variety of institutions and artists. Her lobbying efforts were instrumental in helping the endowment aid music and sculpture, drama and painting, movies and crafts and graphic arts shows.

Miss Hanks traveled across the country to plead the cause of the arts and encourage support from the private sector. In large part through her persistence and persuasive powers, the agency's budget grew from $16 million to nearly $100 million during the two terms she served as its chairman, from 1969 to 1977.

Although not an artist herself, Miss Hanks used her considerable charm to wheedle money out of Congress in behalf of the arts, and was not above a little arm-twisting if need be. Thus, when an American violinist won a prize she would be sure to inform each member of Congress from the winner's home state, and follow it by a reminder around budget time that good artists do not just happen. 'Persuasion as High Art'

When the agency was threatened with budget cuts in 1970, she and her assistant, Michael Straight, buttonholed 150 Senators and Representatives. As a result, about 100 previously negative votes switched sides.

''Miss Hanks has carried the techniques of politial persuasion to a level that is itself high art,'' wrote Harold C. Schonberg, the music critic at The New York Times at the time of her departure from Government. ''She and the National Endowment for the Arts have been involved in the greatest outpouring of cultural money since the days of the Works Progress Administration during the Depression.''

Nancy Hanks was born Dec. 31, 1927, in Miami Beach, the daughter of the late Bryan Cayce Hanks, an investment attorney, who later moved the family to Texas. She studied at the University of Colorado and Oxford University in England before graduating magna cum laude with a master's degree from Duke University in 1949. She also was a member of Phi Beta Kappa.

Miss Hanks's first job in Washington was that of a receptionist at the Office for Defense Mobilization in 1951. Two years later, she joined the staff of the President's Advisory Committee on Government Organization with Milton Eisenhower and Arthur S. Flemming.

The panel was headed by Nelson A. Rockefeller, who chose her as one of his assistants when he was Under Secretary of Health, Education and Welfare. For a time in 1955, she also was a special assistant in the special-projects office of the White House. Worked for Rockefeller Fund

The following year, she went to work on study projects at the Rockefeller Brothers Fund, with the performing arts as one of her areas of responsibility. Working with Mr. Rockefeller and his brothers Laurance S. and John D. 3d, she served as executive secretary of a group that included Henry A. Kissinger and that published ''Prospects for America - The Rockefeller Panel Reports'' on foreign policy, defense, education and social and economic affairs.

Congress created the Arts Endowment and President Lyndon B. Johnson signed it into existence in 1965. When President Richard M. Nixon chose Miss Hanks as its chairman in 1969, she became only the second head of the agency.

Miss Hanks had the reputation of being a populist who logged thousands of miles a year to speak with groups all over the country, and to champion of the smaller centers of culture.

''This country is too big to have the best of everything,'' she once said. ''You've got to have a lot of 'bests.' If we go for just one, nobody is going to stand for it.''

Endowment grants ranged from a few hundred dollars to six figures, and she took great pains to distribute them impartially. As a result, nobody could call her an ''elitist.'' Received Reagan Appointment

Still, she had to tread a narrow line, dealing with various constituencies and keeping on the good side of powerful lawmakers. With great finesse, she maintained her equilibrium, and the huge jump in the arts budget became the monument to her success.

An even-handed administrator, she was willing to accept the avantgarde and, at the same time, marvel at a fine example of Colonial needlepoint. And the money handed out from Washington came to aid some of America's most prestigious cultural groups, as well as some of its humblest.

After she resigned as chairman, she joined the boards of directors of Conoco Inc., the Equitable Life Assurance Society of the United States and Scholastic Inc. Her personal interests moved to the use of urban spaces and the revitalization of cities.

She also was a trustee of Duke University, a regent of the Smithsonian Institution, a director of Independent Sector, vice chairman of the Rockefeller Brothers Fund and a trustee of Jackson Hole Preserve and The Conservation Foundation.

The latest of the many honors she received over the years was bestowed only last month, when the Arts Endowment present her with its first Founders Award for Civic Leadership. In addition, President Reagan appointed her to a newly formed Committee on Arts and Humanities.

Miss Hanks is survived by her mother, Virginia Wooding Hanks of Washington. Also surviving are several cousins, including Van Cliburn, the concert pianist, and the Very Rev. James Parks Morton, Dean of the Cathedral of St. John the Divine.

Plans for a memorial service were incomplete last night.
