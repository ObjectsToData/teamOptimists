RE-IMAGINED IN ENGLISH

By Peter Brooks; Peter Brooks is a professor of French and comparative literature and the director of the Whitney Humanities Center at Yale.
Published: January 23, 1983

THE RANDOM HOUSE BOOK OF TWENTIETH-CENTURY FRENCH POETRY, Edited by Paul Auster. 635 pp. New York: Random House. $25.

THIS bountiful bilingual collection of modern French poetry opens most appropriately with Apollinaire's ''Zone'' and on the page facing it a translation by Samuel Beckett. Apollinaire's poem of 1912, sloughing off the ''ancient world,'' celebrating the airplane and the Eiffel Tower, that anti-artistic monument incarnating a new esthetic of the man-made and the machine, is written in a fragmented lyrical style that reveals an intimacy with the Cubist vision in painting, which Apollinaire had early championed. The poem calls for and illustrates an art self-consciously and dynamically modern. Apollinaire responded decisively to the challenge to poets issued by that great precursor, Rimbaud: ''We must be absolutely modern.'' And in Beckett, a bilingual poet whose translations themselves exemplify cosmopolitan modernism, we have a writer whose own work marks a further advance of the esprit nouveau that Apollinaire announced. The spirit of the new would be fully unleashed in France immediately after Apollinaire's death in 1918 - and the end of World War I - when Tristan Tzara brought Dada to Paris. Subsequently Surrealism took up the torch in the 1920's.

Sixty years later one can still contend that Surrealism was the major ''event'' of 20th-century French poetry, the great revolution in poetic language and consciousness that marked virtually all the poets then writing and gave its inflection to their voices for many years - not only Surrealism's leader, Andre Breton, and his most loyal disciple, Benjamin Peret, but also Robert Desnos and Philippe Soupault, Paul Eluard and Louis Aragon, and the man who holds the best claim to be France's greatest living poet, Rene Char. It is not that most of these poets long gave literal allegiance to the Surrealists' doctrine of ''psychic automatism,'' but Surrealism provided them and others with common ground in the rejection of the past and with a new space for poetic invention. Surrealism also created a broad context of possibility and understanding for those older poets recognized as its predecessors, such as Max Jacob and Pierre Reverdy, and for other poets who were always marginal to the movement but whose work illuminates and is illuminated by it, in particular Henri Michaux and Francis Ponge. The mainstream of French poetry in our century, it seems fair to say, remains resolutely antinaturalist and visionary, intent on the liberation of inner perceptions and the reconstruction of poetic language itself.

In part because of the imprint of Surrealism, French poetry in our time appears to have developed in response to very different imperatives from British and American poetry. It is often difficult and baffling to non-French readers, resistant to our usual ways of finding order and construing meaning in poetry. The reading skills we learned from the New Critics, the tools they gave us for analyzing argumentative structure and imagistic texture, don't seem to take us very far into the trancelike landscapes of Reverdy or the oblique, hermetic reflections of Yves Bonnefoy. In the 20's and again in the 50's, when British and American poets were actively learning the ''tradition,'' as T.S. Eliot taught them to do - rediscovering poets of the 17th century and revaluating the claims of the Romantics - French poets were postulating a distinct and idiosyncratic tradition of the new. They were reaching back to Baudelaire and Gerard de Nerval, exalting Rimbaud, Lautreamont and Mallarme, but excluding almost everyone else. They defined their task as restlessly experimental and modernist, including in this concept both Rimbaud's call to change life itself and Mallarme's supreme games of language in his quest for an ''Orphic explication of the earth.''

An anthology designed to give a fuller historical view to its readers would include these titans, along with the first two major figures in 20th-century poetry, Valery and Claudel, whom many subsequent poets would seek to overthrow or evade. Paul Auster, the editor of ''The Random House Book of Twentieth-Century French Poetry,'' excludes Valery and Claudel, who were born around 1870, as ''belonging to the spirit of an earlier time.'' This may be a wise (or at any rate inevitable) editorial decision if one wants to emphasise the ''new spirit'' and its aftermath, but it does slightly skew this anthology as a representation of 20th-century French poetry.

Making all due allowance for variations and dissents by individual poets, one might say that most Anglo-American poetry has remained intimately linked to the speaking voice, its tones, its movements, with the implication that a listener is being brought into an imagined dialogue. But French poetry, especially since the 50's, has often tended toward a kind of minimal vatic utterance, a discourse bordering on the philosophical. While the colloquial manner of Apollinaire and Blaise Cendrars remains a living model and the high oratorical style of Saint-John Perse an admired example, and there is still a discernibly French tradition of the celebratory and often richly erotic love lyric - a genre renewed by the Surrealists - the verse of such leading contemporary poets as Andre du Bouchet, Philippe Jaccottet, Michel Deguy and Jacques Roubaud appears highly refined, tenuous, enigmatic, and constantly aware of the difficulty of its utterance. It is poetry that often seems on the verge of becoming poetics, a hermetic theory of its own practice. If the language of our own contemporary poetry most often starts from the givens of the world and of speech to move us toward meditation, the French lyric often seems to begin in a separate realm and to make enor-mous demands on the reader who is trying to find its points of reference.
