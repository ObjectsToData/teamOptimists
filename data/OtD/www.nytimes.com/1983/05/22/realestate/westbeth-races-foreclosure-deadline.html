WESTBETH RACES FORECLOSURE DEADLINE

By JOSEPH MALINCONICO
Published: May 22, 1983

COUNTLESS handbills and posters clutter the cork bulletin boards near the mailboxes at Westbeth, the artists' rental housing complex in West Greenwich Village. But amid the colorful cluster, a block of black print on white paper stands out.

It is a letter from Representative Theodore Weiss to Secretary Samuel R. Pierce of the Department of Housing and Urban Development, asking the Federal agency to be more patient and understanding in dealing with the financial problems threatening the complex.

At the bottom of the letter, someone has scribbled, ''Tenants - Stop splitting or we'll all be on the street.'' Not everyone at Westbeth expects to be dispossessed, but uncertainty and disunity continue to mount as June 1 nears. That date is the deadline set by H.U.D. for Westbeth residents to submit a proposal to pay the $2.4 million arrears on the building's mortgage and to establish a workable financial plan for the project.

''Lacking receipt of a viable reinstatement plan by that date, we anticipate proceeding with the foreclosure action,'' Stephen May, assistant secretary of the housing agency, wrote in a letter to Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan in March.

Richard Ochsner, a spokesman for H.U.D., said last week that his agency would sell the building should there be a foreclosure. Westbeth's board of directors. which was formed by the J.M. Kaplan Fund to manage the building when it purchased it for $11.4 million in 1967, mailed its proposal May 13. By late last week, it had not yet received a response.

Tenants, who are protected neither by rent stabilization nor rent control, but rather by rent limits set by the board, are now wondering about their future. ''That's a question no one seems able to answer,'' said Lothar Stiefel, a board member.

Westbeth, which covers the blockfront on West Street between Bank and Bethune Streets, began as an ideal. When it opened in 1970 with a Federal mortgage bearing 3 percent interest, rather than the going rate of about 7 percent, it was the country's first Governmentsubsidized housing development for artists.

The J.M. Kaplan Fund and the National Foundation on the Arts and Humanities -the latter of which provided a $1 million grant - were the principal backers. They wanted to provide housing in the city for artists without subjecting them to the normal financial burdens of living in New York. It was hoped that Westbeth residents could then spend more time on their arts. Reality, however, has constantly tugged at the ideal.

The commercial space in the development, which was expected to supply 50 percent of the income, never produced the anticipated revenue. And rent increases generated only protests and rent strikes, rather than the needed funds. Debts piled up and H.U.D. took over the mortgage in 1975. Now the agency has decided that things have gone far enough.

''It's time they got their act together,'' said Joaquin Loapes, a spokesman for the Federal Department. But getting anything together has been difficult at Westbeth. ''Artists think differently,'' said Hal Miller Jr., an actor who has lived at Westbeth since it opened and a leader of the 1972 rent strike. ''They think individually. They don't think collectively.''

Twice, while standing near his mailbox and offering his views on the situation, Mr. Miller was interrupted by fellow tenants. A woman snapped a few words of disagreement as she breezed toward an elevator; a man nodded with approval, pointed at Mr. Miller and said, ''The man's right.'' Others who talked about the subject also attracted comments.

The people here are not heeding the warning scribbled on the bulletin board. A split continues between those on the Residents Council, who back the proposal of the board of directors to make Westbeth a limited-equity co-op, and the Association of Rental Tenants (known as ART).

The latter group insists that converting Westbeth would not only tarnish its ideal, but would eventually result in the eviction of many of the current tenants.

But since ART is not strongly represented on the Residents Council, it is the cooperative conversion proposal that is now under consideration at H.U.D.

''We're all getting paranoid in this building,'' said Andrea Mihok, secretary of the Residents Council and a tenant representative on the complex's board of directors. ''You never know what the other people are doing. You begin to see bogeymen behind the trees.''

MEMBERS of ART say that only those who would be able to afford a cooperative are in favor of it. Mrs. Mihok discounts that contention, noting out that she and her husband, both actors, are out of work.

A recent study involving 276 of the 384 units showed that 61 percent of the household incomes are $15,000 or less, and that 62 percent of the residents claimed no assets.

Rents at Westbeth range from $230 for an efficiency to $438 for three-bedroom appartments. There are surcharges for those whose incomes exceed certain levels.

The proposal sent to H.U.D. calls for rent increases of 11 1/2, 9 1/2, 8, 5 and 4 percent over the next five years that, along with expected savings in operating costs, would pay the arrearage. The Residents Council estimates that it would cost about $10,000 to buy an average one-bedroom apartment, but there are few other details on making the building a cooperative. So far, the Council says, about 240 tenants have made refundable, nonbinding deposits of $200 toward the proposed co-op.

While the two tenant groups are more upset with H.U.D. than each other, often claiming that their problems stem from apathy on the agency's part, they cannot unite in their response.

The 150 members of ART believe that cooperative conversion would eventually destroy Westbeth because they expect the increased costs to force many people to move out. The Residents Council, however, disagrees.

''Can we fight H.U.D., or can we not?'' Mrs. Mihok asked. ''I don't think we can. I'm a political activist, but not to the point where I'd be willing to risk my home.''

''I think it's time to say no and fight,'' said Lucia Vernarelli of ART. ''I see it as a political thing, not only as a personel thing for artists. The fact that Westbeth would go down the drain and just be another piece of property is a terrible thing to think about.''

''I'd like to play down the idea that this is housing for artists,'' said James Shaw, the president of the board of directors. ''Otherwise, what about housing for plumbers, or housing for streetcleaners?

''We should be less interested in the preservation of housing for artists and more interested in the preservation of housing, period.'' A sculpture stands in the inner courtyard of Westbeth. From a distance, it seems an interesting mass of twisting metal, but after a second glance, its design is ironically obvious. The mass of metal is actually two large cords, solidly and beautifully intertwined.
