ABOUT EDUCATION; NEW HUMANITIES SCHOOL TO SAIL AGAINST THE TIDE

By FRED M. HECHINGER
Published: May 3, 1983

AT a time when all eyes are on science and technology, a small group of independent planners in New York City's public high school headquarters is starting a new High School for the Humanities.

The humanities may look old-fashioned, or even obsolete, in the modern world of video games and laser beams, but the new school expects to chart an unconventional course. It wants to give students old values as an anchor, but at the same time to cut loose from the ways school hours are usually arranged and subjects are taught.

The school, which is scheduled to take in its first group of 9th and 10th graders in September and is to be open to applicants from all over Manhattan, will be housed in the old Charles Evans Hughes High School at 351 West 18th Street which, like so many urban high schools, has been losing students to private schools. The school's planners noted that many successful urban schools are built around a central theme - science, mathematics, music and the other performing arts - that responds to students' interests and creates a sense of common purpose.

''I believe in a multi-ethnic fine liberal arts school,'' said Milton Silver, the project director. ''In a democracy like ours, everybody ought to be entitled to the kind of education that in Europe may be reserved for the elite.''

In an era that considers technological and business education synonymous with a meal ticket, the humanities have largely been crowded out. Despite such bread-and-butter considerations, the advocates of a new school for the humanities believe the American people are looking ''for a reaffirmation of those human values which may have been neglected in our race for technological pre-eminence.'' The plan calls for a school that, without neglecting mathematics and science, would focus on ''the cultural and esthetic qualities of the human experience.''

Such goals appear to meet concerns expressed by the Rockefeller Foundation's Commission on the Humanities. The panel had urged the schools to ''prepare young people for competent and informed participation in community life and for the greatest possible degree of fulfillment in private life,'' warning that none of this was possible without a study of the humanities.

What are the humanities? The new school leans heavily on the definition by the National Endowment for the Humanities, which includes: history, philosophy, languages, linguistics, literature, archeology, jurisprudence, the history and criticism of the arts, ethics, comparative religion, anthropolgy, sociology, political theory and international relations.

The new school's prospectus speaks of using its faculty's joint efforts to deal with communication, literature and social studies, and to integrate them with the study of mathematics and science. It will build into its curriculum the resources of Manhattan as a cultural and business center through independent study, research and internships.

At the heart of the program will be interdisciplinary ''clusters,'' with unifying themes built around a student's area of major interest. For example, a cluster might consist of written communication, the origins of culture in an area such as the Middle East, and the art, music and anthroplogy that relate to it.

Each cluster is to be taught by a team of teachers with special training in both the subject matter and the skill of working as a team. (Teacher training workshops are planned for this summer.)

Although courses in mathematics, science and foreign languages will be taught outside the clusters, they are to be related to them whenever possible.

''The traditional division of the school day into 45-minute class periods would be wholly inappropriate to this program,'' the prospectus says. Instead, varying blocks of time will be allotted flexibly to the clusters so that students may explore some areas in depth.

Equally unacceptable, the planners say, would be the traditional departmental divisions into academic disciplines. Instead, the larger areas - the humanities, mathematics and science, and foreign languages - will each be brought together under the supervision of an assistant principal.

Instead of the traditional home room to which students in a particular grade normally report, the school's basic unit will be the ''family.'' Each family will consist of about 30 students and a teacher; but those small units will be part of an extended family of four teachers and 120 students. The family will be the place for guidance and academic as well as personal decisions. Whenever possible, the family teacher will also be part of one of the students' clusters.

The blueprint considers it basic to the new school's success that students, teaching staff, parents and the community be actively involved in planning and operations.

New York University and the City University of New York have already expressed an interest in working directly with the school, and financing is sought to support cooperation with other institutions of higher learning, such as Columbia, Fordham and the New School for Social Research.

Some of the school's proposed ''options'' are such courses as The Law as Literature, The American Work Experience and Literature, ranging from Mark Twain to Studs Terkel, The Spirit of Revolution, dealing with the relationship between liberty and order, The Rise of Totalitarianism, Issues of War and Peace, and America as a World Power, with readings from such authors as Barbara Tuchman and George F. Kennan.

What about ''practical'' preparation for the job market? The planners of the High School for the Humanities reject the idea that the humanities and making a living are incompatible.

Included in their prospectus are graphic design, ceramics and photography workshops. They will offer career-oriented courses involving computers, film-making, health services, preparation for law and criminology, the publishing trades, and even such oldfashioned skills as typing, home repair and home economics. They believe the study of foreign languages, good writing and public service is by no means useless for future careers.

Predictably, principals of other schools will protest that they are already offering much that the High School for the Humanities plans to do. Perhaps. But many experts insist there is special value in the critical mass - concentration on a central theme - and an organization that supports such a theme. By running counter to present single-minded concern with science and technology, the proposal bears the stamp of courage. It seeks a way of restoring the balance in education and the nation's future.
