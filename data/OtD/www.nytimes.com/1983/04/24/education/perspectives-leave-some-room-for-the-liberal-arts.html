PERSPECTIVES; LEAVE SOME ROOM FOR THE LIBERAL ARTS

By NANCY ZUMWALT; Nancy Zumwalt teaches classics at UMass-Boston.
Published: April 24, 1983

''RENDER therefore unto Caesar the things which are Caesar's; and unto God the things that our God's.'' Jesus's evasive reply to the Pharisees acknowledges the conflicting claims of the secular and spiritual world. The history of the efforts to mediate between the material and the spiritual, moral, intellectual and esthetic - and of giving each fear its just due - has traditionally been told through humanistic or liberal-arts education.

''Liberal arts'' is the translation of what the Roman philosopher Cicero called liberales (or humaniores) artes, ''free'' or ''more human'' arts/ skills. These were the arts proper to a free person - i.e., one who was not a slave - and proper to a human being, an animal who has powers of speech and reason. They included all major branches of learning. The most important aim of liberal-arts education - one seldom questioned until our own time - has been to instill moral values and to develop powers of moral judgment. In classical times this aim was implemented through the reading of literature -Homer, the lyric poets, the dramatists, Vergil. After the advent of Christianity, Judeo-Christian ethics gained governance over the values of classical education.

Today, because of the separation of church and state, Americans have special difficulty with the assumption that education rests on moral foundations, which we confuse with religious dogma. Moreover, we attempt to apply the principles of democratic pluralism to education: knowledge, particularly in the sciences and technologies, is doubling every 10 years. All knowledge is equal. Therefore, the best we can do is to give some kind of fair representation to the competing constituencies of learning. While we admit the legitimacy of programs presenting histories of special groups (blacks, Hispanics, women), we deny the value - even the existence - of traditions all Americans share. America rose from a European matrix of ideas and institutions. These are the major sources of our national identity and the center of our cultural heritage. Yet, given the diversity of our peoples, we cannot afford to return to the Indo-European chauvinism of the past. We make insufficient efforts, however, either to transmit our European heritage or to reflect the cultural diversity of our peoples in our educational programs.

The difficulties of establishing an effective system of universal mass education has brought us to think in terms of minimums and negatives: it is the responsibility of the schools to turn out functional citizens, who are not hindered from being productive through lack of reading, writing, or calculating skills. Since World War II colleges and universities have become heavily dependent on funding from the Federal Government and on business to support research in the physical and social sciences and in technological fields. Effects of this change have filtered down to all levels of education. Not only have those areas of study useful to the government and business been funded at the expense of the humanities, but criteria of excellence and productivity that prevail in these fields have been inappropriately applied. A cost-accounting mentality governs academe. We also suffer from a misconception of the ideal of individualism. We believe in the uniqueness of the individual, we value the political and social rights of the individual, our schools are committed to developing the potential of each individual. But social institutions, especially educational institutions, are by their nature concerned with what is common to all; an individual can define himself or herself only against a community. Overvaluation of individualism gave us the Me and Now generations, and has contributed to the growth of feelings of alienation. Many have cited the negative effects of mass communication, especially television, on our culture. America, proud of the ethnic and cultural diversity of her people, has, through mass communication, created a degree of cultural conformity matched in history perhaps only by medieval Christian culture - without offering a system of values and guidelines for living, as the Church then did. Last, we increasingly ask of our high schools, colleges, and universities that their graduates be qualified wage earners. Education other than technical/ vocational is regarded as a luxury.

Given so many assaults, it is small wonder that liberal-arts education has been severely weakened. Should we come to the aid of this Grand Old Dame of Western Civilization, or is she truly an anachronism? Can we come to her aid, even if we want to? Those who hesitate may object: the ''wisdom of the ages,'' the ''eternal verities,'' and the ''lessons of history'' have not abolished human misery, poverty, racism, sexism, or the injustices of class structure. That is correct. The system of education of which we are speaking developed and flourished in elitist societies. The aristocracy was educated; the majority of people were not. Men were educated; women, if educated, were denied access to most kinds of work. The traditions of Western Civilization are still being adapted to the kind of society we think we want. Studying the liberal arts is essential to the process of re-evaluation. Moreover, the advent of the nuclear age and other 20th-century technological advances have disrupted our sense of continuity. Little learning is required to appreciate the fact that one species has discovered a means of destroying the Earth.

Perhaps we should regard humankind as a somewhat reckless youth, full of his own powers and cleverness, who needs to be restrained and guided by those resources of which our Grand Dame is the keeper. To know history's process is to become patient, to give up the expectation that there are fast and easy answers, to be awed by something other than one's own power.

The major aim of American society today appears to be to achieve world supremacy in economics, business, military power, technology, and science. At home, we are preoccupied with economic growth and bureaucratic efficiency. These aims and concerns are exacting a toll on the quality of our lives. They also violate the ideals on which our nation was founded and the traditions and values that are worthy of our continuing loyalty and dedication.

In our product- and success-oriented society we forget that civilization is a process. It is not always efficient and it does not always progress. Its ''products'' - for those who require products - are spiritual, moral, intellectual, and artistic. To very few of us will it be given to create great products of this kind. But all of us in our own ways can participate in the work of civilization and through it explore our membership in the human community.
