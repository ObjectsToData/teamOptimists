KOCH TO IMPOSE CULTURAL AID LIMIT

By FAY S. JOYCE
Published: July 8, 1983

Mayor Koch said yesterday that he intended to limit the city's financial contributions to cultural institutions to 50 percent of their budgets. The limit was recommended earlier in the day by the Mayor's Advisory Commission for Cultural Affairs and strongly endorsed by Cultural Affairs Commissioner Bess Myerson.

The commission said the so-called cap should be phased in over three years, with the level of city support limited to 60 percent for the fiscal year that begins July 1, 1984, 55 percent for the following year and 50 percent for the fiscal year that begins in July, 1986. A spokesman for the Department of Cultural Affairs said the department would follow that timetable and would make exceptions only in special cases.

''I think implementing that 50 percent cap is a fine recommendation,'' Miss Myerson said. ''I absolutely agree with it. We intend to inform the institutions of that.'' She said eight organizations - museums, theaters, a zoo and a botanical garden located in the outer boroughs - which received more than half of their support from the city government in 1982 should look to business and other sources of funds to make up the difference.

Mayor Koch called the idea ''very reasonable, especially if you phase it in over a three-year period.'' ''It gives them time to absorb the loss,'' he continued, ''and an incentive to solicit contributions from the community. There's nothing like a gap in your budget to concentrate your attention. They better get cracking.'' Likely to Raise Controversy

The Mayor said the Department of Cultural Affairs would provide assistance in raising money. Miss Myerson said: ''The cap will affect the small institutions that might have a problem, but my intention is to work with them. I don't know what will happen if they can't raise the money they need. I don't doubt they'll be able to raise the money.''

She said that she had attended meetings with business groups on Staten Island and in Far Rockaway, Queens, and had urged them to assume responsibility for supporting local cultural institutions. The groups responded enthusiastically, she said.

Mr. Koch added that he hoped the plan would not stir as much controversy as his attempt to collect property taxes from many cultural and nonprofit organizations did. The Mayor was forced to abandon that effort last month.

But the plan to place a cap on the city's contributions to cultural institutions appears likely to raise controversy as well. Martin Segal, chairman of Lincoln Center and a former chairman of the city's Commission for Cultural Affairs, said the proposed policy ''astonishes me.''

''I've heard no public discussion of this,'' Mr. Segal said. ''I can't believe that a group as thoughtful as this commission would pick a figure for worthiness. Certainly having the city cut back is not an encouragement for others to do more.'' Council Approval Not Needed

Randall Bourscheidt, Deputy Commissioner of Cultural Affairs, said the change in policy did not require City Council approval. No plans have been made yet for the savings that would result from the reduction in spending, he said. Mr. Bourscheidt acknowledged that the reduction comes at a time when the state of the economy is making it harder to raise money from private sources and at a time when the Federal Government has cut back its support. He said, however, that the Department of Cultural Affairs had met with representatives of the institutions that will be hit hardest by the change in an effort to help them.

This year the Department of Cultural Affairs, which Miss Myerson heads, will give $46 million to 30 cultural institutions to pay such costs as electricity bills and the salaries of guards and maintenance workers. The city's contribution is expected to make up more than half the budget for the Brooklyn Children's Museum, the Staten Island Zoo, the Staten Island Institute of Arts and Sciences and four institutions in Queens: the Hall of Science, which is being renovated, the Queens Theater in the Park, the Queens Botanical Garden and the small Storefront Museum.

A few major institutions received close to half of their support from the city. These include the Brooklyn Museum, which received 46.7 percent of its support from the city in 1982; the Queens Museum, which received 46 percent of its support from the city in 1982, and the Museum of the City of New York, which received 40.8 percent of its support from the city in 1982.

Although most of the institutions are not as well known as those in Manhattan, several draw substantial crowds. The Queens Botanical Garden received 500,000 visitors in 1982 and the Staten Island Zoo attracted 241,000 to see its 700 animals.

If the city adopted a cap on its contributions, ''It would create a very difficult situation,'' said Lloyd Hezekiah, director of the Brooklyn Children's Museum. The city gave the museum $789,000 in 1982, supplying 65 percent of its money. The museum possesses a large collection of 65,000 objects embracing the arts, sciences and humanities. It already seeks and receives funds from foundations, corporations and other government sources, Mr. Hezekiah said, but doesnot charge admission.

''How can you charge a mother with five or six kids?'' Mr. Hezekiah asked. ''By charging admission you might eliminate a whole segment of the population.''

Roger L. Williams, vice chairman of the Mayor's Advisory Commission for Cultural Affairs, said the new policy ''will hit minorities, yes, but not harder than anybody else.'' Mr. Williams said it would promote self-sufficiency so that the institutions ''can withstand whatever the winds of politics are.''

The Jamaica Arts Center in Queens received slightly more than half its funds from the city's Department of Cultural Affairs in fiscal year 1982. It is the only one of the eight institutions in that category which is expected to receive less than 50 percent of its funds from the city this year. Nevertheless, Steven Tennen, the director, opposes the cap as a dangerous precedent. ''If there's another financial crisis and the percentage gets cut to 30 percent, you're going to have some institutions literally going under,'' he said.

''There's a difference between what happens in Manhattan and what happens in the outer boroughs,'' he added. ''Your fund-raising is different. Most corporations are based in Manhattan and their officers don't live in the outer boroughs.''

John C. Lankenau, chairman of the Mayor's Advisory Commission for Cultural Affairs, said the commission decided on a 50 percent cap because, ''the idea is city funding of these institutions is on a partnership basis.''

He continued, ''It seems to us one should try to make it an equal partnership with other sources like private individuals, corporations, foundations and the state and Federal Government carrying the load.'' He said commission members discussed reducing the city's level of support in future years. ''It's a matter that should be looked at again in three to five years,'' he said.
