THE RESTORATION OF GARDEN AND GROVE AT MONTICELLO

By Joseph Giovannini
Published: July 7, 1983

''No occupation is so delightful to me as the culture of the earth, and no culture comparable to that of the garden. Though an old man, I am but a young gardener.''

- Thomas Jefferson THE best-known image of Monticello is the facade on the back of the Jefferson nickel. But Jefferson - an amateur architect who designed this famous house - was also a gentleman botanist, gardener and landscape architect who at Monticello created experimental gardens and an embellished landscape that have a significance and beauty that rival the building.

While the mansion has gradually been restored since the 1930's, work on the hillside grounds around the house began only in 1976, and has been done in three stages. Restoration of the 12-acre grove northwest of the house was completed three years ago, while the restoration of the vegetable beds, vineyard and orchard to the south is well under way. The northeast orchard has not yet been started.

Done under the auspices of the Thomas Jefferson Memorial Foundation, the approximately $200,000 restoration has been financed with proceeds from admission tickets and gift sales, and archeological work in the slave quarters has been financed with grants totaling $300,000 from the National Endowment for the Humanities. The team effort has involved the historian William Beiswanger, the archeologist William Kelso, the horticulturist Peter Hatch, the landscape architect Rudy Favretti and other specialists.

Because Jefferson left written descriptions but no plans of the grove, the task for the restoration team was to re-create his concept rather than to restore the grove. In ''Jefferson's Monticello,'' to be published this fall, Howard Adams quotes Jefferson about the creation and care of the grove: ''Thin the trees. Cut out stumps and undergrowth. Remove old and other rubbish, except where they may look well. Cover the whole with grass. Intersperse Jessamine, honeysuckle, sweetbrier, and even hardy flowers which may not require attention. Procure a buck-elk, to be, as it were, monarch of the wood.''

Jefferson thought of the grove as an English garden, but because of Virginia's intense sun, he covered what would have been, in England, an open meadow with a high canopy of leafy branches. ''Prune the trees as high as their constitution will allow,'' he said. Today, the carefully planned grove again looks effortless, as though man took no part in its creation.

In the grove, Mr. Hatch has used plant materials mentioned by Jefferson in his letters, garden diary and other papers. Jefferson called for the chinaberry tree - ''now a scorned tree,'' said the horticulturist, who finally found some in Warren City, N.C., ''at the edge of dog pens, hog pens and garbage dumps.'' Jefferson also specified the fine-leafed quivering aspen to contrast with the coarse-leafed catalpa and magnolia - ''a bizarre effort at creating contrasting leaf textures,'' Mr. Hatch said. He estimated that 45 percent of Jefferson's original fruit varieties and 15 percent of his vegetable varieties can still be found in their 18th-century strains.

Jefferson noted in his garden book that seats of stone, each next to a solitary red cedar, should be placed in such groves for appreciating the long views. Jefferson's entire plantation could be seen from the Monticello mountaintop. The plantation was even concentrically organized - an intentionally picturesque composition of farmland and forest that was to be seen and admired from this commanding viewpoint.

Jefferson shaped his plantation esthetically, making it into an ornamental working farm. Like many of his ideas, the concept of the ornamental farm came from Europe, where he had traveled and closely studied the landscapes inspired by the English garden revolution of the 18th century. As yet plans have not been formulated to restore the larger estate.

Taking advantage of the southern exposure for a longer growing season, Jefferson sited his vegetable gardens, vineyards and some of his orchards on the southern slopes of the mountain. At the time, of course, there was nothing new about a kitchen garden. But Jefferson was a botanist, and the garden - with 150 species and varieties of fruits, and 250 of vegetables, including the many-headed cabbage, serpentine cucumber, white eggplant and purple broccoli - was virtually a botanical station. What Jefferson did not plant was not known.

Jefferson tinkered in the garden with slopes, heat banks and retaining walls as ingeniously as he did in the the design of the house, and he pursued his specimens with the diligence of a scientist. While he was interested in cultivating native American plants, including specimens discovered during the Lewis and Clark expedition of 1804-06, he also corresponded widely to procure foreign plants. ''The greatest service which can be rendered any country is to add an useful plant to its culture,'' he wrote. ''Botany I rank with the most valuable sciences.''

In the 1,000-foot-long kitchen garden, archeological excavation has revealed soil stains and differences in soil density, allowing archeologists to reconstruct the patterns of land terracing, gates and paths within the garden. The tourist parking lot that covered part of the site has recently been moved to allow the restoration of the kitchen garden to the period after 1809, when Jefferson left the White House, the time when the estate was most intensively developed. The vegetable garden should be completed within a year. ''It is one of the most accurate garden restorations of its kind in America,'' said Mr. Favretti, the landscape-restoration specialist.

Excavation along Mulberry Row, the lane bordering the garden on which Jefferson's slaves lived and worked, has revealed interesting evidence about black history, Mr. Kelso said. He has deduced from the bones uncovered that the diet of the slaves was primarily pork, prepared in stew-type meals. His evidence indicates that there were more than 80 slaves living in the plantation village near the house - a number substantially larger than recorded.

Restoring a distinguished Southern garden to its early 19th-century state would be only an interesting exercise in horticultural history, but Monticello's restored grounds also exemplify Jefferson's ideal of an agrarian society. Jefferson wrote that cities are ''pestilential to the morals, the health and the liberties of man,'' and with his antiurban and anti-industrial viewpoint, he worked to make his 5,000-acre plantation economically self-sufficient, like an ideal America.

While the plantation may have been a paradigm of his agrarian philosophy, it was also his great love: we feel his enormous curiosity in the variety of his 1,000-foot vegetable garden; we see his impulse to beauty in the foxgloves, peonies, poppies, sweet williams, larkspur and Canterbury bells in the flower gardens he planted near the house - a garden that was restored by the Garden Club of Virginia in the early 1940's.

''I am become the most ardent farmer in the state,'' he said. For Jefferson, Monticello was not only a house, but also a farm, plantation and garden that provided a complete and self-sufficient way of life. ''The motion of my blood no longer keeps time with the tumult of the world,'' he wrote James Madison in 1793. ''It leads me to seek happiness in the lap and love of my family, in the society of my neighbors and my books, in the wholesome occupation of my farms and my affairs, in an interest or affection in every bud that opens.''
