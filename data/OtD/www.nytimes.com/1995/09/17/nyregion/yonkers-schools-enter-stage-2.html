Yonkers Schools Enter Stage 2

By ELSA BRENNER
Published: September 17, 1995

AGAINST a backdrop of threatened teacher strikes and ongoing appeals of a desegregation lawsuit, public schools here in the state's fourth-largest city opened their doors earlier this month, with 22,000 students returning to freshly scrubbed and painted classrooms.

A decade ago, a Federal judge ordered this embattled city to do away with the all-white schools of the east side and the minority classrooms in the poorer sections of town, saying all schools in the district must reflect the racial makeup of the student body, which was 42 percent black and Hispanic.

"It was a fairly smooth transition -- rather quick and rather uneventful," recalled Joseph M. Pastore Jr., the court-appointed monitor overseeing integration of the schools.

But while the so-called numerical desegregation happened within a year and now serves as a model for other cities -- because it relies on magnet schools and not forced busing -- disparities in academic achievement between members of minority groups and nonminority-group members is becoming ever more apparent. Educators cite lower standardized test scores and higher dropout rates among minority group members and say the disparities, while small when students are young, increase as they move into higher grades.

Now the courts, educators and members of the Yonkers branch of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People are focusing on a second stage of the school desegregation plan to correct disparities in achievement, although that is not happening smoothly or without incident.

The local school district is seeking to make the state liable for some of the cost of desegregation; the teachers union is demanding that its two-year contract with a 7 percent pay raise be approved, and the once amicable relationship between the Yonkers Board of Education and the N.A.A.C.P. is growing increasingly sour.

"For us, the school board is like a Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde," said Kenneth Jenkins, president of the Yonkers N.A.A.C.P., one of the plaintiffs in the original desegregation lawsuit in 1980. "They appeared to be our ally in this, but lately the message is that budget concerns have precedence over quality-of-education issues."

The city is also under a Federal Court order to desegregate its housing. But after 10 years, only 200 low-cost units have been built on the city's east side, and another 800 affordable units are yet to be completed.

In contrast to the discord that has marked much of the integration process, another scenario is playing out in the public schools, where 10 years after the judge's ruling, 71 percent of the school-age population is classified as minority-group members.

Educators and students report that black, white, Hispanic and other groups intermingle peaceably in the hallways, the lunchrooms and the courtyards, forming relationships that cross racial boundaries and challenge old taboos. For the most part, students here appear untouched by the rancor that has marked other aspects of the housing and school-desegregation efforts.

The experiences of two junior high school students -- one black and one white -- and the feelings and observations of the girls' parents shed some light on how the desegregation of the schools is progressing on a more personal front. On the east side of the city, Melissa Ann Jackson, a white student, started seventh grade this year at Mark Twain Junior High, a magnet school for 700 students interested in health-related studies, television and the media, the humanities and sports and physical education.

On Sept. 7, the first day of classes, she tied her light brown hair into a ponytail, put on small dangling earrings and wore what she called "a safe outfit" -- denim knee-length shorts, a striped T-shirt and black basketball-style sneakers, with the laces untied. Melissa, who is 11, said she would wait to see what the other kids were wearing before buying new clothes.

On the west side of the city last week, in the section that has a predominantly minority population, Taniya Brandon, 13, who is black, began her second year of junior high school with 1,000 other students at the Museum School. A magnet school on a hill overlooking the Hudson River, it offers programs for the academically gifted, like Taniya, and for those interested in museum studies.

Even though the de rigueur dress code for junior high school girls on both sides of the city includes jeans that are baggy at the bottom, big T-shirts and basketball sneakers with the laces untied, Taniya said that this year she might concede to her mother by occasionally "going girlish" and wearing dresses or skirts. For her first day of the new school year, though, she too chose a safe outfit -- jeans and a T-shirt.

Taniya is growing up just blocks from the low-income housing project where her mother, Symra Brandon, was raised, where poor black and Hispanic residents still live. Mrs. Brandon is now a City Councilwoman, and her husband, Turhan, is a county corrections officer. They have an older child, Turhan Jr., 17, who is a senior at Saunders Trade and Technical School in Yonkers.

Mrs. Brandon, a former president of the local Parent-Teacher Association, said she believed in the public school system and is hoping that it will equip Taniya to get ahead in life.

"We want her to achieve anything she wants, to be able to compete in the world after 2000," Mrs. Brandon said.
