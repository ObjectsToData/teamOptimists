Free Programs And Favorite Agencies Fall In Budget Cuts

By FRANCIS X. CLINES
Published: July 15, 1995

"Woooh, woooh, we do not want to know about it," scolded a derisive Democrat in ghostly tones as the House Republican majority leadership found itself uncomfortably arguing, amid a long day of bare-knuckled budget cutting, against the cost-free use of volunteer bird-watchers in a nature census.

"Something in the sky," the Democrat, Harold L. Volkmer of Missouri, continued his needle, pointing upward in mock alarm as the Republicans glared in discomfort. "No, it is not Superman. It might be a bird. We do not know, we do not want to know. Weird. Weird."

Rebuffing gratis bird-watchers is a minor anomaly of the vast budget-cutting regimen the new Republican majority is hammering through the House these days, as hard-edged budget targets get translated into the rough details of shifted functions, eviscerated agencies and unintended consequences.

The Democrats, still back on their heels after yielding a 40-year run of majority power, actually won a rare floor-amendment victory, defending those bird-watchers as being as American as President George Bush's Thousand Points of Light. And in doing so, they began to craft a motif of complaint for their re-election campaigns next year.

"The bogeyman might get you, rightwingers, watch out," Mr. Volkmer leeringly concluded, castigating the revolutionaries of Speaker Newt Gingrich's fabled first 100 days as the Luddites of today's appropriations fights and tomorrow's Democratic sound bites. "Be careful," the Democrat stage-whispered to them. "Step lightly."

But after yielding on the bird-watchers, the Republicans showed their mettle on the main agenda, setting up a rich spectrum of arts and humanities grants for rapid extinction in two years.

As the cuts are gaveled through, countless agencies and functions are marked for shrinkage and elimination; the Republicans are pushing to live up to their vow to balance the Federal budget soon after the millennium. The program is so wide-ranging that it is difficult to investigate an agency at random and find its budget unscathed. And even when one can be found, as at the Library of Congress, things may not necessarily turn out as they seem.

For the Library, one of the least controversial and most appreciated Federal agencies on Capitol Hill, currently has run afoul of the law of unintended consequences in the tooth-and-claw carpet mart that the appropriations fight has become on the House floor.

The library was initially spared serious cuts. But fleet-footed supporters of an independent agency targeted for wholesale elimination -- the Office of Technology Assessment, which gives scientific expertise to the legislators -- in a last-ditch amendment effort succeeded in tucking it into the Library of Congress. The shift was not accompanied, however, with any budget support, thereby levying a de facto 5 percent, $16.5-million budget cut on the Library's other functions. This life-boat cramming technique is becoming one of the well-used resorts in the floor fights.

"This is a very odd situation," said James H. Billington, the Librarian of Congress, sounding like a man who's realized he just had his pocket picked among jostling well wishers. "To my knowledge, nobody in Congress is deliberately seeking to cripple the Library," he said in response to an inquiry even as he set forth a list of "critical disruptions" that, he now realizes, the little-noted budget switch will mean.

Carefully avoiding warnings of governmental apocalypse that Dr. Billington knows by heart as a Russian studies scholar, he began by gently announcing that the Library -- the powerhouse underlying the cataloguing system of the nation's scholarly and community libraries -- would have to be closed down for the equivalent of a month each year to absorb staff furloughs.

The Librarian said that, barring a budgetary reprieve in the Senate, the institution would severely cut back its signature service -- its cataloguing process used by the nation's local systems. Perhaps a million books, graphics and other items might be rendered inaccessible to researchers across the land, rendered electronically off-line and left as "mysteries" in the stacks, by his estimate.

Dr. Billington is only one of hundreds of agency executives working on triage service plans in the Capitol these days, and his tone is true to his calling, purely informational.

He can go on and fears he will have to, with closed reading rooms and reduced services to such special constituents as the blind and, just across the street, even the lawmakers who use the Congressional Research Service.

"We have to keep reminding people that the Library is very different from all other agencies," Dr. Billington said, making the sort of case that is being heard with increasing frequency as the cuts come down.
