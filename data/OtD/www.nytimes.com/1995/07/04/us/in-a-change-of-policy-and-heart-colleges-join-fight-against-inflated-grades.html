In a Change of Policy, and Heart, Colleges Join Fight Against Inflated Grades

By DAVIDSON GOLDIN
Published: July 4, 1995

A year after Stanford University and Dartmouth College announced changes in their grading policies designed to stem a 30-year trend of grade inflation, a number of other elite colleges say they are considering making high grades the exception rather than the rule.

At Dartmouth, where the average grade increased last year to a B-plus from a B in 1973, transcripts now include not only grades, but also their context -- the class size and class average.

Dartmouth's registrar, Thomas Bickel, said the new grade reporting policy seemed to be encouraging professors to grade more harshly. At the end of the winter semester, grades were down from last year.

Stanford's new policy will bring back the F for the first time in a quarter century, beginning this fall, and will prohibit students from dropping classes late in a semester, a common practice to avoid low grades. Currently at Stanford, 90 percent of all letter grades are A's or B's. The goal is to return the center to C, for average work, and B, for good work.

Ramon Saldivar, Stanford's dean, predicted that Stanford's new grading policies would spark "a slow downward trend that will probably be noticeable in two to three years."

Deans at Cornell University, Columbia University and the University of Pennsylvania said their institutions were likely to follow Dartmouth's lead. The deans said that because getting an A was no longer necessarily indicative of superior achievement, their goal was to provide graduate schools and employers with a way to compare students.

"We feel that providing additional information will allow the readers of transcripts to have a better idea what grades really mean," said Kathryn Youtrakis, the associate dean of Columbia College. "For example, if a student scores a B-minus in one of our chemistry courses if the average grade in the course is a C, knowing that average grade gives you a lot more information about that student."

Even institutions that are not considering drastic changes in either their grading policies or their transcripts, like Harvard and Princeton Universities, said they are reminding professors that high grades should be awarded sparingly.

According to information provided by more than half a dozen leading colleges and universities, grades rose significantly in the late 1960's and early 1970's, leveled off in the mid-70's to mid-80's and began to rise again in the late 1980's. Institutions compiled their grading patterns in different ways, making it difficult to compare individual colleges. The bottom line is that professors award about twice as many A's as they did 30 years ago and less than half as many C's.

At Cornell, the percentage of A's doubled, to 36 percent of all grades in 1994 from 18 percent in 1965, while the number of C's dropped to 12 percent from 40 percent.

At Princeton in that time period, A's rose to 41.2 percent from 16.9 percent, with C's dropping to 9.4 percent from 19.6 percent.

At Harvard, the average grade has risen to an A-minus/B-plus from a B/B-minus in 1965, with more than 40 percent of all grades in the A range.

Institutions also report an increasing disparity in grades between humanities courses and science courses. At Penn, for example, average course grades range from an A-minus/B-plus in small humanities classes to B/B-minus in large natural science classes. That is prompting concern not just about grade inflation, but also about grade fairness.

Kent Peterman, Penn's associate dean for academic affairs, attributed the discrepancy to differences in the ways science and the humanities are taught. He said humanities classes are generally small, allowing students and professors a greater opportunity to develop personal relationships, and grading is based on writing assignments that can be revised or rewritten. Science classes, on the other hand, tend to be larger and generally award grades based on one-time exams.

Even while many institutions are seeking to make sure transcripts include a student's entire academic record, including both the proud and the not-so-proud, Brown University remains intent on making transcripts reflect only achievement. Any grade below a C does not appear on transcripts, and students are permitted to withdraw from classes at any point in the semester. Only 20 percent of a class, however, can graduate with honors.

"When you send in your resume, do you put down all the jobs you applied for that you didn't get?" said Sheila Blumstein, Brown's dean. "A Brown transcript is a record of a student's academic accomplishments."
