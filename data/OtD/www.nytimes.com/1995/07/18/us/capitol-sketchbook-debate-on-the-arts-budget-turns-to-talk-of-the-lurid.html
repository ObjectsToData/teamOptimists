CAPITOL SKETCHBOOK; Debate on the Arts Budget Turns to Talk of the 'Lurid'

By FRANCIS X. CLINES
Published: July 18, 1995

"I have here a brochure," said the Congressman, his eye on wringing still more money from the Federal arts and humanities subsidies proposed for the next Federal budget by the Republican House majority.

"It just goes on and on with things that are just too lurid to talk about, even on a Monday night," said the Representative, Cliff Stearns, Republican of Florida.

He nevertheless kept talking, lingering on the offerings at Highways, a performance arts center in California, that included a comedienne's monologue entitled "Sex with Newt Gingrich's Mother" and an art show, "Ecco Lesbo, Ecco Homo."

Quickly, within the severe time restraints levied on the debate, defenders of the arts were up and angrily asking where in the opposition's denunciations were all the symphonies, all the museums, the many dance centers, children's arts programs and other wholesome beneficiaries of Federal arts aid?

"He has pointed to one grant by the National Endowment for the Arts out of 4,000," said Representative Sidney R. Yates, an Illinois Democrat who knew he was in for a long and losing night of defending the humanities and arts against a minimum cut of 40 percent, the first step toward extinction sought by Mr. Stearns and others.

"Like the swallow that returns annually to Capistrano, the gentleman from Florida has returned to his annual attack on the N.E.A.," Mr. Yates said, facing the reality that this time those supporting cuts for the arts had majority power.

Mr. Stearns held to his single-item critique. "This is sexually explicit homosexual art material," he said. " 'Hot summer nights with hot fags,' " he read, "This has the N.E.A.'s seal of approval."

In a mood of deep frustration, defenders of the arts and humanities tended more in their remarks to Melpomene, the Muse of tragedy, rather than Thalia, Muse of comedy.

"What's at stake is the soul of America," said Representative Jerrold Nadler, Democrat of Manhattan. "To argue that we must sell our soul to pay our bills is irresponsible."

In any idle moment, criticism of avant-garde and aberrant deeds perpetrated in the name of art is an easy target for denunciation and lampooning in Capitol debate. With the Presidential election cycle under way and conservative Republican majorities in both houses, this year the target floats big as a barrage balloon above the debating chambers as legislators look for easy, popular ways to cut the Federal deficit.

For some Republicans, it was a matter of closing in for a long-sought kill on "the arrogance of cultural bureaucrats," as Representative Steve Chabot of Ohio put it.

Mr. Chabot still seemed to be smarting as he told of an Ohio official of the National Endowment on the Humanities who had told him, "If there were no N.E.H., the public intellectual life of Ohio would shrink considerably."

The arts and humanities fight was over $145 million in cuts, less than 10 percent of the overall reduction proposed on the Department of Interior. But the arts and humanities fight was a particular rallying cry for Democrats sensing the change in political philosophy under way in the appropriations debate.

"Don't kill it tonight -- it is vital to this country," pleaded Representative Pat Williams, Democrat of Montana. Responding to Mr. Stearns's contention that the aid was a waste because it could not be justified on the same level as military defense against invasion, Mr. Williams said, "It defends us against invasion by misunderstanding, against the invasion of ignorance."

While the Democratic opposition knew going into the chamber that it was on the losing side, some could not contain their anger.

"Mr. Stearns, your children don't have to count on a public school education," said Representative Louise M. Slaughter of New York, referring to the fact that Mr. Stearns is a millionaire. "It's not your children who are going to be hurt."

Ignoring the gaveled demands that she was exceeding her apportioned time, Ms. Slaughter narrowed her gaze at Mr. Stearns as he held the Highways brochure. "I'm talking about children who create; they do not destroy," she said. "If you ever said to the Pentagon, 'You make a mistake and we won't give you any more money,' I'd like to be there."
