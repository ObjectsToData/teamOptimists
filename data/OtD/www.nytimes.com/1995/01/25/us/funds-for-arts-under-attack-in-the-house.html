Funds for Arts Under Attack In the House

By ROBERT PEAR,
Published: January 25, 1995

Conservative critics of the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities found a receptive audience in Congress today when they attacked the two Federal agencies as intellectually corrupt and biased against what one called "mainstream American values."

But the actor Charlton Heston, who has supported many conservative causes and candidates, pleaded with Congress to spare the agencies. "Art is the bread of the soul," he said, and he went on to quote Prospero's final lines in Shakespeare's "Tempest": "As you from crimes would pardon'd be, let your indulgence set me free."

Mr. Heston said the endowments made the nation's cultural heritage accessible to ordinary Americans.

Mr. Heston made his appeal at a hearing of the House Appropriations Subcommittee on the Interior Department and Related Agencies. Representative Robert L. Livingston, a Louisiana Republican who is chairman of the full Appropriations Committee, said that "a litany of unwise expenditures" by the agencies had raised serious questions about whether they ought to continue to exist.

"I'm not prepared today to take a position on the fate and future of the endowments," Mr. Livingston said. But he added that in a year of extremely tight budgets, members of Congress must ask: "Can we afford the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities? Do we want to afford them?"

William J. Bennett, a former Secretary of Education and onetime chairman of the humanities agency; Lynne V. Cheney, who also once headed the humanities agency, and Edwin J. Delattre, dean of the School of Education at Boston University, urged Congress to withdraw all Federal money from the endowments. The arts endowment gets $167.4 million this year, while the humanities agency gets $177.5 million.

The agencies account for a tiny share of the Federal budget, but critics say they typify the kind of government activity that could be left entirely to private sponsors. The agencies have made tens of thousands of grants since they were created in 1965. A few dozen projects have been criticized as pornographic, blasphemous or biased against the values of Western civilization.

Mrs. Cheney said the agencies subsidized many works that made political statements "rather than revealing truth or revealing beauty."

Mr. Bennett said the Government had no business putting "its official stamp of approval, its imprimatur, upon any particular work of art or scholarship."

He also said the two agencies corrupted the arts and the humanities, causing them to "deteriorate or decline from accepted standards." In seeking Federal grants, he asserted, artists and educators pay "slavish obeisance" to the Government.

A member of the committee, Representative Charles H. Taylor, Republican of North Carolina, said, "We may be liberating the arts by taking away money from some of the corrupting influences."

Another committee member, Representative Barbara F. Vucanovich, a Republican from Nevada, said she tended to agree with Mr. Bennett. She maintained that the arts endowment supported some good projects, but also some works that were "corrupt, immoral and very offensive to people in my district."

Mr. Bennett said that the endowments subsidized people who seemed less interested in creating art or fostering knowledge than in "ridiculing and antagonizing mainstream American values."

Today's hearing was held in a small room hung with posters of paintings by Matisse, Gauguin and Toulouse-Lautrec. By and large, the conservatives said, such artists did not require government money to produce their masterpieces.

But Representative Sidney R. Yates, an Illinois Democrat who is a longtime supporter of the endowments, said the Impressionists did not adhere to the artistic standards that prevailed when they arrived on the scene."

Mr. Heston, who has made several television commercials opposing gun controls, opened his testimony with words that made clear his political sympathies. "May I congratulate you on the revolution," he told the Republicans, referring to their success at the polls last fall. "I'm proud to have been a foot soldier."

"Both endowments have made mistakes, often grievous mistakes," he testified. "Some grants, perhaps a few, have been simply indefensible. This does not lead, in my view, to the conclusion that the endowments should be de-funded. I join many other conservatives in supporting their continuation."

At the end of World War II, Mr. Heston said, theater existed only in 40 square blocks of Manhattan. Now, he said, it flourishes across the country, and "regional theater is one of the spectacular successes" of the arts endowment.

Mr. Heston said he understood that "the endowments will have to share in budget cuts, serious budget cuts," with other Federal agencies. But in an interview, he said, "There is room to do good stuff with a lot less money."

Mr. Yates noted that both Mr. Bennett and Mrs. Cheney had testified in support of the humanities endowment when they headed the agency under Presidents Ronald Reagan and George Bush.

The Founding Fathers would have supported "a modest endowment" for the humanities because they saw learning and liberty as dependent on each other, Mr. Bennett told Congress in 1983. But in his testimony today, he said the agency was too corrupt to be salvaged.
