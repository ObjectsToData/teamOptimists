ARCHITECTURE VIEW; Of Renaissance Visions And American Dreams

By Herbert Muschamp
Published: January 8, 1995

"ITALIAN RENAISSANCE Architecture," at the National Gallery in Washington, is a pale shadow of the sumptuous exhibition mounted last summer at the Palazzo Grassi in Venice. Still, even this severely truncated version is worth a visit, especially when seen in conjunction with "World War II and the American Dream," the National Building Museum's outstanding survey of military construction.

Though the shows explore vastly different periods, styles and building types, they tell similar stories. Both are studies in architectural subterfuge. They show how buildings have helped to disguise as well as drive major shifts in cultural history.

Beware architects bearing models: that's the unstated moral of the Renaissance show (through March 19). On the cover of the catalogue, a painting depicts Michelangelo presenting a scale model of St. Peter's to Pope Paul IV. The presentation is a tribute to God, and to the supreme power of the church. But it is also an act through which the architect enlarges his own cultural power.

While scarcely social equals, the artist and the Pope are in the same picture. Here, in the words of Arnold Hauser, a sociologist of art, we see "the ascent of the artist from the level of the petit bourgeois artisan to that of the free intellectual worker." In time, that worker will exercise ever greater cultural influence. If this period is called the Renaissance, that is partly because history has defined it as a time of intellectual flowering, and partly because that flowering affected the study of history itself.

As in Venice, the stars of the show are the wooden models that enabled architects and their patrons to study designs in three dimensions, though in Washington these have been pared down to 14 models of three buildings: the cathedrals of Florence and Pavia and the basilica of St. Peter's in Rome.

Many of the models are the size of armoires, but two, of St. Peter's and the Cathedral of Pavia, are virtually buildings in their own right. The largest of these, representing an unexecuted design for St. Peter's by Antonio da Sengallo the Younger, is 15 feet tall, weighs six tons, took seven years to build and is perhaps best understood as a Renaissance version of the Trojan horse. From it will emerge -- metaphorically, at least -- the arts, the humanities and the freedom of thought that will ultimately displace ecclesiastical authority in cultural affairs.

At the Palazzo Grassi, they unpacked the whole horse. That exhibition displayed the entire arsenal of tools with which Italian architects attained cultural prominence. There were rooms devoted to architectural theory, the study of antiquity, the development of such techniques as drawing, model making and construction, the figure of the architect, and the ideal city. The models were firmly planted in the history of ideas.

In Washington, where the show was organized by Henry A. Millon, of the National Gallery, this context is reduced to a skeleton crew of about 50 drawings. Handsomely installed in and around the National Gallery's West Garden Court, the selection provides enough detail to convey the reverence with which architects and architecture came to be held in the 15th and 16th centuries.

Brunelleschi's design for the dome in Florence, for instance, aroused as much popular passion as a modern space program, which, in a sense, it was. But one must consult the catalogue (edited by Mr. Millon and Vittorio Magnano Lampugnani, who was co-curator in Venice) to realize that something more than architecture was at stake. Even as Renaissance architects looked back to the ancient world for inspiration, what they were actually modeling was modernity itself.

Modernity of a more familiar stripe is on view at the National Building Museum show, which runs through this year. Here are kitchen appliances, molded plywood Eames chairs, mobile homes, Levittown and, toward the end of the show, a panorama of postwar affluence that unfolds like the vista of a lost Golden Age. But those who recall the 1950's as a time of national innocence may find this show unnerving.

The world of Ozzie and Harriet was prosperous but hardly innocent. The house, the garden, the car and all those labor-saving appliances were wrapped like camouflage around an architecture of war. President Dwight D. Eisenhower warned his contemporaries about the military-industrial complex. This show uncovers the workings of a military-"Joy of Cooking" complex. It's about how, in becoming a military power, the United States lifted itself out of the Depression and propelled itself down the road toward postwar plenty.

Organized by Donald Albrecht, an architect and independent curator, and presented in a superb installation designed by the architect and critic Michael Sorkin and J. Abbott Miller, a graphic designer, the show pulls into coherent shape a wide range of artifacts: photo murals of bomb factories, prototypes for new consumer products, clips from wartime propaganda films, Hollywood pinups, a full-scale Quonset hut and other materials that illustrate the rise of a superpower.
