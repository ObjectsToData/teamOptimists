On the Brink of a Movement

By Charles B. Dew;
Published: March 19, 1995

SPEAK NOW AGAINST THE DAY The Generation Before the Civil Rights Movement in the South. By John Egerton. Illustrated. 704 pp. New York: Alfred A. Knopf. $35.

MAKE room on your library shelf -- the one that holds books like W. J. Cash's "Mind of the South," Lillian Smith's "Killers of the Dream" and Taylor Branch's "Parting the Waters" -- for John Egerton's magnificent "Speak Now Against the Day." His book is a stunning achievement: a sprawling, engrossing, deeply moving account of those Southerners, black and white, who raised their voices to challenge the South's racial mores during the years from 1932 to 1954 when the brackish currents of Jim Crow were running at flood tide.

These critics had their limitations and flaws, Mr. Egerton readily admits, but he is nonetheless convinced that "homage must be paid" to the men and women who were willing to cut against the grain of their native region. By the end of his sweeping narrative, we share Mr. Egerton's conviction, and we are grateful to the author for breathing life into the generation that brought the South to the brink of the civil rights movement.

Mr. Egerton, whose previous books include "Southern Flood" and "The Americanization of Dixie," begins with the onset of the New Deal. The South was a "feudal land," he writes, ruled by a Democratic political oligarchy that drew its energy from "cotton, conservative Protestantism, laissez-faire capitalism and white supremacy." The New Deal rattled the foundations of this entrenched political leadership and brought on real debate over the South's future.

A variety of progressive Southerners found their voices during the 1930's -- writers and journalists, scholars and thinkers, teachers and preachers, labor leaders and a handful of liberal politicians -- and began calling for an honest confrontation with the South's problems. Newspaper editors were often in the progressive vanguard. Mr. Egerton seems to have a special affection for journalists like Hodding Carter Jr. of The Delta Democrat-Times (Greenville, Miss.) and Ralph McGill of The Atlanta Constitution, who supported Franklin D. Roosevelt, condemned lynch law, attacked the poll tax and skewered the Ku Klux Klan. These and others like them (Frank Porter Graham of the University of North Carolina, Myles Horton of the Highlander Folk School in Appalachia, Lucy Randolph Mason of the Congress of Industrial Organizations, James Weldon Johnson of Fisk University, Aubrey Williams of the Southern Conference on Human Welfare) scattered "the tiny seeds of American idealism -- democracy, freedom, justice, opportunity" across the Southern landscape during the 1930's.

In the 1940's, it looked as though these seeds might germinate and grow, Mr. Egerton believes. "It was World War II, more than the Great Depression or the New Deal, that ushered in the modern age," he writes. "The war turned hard times into hopeful times, it moved people up and out, it changed our ways of thinking and working and living." He argues that a huge "silent majority . . . about 75 or 80 percent of the South's population" was waiting for constructive political leadership during and especially after the war. "Looking back on those years from a vantage point in the 1990's," he writes, "it seems to me that a combination of favorable circumstances had opened a narrow window of opportunity through which the South might have reached both internal social reform and external parity with the rest of the nation."

It is at this point that I must reluctantly part company with Mr. Egerton's optimistic analysis. How could reform begin in the region when, as the author admits, "segregation reigned supreme" and "almost anything could be done in the name of white supremacy"? As Mr. Egerton notes, not one Southern political leader spoke out against segregation during the postwar era, and not one newspaper editor called for an end to Jim Crow. Racial violence swept across the South in 1946, and the onset of the cold war plunged the region deeper into the swamp of segregation. "In the South, anti-Communism raced through the culture like an electric current," Mr. Egerton correctly observes, and integration, as one anti-Communist is quoted as saying, was "the Southern version of Communism."

In the end, the voices of those good men and women who called for a better day for all Southerners proved inadequate to the task. "When all was said and done," the author notes sadly, "white Southerners were more willing to stand against racial justice than for it."

THE job of destroying Jim Crow would fall to the Federal court system. It was finally there that judges (many of them native Southerners), the able lawyers of the N.A.A.C.P. and incredibly brave black parents and children from places like Clarendon County, S.C., and Prince Edward County, Va., would come together to bring about an end to the sham that was "separate but equal" education in the South. John H. McCray, the editor of The Lighthouse and Informer, the leading black weekly in South Carolina, summed up the situation neatly and accurately. "The political bosses of this state have never acted decently until and unless they had the club of a policeman over their head," he wrote in 1951. "And the only policeman they fear is the Federal courts."

"Speak Now Against the Day" is an eloquent and passionate book, and it also is an important one. John Egerton has reminded us, at a moment when our collective memory needs jogging, just how deep the roots of institutional racism ran in the South and how difficult it was to pull those roots out of Southern soil. The story he has told -- of conviction and indecision, of courage and cowardice, of tragedy and eventual triumph -- is one we cannot afford to forget.
