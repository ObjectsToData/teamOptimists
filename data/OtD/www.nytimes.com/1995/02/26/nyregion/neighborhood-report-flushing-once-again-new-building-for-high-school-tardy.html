NEIGHBORHOOD REPORT: FLUSHING; Once Again, a New Building For a High School Is Tardy

By DAVID M. HERSZENHORN
Published: February 26, 1995

On Thursday, a week after the opening of Townsend Harris High School's new building was postponed for the fourth time since September, construction officials met in the "lateness office," where tardy students will, eventually, be sent.

"It was just coincidence," said the spokesman for the School Construction Authority, Albert A. Gallardo.

Ever since Townsend Harris reopened a little more than a decade ago in a cramped rented yeshiva on Parsons Boulevard, the school has been promised a new home. But delays, caused by everything from site disputes to budget cuts to behind-schedule contractors, gave the lie to the promises.

In January, it looked as if the wait was over. The Buildings Department issued a temporary occupancy permit for the new five-story building at Melbourne Avenue and 149th Street, on the southwest corner of the Queens College campus.

Boxes of books and supplies were packed up, tagged and stacked in the old building's hallways. But once again, the move was not to be. On Feb. 16, after the principal, Malcolm Largmann, and other school officials toured the new building and found debris in the hallways and furniture unassembled, they postponed the move until the end of April.

"It's just really depressing after a while," said Harry Rattien, the assistant principal for mathematics. "Eventually it will come to pass and I will be elated, but it has been a long time."

The new school can accommodate more than 1,000 students in 19 general classrooms, 6 computer labs, 8 science rooms, a multimedia library center and a film laboratory. A 500-seat auditorium will not be finished until months after students move in.

"The school was devastated" over the latest delay, said Seth Cohen, 17, a senior who is student union president and editor of The Classic, the school newspaper.

"We had planned so many events," he said. "The announcement was made and it just halted everything." The latest edition of The Classic, with a banner headline, "We're Moving Out," had already been shipped to the printer. Mr. Cohen got the paper back and changed the headline to, "We're Moving Out, But When?"

The last time the school packed up all its belongings was in 1942, when it was closed because of budget cuts by Mayor Fiorello H. La Guardia. At that time it was on what was City College's downtown campus, at 23d Street and Lexington Avenue, now the site of Baruch College.

Named for the first United States Ambassador to Japan, Townsend Harris High School was then New York's premier public school for boys. Among its graduates were the scientist Jonas Salk and the author Herman Wouk.

The school, which offers an intensive humanities program, reopened in the yeshiva in 1984. It has the highest graduation and retention rates in the city and ranks among top public high schools like Stuyvesant and Bronx Science.

Townsend Harris and Stuyvesant were rivals in another sense. Who would be the first to get a new building? Stuyvesant won, moving from the Lower East Side to Battery Park City in 1992. Claire Shulman, Queens Borough President, saw the victory as one more example of Manhattan-centrism. "Some people are more equal than others," she said. "That's been a problem when it's Queens versus Manhattan or Brooklyn versus Manhattan." DAVID M. HERSZENHORN
