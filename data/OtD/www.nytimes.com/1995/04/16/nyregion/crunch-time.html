Crunch Time

By CLIFFORD J. LEVY
Published: April 16, 1995

SOUTH RIVER is a workaday middle-class suburb in Middlesex County, but its schools have the feel of urban relics. Learning muddles along in buildings that have stood for many decades and rarely been repaired. It is hard to rouse young minds when rainwater seeps into the classrooms, when textbooks fall apart, when the surroundings gnaw at the spirit.

Every year, local educators plead for more money, and almost every year, residents fuming over rising property taxes reject the district's school budget. Cuts are made; decay marches on.

A few years ago, a large crack split open the wall of an elementary school gym that was also used as a lunchroom and auditorium. There was no money to fix it. The gym was closed after inspectors declared it unsafe, so now students eat at their desks and play dodgeball outside. When it is cold, they are confined to a patch of basement for exercise. In South River, it sometimes seems as if the schools, like the wall of the gym, are giving way, buckling under the growing strains in the way New Jersey pays for public education.

All over the state -- in middle-class districts like South River but also in places rich and poor -- schools and schoolchildren are being caught in the crunch, between the policies and politics of Trenton and a persistent public anger over property taxes. When New Jersey holds annual school-budget elections on Tuesday, the results are likely to provide some important clues to the future of a system that appears to be stumbling toward a crossroads.

At the center of all this turbulence is what is surely the state's knottiest, longest-running public policy debate: how to insure that children in poor areas get an adequate education. It is a debate that has helped to define New Jersey in recent decades, giving rise to political movements, civil rights campaigns and landmark court cases. And it has turned into a crucial arena for Governor Whitman, who is hoping to avoid the brawls over state aid -- nearly one-third of the $16 billion state budget -- that have helped drag down countless politicians in New Jersey, including her predecessor, Jim Florio. If she falters, as he did, her growing national reputation could be damaged.

In the latest of those landmark court cases, the State Supreme Court ruled that the Legislature must pass a law guaranteeing that, by September 1996, poor districts have as much money to spend as wealthy ones.

A year and a half before that deadline, the Legislature and the Governor are still considering how to respond: by overhauling the system -- perhaps even using a different mix of taxes to pay for it -- or just tinkering. But they will look to this week's elections for a sense of how the public is coping with a financial squeeze that has become a fact of life in just about every district.

"This generates a dialogue that is extremely useful," said the Education Commissioner, Leo F. Klagholz. "We are getting information that we are going to use in shaping the process in the longer run."

At its simplest, the issue is how to pay for the state's public schools, which cost nearly $12 billion last year. But it also takes in a range of other matters that can seem maddeningly broad, from taxation and local control to equality and race relations to standards and failings to fundamental decisions about what government can and should do.

This volatile mix is further stirred by a system of school districts, more than 600 in all, that makes reaching consensus difficult because there are so many constituencies. "The funding of schools is the eternal question in New Jersey," said Lynne Strickland, director of the Garden State Coalition of Schools, a lobbying group of 110 prosperous, largely suburban districts. "The personality of New Jersey is such that home rule is very strong here. We have a hornet's nest because we have more people buzzing. And that is a mixed blessing."

The Governor has already begun to cut aid to wealthy districts -- partly because of the court rulings, partly to fulfill her pledge to slash state income taxes by 30 percent -- and that has antagonized some towns that are the bedrock of her support. Residents accuse her of "leveling down" more prosperous districts to raise up poor ones.

Her administration has also begun pushing school districts to clamp down on costs, pointing out that New Jersey spends more per student than any other state. It has issued reports questioning the fiscal prudence of local districts, and in February it caused an uproar by announcing that it would trim aid to districts (most of them affluent) that it said had allocated too much for administrative costs.

The crackdown on spending is linked to the school elections. When budgets are rejected, they go to the municipal councils, which usually make cuts in them. Local educators can appeal to Trenton, which in the past has tended to restore many reductions. But last year, Mr. Klagholz began turning down more appeals. This year, "we will be just as vigorous," he said, adding: "I look at this as overturning the democratic process on taxes, and I consider it a really serious responsibility."

With districts under so much pressure, residents in some affluent areas are being forced for the first time to re-examine the notion that high-quality schools are sacred.
