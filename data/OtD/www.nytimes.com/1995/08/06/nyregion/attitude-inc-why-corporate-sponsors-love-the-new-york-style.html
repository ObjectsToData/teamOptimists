Attitude Inc.: Why Corporate Sponsors Love the New York Style

By JESSE McKINLEY
Published: August 6, 1995

RAWLINSON RIVERA used to pitch. A lanky youth who grew early, he was throwing 85 miles an hour and being scouted by the Yankees before his 17th birthday. Then he started to do in-line skating.

Now, he pitches in a different way. As one of the most talented aggressive skaters in New York, he has been recruited by five separate companies to skate with their equipment, compete with their teams and talk up their products. In return, they give him a little money, lots of clothing and free trips to competitions and exhibitions. His summer job is to skate and talk.

"Kids look up to me," said Rawlinson, 17, who lives in Harlem and attends the High School of the Humanities. The day before, he had gone to Mullaly Park and handed out a box of 50 Hyper wheels. "They'll wear what I want them to wear."

This must be good news to Allan Nelick. As director of promotions for Hyper Wheels, a company based in Huntington Beach, Calif., that makes wheels for skates, it is Mr. Nelick's job to scout and sign skaters like Rawlinson to sponsorship deals. He said he comes to New York three or four times a year looking for skaters with an "in your face" attitude.

"New York skaters are kind of strong and overbearing, but that's the beauty of it," Mr. Nelick said. "If you can throw in a little song and dance, everybody pays more attention."

New York skaters also have an ethnic diversity and a "concrete jungle" feel that appeals to the biggest users of in-line skates: teen-agers. Companies go to great lengths to establish a link between the corporate identity and the teen-age market. (Rollerblade, for example, has had its aggressive team doing shows on the rock band Soul Asylum's tour.) Mr. Nelick said this means getting youths with "interesting personalities" who tap into the "subculture" of aggressive skaters.

"They don't necessarily have to be articulate, but they need to be representative of the sport as life style," he said.

Selling that life style is key, Mr. Nelick said, and he comes to New York not only to find skaters but also to look out for trends in performance styles and fashions. "What they're doing here is what they'll be doing in Omaha in a year," he said.

Hyper Wheels keeps about 35 skaters around the country on sponsorship deals, and about 10 of those get cash retainers to perform and promote for Hyper. Mr. Nelick says his company will add another 30 to 40 skaters nationwide in 1996.

For Rawlinson, Hyper's sponsorship means a $200-a-month retainer, with a bigger deal in the works. He also does exhibitions once or twice a month, earning up to $400 for each.

"You can make a lot of money," he said, sporting a Hyper T-shirt, a Pleasure Tools hat, Roces skates and 9-7-6 shorts, all provided by his sponsors. "And my wardrobe is pretty big."
