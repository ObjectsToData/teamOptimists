PERIPHERALS; Of Digital History

By L. R. SHANNON
Published: August 29, 1995

COMPUTERS were going to eliminate paper, and probably books, too. Yet anyone who works in an office knows that a job is not complete until it has been printed out several times, and anyone who walks into a bookstore knows that books remain healthy, especially computer books.

"Out of Their Minds: The Lives and Discoveries of 15 Great Computer Scientists," by Dennis Shasha and Cathy Lazere, is a fascinating collection of profiles and interviews with some of the men, and these are all men, who brought us here: John Backus, John McCarthy, Alan C. Kay, Edsger W. Dijkstra, Michael O. Rabin, Donald E. Knuth, Robert E. Tarjan, Leslie Lamport, Stephen Cook and Leonid Levin, Frederick P. Brooks Jr., Burton J. Smith, W. Daniel Hillis, Edward A. Feigenbaum and Douglas B. Lenat. ( Mr. Cook and Mr. Levin are grouped together.)

"In most sciences," the book points out, "the seminal thinkers lived in the remote past." But computer science in its modern form goes back only to the 1930's, and most of the pioneers are still alive. It is as if we could speak with Euclid, Archimedes and Galileo.

"Out of Their Minds" bears a new imprint, Copernicus, of the German-based publishing house Springer-Verlag. The first eight titles, books on science and mathematics ranging in price from $13 to $29, should be available in a week or two. The number for information is (800) SPRINGER, or (800) 777-4643. Lives of a Processor

The 8088, the Intel chip that powered the first I.B.M. Personal Computer, had 29,000 transistors and could carry out 330,000 instructions a second. That sounds like a lot, and it was a lot 15 years ago. The P6, the code name for the microprocessor that will succeed Intel's Pentium, will have close to 6 million transistors and carry out 300 million instructions a second.

Most of us think of chips as parts of computers, and the 8088 and the Motorola 68000, the first Macintosh central processing unit, are both dead to us. Michael S. Malone, in his book "The Microprocessor: A Biography," reminds us we are surrounded by them. The earliest microprocessors are alive and well running microwave ovens and V.C.R.'s, car transmissions and cellular phones.

"The Microprocessor" is a readable history of the primary invention of our time, covering the period from 1970 to this moment and looking ahead to the early 2000's. Sections on how chips are actually made, in rooms cleaner than anything else on earth, are surprisingly accessible. The book is $29.95 and is published by Telos, also part of Springer-Verlag. File Under 'Whimsy'

As you should very well know, you should never answer E-mail from a stranger named "Bambi." If you don't know, pick up a copy of the whimsical "I Lost My Baby, My Pickup, and My Guitar on the Information Highway," by Judy Heim (No Starch Press; $9.95). If you can discover the date your computer was built, you can check its horoscope here. My computer, like Einstein and me, is a Pisces, and is "prone to daydreaming about the shape of the universe." The number for No Starch Press is (415) 334-7200; for orders, it is (800) 420-7240.

Speaking of the Information Highway, one wonders whether the Internet will be the hula hoop or the Japanese car of the 90's. "Cultural Treasures of the Internet," by Michael Clark (Prentice Hall PTR; $22.95), is a guide to hundreds of sites related to the arts and humanities -- the University of South Carolina Press, Rodger Friedman Antiquarian Books and Project Gutenberg, to pick three at random. There are also nontechnical explanations for readers who may be more comfortable with Yeats and El Greco than with the search tools Archie, Veronica and Jughead. The numbers for Prentice Hall PTR are (800) 947-7700 and (515) 284-6751.

The "World Wide Web Directory," by Kris Jamsa and Ken Cope (Jamsa Press; $39.95) is a big book that displays the home pages of 4,000 Web sites, the keywords and addresses for 4,000 more, and, on the accompanying CD-ROM, more than 8,000 home pages as they appear on the Web, plus Internet software. If you click on a home page, you can see the HTML programming code used to develop it. The number for Jamsa Press is (800) 432-4112.
