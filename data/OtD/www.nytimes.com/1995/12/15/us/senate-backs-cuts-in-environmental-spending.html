Senate Backs Cuts in Environmental Spending

By JOHN H. CUSHMAN Jr.
Published: December 15, 1995

The Senate today approved two spending bills that cut conservation and environmental programs deeply, but the White House remained adamant that both would be vetoed.

The measures, both of which have already passed the House, were approved by the Senate after relatively brief debate. In both the House and Senate, each measure was approved with less than the two-thirds majority needed to override a veto.

One bill contains $12 billion for the Interior Department and other agencies, and deals mainly with how natural resources are used on public lands. The other provides $80 billion for housing, veterans, environmental and space programs, making especially deep cuts in Environmental Protection Agency programs, particularly in its spending on enforcing environmental law. The agency would be allowed to spend 14 percent less in the current fiscal year than in the fiscal year 1995, which ended Sept. 30.

The Interior Department bill passed 58 to 40. The second measure was approved 54 to 44.

In both cases, the differences between the Republican-controlled Congress and the Administration go well beyond the amount of money to be spent, touching on how the Government regulates pollution, protects endangered species, and the like.

As they drafted the bills, Republicans in both chambers attached many policy provisions, known as "riders," to the bills, raising the political stakes at a time when the Administration was seeking increasingly to make environmental issues a defining difference between the parties.

In Congressional negotiations over the bills, some of those provisions that the Administration objected to were watered down or eliminated, but others remain, reinforcing the White House's determination to veto the bills.

"They want to balance the budget by unbalancing the environment," Vice President Al Gore said today, during an appearance at a water treatment plant near Washington. "President Clinton will not accept a budget so out of touch with American values."

Among other spending cuts, the bill paying for the environmental agency's programs reduces by hundreds of millions of dollars the amount of money that the Federal Government would give states for sewage and drinking water treatment plants.

The Administration's veto threats are complicated by the broader talks involving the overall Federal budget, which are reaching a critical stage, and by the prospect that Congress might refuse to revise the two bills in ways that Mr. Clinton would accept. Instead, lawmakers have threatened to haul in the reins on environmental agencies by passing stop-gap spending measures that keep the agencies open indefinitely, but sharply curtail their spending.

Senator Christopher S. Bond, Republican of Missouri, the leading sponsor of the bill paying for the E.P.A.'s programs, called on the Administration to "quit the grandstanding, accept reality, and begin to negotiate in good faith with this Congress on the budget."

He said that despite the bill's cuts, there would be more money to spend on environmental protection if it is enacted than if no bill at all is passed.

Senator Robert C. Byrd, a Democrat of West Virginia, during debate on the Interior Department bill, urged the Administration not to veto it.

"The controversial features will not go away if the bill is vetoed," he said. "I urge the Administration to think carefully -- once, twice, three times -- and then think again, before issuing a veto."

In the Interior bill, the House and Senate, in a concession to the White House, agreed after prolonged negotiations to extend for another year a moratorium on selling mineral-rich public lands to mining corporations for a fraction of what the deposits are worth.

But the bill would ease restrictions on logging in the Tongass National Forest in Alaska, extend a moratorium on listing new species for protection under the Endangered Species Act, prevent the National Park Service from managing a newly created East Mojave Desert preserve in California and cut financing for an ambitious fish and ecosystem protection program in the Columbia River Basin of the Northwest.

The Administration also has objected to deep cuts in spending by the National Endowment for the Arts and the National Endowment for the Humanities contained in the Interior Department measure.

In the other bill, the Administration has complained about changes in enforcement of fair housing laws, and about the elimination of funding for AmeriCorps, Mr. Clinton's national service program.
