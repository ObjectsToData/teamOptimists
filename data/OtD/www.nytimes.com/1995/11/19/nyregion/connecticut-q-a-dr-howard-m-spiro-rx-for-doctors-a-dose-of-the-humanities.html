Connecticut Q&A: Dr. Howard M. Spiro;Rx for Doctors: A Dose of the Humanities

By ROBERT A. HAMILTON
Published: November 19, 1995

HOWARD M. SPIRO is a gastroenterologist at Yale-New Haven Hospital and an instructor at the Yale University School of Medicine.

Dr. Spiro, 71, a graduate of Harvard Medical School with privileges at 10 Connecticut hospitals, is widely published in the conventional medical literature. But he also devotes a considerable portion of his time to areas considered rather unconventional in the medical community.

He directs Yale's program for Humanities in Medicine, which this year is presenting a lecture series with topics like "Healing Through Visualization" and "The Patient Is Not a Thing." He is also the author of "Empathy and the Practice of Medicine" and "Doctors, Patients and Placebos."

Dr. Spiro recently discussed his efforts to promote the humanities in medicine. Following are some excerpts from that conversation.

Q. Isn't science enough for doctors?

A. Studies have shown that about 80 percent of all cases that a doctor sees, or who are seen on an outpatient basis at clinics and hospitals, have a psychosocial basis.

Those figures come from Massachusetts General in the 1890's and the 1970's. I haven't checked in the last 20 years but I would guess the numbers haven't changed.

These people may be having a problem, but mostly what has happened is they've learned that if they say they have pain they get the doctor's attention. The complaint of pain is really only a word to get the doctor's attention. It can be suffering, anguish, it can be sorrow. But the only thing that makes the doctor sit up and take notice is pain.

Q. What kind of reaction do your ideas get from other doctors?

A. One gets bemused or amused tolerance. But there is tolerance because I do not reject the value of science. We make rounds with the surgeons every week. It's good stuff and it keeps me down to earth. You need to have a foot in both camps.

Q. How long has the Humanities in Medicine program operated at Yale?

A. It started in 1983. A lot of universities have a program like this now. It's trendy. But they're all supported rather niggardly.

I started the gastrointestinal program at Yale in 1955, and that will go on as long as people have gastrointestinal disease. The humanities program, they pay my salary; they pay for half a secretary. But when I'm gone, what will happen to this program?

If someone said "Here's $10 million to put into Yale Medical School not for more molecular biology but to make doctors more humanistic," the whole thing would change. Money speaks, talks -- it shouts.

Q. Why is the support lacking?

A. Two of my kids are psychiatrists, and they like to point out that the kinds of things you like to do, you don't get credit for. I have some rich patients, and they say, "Why should I pay for a doctor to enjoy himself?"

The people and the foundations that should be supporting these kinds of things would rather be supporting research into viruses than paying doctors to listen to their patients. If you did that you'd stop doing so many tests.

Q. Aren't tests important for rooting out the basis of disease?

A. One of the problems is that it's easier to do that test than to listen to the patient. Plus, patients demand the tests because they associate more tests with better care. And the fear of litigation drives a lot of it. If I get on the phone with a lawyer, I find myself regarding the patient as a potential adversary rather than someone to be helped.

If you know that doing a test is going to make you some money, make the patient happier and maybe give you some information, you're going to find reasons to do the test.

Q. Do doctors try too hard to find a biological basis for illness?

A. Yale has a wonderful neurobiology department, several departments in fact. They're convinced that they'll find everything if they measure everything and chase down everything. But will they find ambition? Will they find greed? How are we going to explain the seven deadly sins by the biology of the cell?

Or take peptic ulcers. Fifty years ago, people would take an ulcer as a warning, that it was time to change their life, that their body was telling them something. In 1995 doctors are saying it has nothing to do with stress; it's bacteria. I believe stress has a lot to do with things.

The notions of psychosomatic medicine are totally lost at the moment. Doctors tend to take a unitarian kind of view of things, that there is one cause. They don't believe there might be two or three or four things.

Q. With all the medical advances, is there a tendency to also overuse technology?

A. Well, take gall bladders. A major advance was laparoscopic cholecystectomy, taking the gall bladder out through the belly button. It made the procedure absolutely perfect.

But what's happened is now doctors say, "Well, you've got a little belching, a little bit of heartburn, you've got that gall bladder that's not doing you any good so let's take it out." So they take it out, and the patient isn't any better, then they do 6, 8, 10 more tests. What we have to do is listen to the patient.
