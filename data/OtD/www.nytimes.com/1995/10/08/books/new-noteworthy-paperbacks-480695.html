New & Noteworthy Paperbacks

By LAUREL GRAEBER
Published: October 8, 1995

GAL: A True Life By Ruthie Bolton. Onyx, $6.99.

A young black woman from Charleston, S.C., tells the story of an impoverished and horrifically abusive upbringing that first propelled her toward degradation and later led her to strength. Written pseudonymously, this memoir "reads with the vivid urgency of fiction one can't put down," Marianne Gingher said here last year.

NONE TO ACCOMPANY ME By Nadine Gordimer. Penguin, $11.95.

This Nobel laureate's fictional setting is again her native South Africa, but in this novel it is the country of the present, finally freed from the bonds of apartheid. The central character, Vera Stark, is a white lawyer who has devoted herself to ending the old political system and now finds the future tugging her away from her family and her roots. Last year our reviewer, Richard Bausch, wrote, "Ms. Gordimer has, once again, provided a clear window through which to witness the ramifications of . . . momentous changes on particular lives." The struggle for change is also at the heart of THIS DAY AND AGE, by Mike Nicol (Vintage, $11), in which the events of South African history are transmuted into a fable about the rise of a messianic black leader intent on both freedom and revenge. The author "surely captures the ambivalence with which many white South Africans, even those who have opposed apartheid, regard an 'afterward' whose shape remains unclear," Michael Gorra said in the Book Review in 1992. THE WESTERN CANON: The Books and School of the Ages By Harold Bloom. Riverhead, $15.

An impassioned critic of both literature and universities discusses his fear that social motives will soon overwhelm esthetic merit in the choice of authors young Americans study. He then gives his own somewhat idiosyncratic selections of the works that ought to endure. The result "is a heroically brave, formidably learned and often unbearably sad response to the present state of the humanities," Norman Fruman wrote in these pages last year. That state -- as well as that of American politics -- is equally deplored in DICTATORSHIP OF VIRTUE: How the Battle Over Multiculturalism Is Reshaping Our Schools, Our Country, and Our Lives, by Richard Bernstein (Vintage, $13). The author, a book critic for The New York Times, examines the ways in which the drive for diversity has been its own worst enemy. In 1994, our reviewer, Leon Wieseltier, disagreed with some of Mr. Bernstein's analyses, but called the study an "important book." WOMEN AND GHOSTS By Alison Lurie. Avon, $9.

This novelist's first short-story collection is full of supernatural occurrences, but the geniuses behind them are by no means always the souls of the dead. "Dorothy Parker meets Edgar Allan Poe" is the way David Leavitt described these 10 tales here last year. CIVIL WARS: From L.A. to Bosnia By Hans Magnus Enzensberger. New Press, $9.95.

The author argues that the sectarian violence taking place in so many nations is characterized by an odd detachment from ideology: whatever the leaders claim, these brutal conflicts are devoid of both ideas and ideals. Writing in these pages last year, Francis Fukuyama found these essays "consistently interesting and full of small but striking insights." A portrait of one of the world's most divided regions emerges in THE IMPOSSIBLE COUNTRY: A Journey Through the Last Days of Yugoslavia, by Brian Hall (Penguin, $11.95). For several months in 1991, the author lived among the Serbs, Croats and Muslims, whose only common bond seemed to be the virulence of the ethnic stereotypes they embraced. His is "a brilliantly written account" that is "intelligent, witty and full of precious details," Anna Husarska said here last year. BELFAST DIARY: War as a Way of Life, by John Conroy (Beacon, $12), shows the bitter factions in Northern Ireland sharing a poverty and pessimism that may ultimately prove more destructive than their religious quarrels. The author, who has added a new afterword to this edition, "offers a well-written, sympathetic and clear-eyed view," our reviewer, Martin F. Nolan, wrote in 1987. THE AMERICAN WOMAN IN THE CHINESE HAT By Carole Maso. Plume, $10.95.

This novel depicts a journey into madness. Catherine, the narrator, is a writer devastated by the departure of her lover and the death of her brother; the reader shares her fragmented perceptions as her psyche disintegrates. The artistry of this portrayal is "shrewd, subtle, unsettling," Tom Sleigh said in these pages last year. Two of Carole Maso's earlier works have also been reissued. In THE ART LOVER (Ecco, $13), the novelist herself steps into the narrative. An exploration of the ability of art to mediate grief, the story links the experiences of a fictional woman with that of the author, who lost a close friend to AIDS. The result is "fully coherent, moving and elegiac," William Ferguson wrote in the Book Review in 1990. Ms. Maso's first novel, GHOST DANCE (Ecco, $13), introduces several of the themes she explores in her later fiction. The narrator, a young woman, tells the story of the family she has lost: her brother, who has disappeared, and her mother, a poet whose genius was marred by insanity. In 1986 our reviewer, Meredith Sue Willis, praised "the power and beauty of many bravura passages." THE ARABISTS: The Romance of an American Elite By Robert D. Kaplan. Free Press, $13.

The author's subject is the diplomats who have determined and guided the relationship between the United States and the Arab nations. He shows them to be capable of both extreme folly (trusting Saddam Hussein) and genuine heroism (rescuing the Ethiopian Jews). This is a book "that does much to illuminate American policy toward the Middle East," Evan Thomas wrote here in 1993. LAUREL GRAEBER
