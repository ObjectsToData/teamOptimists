Journal;Up at Newt's Place

By FRANK RICH
Published: June 1, 1995

Nearly six months after his ascendancy, Newt Gingrich still has not met with Jane Alexander to hear her case for the National Endowment for the Arts. But he did make time to see Melanie Griffith, an N.E.A. proponent whose acting credits include "Working Girl" and "Something Wild" rather than Ms. Alexander's somewhat less lubricious "First Monday in October."

"Whoever thought of bringing Melanie, it was genius," said the playwright Wendy Wasserstein, another member of the cadre that lobbied the Hill last week for N.E.A. programs supporting writers. Republican lawmakers tripped over themselves to press her flesh and pose with her for pictures. "I saw Strom Thurmond come back to life shaking Melanie's hand," said Ms. Wasserstein.

Speaking by phone from Aspen, Ms. Griffith said she had rehearsed carefully for her Washington appearance: a lobbyist took her on a dry run that included meeting Bob Dole. For last week's visit she wore a white Versace suit, Susan Bennis/Warren Edwards pumps and an ankle bracelet. Her pitch? "I'm very simplistic about it. I don't know how to speak politics. I go by my heart. . . . We need to have a national defense, but why could they just not make three planes and keep the N.E.A.?"

How did meeting real Congressmen differ from playing a fictional bombshell who storms the Capitol in the movie "Born Yesterday"? "It was cool," Ms. Griffith said. "Fred [Thompson], who played a Senator in the movie, is now a Senator!"

As for Mr. Gingrich, he did not show Ms. Griffith his etchings -- but he did invite her and her fellow lobbyists into his office to see a tyrannosaurus rex skull. He also told Ms. Griffith there were some parts for her in his forthcoming novel -- presumably if it's Hollywood-bound. And where does the Speaker now stand on the N.E.A., which is targeted for extinction in the House? No one could tell.

Both Ms. Wasserstein and Ms. Griffith were impressed by other Republicans, like Bill Frist and Hank Brown, who engaged in open-minded conversation about the endowment. Last Thursday three other arts-minded Republican Senators -- Nancy Kassebaum, Jim Jeffords and Alan Simpson -- joined Democratic colleagues in introducing a Senate bill reauthorizing the N.E.A. Though the bill streamlines the agency and inevitably trims its budget, Ms. Alexander has rightly applauded it as a serious bipartisan effort to uphold the Federal role in the arts.

Without Federal leadership, explains Robert Lynch of the National Assembly of Local Arts Agencies, state and local arts funding would probably decline, too; such funding was minuscule before the N.E.A.'s seed-money grants began to stimulate local giving in 1965. Nor could that Gingrich panacea, "privatization," replace the N.E.A.; private philanthropy to the arts and humanities fell nationally in 1994 even as charitable contributions rose.

But the Senate bill hardly assures the endowment's survival. It is just one more skirmish in a war over the agency's identity and funding that will play out in both houses. Mr. Gingrich's role remains both pivotal and ambiguous. He panders to the Christian Coalition, whose contract would kill the arts and humanities endowments. Yet he speaks of his daughters' affection for the ballet, and, as a big-time author, expressed solidarity with Ms. Wasserstein and another co-lobbyist, the novelist Walter Mosley. As a historian and dinosaur aficionado, the Speaker is also known to revere museums, which crave endowment support.

Would Ms. Griffith make the ultimate sacrifice of appearing in a Newt Gingrich movie to win his support of the N.E.A.? "I would do whatever it takes," she giggled.

But surely it would be a far, far better thing if the Speaker listened to reason about the N.E.A., whether from less throaty voices in his own party or the many prominent artists nurtured by endowment grants early on. As one such grantee, Ms. Wasserstein, argued in Washington, an exclusively market-driven culture will always produce "Die Hard With a Vengeance," not the ballet. Does Mr. Gingrich, self-styled champion of American renewal, really want history to record that he presided over what may well be the first legislative body in the West to eliminate support for the arts?
