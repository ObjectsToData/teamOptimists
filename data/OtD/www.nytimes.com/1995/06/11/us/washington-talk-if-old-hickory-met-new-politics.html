Washington Talk; If 'Old Hickory' Met New Politics

By ADAM CLYMER
Published: June 11, 1995

A major party's Presidential candidate was revealed last month to have sworn an oath of allegiance to a foreign country, pledging to report all conspiracies against its sovereign and to support him in battle if necessary.

The candidates' opponents have not yet made use of the charge, probably because they have been dead for more than a century.

C. Douglas Inglis, a historian working in Seville, Spain, recently found that Andrew Jackson had sworn such an oath as "vassal of his Catholic majesty," 35 years before he ran for President. Dr. Robert V. Remini, a leading Jackson biographer, reported and discussed the discovery last month in the Tennessee Historical Quarterly.

Neither Dr. Remini nor a number of today's political consultants would guess how the revelation would have played in 1824, when John Quincy Adams defeated Jackson in an election thrown into the House of Representatives even though Jackson won more popular votes, or in 1828, when Jackson won over Adams, or in 1832, when he defeated Henry Clay.

They recalled that Jackson was already the target of the first vicious Presidential campaign in American history, confronted with accusations that he was an adulterer. Some argued that his reputation for winning the battle of New Orleans would have overcome criticism that he had once sold out his country.

But they were intrigued with how the news would have played if Adams had known of the oath, and how such dirt might mesh with today's made-for-television, slash-and-burn tactics. "Servile to Seville" might have been the sound bite attack against him, suggested Bob Shrum, a Democratic consultant who cautioned that he would have been working Jackson's side in 1824. "It could have been the first 'America -- Love It or Leave It' campaign," he said.

Professor Kathleen Hall Jamieson, dean of the Annenberg School of Communication at the University of Pennsylvania, said modern techniques might have meant an advertisement featuring a young actor at least faintly resembling Jackson, raising his hand to take an oath. "You would darken the video," she said, to "make it as sinister as possible," and use identifiably Spanish music. And, because attack ads are more effective if they are funny, she said, "have someone say 'Ole!' at the end."

Lyn Nofziger, a Republican consultant, said he thought a better ending would be either "Hasta la vista, Andy" or "Ole Hickory," to play off the Tennessean's nickname, "Old Hickory."

Dr. Remini, a retired professor of history and research professor of humanities at the University of Illinois at Chicago, explained in his article that when Jackson took the oath on July 15, 1789, such a profession of loyalty was required to do business in Natchez, then governed by Spain but now a part of the state of Mississippi.

He wrote: "To conduct business and own property in Natchez, every male immigrant was obliged to take the oath of loyalty. Like other American farmers, merchants and professionals, Jackson did not hesitate to obey Spanish law."

Twenty-two years old at the time, Jackson swore on a Bible and signed with 13 others an oath in Spanish that the undersigned "would not offend directly nor conspire against the Spanish nation; to the contrary, they would defend it and help it with all their might, will and power, especially obligating them to take up arms against any enemy that attempt to attack this province; obligating themselves to inform the Governor of any conspiracy."

They promised to maintain "faithful and inviolably the orders, proclamations, edicts of the kingdom and the Government, obeying in all the same exactness as the rest of the vassals of his Catholic majesty in whose class and standing from now they are included."

The professor concluded that "Andrew Jackson was a pragmatic realist" and "undoubtedly took the oath without giving it a second thought, the way a great many other Americans did at the time."

But the professor agreed in an interview that whatever Jackson's reasons, it could have been used against him at the time.

If the oath had been so used and had resulted in a more definitive victory for Adams in 1824, said Dr. Remini, who has also written a biography of Henry Clay, then Clay's Presidential hopes could have been rescued, for his accepting the post of Secretary of State under Adams would not have seemed like a payoff for the votes in the House that helped Adams defeat Jackson.
