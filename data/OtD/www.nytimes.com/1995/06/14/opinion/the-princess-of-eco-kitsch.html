The Princess of Eco-Kitsch

By Simon Schama;
Published: June 14, 1995

Wise Use warriors, beware. Anti-environmentalists, hold that backhoe. Clean Water Act amenders, mend your ways. You may have thought you could ride the wave of hostility to Federal regulation all the way to developers' paradise, but you'd better think again.

Disney's new movie, "Pocahontas," makes it clear that you are faced with a far more formidable champion of the environment than anything the Wilderness Society can mobilize. The Mouse has gone Green, and this summer millions of 6-year-olds will respond to those Singalong Bouncing Ears and follow the leader deep into the virgin woods.

The movie's premiere in Central Park on Saturday night was likely the leafiest ever. Yet while Pocahontas was warbling about nature, most of the real trees were blocked from view by the eight-story screens, the gigantic projection towers and the 50-foot inflatable Mickey: the awesome artillery of the Disney Rapid Deployment Force.

This was not what Calvert Vaux and Frederick Law Olmsted had in mind when they designed the park as pastoral therapy for the brutishness of modern life. The sunken transverse roads and sweeping greensward were to create a refuge from the city, perfumed by honeysuckle and fresh-mown grass, and sonorous with birdsong.

Disney-pastoralism is something else, electronically delivered and determined to bring the microchip world into the greenery. Yet it has a sedative effect comparable to Olmsted's idea: an invulnerable sense of sweetness and security, the wild things safely fenced out, copious doses of cheerfulness and chuckles.

And for a crowd forced to endure six hours of warm-up acts before the movie -- endless renditions of "Hakuna Matata," a song from "The Lion King" the title of which means "no worries" -- the atmosphere of bovine contentment on the Great Lawn was little short of miraculous.

It was not, however, all popcorn and contentment. In one of the few moments when the terminally earnest Pocahontas is allowed to be the "frolicksome" girl 17th-century chroniclers described, she rejects the suitor assigned by her father, Chief Powhatan, with the Valley Girlish objection that "he's so serious."

Not as serious, however, as the movie makers, who evidently (and admirably) want Pocahontas to be a consciousness-raiser for the environment. To make the opposition between the rapacious, tree-chopping, gold-crazed Brits of the Virginia Company and the nature-venerating Powhatans evident even to a 6-year-old (especially to a 6-year-old), the movie artfully draws on nature myths and fables to tell its story of moral redemption.

Paradoxically, these myths are invariably from the Western tradition. The trees of the Powhatans' woodland home are first seen drawn up in columnar aisles, forming the nave of an ancient, living cathedral. Check off: "Holy Arboreals." (Disney's animators surely studied up on American and German Romantic art.)

Later in the film, Pocahontas feels unaccountably restless, possibly because she's been abruptly converted from a girl to a nubile woman. She deals with this by a burst of heavy paddling upstream, considering in song which of the two approaching watercourses represents her Destiny. Check off: River of Life. Even the talking tree that offers her guidance has a venerable mythical prototype in a tradition that has Alexander the Great offered counsel by talking vegetables in whose branches lie the images of the sun and the moon.

This is is not to say that the Algonquin Powhatans did not have their own potent and poetic nature religion. But the version offered here is about as authentically Algonquin as the melodies sung by Pocahontas.

A beautifully stylized ancient oak provides the setting for the polemical centerpiece: an exchange between Pocahontas and John Smith (strangely but aptly resembling John Tesh, the blond, lantern-jawed host of "Entertainment Tonight") on their attitudes toward the land. "We can improve the lives of savages all over the world," says Smith breezily, sounding like an advocate for Contract with America block grants. "We'll build roads and decent houses."

"The earth is just a dead thing you can claim," she retorts sarcastically. "Come roll in all the riches around you and for once never ask what they are worth."

He tries to explain to her about gold: "It's yellow, valuable and comes out of the ground." She triumphantly produces a corn cob. Smitten, he enrolls in a short musical tutorial in the wise ways of nature. This is at least preferable to the insatiable gold lust of the English governor, who slobbers "it's mine, mine, mine" as trees crash, pickaxes ring and the earth explodes in the kind of ecological Armageddon that would make even Republican Congressmen from Idaho wince.

For all the guilt and piety, the film's revisionism has some merit. Native Americans did make less exacting demands on their land and understood the need to sustain it. But to make the Powhatans uniformly noble and sensitive to the earth is to substitute one stereotype for another, to condescend through idealizing.
