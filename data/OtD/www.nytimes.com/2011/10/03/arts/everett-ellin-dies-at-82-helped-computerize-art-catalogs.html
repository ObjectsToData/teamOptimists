Everett Ellin Dies at 82; Helped Computerize Art Catalogs

By MARGALIT FOX
Published: October 3, 2011

Until the mid-20th century, the world’s greatest artworks were cataloged in shoeboxes. Then along came the computer, and for the first time, museums had a more efficient way to organize their holdings than the boxfuls of dog-eared index cards that had served them for generations.

But they kept on using the shoeboxes.

Everett Ellin, a retired museum official who died last month, deplored those boxes. As the first executive director of the Museum Computer Network, a nonprofit organization begun in 1967, he helped bring about the reluctant marriage between the conservative world of art museums and wild frontier of information technology.

The marriage prefigured the ready electronic access to museums and their holdings that is taken for granted today.

“Few bastions of the humanities have withstood the march of technology more tenaciously than the art museum,” Mr. Ellin wrote in a journal article in 1969. “But now, at long last, the computer has entered the house of the Muse and — like the man who came to dinner — the guest is here to stay. It would behoove the host to know something about his visitor’s care and feeding.”

Mr. Ellin, who was also an art dealer, engineer, lawyer and former Hollywood talent agent, died on Sept. 16, at 82, at his ranch in Diana, Tex. His death, of pneumonia, was confirmed by his sister, Joyce Orenstein. He had also been ill with Alzheimer’s disease in recent years.

If it is churlish to call midcentury art museums hidebound, it is certainly accurate to call them tradition-bound. By the late 1960s, as a direct consequence of their technological reluctance, millions of items lay cataloged insufficiently (or not at all) in museums throughout the United States.

A scholar who wanted to write a book, say, about Hudson River School painters, or a curator who wanted to assemble an exhibition on the subject, would typically visit dozens of museums, ransacking each individual card file — and, for uncataloged treasures, each curator’s memory — to learn who had what.

The Museum Computer Network originally comprised 15 New York museums, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art and the Museum of Modern Art, plus the National Gallery of Art in Washington.

Its early mandate was simple if Sisyphean: to compile, using a hulking I.B.M. mainframe computer, a collective database of holdings that could be used at specially designated terminals in museums, universities and libraries.

The technical groundwork was laid by Jack Heller, a computer scientist at New York University and later the State University of New York at Stony Brook, and by David Vance, MoMA’s registrar.

Mr. Ellin, the money man and highly visible evangelist, secured grants and, through his lectures and writings, promoted the new technology to anxious curators worldwide.

Everett Bernard Ellin was born in Chicago on Oct. 5, 1928. After receiving a bachelor’s degree in engineering from the University of Michigan in 1949, he received a law degree from Harvard in 1952.

Mr. Ellin’s early jobs included law clerk to the chief justice of the California Supreme Court, house counsel at Columbia Pictures and aide to a vice president of the William Morris Agency.

In the late 1950s and early ’60s he ran the Everett Ellin Gallery in Los Angeles, exhibiting Bruce Beasley, Jasper Johns and David Smith, among other contemporary artists. Afterward, he worked for the Marlborough Gallery in New York before joining the Guggenheim Museum .

He was the Museum Computer Network’s executive director from 1967 to 1970 and in the early 1980s founded Scantech, which made digital imaging systems.

Mr. Ellin’s marriage to Joan Jacobs, a painter, ended in divorce. Besides his sister, Ms. Orenstein, he is survived by his companion, Reneta E. Nunn.

Today the Museum Computer Network is the chief professional organization for museum-technology specialists in North America and elsewhere, with a membership of more than 350 people. It has not, however, achieved its founding mandate: there are simply too many objects in too many museums, and too many simmering intellectual-property issues, to make a unified database feasible even now.

But in the end, perhaps that does not matter, for by dragging museums kicking and screaming into the information age, Mr. Ellin and his colleagues emphatically made their point: The man who came to dinner would not bite, and he would prove an unassailable asset to museums that had first balked at the sight of him.

“Everyone has the expectation that if you want to know what a museum has — see what a museum has — you simply go to the Internet and find it,” Leonard Steinbach, president of the Museum Computer Network in 2001 and 2002, said in a recent telephone interview. “That had to start with a dream. Everett had that dream at a time when museum information meant scribblings on file cards.”
