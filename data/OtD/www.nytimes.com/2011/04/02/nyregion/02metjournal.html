A High School Poised at a Moment of Pride and Great Anxiety

Todd Heisler/The New York Times

Rice High School students in the gymnasium. If the school fails to meet goals for fund-raising and enrollment, it will close.

By JENNY ANDERSON
Published: April 1, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

On a cold, sunny Tuesday morning, the entrance to Rice High School in Harlem is bustling. Television reporters are milling about, waiting to talk to the school’s current and former basketball coaches about Kemba Walker, the Rice alumnus who will lead the University of Connecticut Huskies in the N.C.A.A.’s Final Four this weekend. The students are excited because a busload of girls from a Catholic school in Brooklyn have arrived for a pre-prom get-to-know-you in the gym. And President Obama is visiting the neighborhood later in the day.

There is little sense that this all-boys Catholic prep school in Harlem is teetering on the brink of bankruptcy.

According to Stephen Fitzgerald, chairman of the board of directors, Rice needs donors to commit $5 million, spread over five years, to erase a $200,000 deficit and sustain the gap between the cost of educating a student ($10,000) and the tuition ($5,750). Some of that money will be needed to renovate the school’s proposed new home, an elementary school on 130th Street.

If Rice cannot secure the $5 million, and enroll 60 boys in the incoming freshman class, by May 15, it will shut down.

“Each year it’s more and more of a struggle,” Mr. Fitzgerald said.

But this year, the endowment ran out. From a peak of $3 million 10 years ago, it stands at $160,000, all of which has been committed to pending bills.

Now the board is racing against the clock to develop a five-year plan to assure potential doors that money will be invested in a sustainable school and not just a ballooning deficit.

“I’ve been putting Band-Aids on,” Mr. Fitzgerald said. “I guess I should have done a five-year plan earlier. I was an optimist.”

Rice, named after the Irish missionary and educator Edmund Rice, was founded in 1938 by the Congregation of Christian Brothers, a teaching order dedicated to educating the poor. Rice has embraced that mission: 70 percent of students receive some form of financial aid. That exacerbates the school’s deficit. In past years, the Christian Brothers have bridged that deficit with $100,000 to $300,000 a year, but they can no longer afford to help Rice.

Like many other faith-based schools, Rice, which is 80 percent African-American and 20 percent Hispanic, focuses on character as much as grade point average and SAT scores.

“It’s countercultural,” Sister Patricia Ells, the head of school, said. “It tries to think about the future, going to college, and how to get there.”

It works. Though the school is better known for its basketball stars, all of its graduates have gotten into college for the past four years.

“This is the real trophy case,” Mr. Fitzgerald said, ignoring the NBC crew in front of the basketball awards and showing off another trophy case lined with every single college acceptance letter students have received this year.

Boys at the school refer to the school as “a family,” their fellow students as “brothers” and their teachers as “parents.”

Sister Patricia recently told the students about the school’s plight. One student suggested the school host a dance and charge $10 instead of $5. Another student proposed that the school charge the students $3 a week to have dress-down Fridays.

Three Rice seniors, speaking over the raucous din of a packed gym of recently connected teenage boys and girls, spoke eloquently about becoming “Rice men” — men of responsibility, integrity, courage and empowerment. Charles Scudder, 17, pointed out that Rice students have a good reputation in Harlem. They are committed to giving back to Rice.

“I want to be an accountant and make lots of money, and if Rice is still running, I want to donate and keep this building going,” said Devaughn Reid, 17, who plays point guard for the basketball team.

Rice might need it. The seven-story red and white brick building on West 124th Street is a money pit: Maintenance for the past two and a half years has cost $425,000.

If the school gets the money it needs, it will move to a recently closed elementary school on 130th Street that the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New York agreed to lease to it. Costs would be lower there.

The 124th Street building had been a Y.W.C.A. The pool has not worked since the 1960s, Sister Patricia said. The school has a gym, but it is not regulation size, so Rice has to rent one to play games.

First, Rice needs the money. The hurdles are high. The economy is sluggish, enrollment has dropped 40 percent since 2003 and procrastination seemed to trump long-term planning when it came to the school’s balance sheet.

“Rice has a long tradition of doing a lot of heavy lifting, helping disadvantaged children,” said Patrick J. McCloskey, author of “The Street Stops Here: A Year at a Catholic High School in Harlem,” a book he wrote about a year that he spent observing Rice. “Unfortunately, they haven’t dealt with the financial side as well as they could have.”

As Rice lingers on the edge, it is clear that neither faith nor family can heal the school’s finances. Rice has had a vacuum of leadership, churning through five heads of school in 10 years before settling last year on Sister Patricia, the first non-Christian Brother to serve in the position. Rice has failed to institutionalize fund-raising and alumni outreach: before the school hired a head of development last fall, the position had been vacant for two years.

The clock is ticking. Mr. Fitzgerald and Sister Patricia are dialing for dollars while they also scramble to secure students for the fall. Rice has 34 signed up, and another 17 have committed. It needs at least 9 more.

To many inside Rice who have seen the school threatened with extinction many times before, there is nothing to worry about.

“We have a nationally recognized basketball team with no gym, and a competitive baseball program which are gypsies,” said Brother William J. Sherlog, a 16-year veteran of the school who teaches 9th-grade religion, 9th-grade humanities and 11th-grade American history, and also is the athletic director, the baseball coach and the bus driver.

“We are used to doing without,” he said with an air of intoxicating calm.
