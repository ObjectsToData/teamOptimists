Staging the Politics of Abortion

By PATRICK HEALY
Published: April 13, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single-Page
  • Reprints

NEWARK, Del. — When the playwright Theresa Rebeck was writing for the ABC drama “NYPD Blue” in the 1990s, she proposed a story line in which a main female character considers an abortion — and then has one, a rare outcome on American television. But the idea was rejected as toxic for the character, she said. Ms. Rebeck later pitched an abortion-themed project to Lifetime, then known by the tag line Television for Women, but that cable channel passed, too.

Ms. Rebeck, an Off Broadway fixture who was a finalist for the Pulitzer Prize in drama in 2004, is now telling the story, in even more explosive terms — and in a far different setting — than she originally imagined.

In a first, the English and theater departments at the University of Delaware gave Ms. Rebeck a $50,000 commission last year (the funds came from a private foundation) to write a play blending American history and contemporary issues. She has produced “O Beautiful,” a satirical look at the politics of the Tea Party, Glenn Beck and the failed Senate candidate Christine O’Donnell, as dramatized through the abortion story and a related, fictitious incident of bullying at a high school. The university’s Resident Ensemble Players, a professional Equity company, will perform the play on campus from Wednesday to May 15.

“O Beautiful” also deals with suicide, date rape, gun rights, the founding fathers and, of course, abortion — in a subplot starting with the first scene, in which a pregnant teenager, Alice, seeks counsel from Jesus Christ, who wanders through the high school and characters’ homes without explanation. Jesus, rendered as sympathetic to everyone irrespective of their politics, reaches a provocative conclusion when Alice, who has been raped, asks where in the Bible he opposes abortion.

Alice: Did you ever say, “I’m Jesus, and I say that stupid girls who let guys talk them into going to the back seat of their cars have to have babies?” Did you say that ever?

Jesus: No.

Alice: All you talk about is, be nice to each other! You never said nobody’s allowed to have an abortion.

Jesus: No.

Alice: So can I? Can I? Can I?

Jesus: Honestly, I — I don’t really have an issue with it.

Ms. Rebeck, who was raised Roman Catholic in what she called “Republican territory” outside of Cincinnati, had been following Ms. O’Donnell’s candidacy in Delaware in 2010 and watching Tea Party rallies on television when she received a call last spring from Sanford Robbins, the University of Delaware’s theater department chairman. His grant proposal for a $50,000 play commission — more than twice as much as Ms. Rebeck usually receives, like most playwrights, from nonprofit theaters — had been approved by the school’s Interdisciplinary Humanities Research Center. Mr. Robbins wanted characters tailored for his ensemble company of 10 Equity actors as well as several acting students. In other words, it was a rare chance to write a sprawling work at a time when a cast of five actors is seen as large for a new play. Was she interested?

“I’d never had an opportunity like this, so I jumped at it,” Ms. Rebeck said during an interview last week in the gymnasium-size rehearsal hall for “O Beautiful.” “Including undergraduates appearing in smaller roles, we have a cast of 40. Incredible. So I wanted to tell a sweeping story that befits the commission. And one thing that has stayed with me over time is that so many women’s stories were not being told in the culture.”

“So I started with Alice’s story, but the politics of our day was also on my mind, and I felt particularly disturbed by how Jesus was being distorted over and over on the right,” added Ms. Rebeck, a University of Notre Dame graduate who, in her 20s, was a ghostwriter of Mass homilies for a company that paid $50 for weekday services and $150 for Sundays. “I have huge admiration for Jesus Christ and for his incredible compassion for all people.”

Mr. Robbins, a soft-spoken director who displayed a teacher’s encouraging hand during rehearsal, said Ms. Rebeck had been his first choice for the commission because he had long been an admirer of her “edgy, fearless work,” which includes the plays “Mauritius” on Broadway and “The Understudy” Off Broadway. She was a Pulitzer finalist for “Omnium Gatherum,” which she wrote with Alexandra Gersten-Vassilaros, about a dinner party in hell after 9/11; most recently she wrote the script for “Smash,” an NBC pilot about the making of a Broadway musical that the network is expected to assess in the coming weeks.

Depictions of Jesus have caused controversy before, like the gay Christ in Terrence McNally’s play “Corpus Christi,” as well as Andres Serrano’s photograph of a crucifix submerged in urine and the recent furor at the Smithsonian over David Wojnarowicz’s work, especially his imagery of ants crawling over a crucifix. Yet even as Ms. Rebeck began sharing plots for “O Beautiful,” Mr. Robbins said he did not flinch at the idea of a state-supported university’s providing $50,000 for a play that includes Jesus accompanying Alice on a road trip to Connecticut — with Big Gulps in hand — to evade Delaware’s parental consent laws on abortion.
