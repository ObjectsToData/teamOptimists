The Age of Purification

China Photos/Getty Images

The Mexican healer Doña Lucia treats people with alternative methods like cupping, which is believed to release toxins.

By GUY TREBAY
Published: May 20, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

“SUÉLTALO!”

Enlarge This Image
Scott Kleinman/Getty Images

Thumb and forefinger pressing into my navel, Doña Lucia issued a command.

“Suéltalo!” she repeated. “Release it, man!”

Then Doña Lucia, a Mexican folk healer (or curandera), pressed the full weight of her body down toward my internal organs, down in the direction of my spine, down toward the floor of the fussy Upper East Side apartment where I lay imagining her hand like some horror from “Alien” pressing through me and out the other side.

“What are you holding onto, man?” demanded Doña Lucia, as a barefoot, turbaned associate of hers darted about us waving a stick of incense and uttering a chant about the breath of God. That one was easy to answer. Was it negative energy I clung to with increasing desperation? It was not. It was a scream of pain.

Once each spring Doña Lucia, a traditional Nahua healer from the mountains of central Mexico, arrives in Manhattan, an apparition out of a Rivera mural in her embroidered cotton huipil, gray hair knotted in Tehuana braids, front teeth outlined in silver half-caps.

Scoring an appointment with the healer, a legend among a certain sector of local spiritual seekers, is close to impossible, so hotly anticipated are her visits by New Yorkers eager to have their chakras aligned, “heart energy” released, sluggish energies kick-started, psyches given a seasonal cleansing of all those “toxins” one hears so much about.

It was toxins that led me to Doña Lucia; anyway, she was a stop I made on a little foray into the purification-industrial complex after a friend offered me her slot with the healer. Nothing as common as money is exchanged in payment for the session, she explained, suggesting instead a generous donation to Dona Lucia’s village.

My friend, like everyone else around, seemed to believe that mysterious, amorphous sludge had lodged in the anatomical crannies of half the local adult population. Unseen toxins were lurking, like Communists during the Red Scare.

The “toxins” required elimination, somehow, and thus at lunches, at cocktails, at dinner parties, normal conversations turned abruptly from the day’s news to progress reports on juice fasts, energy alignments, radical purging. From painful sessions with traditional healers to toxin-leaching treatments designed, it might seem, to clean out not just body but wallet, a surprising number of New Yorkers (not all of them well-to-do neurotics) are caught up in a new New Age, the Age of Purification.

How had it happened, I wondered, that so many otherwise sensible, urban people found themselves in the grip of a dreadful feeling that systems are down? “I just bought five pounds of carrots, ginger and kale and put it all in my Breville juicer and pounded that all day,” said a corporate adviser of my acquaintance, far from a credulous woo-woo type.

This woman was in the homestretch of a 10-day juice fast that sounded a lot like ritual starvation. “I do it every year at this time,” she said. Why? “There is a ... ‘moral’ may be too strong a word, but a positive psychic effect of clearing the toxins. Think about Easter, about rebirth, about sloughing things off.”

Anybody not pounding carrots, kale and ginger is getting oxygen therapy, it seems, scheduling a colonic, lining up a date with a Reiki healer or doing a Remove Cleanse calling for costly foil packets of stuff like prune powder and dandelion root. They are applying double-distilled bamboo vinegar Kinoki foot pads to their feet. And, reader, it is fact: I tried this, too.

Without exactly knowing I was doing so, I was building up endurance for Doña Lucia by scheduling sessions of Reiki, Qi Gong, refloxology, all Eastern therapeutic methods that, though considered dubious by some skeptics, are well on the way to becoming as mainstream as acupuncture, another ancient practice Western medicine long considered quackery.

I visited Chinatown joints like Relax Foot Spa, enduring the therapeutic ministrations of practitioners with sketchy credentials and an unerring knack for finding that spot in one’s arch where a well-placed knuckle guarantees either the end of toxic liver congestion or else an ability to stand erect.

I met with a Reiki master, whose hands hovered gently above my partly clad person, shifting my “energy” this way and that. And it felt great, I must say, although not quite as electrifying as the feeling I experienced once during a Reiki session in remote Tanzania when, as I lay on a table — how to put this politely? — an itsy bitsy spider climbed up the water spout.
