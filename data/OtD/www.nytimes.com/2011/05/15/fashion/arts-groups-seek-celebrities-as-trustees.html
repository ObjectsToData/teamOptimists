Romancing the Stars

Casey Kelbaugh for The New York Times

Sarah Jessica Parker and the Brooklyn Museum's director, Arnold Lehman, at the April 27 gala. More Photos »

By KATE TAYLOR
Published: May 13, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

AS Carrie Bradshaw would have recognized, a museum’s cultivation of a potential patron is a form of courtship, with the same flirtation, grand gestures, ardent hopes for a future together — and then, sometimes, of course, the realization that the person you’re wooing is, well, just not that into you.

The last was the case for the Brooklyn Museum recently, where Sarah Jessica Parker’s agreement to serve as “honorary co-chair” at its April gala, essentially just lending her name to the event, kindled museum executives’ dreams of a long-term relationship, involving more events and possibly even at some point a seat on the museum’s board of trustees.

Then, as the date of the gala approached, there was bad news. Ms. Parker had a conflict — she could stay only for cocktails. On the evening in question, she stayed for a half-hour, posing for pictures in her Chanel coat and bejeweled Manolo Blahniks and allowing herself to be ushered by a publicist into a rather unnatural tête-à-tête with her co-host, Liv Tyler, so that photographers could get a shot of the two actresses together. She complimented the choice of the evening’s theme, which was honoring Brooklyn artists; praised the decoration on the tables; and said she had been “delighted to be asked” to co-host the evening — even if, in the end, she could make about only one-sixth of it.

Then, “I’m sorry to rush off,” she said, as she headed back to Manhattan for the opening of “The Normal Heart,” before many of the evening’s guests had even arrived.

Although it seemed that this relationship was not to be, the museum’s leaders could hardly be faulted for having briefly harbored fantasies that it might join a select and envied group of New York City cultural institutions: those with a celebrity trustee.

While New York’s arts organizations have long sought to have Hollywood actors, fashion designers and pop stars as guests at their galas, some have recently gone a step further, asking celebrities to sit on their boards alongside the bank executives, real estate scions and wealthy socialites who traditionally occupy those positions. Last year, Ms. Parker became a trustee of the New York City Ballet, joining people like the oil-and-gas heiress Marlene Hess and the real estate developer Daniel Brodsky. In March, the New York Philharmonic announced that Alec Baldwin, already the voice of the orchestra’s radio broadcasts, had joined its board.

And other arts leaders are hoping to imitate them. At a Crain’s New York Business panel in March on how to build a strong board, George Steel, the general manager and artistic director of the struggling New York City Opera, spoke enviously of the American Museum of Natural History having the “Saturday Night Live” executive producer Lorne Michaels as a trustee, and suggested that arts organizations should try to enlist professional athletes as board members.

Behind the interest in recruiting celebrities to boards is a general sense that arts organizations now have to work harder than ever, both to raise money and to get the attention of a public that is overwhelmed with entertainment options, many of them a mere touch-screen click away. Having a celebrity on the board offers numerous advantages, from having a prominent spokesperson to being able to lure other donors with the opportunity to rub shoulders with someone famous.

“There’s so much competition to gain attention and money right now,” said Laurie Beckelman, a consultant to several arts organizations, adding that it was leading to a healthy effort to be more creative in seeking board members. “I think it’s great,” she said of the growing group of celebrity arts patrons. “Many of these boards should be shaken up.”

At the same time, some arts managers caution that having a major celebrity as a trustee can be a double-edged sword, particularly if expectations aren’t spelled out and mutually agreed upon beforehand. In many cases, celebrities aren’t required to make the same minimum annual contribution as other trustees, leading them potentially to be less generous. Meanwhile, because they have busy schedules, unless they’re particularly devoted they can be infrequent presences at board meetings and other events.
