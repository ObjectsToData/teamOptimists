Spare Times for Jan. 13-19

By ANNE MANCUSO
Published: January 12, 2012

  • Print

Museums and Sites

The Jewish Museum: Hanukkah Lamps (through Jan. 29) “An Artist Remembers: Hanukkah Lamps Selected by Maurice Sendak” features, along with 33 lamps from a variety of eras in many styles, an audio recording of Mr. Sendak that illuminates his choices through personal anecdotes. Saturday through Tuesday, 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; Thursday, 11 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Friday, 11 a.m. to 4 p.m. Jewish Museum, 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street, (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org; $12; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for children under 12.

Morgan Library & Museum: Charles Dickens and Robert Burns (Friday and Saturday, Tuesday through Thursday) The writers Charles Dickens and Robert Burns are subjects of exhibitions here. On view through Feb. 12 is “Charles Dickens at 200,” a display of his letters, stories, photographs and other items. Also on view, through Feb. 5, is “Robert Burns and ‘Auld Lang Syne’,” an exhibition of that Scottish poet’s writing, including a handwritten version of the poem that gave birth to the lyrics for the New Year’s Eve chestnut, “Auld Lang Syne.” Tuesdays through Thursdays, 10:30 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Fridays, 10:30 a.m. to 9 p.m. 225 Madison Avenue, at 36th Street, (212) 685-0008, themorgan.org; $15, or $10 for students, 65+ and children; free for members and children under 12.

Morris-Jumel Mansion: Satirical Cartoons and Wine (Saturday and Thursday) Satirical cartoons from the time of the War of 1812 will be discussed by Dr. Allison Stagg, an art historian, on Saturday at 2 p.m.; the talk, also sponsored by the New York Council for the Humanities, is free but requires advance registration at (212) 923-8008. On Thursday at 7 p.m., the subject will turn to wine for a tasting and discussion presented by Coogan’s, a Washington Heights restaurant; tickets are $25, or $20 for members. On view through Jan. 27 are two exhibitions that focus on fashions of the 18th and 19th centuries: “Dressed for the Holidays,” which includes day and night gowns, and “Dressing the Part,” which focuses on accessories like boots, stockings and garters. Wednesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. 65 Jumel Terrace, at Sylvan Terrace, Washington Heights, (212) 923-8008, morrisjumel.org; $5, or $4 for students and free for members and children under 12.

Museum of the Moving Image: ‘Nothing But a Man’ Screening (Monday) In celebration of Martin Luther King’s Birthday, a screening at 3 p.m. will feature this 1964 film by Michael Roemer about black life in 1960s Alabama. Part of the National Film Registry, the film stars Ivan Dixon and Abbey Lincoln and includes music by Stevie Wonder, Martha and the Vandellas, the Miracles and Mary Wells; free with admission: $12; $9 for 65+ and college students; $6 for ages 3 to 18; free for under 3. 35th Avenue at 37th Street, Astoria, Queens, (718) 784-0077, movingimage.us.

New York City Police Museum: ‘9/11: A Uniform Response’ (through Monday) This is the final weekend to view photographs of the Sept. 11 attacks and response, taken by The Associated Press. Friday, Saturday and Monday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; and Sunday, noon to 5 p.m. 100 Old Slip between Water and South Streets, Lower Manhattan, (212) 480-3100, nycpm.org; $8, or $5 for students, children and 65+; free for children under 2, members of the service and museum members.

New-York Historical Society: ‘Revolution! The Atlantic World Reborn’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Held in the newly renovated New-York Historical Society, this exhibition weaves together the American, French and Haitian revolutions’ common goal of popular sovereignty. Among the objects on display are the original Stamp Act and the sole copy of Haiti’s Declaration of Independence. 170 Central Park West, at 77th Street, (212) 873-3400, nyhistory.org; $15, $12 for teachers and 65+; $10 for students; $5 for ages 7 to 13; free under 7; through January, a $5 discount off adult admissions is available through NYC & Company; information, nycgo.com\culturespot.

New York Transit Museum: ‘ElectriCity: Powering New York’s Rails’ (continuing) A historical display of items from the museum collection, including switches and circuit breakers, that illustrates how electricity powers the subway system. Tuesdays through Fridays, 9 a.m. to 4 p.m. Boerum Place and Schermerhorn Street, Boerum Hill, Brooklyn, (718) 694-1600, mta.info/mta/museum; $7; $5 for those ages 2 to 17 and 62+; free for members.

Paley Center for Media: ‘Hey, Hey, Hey ... It’s Bill Cosby’ (through Jan. 22) To celebrate the 50th anniversary of Mr. Cosby’s show business career, the museum is presenting the first part of a screening series devoted to his television work. Two of his specials, the first in 1968 and the second in 1969, will be shown Wednesdays through Sundays at 12:30 p.m. 25 West 52nd Street, Manhattan, (212) 621-6800, paleycenter.org; $10, $8 for students, $5 for children under 14 and free for members.

Paley Center for Media: Project Runway All Stars (Thursday) A behind-the-scenes look at the new season of the Lifetime fashion reality show “Project Runway” will be offered by a group from the series, including the designers Isaac Mizrahi and Georgina Chapman, who serve as judges; the designers Austin Scarlett, Kenley Collins, Mondo Guerra and Kara Janx; the executive producer, Rob Bagshaw; and Joanna Coles, the editor in chief of the American edition of Marie Claire, who serves as mentor. The model Angela Lindvall is the host. At 6:30 p.m., 25 West 52nd Street, Manhattan, (212) 621-6800, paleycenter.org; $20, or $15 for members.

Gardens

New York Botanical Garden: Holiday Train Show (through Monday) Special hours are in place for this last weekend of the train show. On Friday, the garden will remain open from 6 to 8 p.m., with a cash bar available. On Saturday and Sunday it will be open from 10 a.m. to 7 p.m., and on Monday, from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. In addition to the 20th annual show itself, visitors can visit a studio where the construction of Kykuit, a landmark featured in the display, is explained. Bronx River Parkway, Exit 7W, and Fordham Road, the Bronx, (718) 817-8700, nybg.org; $20, or $18 for students and 65+; $10 for children 2 to 12; members and children under 2, free.

Events

‘Culture of Terezin’ (Tuesday and then selected days through Feb. 16) During the Holocaust, the ghetto of Terezin was known as a holding point for Czech Jews bound for death camps, but it was also the site of much artistic creation. “Will to Create, Will to Live: The Culture of Terezin,” a series under way at 92nd Street Y, features concerts, discussions and exhibitions that highlight this creativity. Reproductions of performance posters as well as original artifacts like ticket stubs, currency and identification cards are included in a historical display on view on selected days through Feb. 16. Ticket-holders can view the display on the days when they are attending events. It will be free and open to the public on Tuesday from noon to 4 p.m.; other dates are listed on the Web site. 92nd Street Y, 1395 Lexington Avenue, (212) 415-5500, 92y.org.

High Line Channel: ‘City Slivers’ (through Jan. 24) The urban landscape chronicled in the 1976 short film “City Slivers” by Gordon Matta-Clark is itself the vehicle for viewing the film, which is projected on the exterior of a Chelsea building at West 22nd Street and 10th Avenue, near the High Line, daily from dusk to 10 p.m. The film was first shown in a similar fashion. The screening is part of a series sponsored by High Line Art. thehighline.org; free.

‘Mad. Sq. Art’ (through March 12) Those animal sounds and images in Madison Square Park may not be the real thing. They could be among the animated video projections drawn from nature by the Dutch artist Jacco Olivier that are on display throughout the park, daily from 6 a.m. to midnight. The free display is part of the Madison Square Park Conservancy’s public art program. Madison and Fifth Avenues, between 23rd and 26th Streets, (212) 538-1884, madisonsquarepark.org; free.

Martin Luther King Jr. Day of Service Activities and Performance (Monday) Those who want to honor the memory of Dr. King by volunteering for community projects can choose from a variety of them planned by the JCC in Manhattan, like preparing meals for a soup kitchen, visiting a home for the elderly and assembling food and toiletry kits for the homeless. Registration begins at 10 a.m.; a fee of $5 will be used to purchase materials for the projects. A free performance celebrating racial harmony and shared cultural heritages sponsored by the JCC, with music, poetry and spoken word, will take place at Symphony Space beginning at 6:30 p.m.; doors open at 6 p.m., and admission is first come first serve. JCC in Manhattan, 334 Amsterdam Avenue, at 76th Street, Manhattan, (646) 505-4444, jccmanhattan.org; registration fee, $5. Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street; free.

Music and Interfaith Celebrations for Martin Luther King’s Birthday (Sunday) A day of religious reflection and music, including stops at three houses of worship, will begin at 2 p.m. at the Islamic Cultural Center of New York, 1711 Third Avenue, at 97th Street. There, the imam and others will take part in “Words of Inspiration.” The next stop, at 3 p.m., is the Sutton Place Synagogue, 225 East 51st Street, where the rabbi and others will also give inspirational talks. The last stop is St. Bartholomew’s Church, Park Avenue and 51st Street, where choirs from churches around the city, as well as the United Nations Singers and a children’s choir from the Bronx, will take part in a service beginning at 4 p.m. Visitors can drop into one or all of the events, which are free and sponsored by the houses of worship as well as the New York Metropolitan Martin Luther King Jr. Center for Nonviolence and other organizations. Limited, free bus rides to the Sutton Place Synagogue from the Islamic Center are available through the Center for Nonviolence, but reservations are necessary and can be made by calling (718) 589-7858, Ext. 30.

New York Studio School Exhibition (closes this Friday) The works of more than 20 graduates of this art school will be on view daily from 10 a.m. to 10 p.m. New York Studio School, 8 West Eighth Street, Greenwich Village, (212) 673-6466, nyss.org; free.

Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture: Screening and Discussion (Saturday) Daisy Bates was an activist and a pivotal figure in the civil rights movement, best known for her role in desegregating the all-white Central High School in Little Rock, Ark., in 1957. Her fight for equality is the subject of Sharon La Cruise’s “Daisy Bates: First Lady of Little Rock,” which will be screened at 4 p.m.; a discussion with Ms. La Cruise will follow. 515 Lenox Avenue, at 135th Street, Harlem, (212) 491-2040, schomburgcenter.eventbrite.com; free.

Shen Yun Classical Chinese Music and Dance (Friday through Sunday) The performers are New York-based, but the traditional Chinese art forms presented by Shen Yun Performing Arts could be found on any Beijing stage. This year’s show, Shen Yun 2012, has performances at 7:30 p.m. on Friday and 2 p.m. on Saturday and Sunday. David H. Koch Theater, Lincoln Center, Columbus Avenue and West 64th Street, (800) 818-2393, shenyunperformingarts.org; $80 to $250.

Squash Competition (Thursday) On any given day, Vanderbilt Hall is filled with thousands of commuters moving through Grand Central Terminal. Starting Thursday it will be the site of an annual squash competition featuring more than 80 international players. This year’s contest, sponsored by J. P. Morgan, begins with sets at 7:30 p.m. and continues through Jan. 26. A 10-ton glass squash court will include stadium seating for 500, with tickets ranging from $8 to $160; a free standing-room area is also available. Set times can be viewed at tocsquash.com. Park Avenue and 42nd Street, (718) 569-0594.

Talk on Terror (Wednesday) Matthew M. Aid, a historian, media commentator and author, will discuss his new book, “Intel Wars: The Secret History of the Fight Against Terror” (Bloomsbury). Mr. Aid, who is an expert on the National Security Agency, will speak about American intelligence operations around the world, including Iraq and Afghanistan. At 6:30 p.m., the Cooper Union, Menschel Boardroom, Seventh Street and Third Avenue, East Village, (212) 353-4195, cooper.edu; free.

‘The World of Duncan Phyfe: The Arts of New York, 1800-1847’ (Friday, Tuesday through Thursday) Cabinets made by Duncan Phyfe during his nearly 50-year career will be on display alongside pieces from his contemporaries, including Michael Allison and Thomas Constantine. The exhibition, which also has New York-made silver and porcelain works, will run through Feb. 17. Tuesdays through Fridays, 9:30 a.m. to 5:15 p.m.; Saturdays, 9:30 a.m. to 4:45 p.m.; closed Mondays; by appointment Sundays. Hirschl and Adler Galleries, 730 Fifth Avenue, (212) 535-8810, hirschlandadler.com; free.

Spoken Word

A Conversation with Stephen Sondheim and Tony Kushner (Tuesday) These prominent figures of the American theater, both Pulitzer Prize winners, will meet along with Rocco Landesman, the chairman of the National Endowment for the Arts, to discuss their lives in the theater at a forum sponsored by the Public Theater; all three took part in a similar forum in 2010. This discussion, at 8 p.m., will include a look at the Sondheim musicals covered in his memoir “Look, I Made a Hat” (Knopf), published in December, which includes “Sunday in the Park With George,” for which he (along with James Lapine) won the Pulitzer in 1985. Mr. Kushner won the Pulitzer for drama in 1993 for “Angels in America: Millennium Approaches”; Mr. Landesman was one of the play’s producers. The discussion is co-sponsored by the New York University Skirball Center. Skirball Center for the Performing Arts, 566 La Guardia Place, at Washington Square South, Greenwich Village, (212) 352-3101, publictheater.org; $35, or $30 for members.

‘I Like You, Maude’: StorySLAM (Tuesday) Those who find it hard to keep a story to themselves will find a rapt audience at this monthly gathering of experienced and novice storytellers. Lisa Lewis, a playwright and essayist, is the guest storyteller, but the microphone is open to all for stories of five minutes duration, beginning at 7:30 p.m. Tea Lounge, 837 Union Street, between Sixth and Seventh Avenues, Park Slope, Brooklyn, (718) 789-2762, tealoungeny.com; free.

Museum of Jewish Heritage: Holocaust Diary (Wednesday) David Koker, a Jewish college student, kept a diary of his life while interned in a concentration camp in the Netherlands in the mid-1940s. His diary was smuggled out of the camp, and while he did not survive his captivity, his story lived on in a book that was published in the Netherlands in 1977. An English translation of the book — “At the Edge of the Abyss: Concentration Camp Diary, 1943-1944” (Northwestern University Press), edited by Robert Jan van Pelt — is scheduled for publication in spring. At 7 p.m., Mr. van Pelt will discuss the book with David G. Marwell, a Holocaust scholar and director of the museum, who assisted in the prosecution of Nazi war criminals. 36 Battery Place, Lower Manhattan, (646) 437-4202, mjhnyc.org; $10, or $5 for members.

Walking Tours

Author Talk and Tour Through Green-Wood Cemetery (Saturday) Ben Feldman, a historian and author, will discuss the lives of two notorious New Yorkers who are the subjects of two of his books and who are both buried at Green-Wood Cemetery — Edward West Browning, a real estate tycoon of the early 1900s infamous for his underage lover, who is profiled in “Call Me Daddy”; and Dr. Harvey Burdell, a mid-19th-century dentist whose murder was never solved and who is the subject of “Butchery on Bond Street: Sexual Politics and the Burdell-Cunningham Case in Ante-Bellum New York”; both books were published by New York Wanderer Press with the Green-Wood Historic Fund. The talk by Mr. Feldman, which is free, begins at 1 p.m. in the Chapel, followed by a trolley tour through the cemetery. Reservations are recommended. Green-Wood Cemetery, Fifth Avenue and 25th Street, Greenwood Heights, Brooklyn, (718) 210-3080, green-wood.com/toursevents. While the talk is free, the trolley tour is $20, or $15 for members.

Grand Central Terminal Walking Tour (Wednesday) The historic terminal, which dates to 1913, is the subject of weekly tours on Wednesdays at 12:30 p.m. led by the Municipal Art Society, meeting at the information booth in the main concourse. Photo IDs are required: (212) 935-2075, mas.org/tours; $10.

Harlem Tours in Honor of Martin Luther King’s Birthday (Saturday through Monday) NYC Discovery Tours will lead a tour of historic sites like the Apollo Theater and the Lenox Lounge on Saturday and Sunday at 2 p.m.; the cost is $18, and the meeting place will be given with reservations: (212) 465-3331. On Monday a walking tour and video presentation offered by Harlem Heritage Tours will meet at 1 p.m. at the Harlem Heritage Tourism and Cultural Center, 104 Malcolm X Boulevard, near 115th Street. Tickets are $25; (212) 280-7888, harlemheritage.com. Also on Monday, the history, architecture and residents of the neighborhood will be covered on a tour sponsored by Big Onion Walking tours, meeting at 1 p.m. on the northwest corner of Lenox Avenue and 135th Street; the fee is $18, or $15 for students and 63+. (888) 606-9255, bigonion.com.

Jazz Tour of Bedford-Stuyvesant (Friday) A walk through this historic brownstone neighborhood in Brooklyn will be followed by a jazz concert with refreshments. The tour, sponsored by Metro Tour Service, meets at 6 p.m. in Brooklyn at 107 Macon Street, near Marcy Avenue. (718) 789-0430; $40.

Municipal Art Society: Bedford-Stuyvesant and ‘Edith Wharton’s New York’ (Saturday and Monday) On Saturday sites in Madison Square and Gramercy Park associated with Edith Wharton’s personal and literary lives will be covered on a tour meeting at 2 p.m. on the northwest corner of Fifth Avenue and 23rd Street. On Monday at 11 a.m., the noted residents and renaissance of Bedford-Stuyvesant are the focus of this Brooklyn tour, meeting on the southwest corner of Fulton Street and Franklin Avenue. (212) 935-3960, mas.org/tours; each tour is $15, or $10 for members.

Mural Walk (Wednesday and Thursday) A tour of Midtown murals and a discussion of the artists who created them will be led by Glenn Palmer-Smith, a mural painter, meeting at noon in the lobby of the AXA Equitable Building, 787 Seventh Avenue, at 51st Street, Manhattan, (917) 385-5462; $20.

‘Union Square: Crossroads of New York’ (Saturday) A tour focusing on the area’s political history meets at 2 p.m. by the statue of Lincoln in Union Square Park, near the 16th Street transverse. Sponsored by the Union Square Partnership, (212) 517-1826, unionsquarenyc.org; free. ANNE MANCUSO
