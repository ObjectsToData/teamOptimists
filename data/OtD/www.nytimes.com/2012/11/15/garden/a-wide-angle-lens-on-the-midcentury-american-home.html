A Wide-Angle Lens on the Midcentury American Home

Kevin Scanlon for The New York Times

Jennifer A. Watts, left, edited a book on Maynard L. Parker, a photographer of midcentury homes.

By ALEXANDRA LANGE
Published: November 14, 2012

The gate of the white picket fence on the cover of the new book “Maynard L. Parker: Modern Photography and the American Dream” (Yale, $65) is ajar, inviting you down a flagstone path. Blue and yellow director’s chairs are set out around a shaded table. The lady of the house hovers at your side; she’s just cut a few flowers.

These elements — casual welcome, outdoor living, brilliant color — were all hallmarks of the midcentury California photographer Maynard L. Parker’s signature style. Mr. Parker, who began his career shooting Hollywood interiors and designer jewels, translated his gift for creating a glamorous narrative into a 30-year career as a photographer of midcentury homes, primarily for House Beautiful. His modern Los Angeles was a very different place from that of his contemporary, Julius Shulman: the houses were smaller and filled with more stuff, the gardens were child-friendly, and the aesthetic, as described by Elizabeth Gordon, editor of House Beautiful from 1941 to 1964, could be labeled “The Station Wagon Way of Life.”

Jennifer A. Watts, photography curator at the Huntington Library, Art Collections and Botanical Gardens, edited the book and oversees the Huntington’s 58,000-image Parker archive. She spoke to a reporter last week by phone from San Marino, Calif.

How did you get involved with this project?

In 1995, Parker’s two daughters were getting ready to sell the Echo Park house that housed Parker’s studio. I went over there with another curator, and it was like walking into a time capsule from 1976. We were able to get a National Endowment for the Humanities grant and spent two years cataloging the collection. After that was done we could see it was much richer, deeper and more extensive than we realized, so we started casting about for excellent writers to contribute to a book.

I had never heard of Parker.

He existed in this nether world of people interested mostly in interior design. He was part of design circles with people like T. H. Robsjohn-Gibbings, Samuel Marx and more regionally specific architects. Scholars were tracking Parker down through shelter magazines.

What was his special talent?

Showcasing residential interiors for a new American way of living. He was a merchandiser, showing people what they should buy and how they should live. In the 1930s, he had been working for some of the top interior designers and in the design departments of stores like Barker Brothers. He developed a dramatic floodlit storytelling aesthetic Elizabeth Gordon definitely recognized.

Gordon used Parker for decades, and that seems to have been his major professional relationship. What was her agenda for the magazine?

Gordon was pushing back against the snob appeal of modernism as only for a select few people. She saw European modernism as sterile, problematic, not livable. She really believed Americans didn’t understand what good taste was and it was her responsibility to teach them through the pages of House Beautiful. I found a letter Maynard Parker’s wife had written to Gordon after his death. She wrote, “You were the only editor we ever worked with who had a dream.”

What were the style’s tenets?

What was going on in California was pretty distinctive. There was a sense of year-round indoor/outdoor living, relaxed entertaining. A classic Parker photograph shows people looking though windows outside with children in bare feet playing with their toys.

One of Parker’s strengths was that he was able to do a lot with camera angles and staging, often an elevated sweeping view down into a living area. It appeared people had just walked off. A magazine was open on the table. The way he would light and stage was reminiscent of what he was doing with celebrity homes.

Another strength was the way he used wide-angle lenses. Many of the houses they were showing were tiny, and he was able to give a sense of spaciousness. That’s why model home developers loved him. It was showmanship. He was great at that.

He also had a very symbiotic relationship, described in a Daniel P. Gregory essay, with the builder and developer Cliff May.

May realized early on the power of photography to sell his product, which was ranch-house living. He needed a very astute visual voice to do what he wanted to do. What I found fascinating was the machinations on the part of the developer, using his buildings, using his family, using every contact he could get. It is a way of lifting the veil on what goes on.

The book also has many behind-the-scenes shots, showing Parker at work. Where did you find those?

When I started writing I realized what was missing was how the sausage was made. Those photographs come from the family and from looking in other archives, like the May archive. My favorite is the one of Parker on a rooftop looking down at a model in a swimsuit with her dachshunds. I knew immediately where it was, the house of his friends Mr. and Mrs. Jack Moss overlooking the city in Pacific Palisades. I heard he would go to any extremes to get the shot, and here he was up on the roof getting his signature aerial shot onto the bathing beauty with the dachshunds. Fleeta Woodroffe, an editor of Better Homes and Gardens, is standing in the shadows in her outrageous hat and wool suit. It was a collaboration.

What’s your favorite image in the book?

One of the ones I was thrilled to find is of the Linda Vista shopping center in San Diego. I love this idea of a shopping center where you leave your kids outside unattended. It was an outdoor consumer paradise. Mothers are inside shopping while the kids are outside frolicking.
