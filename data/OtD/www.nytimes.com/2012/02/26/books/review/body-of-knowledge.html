Letter

Body of Knowledge

Published: February 24, 2012
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

To the Editor:

Elizabeth D. Samet’s essay on failed literary allusions (“Grand Allusion,” Feb. 5) was insightful and entertaining, but tracking down allusions on the Internet is only part of the problem. Anyone with a computer now has a second brain. The larger problem is, what do we need to know that can’t be figured out by turning to whatever search engine is at hand? What ideas, facts and history do we carry in our heads and in our souls?

Teaching should not be trivial pursuit, nor should the reading of poetry and prose be reduced to identifying allusions or even explaining what passages “mean.” In these days of multitasking and constant texting, the typical classroom is outdated. True education — as opposed to teaching students to do well on tests — needs to be re-examined closely.

That said, I’m frequently perplexed, startled and annoyed when confronted by a sea of blank faces after I ask college students a question whose answer cannot be looked up (“Where can we find that answer?” is the question of the day), or when I ask them to demonstrate some knowledge of the Civil War, or World War I, or World War II. Are those allusions for the next generation, or simply illusions?

LOUIS PHILLIPS
New York
The writer is a humanities professor at the School of Visual Arts.

A version of this letter appeared in print on February 26, 2012, on page BR6 of the Sunday Book Review with the headline: Body Of Knowledge.
