The Washington Wives’ Book Club

Illustrations by Raúl Colón, top, and Susan Arciero, above

Top, Jill Biden's "Don't Forget, God Bless Our Troops" will be published in June. Above, Callista Gingrich, the author of "Sweet Land of Liberty."

By PAMELA PAUL
Published: February 17, 2012
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

In times of partisan gridlock, economic woe and ­public-opinion doldrums, Hillary Clinton, Lynne Cheney and Laura Bush all wrote them. Callista Gingrich recently wrote one. And now Jill Biden, the wife of Vice President Joe Biden, is coming out with one.

Enlarge This Image

Carole Geithner's "If Only," appearing in March, is a novel about grief and loss.

Could children’s books be the wifely calming salve for this election cycle’s blistering political environment?

Last month, Simon & Schuster announced that it would publish Dr. Biden’s first children’s book, “Don’t Forget, God Bless Our Troops,” illustrated by Raúl Colón, in June. “Drawing on my experience with my own grandchildren as well as my visits with military families around the country,” Dr. Biden said in an e-mail interview, “I thought that a children’s book would be the best way to reach children and their parents to help them better understand the impact of having a parent deployed.”

Her share of profits from the book will go to charities that support military families. The publisher, too, will offer a cut to charity, though of course it still stands to profit — because children’s books by prominent Washington figures and members of their extended families sell. Since its publication in September, Mrs. Gingrich’s book, “Sweet Land of Liberty” (whose title comes from the same patriotic hymn as one of Barack Obama’s), has sold more than 75,000 copies, according to Regnery, the publisher. Caroline Kennedy’s anthologies — the latest of which, “Poems to Learn by Heart,” will be published this October — routinely hit the best-seller list.

In a publishing landscape battered by competition from assorted screen media, children’s literature, especially in the realm of picture books, has been a holdout against electronic distractions. E-books account for only an estimated 5 percent of total sales of books for children under 8, compared with 25 percent for some categories of adult book sales. Published in 2010, Obama’s picture book, “Of Thee I Sing: A Letter to My Daughters,” sold more than 400,000 copies, according to the publisher.

“We’re going through a very intense political time, and that’s why you’re seeing a lot of children’s books coming out of Washington,” said Kate Jackson, editor in chief of HarperCollins Children’s Books, who has edited offerings from Laura and Jenna Bush. “Picture books and books for tweens are always a great way to put complex issues like politics into a context that young children can understand,” she said. “They get the conversation going.”

For Washington wives, writing a children’s book has become almost an expected spousal counterpart to the politician’s campaign tract or argument book. “Spouses have one mandatory obligation — ‘First Do No Harm’ — and one optional assignment: provide a positive magnifying force,” Mary Matalin, editor at large for Threshold Editions, a division of Simon & Schuster, and a former member of Dick Cheney’s staff, wrote in an e-mail. “Children’s books fulfill both.”

After all, what greater, brighter, cleaner route to a voter’s heart than through the influence of his or her children — our nation’s future! — and how better to endear a politician to that voting parent than through a treasured reading experience? George W. Bush’s fiasco with “The Pet Goat” aside (that story may forever be associated with President Bush’s astonished mien as he continued to read to a grade-school class he was visiting in Florida after being told about the attack on the World Trade Center), children’s literature is generally considered a powerful and positive force in the grinding political P.R. machine.

Reaching children can also serve a more direct political purpose. When Theodore H. White was gathering material for his book “The Making of the President, 1960,” he accompanied one Democratic contender, John F. Kennedy, on a long day driving around the far reaches of a desolate district in Wisconsin. Some 1,200 of the 1,600 people Kennedy interacted with were children, all well below voting age. What, White wanted to know, was the point? Kennedy made it very clear: “It was important to talk to schoolchildren because schoolchildren talked to their parents when they went home,” White wrote. In terms of campaigning, visiting children was better, in its way, than pinching the cheeks of babies.
