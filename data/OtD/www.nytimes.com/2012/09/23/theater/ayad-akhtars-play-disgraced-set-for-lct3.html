A Writer’s Unusual Rituals Yield Results

Chester Higgins Jr./The New York Times

Ayad Akhtar at home.

By ALEXIS SOLOSKI
Published: September 20, 2012

AYAD AKHTAR protects his furniture. On a late summer afternoon in his book-crowded Upper Manhattan home office he gently but firmly dissuaded his agent, a photographer and me from sitting in a particular armchair. It doesn’t seem antique or especially delicate, yet the chair plays an essential role in Mr. Akhtar’s writing process.

When creating a piece of fiction he imagines one of his characters seated there. Once the chair feels fully endowed with that character, Mr. Akhtar said, he sits in it himself, finding there “many of my most unexpected revelations.”

Odd as it seems, the method must work, as it contributed to Mr. Akhtar’s debut novel, “American Dervish,” about a Muslim boy’s coming of age in a Wisconsin town. Adam Langer praised it in The New York Times as “self-assured and effortlessly told.”

Mr. Akhtar also applied this shamanism via interior design to his first play, “Disgraced,” which had its debut in January at the American Theater Company in Chicago and will begin a New York run at LCT3’s Claire Tow Theater on Oct. 7, with a revised script and a fresh cast, including Aasif Mandvi from “The Daily Show.”

“Disgraced” centers on Amir (Mr. Mandvi), a Pakistani-American lawyer who has altered his last name to disguise his Muslim heritage. A fierce opponent of Islam, Amir must confront his prejudices, his fears and the deep-seated remnants of his upbringing during a dinner party that begins uneasily and ends disastrously.

Kimberly Senior, who directed the play in Chicago and will direct the New York production, said when she first read the script, “it got me really excited and a little frightened.” Chris Jones in The Chicago Tribune described the play as an arresting drama that “builds all the way up to a howl of passionate rage.”

Passionate rage doesn’t seem to afflict Mr. Akhtar, 41, who has a gentle appearance and a ready smile. Both intensely present and vaguely abstracted, much in the manner of the humanities professor his parents once thought he might become, he often speaks in a manner he terms “highfalutin .” But this doesn’t inhibit him from citing a “Brady Bunch” episode as a formative aesthetic experience.

Compulsively genial, he seems a world away from the furious, confused men he so often describes, like Amir or Naveed, the antireligion father in “American Dervish,” or Hassan, the conflicted terrorist of “The War Within,” the independent film Mr. Akhtar wrote with two Columbia film school friends and in which he stars. These men seem to share his background, though not his temperament or habits, which tend, he said, toward the “semi-monastic.”

He writes from 9 in the morning to 2 in the afternoon and again from 8:30 to 11 every night, meditating for at least an hour as well. His friend Mr. Mandvi said Mr. Akhtar practices another unusual writerly ritual, taking frequent baths, up to six a day, when a literary problem presents itself. Asked if this schedule permits him any time to socialize or date, Mr. Akhtar appeared momentarily flustered. “I feel like there’s been this explosion of creativity in my life,” he said, “and I am just very, very grateful for it, but I feel very responsible to it.”

Like Hayat, the hero of “American Dervish,” Mr. Akhtar grew up in Wisconsin, the child of Pakistani immigrants. Though his household was largely secular, Mr. Akhtar had a period of devoutness, though it fell away in late adolescence when he became troubled by what he saw as contradictions in the Koran. At the same time a watershed world literature class oriented him toward writing. He didn’t pursue theater initially, he said, as “it was the uncool thing to do, and I was very obsessed with being cool.”

He enrolled in a creative writing seminar at the University of Rochester but was so unsettled by the lavish praise his first short story received that he dropped out of college and lapsed into depression, which a role in a friend’s production of David Mamet’s “Sexual Perversity in Chicago” helped to alleviate. “I found that it got me out of myself in such a healthy way,” he said.

He went back to school, this time to Brown, where he produced, directed and acted in various plays: “The Winter’s Tale,” Genet’s “Maids,” more Mamet. After graduation he spent several years in Europe, studying with the director Jerzy Grotowski and leading workshops in Germany, France and Italy.

Eventually he returned to New York, enrolling in Columbia’s film school, where he wrote and helped write nearly a dozen screenplays, one of which become “The War Within.” Newly confident in his writing, he took up prose again and then turned his attention to plays. He has already followed up “Disgraced” with “The Invisible Hand,” a thriller about Islamic terrorism and monetary policy slated for Artists Repertory Theater in Portland, Ore., in the spring.

Mr. Akhtar first contemplated “Disgraced” when he saw his friend Firdous Bamji in a reading as Hamlet, the kind of role rarely afforded South Asians in America. He decided to write a part “that gives a Pakistani- or Indian-American actor a chance to express this kind of range — rage and charm and intelligence and vulnerability.” So he wrote the role of Amir. Mr. Mandvi, who plays the part, said, “In the theater last time I got to do a role like that, I had to write it myself.”

“Disgraced” also drew on a dinner party Mr. Akhtar and his former wife once gave, in which a discussion of Islam created tensions in the room, even among his close friends. “Their perception of me, in some subtle and not-so-subtle ways, shifted over the course of just an evening,” he said.

A naturalistic play, “Disgraced” is meticulously modeled on Aristotelian tragedy, in which a mostly good character (Amir) is brought low by an unfortunate action, in this case his too vigorous rejection of his inheritance. The dialogue is brisk, the structure tidy, the characters laudably complex. Mr. Akhtar acknowledged that in earlier drafts he had expressed the play’s themes (identity, self-loathing, bias) somewhat blatantly, but he said he hoped new drafts had softened this.

With anti-Islamic prejudice a continuing problem, it seems provocative to offer a character with a Muslim upbringing who reveals vestigial racism, anti-Semitism and a twinge of pride regarding terrorist attacks. But the director, Ms. Senior, cautioned: “The reality is that Amir is not representative of a faith. He’s representative of a man.” At various points the play celebrates, defends, probes and, yes, derides Islam, as when Amir calls the Koran “one very long hate-mail letter to humanity.”

Because of his portrayals of Muslim Americans, Mr. Akhtar has received occasional hate mail, though fan mail arrives far more frequently. He actively rejects the notion that his fictions endorse any particular attitude or argument regarding religion or identity. What matters in the scripts, screenplays and novels is that “the work be deeply engaging and alive and vibrant,” he said. With the aid of his supple intelligence, his deep sympathy, his dedicated work ethic and the occasional cloth-backed chair, “I’m trying to tell the best possible story I can.”
