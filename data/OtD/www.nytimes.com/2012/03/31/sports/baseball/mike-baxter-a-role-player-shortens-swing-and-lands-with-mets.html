Role Player Tailors Abilities to Fit Mets’ Needs

Barton Silverman/The New York Times

Mike Baxter, right, will be part of a major league team’s opening day roster for the first time at age 27.

By ANDREW KEH
Published: March 30, 2012

PORT ST. LUCIE, Fla. — The Temple of Apollo at Delphi had inscribed on its portico an aphorism that went tragically unheeded by numerous figures in Greek mythology: Know thyself.

As a student at Columbia, Mike Baxter should have encountered this maxim while reading the plays of Sophocles in Literature Humanities, a freshman seminar in the college’s core curriculum.

His actions this winter at the least suggest a familiarity with the concept.

After being nontendered by the Mets in December, Baxter, 27, returned to the team a few days later on a minor league contract, and, from there, engaged in a round of pragmatic self-evaluation.

His aim, like all ballplayers, was to be a regular contributor to a major league team. He took an honest measure of his capacities, set reasonable objectives and recalibrated his preparations to fit these realities.

“The more time I got in the big leagues, the more I came to realize my role was as a person coming off the bench,” Baxter said. “That’s something that I began actively tailoring my game toward.”

Baxter is set to reap the benefits of his self-awareness.

Before a Grapefruit League game Thursday night, Baxter learned that he would be on a major league team’s opening day roster for the first time in his career. Manager Terry Collins pulled him aside, offered his congratulations, and said he would be the Mets’ primary left-handed bat off the bench, barring unforeseen free-agent signings.

“So, hopefully, there aren’t any unforeseen signings,” Baxter said with a smile Friday morning.

The Mets came to their decision after several weeks of evaluating Baxter and Adam Loewen, the other candidate for the job. Baxter, who has gone 14 for 43 this spring, performed better than Loewen, who went 11 for 43 but struck out 20 times before he was reassigned to the minor league camp Thursday.

But more than statistics, Collins said Baxter was picked based on what coaches and officials had observed in his approach at the plate.

“The decision had to do with what we saw in his swing,” Collins said. “With Mike, his swing is a little more compact, and that’s what we needed.”

What the Mets observed was no coincidence. As Baxter trained this winter on the campus of Vanderbilt University, where he transferred after a year at Columbia, he worked primarily on shortening his stroke.

His logic was that, coming off the bench, his emphasis should be on putting the ball in play and taking advantage of the few looks he would get on a given day.

“I’m making sure I have that short swing, so when I get in I can capitalize on fastballs early in the count or whenever I get my pitch,” Baxter said.

Baxter said he knew his stock would improve with more versatility in the field. To that end, he spent significant time fielding fly balls at the three outfield positions and taking grounders at first and third base.

The extra work helped Baxter win a job, but he knows occupational security in baseball is, for most, fleeting. And Collins said the Mets were basically “taking a guess” with Baxter, as he had no experience in a long-term pinch-hitting role.

But Baxter said he pushed on because he knew he could help a team, even if it was as a role player.

After six years in the minors, Baxter made his major league debut for the San Diego Padres in September 2010. He took inspiration from conversations he had with Dave Roberts, a coach with the Padres.

Roberts did not break in to the major leagues as a player until he was 27, but he went on to earn a World Series ring with the Boston Red Sox in 2004.

“I’ve been doing this long enough now that I know some guys develop early and some guys develop late and some guys don’t get a chance till late,” Baxter said. “I kind of put my trust and faith into that idea, and I thought, ‘When I get my chance, I’m going to maximize it.’ ”

This spring, Baxter got his chance and grasped it, because he knew what he needed to do — and because he knew himself.
