The Class Multiplies, but the Math Divides

 By SAMUEL G. FREEDMAN
Published: October 20, 2004

SSINING, N.Y.

AS Anita Dempsey began math period for her class of fourth graders at Claremont School one recent afternoon, she instructed the children to open their workbooks to a page with a grid of integers from 1 to 100. She reminded the boys and girls what they had learned last year, about how to count by twos, and she told them such numbers were called multiples of two. When she asked for examples, the children called out 86, 28, 38, 54.

Ms. Dempsey circled all those numbers on her own chart, which was being projected onto the blackboard. Now, she said, everyone in the class should color in all the multiples of two on his or her page. The students uncapped their yellow markers and set about filling in the appropriate boxes, noting the patterns they formed.

"Wonderful," Ms. Dempsey said, looking over one child's completed worksheet. "Just awesome."

At one particular desk, though, Jimmy was solving a different problem. He had just transferred to Claremont from a nearby Catholic school, and during the lesson he had whispered to an educator who happened to be visiting the room, "I know all my facts," by which he meant his multiplication tables.

So that educator, Ferzeen Bhana, the math coordinator for Ossining's elementary schools, gave him a problem to try: 23 times 16. Within a minute, Jimmy delivered 368, the correct answer. Ms. Bhana asked him how he had gotten it. Jimmy offered her a shy, yearning face and said nothing.

That brief moment, one moment in one school in one middle-income town, described the divide of the math wars in America. It was evident to Ms. Bhana that Jimmy had learned multiplication the old-fashioned way, with drills, algorithms and concepts like place-value. The rest of the students were using a curriculum called Investigations, one of the new constructivist models, which teaches reasoning out a solution.

In the conflict of tradition versus modernism in math, a conflict of dueling perceptions as irreconcilable as those of whites and blacks viewing the O. J. Simpson trial, what was the truth?

Was the truth that Jimmy was easily answering a problem more difficult than his classmates would even attempt for months? Or was the truth that he was regurgitating a factoid drummed into him during hours of rote repetition?

The battle is not an especially fair one these days. For the last 15 years, public school districts across the country, including New York's, have been adopting and embracing constructivist math, so named because proponents say students learn best when they construct their own knowledge. Such curriculums have been endorsed by the National Council of Teachers of Mathematics and the National Science Foundation and propounded by the colleges and graduate schools of education.

College and university professors of mathematics and various sciences have stood against this new orthodoxy. Some 200 formally protested against constructivist math to Richard W. Riley, the federal secretary of education in the Clinton administration.

In New York City, a commission on math education led by Matthew Goldstein, chancellor of the City University of New York, condemned the fashionable curriculums as being incapable of preparing students "to become scientists, engineers, mathematicians, computer scientists, physicians and educators of mathematics."

The story of how Investigations came to Ossining, then, and how it has fared, offers a case history of how the constructivists are winning. With a student body that is roughly half white and an income range centered in working and middle classes, this amalgam of factory town and commuter suburb 30 miles up the Hudson River from Manhattan could be Anydistrict, U.S.A.

Ossining went hunting for a different math curriculum for its elementary school students three years ago for entirely logical reasons. Teachers were using their personal variations of the material. Many felt themselves and their students bored by the traditional approach. The district's score on state tests, adequate in fourth grade, slumped as children grew older, raising doubts about how many would be able to reach newer, more stringent standards for graduating from high school.
