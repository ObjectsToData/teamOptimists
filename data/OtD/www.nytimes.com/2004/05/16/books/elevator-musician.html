Elevator Musician

By Noah Isenberg
Published: May 16, 2004

THE CELLO PLAYER

By Michael Krüger.

Translated by Andrew Shields.

200 pp. New York:

Harcourt. $23.

''Some people can do anything, others next to nothing.'' This formulaic if truthful statement serves the first-person narrator of Michael Krüger's slim, elegant novel, ''The Cello Player,'' as a means of understanding the history of music. It also helps explain his life story. A onetime musical wunderkind and reformed 1960's idealist, he's now a successful composer of ''terrible theme songs'' for German television. In exchange for his wealth, he faces chronic disappointment in his efforts to complete his masterwork, an opera dedicated to the great Russian poet Osip Mandelstam -- an elusive grail he spends his spare time trying to capture.

Though fleetingly called ''György,'' the narrator remains essentially nameless throughout, a member of that anonymous clan of artists at midlife whose worst fear is that they will be overlooked by history's merciless eye. Despite his material gains, and the fact that he can

hear his TV melodies whistled on the street or played in the supermarket, he perceives himself largely as ''a man overcome by the demon of failure.'' Twice married, twice divorced, he enjoys but a single hour, between 6 and 7 in the morning, when he feels artistically free and existentially at peace. Otherwise, he realizes he leads a life of ''frightening regularity,'' the mundane, ordered existence of a Munich burgher.

These seemingly immovable strictures are what make the sudden arrival of Judit, a 22-year old cellist from Budapest who ''slipped into his life,'' so unsettling. He never quite surmises her true motive; she appears under the pretense of continuing her musical education, but to his mind it's as if she had been sent as an emissary from his past. Not only does she bear an uncanny resemblance to her mother, one of his former lovers and a persistent focus of many of his wistful reflections, but she single-handedly upends his life. She eventually takes over his entire apartment, which he pronounces ''Judit's empire.'' A band of relatives from Hungary soon follows to join in celebrating her birthday, and the narrator finds himself nearly driven from his own home.

He puts up with this mayhem because Judit is for him a kind of Medea figure, an explosive source of both spiritual enrichment and antagonism. She challenges his aesthetic assumptions and his career compromises, reminding him of the dynamic he once shared with her mother -- even suggesting a faint possibility that she could be their daughter -- and stripping him

of the sense of stable independence he once enjoyed.

The pair's extended stay at his summer house in southern France, described in the book's final chapters, turns out to be the ultimate test. In a face-off of sorts, Judit effects a total makeover, changing his decades-old décor, relandscaping his garden and banishing his favorite dishes. Most significantly, she mocks the tired ambitions of a man who once composed string quartets interwoven with poems by Anna Akhmatova but ends up dashing off kitsch for the culture industry. ''You should have written a Mandelstam opera 20 years ago, when you met my mother,'' she snaps. ''That would've been revolutionary. But today? Today it's too easy to put something on the Nuremberg Opera stage to mourn a poet murdered by Stalin.''

An accomplished poet, writer and publisher who came of age in the 60's, Krüger is especially adept at depicting the narrator's memories of his student days and the uneven path his life took in that era, perhaps a partial explanation of his desire for order in his later years. Although at the time he retreated from Berlin (''too much revolution, too few string quartets''), Budapest remains a key in his life. As he puts it, wryly, ''Others went to India to discover the narrow corner of their soul; I had gone to Budapest.'' As a young man, equipped with a handsome grant from the Bavarian minister of culture, he embarked on a fateful journey to a city he thought of as ''salvation'' and gained instead an irreversible entanglement with Maria, Judit's mother, and her family. Despite explicit warnings of the family's collusion with the Hungarian secret police, and the threat that only trouble will result from maintaining close ties, he remains drawn to them even after his breakup with Maria. When Judit arrives on his doorstep and shatters his world, the gloomy forecast proves accurate.

Postwar German literature is not long on humor -- at least, it is not often thought of as a place to which readers turn for a good laugh. Krüger defies this expectation, as he has done in previous novels, offering up caustic observations and many hilarious episodes. In one incident, the narrator meets with a famous Italian writer and translator of Mandelstam, who is visiting Munich. He hopes to enlist his support in the opera project, but instead he finds himself aiding the helpless man in an emergency underwear purchase. Later, musing on Judit's family visit, he relates the story of her Uncle Sandor, an unhinged former Marxist who exchanges his ''collapsed world view'' for an insatiable appetite for German cable television. In a jab at the tedium of academic discourse, a deadly lecture in Budapest, on ''The High Tone in Contemporary German Poetry,'' is given by a professor from the ''College of Retail Sales in Clausthal-Zellerfeld.''

Meticulously rendered by Andrew Shields -- who captures much of the original idiom, cadence and narrative flow of Krüger's lively German prose -- the novel preserves a strong European flavor in English translation. Owing to locations evoked, and to Krüger's narrative style, ''The Cello Player'' has perhaps less in common with other postwar German fiction than with the writing of Milan Kundera or Vladimir Nabokov, both of whose romantic exposition and fine ironic touches resonate here.

Packed into this small, powerful novel is a dazzling array of well-chiseled, colorful characters, extracted from the grand tableau of history and from the author's imagination. If his narrator's dictum on music history holds any water -- even if it doesn't -- Krüger lands on the side of those who can do anything.
