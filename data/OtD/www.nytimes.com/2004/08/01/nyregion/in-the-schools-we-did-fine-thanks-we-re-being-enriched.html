IN THE SCHOOLS; We Did Fine, Thanks. We're Being Enriched.

By MERRI ROSENBERG
Published: August 1, 2004

THERE is no homework. A teacher may read aloud from a novel in an English class. Students may develop pie charts in a math class or hone writing skills on a computer program.

Yes, it's summer school. But, no, it's not remedial.

Middle School Summer Academies in the New Rochelle district are providing about 246 students with a chance to raise their level of achievement.

''These are students who need a little more enrichment,'' said Dr. Diane Massimo, assistant superintendent of secondary education for the New Rochelle school district. ''The academies are not for children who have failed. They have been identified as needing more support in literacy and math. It's a skills-building enrichment academy in literacy, math and computer technology. There are no drill sheets, no work books, no ditto sheets.''

And the approach seems to be working.

In an English class at the summer academy at Albert Leonard Middle School, 10-year-old Jade Lee couldn't contain herself. Eagerly raising her hand to answer a question about a book the class was discussing, Jade couldn't wait for her teacher, Mary Breslin, to call on her.

Such intensity would be enviable during the school year. In a summer school program aimed at students who educators feel could benefit from additional academic support before starting the next school year, it's particularly striking.

Class sizes in the academies are kept small, ranging from 9 to 13 students in a given subject area. Classes are offered in areas like literacy, which includes reading, writing and listening; computer technology; and math. All students must read one required book during the four-week session, as well as another book of their own choosing. They also produce a portfolio of four writing assignments. Academy attendance is not mandatory, and students, based on their classroom performance, are recommended for the program by their teachers.

New Rochelle does provide an evening program at New Rochelle High School for students who need remedial help in material covered in seventh and eighth grades.

Dr. Marshall George, director of the Albert Leonard summer academy and humanities chairman at the school, said of the academy's program: ''It's not test driven. The goal is to raise the level of performance of all students.''

New Rochelle runs a comparable program, with some modifications at Isaac E. Young, the district's other middle school. New this year is a program for incoming sixth graders, to ease their transition to the middle school.

''For the entering sixth graders, it's also an orientation process, helping them feel comfortable,'' Dr. Massimo said. ''One of our objectives is to improve their attitude. We emphasize transitions and give them a sense of community.''

The program, which costs about $90,000 for both schools, is financed by grants, and is not part of the taxpayer-financed school district operating budget. The amount covers staff salaries, program materials and supplies, bus transportation and snacks.

For Kristen Walsh, who taught math during the summer session, it was a chance to get students to feel comfortable with the class from the beginning.

''At the first lesson, when they came in, I had them get out their sentiments about math,'' said Ms. Walsh, an eighth grade social studies teacher. ''I said it was O.K. not to like it. By the end, students were coming up and saying, 'Can I work on my graph?' I let them create their own survey based on their interest.''

For 12-year-old Jason Disisto, who will enter seventh grade in September, attending the summer academy has made a difference.

''In regular school, there are lots of kids and teachers don't have time to help,'' he said. ''Here, there are only nine students. The teachers have more time to talk to you and help you be successful.''

Expectations change for teachers, too.

''I didn't know what to expect,'' said Ronni Black, a sixth grade science teacher who taught math in the summer academy in New Rochelle. ''As a teacher, when you see blank stares, you die. I was doing fractions with a group, and they understood. You could see the light bulb go on.''
