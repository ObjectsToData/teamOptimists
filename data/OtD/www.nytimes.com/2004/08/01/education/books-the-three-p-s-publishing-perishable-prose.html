Books; The Three P's: Publishing Perishable Prose

By Lindsay Waters
Published: August 1, 2004

Enemies of Promise: Publishing and the Eclipse of Scholarship

Prickly Paradigm Press, paperback

Russell Jacoby's book Reviling Utopia, ''Revising Utopia'' will be published next year (Columbia University Press).

Anemona Hartocollis is a Times reporter and author of ''Seven Days of Possibilities: One Teacher, 24 Kids, and the Music That Changed Their Lives Forever'' (PublicAffairs).

Russell Jacoby

When an oil company announces that a tanker is missing, it is probably too late for the crew on board. When Lindsay Waters, an executive editor at Harvard University Press, announces that university presses are sinking, is it too late for us professors and writers?

Mr. Waters has written a polemical pamphlet for a new polemical pamphlet series, Prickly Paradigm, itself the successor to a British series, Prickly Pear. Both series reflect the orientation of the founding editors, who are anthropologists, but the American series also seeks to publish ''challenging and sometimes outrageous pamphlets'' on various academic issues.

With great vigor, ''Enemies of Promise'' takes on the commercialization of the university generally and publishing specifically. In the latter domain, Mr. Waters scores the greatest hits: He believes the country's professors of humanities have timidly abdicated their obligation to judge scholarly work. Instead they have outsourced that decision to university presses. The presses, pushed by faculties and compliant referees, crank out mountains of unreadable prose. ''Publication for its own sake'' buries real thinking and perhaps even original writing. The diagnosis is grim. Bureaucratic conformity rules the university; the fire of new ideas burns low.

Anyone who attacks academic conformity has my vote, but Mr. Waters's polemic is contradictory and often too tame. How can commercialization be the curse and unsellable books the consequence? The cure for the latter might be the former, real commercialization of academic publishing -- an idea advanced by conservatives like Richard Posner. Moreover, Mr. Waters remains safely vague in his broadside. What monographs, series or intellectual movements exemplify the new timidity? He does not say. He criticizes specifically only Stanley Fish, the literary scholar and dean, for a cynical antitheoretical attitude -- and conveniently plumps Harvard University Press for its boldness in publishing Bruno Latour, the noted Continental theorist.

That brings us to Mr. Waters's embrace of ''theory.'' He laments the decline of ''big ideas and grand narratives'' as part and parcel of the intellectual acquiescence. Fine. But as a publisher and scholar Mr. Waters himself has long been associated with postmodern theorists like Paul de Man, who pioneered opaque irresponsibility and who dumped the narrative as simplistic. The tidal wave of unreadable faux-theoretical monographs derives partly from publishers like Mr. Waters.

Perhaps the title of the next Prickly polemic should be ''The New Scholasticism: How Postmodern Theorists and University Presses Conspired to Render Knowledge Opaque, Irrelevant and Conformist.''

The Red Pencil: Convictions From

Experience in Education

By Theodore R. Sizer

Yale University Press, hardcover

Keeping School: Letters to Families From Principals of Two Small Schools

By Deborah Meier, Theodore R. Sizer

and Nancy Faust Sizer

Beacon Press, hardcover

Review by

Anemona Hartocollis

Theodore Sizer and Deborah Meier are among the secular saints of progressive education, though as with all saints, they have their share of detractors both within and outside their particular educational faith. But in an age when dissent is sometimes devalued, one of their most admirable qualities may be their determination to continue being provocative.

In ''The Red Pencil,'' Mr. Sizer, author of the influential ''Horace's Compromise,'' uses anecdotes about his encounters with other thinkers, from Daniel Patrick Moynihan to Jacques Barzun to Mortimer J. Adler, to reflect on the shortcomings and potential of public education. Talking about what he calls the ''Paideia'' books -- a kind of call to arms drafted by Mr. Adler that distills a series of forums on school reform -- Mr. Sizer says: ''It was a provocation, not a road map.'' This seems a reasonably good description of Mr. Sizer's own goals as theorist, practitioner and essayist.

Mr. Sizer joins with his wife, Nancy Faust Sizer, and Ms. Meier to speak more directly to parents in ''Keeping School.'' The book compiles newsletters that they sent home as the principals of, in the Sizers' case, the Parker Charter School in the wealthy exurb of Harvard, Mass., and in Ms. Meier's case, the Mission Hill School, a public elementary school in Boston. Ms. Meier's advice ranges from tips she has shared at parent conferences to criticism of lazy homework assignments.

The Sizers often talk about education through the prism of their own family life. This book is a potent reminder of how much school culture is a matter of personal chemistry, rather than distant policy making.
