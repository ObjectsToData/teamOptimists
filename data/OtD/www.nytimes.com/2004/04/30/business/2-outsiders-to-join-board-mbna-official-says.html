2 Outsiders to Join Board, MBNA Official Says

By PATRICK McGEEHAN
Published: April 30, 2004

Racing to head off a showdown with some big shareholders, the MBNA Corporation has chosen two new directors who have no financial or family ties to the company, according to the company's general counsel.

MBNA, the second-biggest issuer of credit cards in America behind Citigroup, is expected to announce before its annual shareholders meeting on Monday that it has appointed Mary M. Boies and Laura S. Unger to its board. Ms. Boies, 53, is an antitrust lawyer and the wife of David Boies, who represented the Justice Department in its antitrust suit against Microsoft. Ms. Unger, 43, former acting chairwoman of the Securities and Exchange Commission, is a director of the Ambac Financial Group and Borland Software.

They would succeed two directors who are stepping down, Norma Lerner and Michael Rosenthal. Mrs. Lerner is the widow of MBNA's former chairman, Alfred Lerner, and the mother of Randolph Lerner, who succeeded his father as chairman in late 2002. Mr. Rosenthal is a humanities professor at Columbia University.

Louis J. Freeh, MBNA's general counsel and the former director of the F.B.I., said he expected that Ms. Boies and Ms. Unger would be ''very well received and very favorably received'' by shareholders. ''They are really the ideal outside independent directors,'' he said.

Calls left for both Ms. Boies and Ms. Unger were not returned yesterday.

MBNA has been under fire from managers of large pension funds for being unresponsive to shareholders and for having too few directors who are independent of the company or the Lerner family. It is not clear whether the new appointments will appease the company's critics.

TIAA-CREF, which manages retirement funds for teachers, has been rallying support for its proposal that the majority of MBNA's directors be independent of company management. This week, two large California pension funds said that they would vote against MBNA's entire board and that they considered four of the company's seven directors who are standing for re-election to be insiders.

The California Public Employees Retirement System, known as Calpers, and the California State Teachers Retirement System, known as Calstrs, said they would not vote for any of the seven nominees because MBNA had ignored a proposal that was favored by a majority of its shareholders last year. That proposal called for the company to start counting the value of stock options it gave to employees as an expense.

Mr. Freeh said that MBNA has agreed to start treating stock options as an expense in 2005.

Calpers has shaken up what traditionally had been a perfunctory process of electing corporate directors by withholding votes for a variety of reasons. Indeed, Calpers said it would withhold its votes for Ms. Unger as a director of Ambac because she is a member of a committee that approved paying Ambac's auditors to provide other services to the company.

Contrary to MBNA's view, Calpers and Calstrs said that two of MBNA's longtime directors, Benjamin Civiletti and James Berick, are not independent because they worked for law firms with ties to MBNA.

Mr. Civiletti, a former attorney general of the United States, is chairman of Venable L.L.P., a firm in Washington that provides legal services to MBNA. Mr. Berick is a retired partner in Squire Sanders & Dempsey, a law firm in Cleveland that does work for the company. Those associations disqualify the directors as independent according to the pension funds' standards, though not by the standards of the New York Stock Exchange.

Calpers said it would not vote any of its 7.9 million shares for the company's nominees, including Randolph Lerner and the chief executive, Bruce Hammonds. Calstrs said it had voted its 4.2 million shares against the entire slate.

Mr. Berick is Mr. Lerner's godfather and the Lerner family's lawyer and has a son who is a partner in Squire Sanders & Dempsey.
