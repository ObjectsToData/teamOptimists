A Life Knocked Off Balance by National Acclaim

 By ROBIN FINN
Published: November 30, 2004


Marvi Lacar for The New York Times
'As grateful as I am for the award, I have to admit it's slightly destabilizing,' says author Lily Tuck.

Tuck, Lily


New York City

HERE are noisy renovations in progress at the novelist Lily Tuck's well-appointed Madison Avenue apartment, a setting as elegant and genteel but, at least temporarily, as discombobulated as she is. Winning the National Book Award for fiction has thrown the silver-haired Ms. Tuck, 66, into a spasm of dislocation worthy of one of her fictional heroines. Through four novels and a collection of short stories, she has specialized in what happens, or does not, to a woman's powers of reinvention when she finds herself marooned in a strange or exotic environment. Usually, strange men figure prominently.

At the moment, Ms. Tuck, a widow, is specializing in trying to keep her cool while her literary reputation goes through its own series of renovations.

Receiving this honor - in her view, the next best thing to a Pulitzer - for her fourth novel, "The News From Paraguay," has been intoxicating (she says 65,000 paperback copies are being printed, a career high) and distracting (no time to work). Debilitating, too: it was not kind of Thomas McGuane, a writer she respects, or used to, to complain in The New Yorker that the award is "apparently tanking." And Mr. McGuane had company. Critics disparaged the work of all five finalists as lacking "greatness" and "commercial appeal." Vexing.

The germination of her novel, an epic set in the 19th century, was painful for other reasons: "So many bad things happened while I was trying to write this book," she explains. "First my editor died, then came 9/11, and finally the most personally traumatic ordeal for me, my husband died." They were just shy of celebrating their 25th anniversary in what had been, she says, a completely felicitous second marriage, a merger that had created a household with six children and demanded that she rent a separate studio for writing.

Ms. Tuck is candid about her career. Thanks to her husbands' income, she never had to write to support herself. She paraphrases Don DeLillo about why she writes: "He says he writes in order to know what he's thinking, and I think I'm the same way."

But she has not written a coherent sentence in more than a month, not since "The News From Paraguay" surprised her by bucking its lackluster reviews and making the short list of finalists from a field of 272 submissions that contained works by household names like Roth, Oates and Ozick.

So award season is posing a case of identity withdrawal for a writer obsessed with her craft. Writing, she says, is a daily chore, same as brushing her teeth. Not to do it seems unhealthy. She wrote her first attempt at a novel at 10; the main character was a horse. It was unpublished, as was virtually everything else she wrote until "Siam, or the Woman Who Shot a Man," earned her a $3,000 advance (she splurged on a gold-and-ruby Schlumberger ring from Tiffany) and a PEN/Faulkner nomination in 1991.

"I think most writers are obsessive about their work," Ms. Tuck says quietly, as she seems to say everything. "I think you almost have to be; otherwise, it's just too hard."

In her red-walled study, the decorations now include a National Book Award medal dangling from a reading lamp and a heavy bronze sculpture in the quaint shape of a rolled-up manuscript. There is also a $10,000 winner's check. Perhaps it will finance her trip to the American Academy, an arts and humanities center in Rome, in January to research her next book, an imagined biography set in Italy, where her filmmaker father lived after he and her mother split.

Since winning the award two weeks ago in a category that ignited an inner-industry tempest because its nominees were five relatively obscure, relatively noncommercial women from New York, her daily routine - a little yoga and a lot of writing - has disappeared, replaced by trips to the hairdresser and interviews. Her hair looks swell, but her self-image is suffering.

"I don't even feel like a writer anymore," she says, perching nervously on the edge of her sofa, her fists balled up in front of her face as if for protection. "As grateful as I am for the award, I have to admit it's been slightly destabilizing."

She apologizes for the unmusical hammering and sawing in the background, where her husband's dressing room - he died in 2002 - is being transformed into a guest room for their grandchildren. She purposely scheduled the construction to coincide with the announcement of the award, assuming that a little cacophony in her otherwise silent and solo living space might distract her from the disappointment of losing. Instead, her historical epic about a Paraguayan dictator and the Irish mistress who loved and advised him won the prize. So now she is doubly distracted.

BORN in Paris, she fled to Peru with her mother in 1939. Her father joined them, and they stayed in South America for five years before returning to Europe. The breakup of her parents' marriage sent her and her mother to New York City, where she again felt like an outsider. She went to Radcliffe, married and moved to Thailand with her husband, an independently wealthy artist. After having three children in five years, she divorced him and moved first to California and then to Paris, where she studied at the Sorbonne and wrote her first novel (It was never published.) She moved back to New York City in 1977, remarried and in 1989 studied writing with Gordon Lish. It was a breakthrough.

"He not only taught me how to write, but he taught me to take myself seriously as a writer," she says. "Until then, when people asked me what I did, I'd tell them I typed."
