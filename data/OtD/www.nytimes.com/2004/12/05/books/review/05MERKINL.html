'Exuberance': Ode to Joy

 By DAPHNE MERKIN

O be brutally honest about it, the topic of other people's happiness has never set anyone's pulse racing. Misery loves company, and the inner schadenfreudist in us generally triumphs over our delight in someone else's good fortune; it is, in fact, unhappiness that we find riveting to hear and read about: marriages that break up, children who turn out badly, careers that flounder, winners who become losers. The limitations of happiness as a literary theme were famously observed by Tolstoy in the opening of ''Anna Karenina'': ''Happy families are all alike; unhappy families are unhappy each in its own way.'' There may be 50 ways to leave your lover, 11 kinds of loneliness, 7 types of ambiguity, 3 unities of tragedy, etc., but happiness seems unconducive to psychological parsing. Writers are not renowned for being blithe spirits, of course, and there have always been those, like the poet Philip Larkin, who simply didn't believe in the authenticity of any experience but misery. Then there was Colette, whose briskly pragmatic exhortation I once taped over the bed in my dreary graduate-school apartment: ''Who said you should be happy? Do your work.''

Kay Redfield Jamison, a professor of psychiatry at Johns Hopkins, has previously focused her authorial sights on the dark side of the moon. Her books include ''Touched With Fire,'' which examines the connection between creativity and mood disorders; ''An Unquiet Mind,'' a best-selling memoir of manic-depression; and ''Night Falls Fast,'' a study of suicide. But now she has turned to the bright side of the emotional spectrum in ''Exuberance: The Passion for Life.''

She points out that exuberance and kindred feelings like happiness have intrigued the mental health industry as little as they have writers and ordinary folk: ''Psychologists, for reasons of clinical necessity or vagaries of temperament, have chosen to dissect and catalog the morbid emotions -- depression, anger, anxiety -- and to leave largely unexamined the more vital, positive ones.''

A large part of ''Exuberance'' is given over to case studies of propulsive, testosterone-driven personalities -- the sort of relentlessly forward-march people who, as P. T. Barnum put it, ''throw up skyrockets.'' Winston Churchill, who had an unerring ability to leaven his own sense of grandiosity with self-effacing humor, described the type another way: ''We are all worms. But I do believe that I am a glow-worm.'' Jamison includes both Barnum and Churchill as examples of this incandescent species, along with Robert Louis Stevenson and Charles Schulz, creator of ''Peanuts.''

The book is filled with informational tidbits (the word ''exuberance,'' we are told, is derived from the Latin ex, ''out of,'' and uberare, ''to be fruitful, to be abundant'') and quotes from the usual Humanities 101 list of Deep Thinkers, like Keats, Emerson, Gerard Manley Hopkins, William James and Walt Whitman. And yet, despite the literary name-dropping, Jamison's real interest lies with the phenomenon of exuberance as it is experienced by the scientifically adventuresome -- the sort of person who is ''a genius with celestial pinball.'' This group includes Richard Feynman, James Watson and Robert Gallo, the co-discoverer of the AIDS virus. The four women who most securely hold Jamison's attentions are also scientists: the elephant biologists Katy Payne and Joyce Poole, the naturalist Hope Ryden and the astronomer Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin.

Scientific or literary, Jamison's characters come across as insufferable. (The exceptions are Churchill and Stevenson, both of whom had the grace to fall into periods of deep depression when they weren't levitating with buoyancy.) Most of them seem untroubled by self-doubt, and are constitutionally oxygen-eating (Gallo tellingly defines exuberance as ''an overaverage bite of life''); they tend to express their passion for doing groundbreaking work in the manner of car salesmen: ''If you get excited you are more motivated and more competitive. You are motivated to work extra hours. Competition makes everything happen.''
