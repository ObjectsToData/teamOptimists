Art Reviews | 'Sketches on Glass', 'Monumental France'

Sleight of Camera, Capturing Fleeting Impressions

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
By KAREN ROSENBERG
Published: March 14, 2008

Pity the photographic negative, consigned to obscurity as digital photographs are manipulated with ease on the computer screen. At the moment, however, the negative is starring in a pair of exhibitions at the New York Public Library that focus on the experimental period after the birth of photography (the 1850s through the 1870s). Film had not yet been invented, but glass-plate and paper negatives allowed artists to micromanage the camera’s performance or to bypass it entirely.

“Monumental France: The Photographs of Édouard Baldus” gathers 38 prints of French architectural landmarks. “Sketches on Glass: Clichés-Verre From the New York Public Library” presents prints made with hand-drawn glass plates on light-sensitive paper (no camera required).

Together they reveal two very different aspects of photography in mid-19th-century France. Baldus made state-sponsored and commercial photographs of important buildings. Barbizon School artists like Camille Corot and Charles-François Daubigny used the cliché-verre to capture fleeting impressions of the countryside.

At the same time, these shows intersect on a technical level: Baldus took artisanal liberties with his negatives, just as the Barbizons viewed the glass plate of the cliché-verre as a fancy sketchbook. In both cases the negative was more of a ground for improvisation than an objective representation of reality.

France was beginning a radical transformation. Under Napoleon III’s prefect, Baron Georges-Eugène Haussmann, the medieval alleys of Paris were being razed to make way for grand boulevards. In 1851 the French government dispatched five photographers, including the Prussian-born Baldus (1813-1899), to take pictures of historic sites. The goal of the Missions Héliographiques, as they were known, was to aid in preservation and restoration.

Baldus experimented with the full range of the photographic technology available in his day. He worked with both glass and paper negatives, and made albumen and salt prints. Sometimes he combined pieces of different negatives, as in an image of the interior of the cloister of St. Trophime in Arles.

Baldus would also paint directly on his negatives, both to add information (cloudy skies that early cameras could not fully capture) and to edit it (painting out parts of the background to emphasize the main subject, as in an 1850 picture of the Arc de Triomphe).

“Monumental France” includes rare prints from the Mission Héliographique, as well as several photographs from an 1855 commission to document new construction at the Louvre. Baldus’s large-format views of the museum’s impressive pavilions communicate a sense of hauteur, but some of his most beautiful images were made during excursions to the southern city Avignon. Nature trumps architecture in two photographs of the city taken during a flood in 1856 and printed, unlike most of the architectural images, on salted paper that makes the water appear hand-painted.

Because the negatives from his Mission Héliographique commission were owned by the government, Baldus rephotographed several of the sites for his own commercial purposes. The artists of the Barbizon School, meanwhile, had discovered a way to draw their own negatives with etching tools on glass plates.

The library’s selection of 49 clichés-verre (part of a collection of prints donated by Samuel Putnam Avery in 1900) includes 27 works by Corot and 18 by Daubigny. Prints of some of the same images, from a different collection, were recently exhibited at Peter Freeman gallery in a small but fascinating show called “The Hand-Drawn Negative.”

Corot’s clichés-verre most often take the form of loose landscape sketches. A few, like the stunning “Mary Magdalene Praying” and “Hagar and the Angel,” depict specific biblical characters. Mostly, however, Corot’s figures are tiny, anonymous blips in the lower right or left corner of some verdant paradise.

Often the setting is the Roman countryside. Corot recorded some of his most inspired clichés-verre, including “The Gardens of Horace” (1855), “Souvenir of Ostia” (1855) and “The Banks of the Po River” (1858), during several trips to Italy. He often referred to these “souvenirs” when making paintings, but they are atmospheric landscapes in their own right.

Corot produced 66 cliché-verre plates but left the printing process to others. His contemporary Daubigny, meanwhile, embraced the medium’s technological challenges. The ease of Corot’s line is unparalleled, but Daubigny’s clichés-verre have a superbly crisp look and varied density of marks. The upper margin of “Pollard Willows” (1862), one of the show’s rarest works, is filled with test strokes of different widths.

The library’s set of exhibitions shows a photographer thinking like a painter, and a group of painters flirting with photography. All produced printed images that longed to be unique objects.

“Sketches on Glass: Clichés-Verre From The New York Public Library” and “Monumental France: The Photographs of Édouard Baldus” are on exhibit through June 28 at the Humanities and Social Sciences Library, New York Public Library; (212) 592-7730, nypl.org.
