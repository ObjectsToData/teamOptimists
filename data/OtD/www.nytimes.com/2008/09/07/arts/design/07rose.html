Reasons That We’ll Always Have Paris

  • Sign In to E-Mail or Save This
  • Print
  • Reprints
By KAREN ROSENBERG
Published: September 7, 2008

THE flagging economy is one possible explanation for museums’ sudden surge of interest in the late 1920s and early 1930s. No fewer than four fall exhibitions in Manhattan will focus on this fertile period in modern art, when the markets had dried up but ideas flowed freely across the Atlantic.

For artists on both sides of the ocean, all roads led to Paris. It was there that Joan Miró declared, in 1927, “I want to assassinate painting.” The aggressive side of this Catalan artist will be explored in “Joan Miró: Painting and Anti-Painting 1927-1937,” opening at the Museum of Modern Art on Nov. 2. The lesser-known Mirós in this exhibition, many of which are traveling here on foreign loans, flaunt grotesque forms and combative techniques. A highlight is the first American viewing of “Portrait of a Dancer” (1928), composed of a feather and a piece of cork attached with a hatpin to unprimed canvas.

Similarly spirited figures abound in “Alexander Calder: The Paris Years, 1926-1933,” which opens at the Whitney Museum of American Art on Oct. 16. A young Calder arrived in Paris as a realist painter and illustrator; within seven years he had been transformed into a Surrealist sculptor whose playful “drawings in space” were admired by Marcel Duchamp, among others. The Whitney-owned “Circus” will be at the center of the show, accompanied by sketchbooks, mobiles and films (including one of “Circus” in action).

The symbiotic relationship between New York and Paris in the 1920s and ’30s is also the focus of a design exhibition opening on Oct. 3 at the Museum of the City of New York: “Paris/New York: Design Fashion Culture 1925-1940.” Among the offerings will be furniture, costumes, photographs and other objects exuding transcontinental Modernism. One highlight is Jean Chassaing’s portrait of Josephine Baker; another is the promotional photograph of the Normandie cruise ship superimposed on a New York streetscape.

“Art Deco Design: Rhythm and Verve,” which goes on view Friday at the New York Public Library’s humanities and social sciences branch in Midtown, is a smaller, New York-centric look at the popular decorative style. Works by Sonia Delaunay and others will be featured alongside journals, hand-colored “pochoir” stencil prints and photographs of skyscrapers, all accompanied by the music of George Gershwin and Irving Berlin.

These exhibitions offer, among other things, reassurance that creative exchanges can transcend market cycles.
