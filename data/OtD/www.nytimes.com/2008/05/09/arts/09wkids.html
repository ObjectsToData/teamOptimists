Spare Times: For Children

By LAUREL GRAEBER
Published: May 9, 2008

FOR CHILDREN

ASTRONOMY DAY On Sunday Mike Francis will bring “Siderius Nuncius” to life.

To children that may sound like a mythological creature, a Frankenstein-like monster or some other fearsome invention by a mad scientist. But “Siderius Nuncius” was created by an eminently rational scientist: Galileo, whom Mr. Francis, above, has been portraying for 17 years. In doublet and breeches he will dramatize “Siderius Nuncius” (“Starry Messenger”), Galileo’s 1610 treatise on the heavens, as part of Astronomy Day at the Hudson River Museum in Yonkers. And his demonstrations won’t be dryly academic, any more than Galileo’s were.

“He was the party animal of the 17th century,” Mr. Francis said. “Everyone wanted him at their house for dinner to tell them what was going on in the science world.”

So museum visitors will encounter an ebullient guest, who may let them look through his telescope (a model of Galileo’s). They can also take part in “Galileo Galilei: The Starry Messenger,” his 50-minute show. Some will play features of the lunar surface; others will portray Jupiter’s moons, of which Galileo discovered four.

“For the Moon’s craters I like to use kids, because they disappear behind the adults, who are mountains,” Mr. Francis said. By lining up theatergoers of different heights, he illustrates how Galileo used observations of light and shadow to deduce that the lunar surface isn’t smooth. Similarly, he has audience members stand around him, altering their positions to show how Galileo’s perception of “the little dots” around Jupiter changed on successive viewings, proving that they weren’t stars, but moons.

Sunday’s other festivities will include planetarium shows; a presentation about animal constellations; early space movies; astronomical crafts; and “Rockets Away,” a demonstration including an air-rocket launching. And while Mr. Francis will perform only at 2:30 p.m., he will visit all day, answering questions as Galileo. He also likes to pose a question to learn if his audience members are scientists or philosophers. He wouldn’t reveal the question, noting that about 99 percent prove to be philosophers. “They’ll find out what they need to do to be a scientist,” he said. “You need to look.” But for now, “that’s as far as I’ll go.” (Astronomy Day, noon to 5 p.m., 511 Warburton Avenue, Yonkers, 914-963-4550, hrm.org. Free with admission: $5; $3 for 5 through 16 and 62+.) LAUREL GRAEBER

‘ADVENTURE THEATER! AN INTERACTIVE THEATRICAL ADVENTURE FOR HEROES OF ALL AGES’ (Saturday and Sunday) Children are expert improvisers, and in this inspired production they can use all that playtime experience with the help of adults who improvise professionally: the members of Freestyle Repertory Theater. Intended for ages 5 to 13, this show will engage them in devising the dialogue and action for a story about a mortal leader who goes to liberate a magic land from an evil ruler; the hero is a child from the audience. At 11 a.m., Metropolitan Playhouse, 220 East Fourth Street, East Village, (212) 995-5302, metropolitanplayhouse.org; $10; $8 for 12 and under.

‘ALICE IN WONDERLAND’ (Friday through Sunday) This time the rabbit hole is in Greenwich Village. In this adaptation by Brainerd Duffield, Alice is a 7-year-old resident of the neighborhood, and her mom and dad have packed her schedule with classes and activities, as New York parents tend to do. Presented by the New Acting Company, the production features four adults and five young actors portraying the characters involved in Alice’s Wonderland escape. (Through May 25.) Friday at 7 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday at 3 and 7 p.m.; the Philip Coltoff Center, 219 Sullivan Street, near Bleecker Street, Greenwich Village, (212) 868-4444, smarttix.com; $16 in advance; $20 at the door.

‘ARCHAEOLOGY ZONE: DISCOVERING TREASURES FROM PLAYGROUNDS TO PALACES’ (Saturday through Thursday) Children will step into the shoes of an explorer like Indiana Jones in this exhibition at the Jewish Museum, but the adventures will be purely scholarly. Still, there is plenty of excitement in analyzing artifacts like a jar handle, a clay jug and a bangle, and figuring out the purpose behind ancient pieces like a Greek helmet and a bull-shaped vessel. This interactive show also includes a recreated home from the Ottoman period (about 1900), where young archaeologists can dress in costume. (Through June 15, 2009.) Saturday through Wednesday, 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m., and Thursday to 8 p.m., 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street, (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org. Free with admission: $12; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for under 12, members and for all on Saturdays.

BABY LOVES DISCO (Saturday) And so do Mommy and Daddy, who can enjoy these afternoon dance parties — with bona fide D.J.’s and ’70s and ’80s hits — along with their offspring. Unlike clubs, these disco gatherings feature diaper-changing stations, and unlike playgrounds, they also have fully stocked cash bars. From 2 to 5 p.m., Club Element, 225 East Houston Street, Lower East Side, babylovesdisco.com; $15 for those big enough to walk.

BIG APPLE CIRCUS (Saturday through Thursday) Children aren’t usually interested in 30th-birthday parties, but they’ll surely make an exception when the honoree is a circus. In its new show, “Celebrate!,” Big Apple salutes three decades of entertaining New Yorkers. The revelers include dogs, cats, horses and, of course, acrobats, jugglers, gymnasts and clowns. (Through May 26.) Weekdays at 11 a.m. and 6:30 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays at 12:30 p.m. and 4:30 p.m.; Cunningham Park, Francis Lewis Boulevard and Union Turnpike, Hollis Hills, Queens, (212) 307-4100, bigapplecircus.org; $15 to $38.

‘THE CAT WHO WENT TO HEAVEN’ (Saturday and Sunday) At least one of the cats involved with the jazz in this production is the kind that meows instead of scats. Nancy Harrow, the creator of the jazz-and-puppet show “Maya the Bee,” has returned to Culture Project with a new piece based on the Newbery Medal-winning book by Elizabeth Coatsworth. It’s all about a struggling Japanese artist and the mysterious cat that seems to change his fortunes. (Through May 18.) Saturday at 11 a.m., Sunday at 7 p.m., 55 Mercer Street, SoHo, (212) 352-3101, cultureproject.org; $20.

‘LA CENERENTOLA’ (Friday and Saturday) Children may not recognize the name, but they will surely know the character: Cinderella, given an aria-filled life by Rossini in this Dicapo Opera Theater production. They won’t have trouble understanding the piece, either: this family staging, for ages 4 and older, is sung in English. Friday at 11:30 a.m., Saturday at 3 p.m., St. Jean Baptiste Church, 184 East 76th Street, Manhattan, (212) 288-9438, Ext. 10, dicapo.com; $20; $10 for 12 and under.

CHILDREN’S BOOK WEEK KICKOFF (Saturday) Any week is a good week to read, but this literary celebration, through May 18, includes events especially for young bookworms. It all begins on Saturday in the Bryant Park Reading Room, which will feature appearances by favorite characters, like Clifford the Big Red Dog, and readings and signings by authors like Marilyn Singer and Marc Aronson. The room will also be packed with 1,000 books from publishers belonging to the Children’s Book Council. From noon to 5 p.m., Bryant Park, Avenue of the Americas at 40th Street, bookweekonline.com/index1.html; free.

‘DANCE MAKERS AND GROUND BREAKERS’ (Saturday) They may be only 6 to 17 years old, but these dance makers and groundbreakers, all students in the programs of Loco-Motion Dance Theater for Children, are still sophisticated. Their program of 13 original dances tackles topics like civil rights, immigration and the Bush administration (in a piece called “Dear Mr. President”). At 7 p.m., Miller Theater, 2960 Broadway, at 116th Street, Morningside Heights, loco-motiondancetheatre.org; $10.

‘GUSTAFER YELLOWGOLD’S MELLOW SENSATION’ (Saturday) Ready for a new kind of mellow yellow? Hailing from the Sun and looking like an animated drop of butter, Gustafer Yellowgold is the creation of the illustrator and composer Morgan Taylor, now presenting Gustafer in his Off Broadway debut. Like Gustafer’s other appearances, this run includes live music, slides and narration. (Through May 17.) At 11 a.m. and 2 p.m., DR2 Theater, 103 East 15th Street, Manhattan, (212) 239-6200; $20.

HARRY AND THE POTTERS (Saturday) These young magicians work their enchantment with guitars rather than wands. Created by a pair of Boston brothers — Paul DeGeorge, 28, who portrays Harry, Year 7, and Joe DeGeorge, 20, who performs as Harry, Year 4 — the band, in full Hogwarts costumes, will play Potter-theme rock tunes for ages 12 and older. At 7 p.m., Celeste Bartos Forum, Humanities and Social Services Library, New York Public Library, nypl.org. Free tickets, available in Astor Hall at the library, will be distributed, first come first served, on Friday, noon to 3 p.m., and on Saturday, noon to 5 p.m.

‘IF YOU GIVE A MOUSE A COOKIE’ (Saturday and Sunday) Manhattan Children’s Theater has revived Jody Davidson’s witty adaptation of Laura Joffe Numeroff’s picture book, which suggests that if you give a mouse a cookie, it’s going to want a glass of milk, and if you give it the milk, it’s going to want a napkin, and if you give it the napkin, it will request a mirror, and so on. And if you give this show your time, it will reward you greatly. (Through May 18.) At noon and 2 p.m., 52 White Street, near Church Street, TriBeCa, (212) 352-3101, mctny.org; $20, $15 for children and 65+.

‘THE KINDEST OF MEN: THE LIFE AND MUSIC OF JOSEPH HAYDN’ (Sunday) Not all geniuses qualify as nice guys, but Haydn apparently did. The cellist Steven Isserlis, another nice guy, will lead an exploration of Haydn’s work as part of his Family Music series at the 92nd Street Y, concerts for ages 6 to 10 that enable children not just to hear great composers, but also to understand their inspirations. In addition to Mr. Isserlis, the Orion String Quartet and the pianist Jeremy Denk will play. At 3 p.m., Kaufmann Concert Hall, 1395 Lexington Avenue, (212) 415-5500, 92y.org; $16.

‘ONCE ON THIS ISLAND’ (Friday through Sunday) The story of Hans Christian Andersen’s “Little Mermaid” is transported to the Caribbean in this musical adaptation by Lynn Ahrens and Stephen Flaherty. But while the accents and setting may have changed, the plot is truer to Andersen than anything Disney has done. Presented by the Pied Piper Children’s Theater of NYC, the show has actors aged 13 to 18. Friday at 8 p.m.; Saturday at 3 and 8 p.m.; Sunday at 4 p.m.; Holy Trinity Church, 20 Cumming Street, near Dyckman Street, Inwood, (212) 544-2976; $10; $7 for under 16 and 65+.

‘PAST HALF REMEMBERED’ (Friday through Sunday) At how many theater productions do the actors play musical instruments, speak several languages onstage and serve the audience tea? That’s just part of what goes on in this production, presented by the New Victory Theater and recommended for ages 12 and older. Presented by the New International Encounter Visual Theater, the show deals with the story of Maria, a 100-year-old Russian with a very eventful life. (Through May 25.) Friday at 7 p.m. (followed by a discussion with the cast); Saturday at 2 and 7 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; the Duke, 209 West 42nd Street, Manhattan, (646) 223-3010, newvictory.org; $25.

‘PICTURE THIS!’ (Friday, and Monday through Thursday) Children are often adorable in front of the camera, but put them behind it, and they can produce fascinating work. This exhibition displays photography, animation and documentary video by students in elementary, middle and high schools in all five boroughs of New York. The pieces were done during residency programs sponsored by Magic Box Productions, an arts education organization that sends teaching artists into the schools. (Through June 30.) From 8:30 a.m. to 5:30 p.m., the Gallery at 180 Maiden Lane, between Front and South Streets, Lower Manhattan, magicboxproductions.org; free.

‘PINKALICIOUS, THE MUSICAL’ (Sunday) It’s time to think pink again. This show from Vital Children’s Theater has been revived, so if you missed it last year, you can now catch it at New World Stages. Elizabeth and Victoria Kann adapted their children’s book, in which the pink-obsessed title character finds out that sometimes being in the pink can be too much of a good thing. (John Gregor wrote the score and some of the lyrics.) (Through May 25.) Sunday at noon; 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, vitaltheatre.org; $29.50.

‘PIPPI’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Children’s literature had at least one feminist heroine long before the feminist era: Pippi Longstocking, the pint-size, pigtailed dynamo who lived independently and fought off villains with the help of pluck and superhuman strength. Now the Swedish Cottage Marionette Theater is offering a musical based on the Pippi stories and celebrating the centennial of their creator, the Swedish author Astrid Lindgren. With a book by Zakiyyah Alexander and a score by Darryl Kojak, the production features almost two dozen new, handmade marionettes. Friday and Tuesday through Thursday at 10:30 a.m. and noon; Saturday and Sunday at 1 p.m.; 79th Street and the West Drive, Central Park; $8; $5 for 18 and under. Reservations required: (212) 988-9093; cityparksfoundation.org.

‘STREET DREAMS: THE INNER CITY MUSICAL’ (Friday through Sunday) The real star of this show, which played on Broadway in 1971 (as “Inner City”) is a nameless urban block, brought to life by an ensemble cast. In this revival the actors are all students at Amas Musical Theater’s Rosetta LeNoire Musical Theater Academy, who play the streetwise citizens in Eve Merriam’s script and Helen Miller’s songs. (Through May 18). Friday at 7:30 p.m.; Saturday at 3 and 7:30 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; American Theater of Actors, 314 West 54th Street, Clinton, (212) 352-3101, amasmusical .org; $20; $15 for students and 65+.

‘TEACH YOUR CHILD TO RIDE A BIKE’ (Saturday) This workshop, sponsored by Bike New York and the Parks and Recreation Department, promises to reduce both skinned knees and frayed nerves. To be held from 10 a.m. to 1 p.m., it will teach children how to balance and how to start, stop and steer. Participants must take their own helmets and bicycles. At three locations: Bayswater Park, Beach Channel Drive and Bay 32nd Street, Far Rockaway, Queens; J. J. Byrne Park, at Fourth Street and Fifth Avenue, Park Slope, Brooklyn; and Midland Beach Park, parking lot at Jefferson Avenue and Father Capodanno Boulevard, Staten Island. Free, but preregistration required: bikenewyork.org.

UNCLE ROCK (Saturday) Robert Burke Warren, a k a Uncle Rock, should know how to make music for children: he’s a former stay-at-home dad and former member of rock bands. A current star among the young, he is inspired by sources like Roald Dahl, Pete Seeger and Shel Silverstein. At 5 p.m., Jessie’s Brooklyn Kitchen, 200 Smith Street, at Baltic Street, Carroll Gardens, (718) 858-8807, jessiesbrooklynkitchen.com; $10.

‘THE VELVETEEN RABBIT’ (Saturday and Sunday) Literally Alive Children’s Theater, which dramatizes literary works, offers a musical “Velveteen Rabbit” that manages no easy feat: it is both upbeat and faithful to its source, Margery Williams’s poignant 1922 children’s classic. Before the show, which has a book and lyrics by Brenda Bell and music by Mark McGee, young audience members can join a workshop to help decorate the stage and make themselves rabbit ears. Workshop ($5, children only) at 11 a.m., show at noon, Players Theater, 115 Macdougal Street, near West Third Street, Greenwich Village, (212) 866-5170, literallyalive.com; $25; $20 for children.

WHEELCHAIR BASKETBALL CLINIC (Saturday) Playing sports may seem like an unattainable pleasure for many disabled children, but this program will show that that doesn’t have to be true. The clinic will provide wheelchairs specifically designed for basketball, and coaches and players from nine men’s and women’s wheelchair basketball teams will offer instruction and encouragement. From 10 a.m. to 2 p.m., Bulova Park, 25th Avenue at 77th Street, Jackson Heights, Queens, (917) 519-2622 or (via e-mail) jhamre@wheelchairsportsfederation.org; free.

WILDFLOWER DAY (Saturday) Color a plant, pot a plant, portray a plant — they’re all possible in this celebration for ages 3 to 12 at Solar One, the solar energy arts and education center. Part of the city’s first Wildflower Week, the event will include making plant costumes and a “Pretend You’re a Plant” interactive park tour. From 10 a.m. to 2 p.m., Stuyvesant Cove Park, East 23rd Street and the East River, Manhattan, (212) 505-6050, solar1.org; free. LAUREL GRAEBER
