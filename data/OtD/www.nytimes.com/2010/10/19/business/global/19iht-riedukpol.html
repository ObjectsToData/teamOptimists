Students in England Brace for the Possible Arrival of Hefty Tuition Fees

By BETH GARDINER
Published: October 18, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

LONDON

With the government ready to announce huge cuts in subsidies for higher education in England, student and faculty groups are warning that soaring tuition will deter the poor from going to college and saddle young graduates with heavy debts.

“What we are probably going to be facing over the next few years is a huge restructuring of our higher education system,” said Paul Cottrell, head of policy at the University and College Union, which represents faculty members. The nation’s higher education sector is likely to emerge from several years of turbulence with “an American-style system where all teaching is funded by student fees. Ultimately that is where we’re heading,” he said.

Prime Minister David Cameron’s plan to cut university funding means that students are likely to soon pay hefty fees in a country that as recently as the late 1990s offered college degrees free.

George Osborne, the Treasury chief, is expected to announce cuts of between 25 percent and 40 percent in public spending on universities when he sets out the government’s plan for tackling Britain’s gaping deficit on Wednesday.

The announcement comes about a week after an officially commissioned review set off an uproar by recommending an end to the annual cap of £3,290, or $5,225, on college tuition.

Top research institutions like Oxford and Cambridge have argued for years that the ceiling was depriving them of the resources they needed to remain competitive with the world’s best universities, many of them in the United States.

And since it comes at the same time as the expected loss of billions of pounds in public funding, even top schools able to charge steep fees are unlikely to be much better off than they are now.

Universities say the cut in government support could include an 80 percent reduction in the funding stream directed at teaching and may mean there will be no public subsidy at all for subjects like arts and humanities, which are seen as less important than science and engineering for the country’s economic competitiveness.

The withdrawal of such a large chunk of higher education subsidies, part of the Conservative-Liberal Democrat coalition government’s plans for a broad scaling back of public services, represents a major shift in the nation’s priorities. The coalition has set a daunting target of eliminating Britain’s structural deficit within five years and has chosen largely to forgo big tax increases to do so, meaning it must make the biggest spending cuts in its postwar history. The cuts to be announced Wednesday are expected to be 25 percent or more in some government departments.

“What the proposals represent are a significant shift in funding from the public to the individual,” said Paul Clark, director of policy at Universities UK, which represents university administrators. “If you take that much money out of the system, and particularly if you do it very rapidly, that could be very, very destabilizing.”

Some institutions will simply close, while elite ones increase fees, said Mr. Cottrell of the University and College Union. Less prestigious schools may avoid steep prices because they fear students will not pay, and will instead cut costs, leading to fewer lecturers, bigger classes and declining quality, he said. Eventually, more private universities could open, and cheaper, two-year college degrees may become more common.

John Browne, the former BP chief executive, whose government-commissioned review recommended last week that universities be allowed to raise fees as high as they like, says that kind of competition would bring new vigor to the system and improve quality.

Universities and student groups argue that pulling money from research and teaching will hit Britain’s economic competitiveness.

Under Mr. Browne’s proposals, which the government has welcomed and is now considering, students would not pay anything up front. Instead, they would pay for college after graduation, starting when their income reaches £21,000 a year. Repayments, including interest, would be set at 9 percent of a graduate’s pay for a maximum of 30 years. Supporters say that makes Mr. Browne’s plans more progressive than the current system, under which repayments begin when income hits £15,000 and part-time students are not covered.
