A Dissertation on Mr. Ed?; New Scholarly Center to Study Pop Television Culture

By LAWRIE MIFFLIN
Published: October 15, 1997

As a typically compulsive undergraduate at the University of Chicago, Bob Thompson used to allow himself just one half-hour of television viewing a week, on Sunday night. And no matter how hard he tried to watch PBS, he always succumbed to ''CHiPS,'' the drama about California motorcycle cops starring Larry Wilcox and Erik Estrada.

This disturbed him. Why, Mr. Thompson, a budding political scientist, asked himself, do smart people love to watch dumb television shows?

From that query came a quixotic academic career. Now Dr. Robert J. Thompson, a 38-year-old Syracuse University professor, he is one of the nation's leading scholars in television studies, a relatively new and still formless discipline. This weekend, he will take charge of a new academic research center at the S. I. Newhouse School of Public Communications at Syracuse, to be christened the Center for the Study of Popular Television -- emphasis on the word ''popular.''

Television, even television that appeals to the lowest common denominator, can still be great art, Professor Thompson contends, a sort of visually enhanced form of literature. Shakespeare, as people are so often reminded, was a popular artist. Jazz, once dismissed as popular, is now recognized as a discipline worthy of serious study.

Indeed, in a statement announcing the creation of the research center, the dean of the Newhouse School, David Rubin, declares that it will ''study television entertainment programs with the same care and passion as musicologists study Mozart and Ellington, or professors of English study Melville and Pynchon.''

Poppycock, some other scholars say.

''There's a fallacy there, in assuming that the common run of literature is comparable to Melville, or that the musical norm is comparable to Mozart's work,'' said Mark Crispin Miller, a leading media scholar who is soon to leave Johns Hopkins University to join New York University. ''Those were the greats, who stood out. In the Renaissance, you'd have a hard time finding a play that's even readable other than Shakespeare's plays.''

While conceding that the best television programs merit academic attention, Professor Miller said Syracuse's emphasis on the word ''popular'' concerned him.

''The fact is that most TV drama is esthetically appalling, and most TV comedy is too,'' he said. ''For the most part it's utterly derivative, a pale imitation of whatever seemed to sell six months ago.''

Such attitudes drive Professor Thompson to become ''even more zealous,'' as he put it, in defense of his research. He calls such criticism ''belle-lettristic apartheid, much of it plain old-fashioned cultural elitism,'' mostly from people who have scant knowledge of popular television.

''Just because television is filled with bottom-line-oriented profits and is generated by 500 people's hands, we shouldn't be reduced to silence about it,'' he said.

Professor Thompson in fact admires Professor Miller's work. But he said he believed that enough academic research was being done on subjects like television news coverage; television's influence on politics, violent behavior or sexual mores, and the economic forces behind mass media. What is missing, he said, is the ''art appreciation'' aspect.

''It is tragic and in many ways dangerous that we are so ignorant as intellectuals about a medium that has such a profound effect on us,'' he said. ''How we perceive art and storytelling is infected and affected by the television era. Look at any modern novel after the year 1960 -- look at Don DeLillo's new book -- and you can't help but see how much television has moved in.''

Horace Newcomb, a communications professor at the University of Texas at Austin who was one of the first scholars in this field, as early as 1970, agreed.

''Popular television has been the central storytelling system of our time,'' he said. ''To say it's not important to study that is like saying one shouldn't have studied Dickens or Scott Fitzgerald. I happen to think fiction is crucial to people's lives, and if they're experiencing it this way, or through literature or the stage or the big screen, all are equally deserving of study.

As for Professor Thompson's own research, his most recent book was ''Television's Second Golden Age: From 'Hill Street Blues' to 'E.R.' '' (Continuum Press, 1996); his next, due next year, is ''Good TV: The 'St. Elsewhere' Story.''

''I think 'St. Elsewhere' is probably the best show ever on American prime-time TV,'' Professor Thompson said, because of the intricacy of its writing.

After switching from political science, his undergraduate major, Professor Thompson earned a doctorate in film and television studies from Northwestern University. He taught there and at the State University of New York at Cortland before moving to the Newhouse School at Syracuse seven years ago.

Along the way, he became convinced that ''there was enormous vacuum to be filled'' in terms of ''inventing the discipline of television studies as a serious academic pursuit.''

His dream of creating a center for such work was endorsed by Dr. Rubin, the Newhouse School dean, who helped raise more than $430,000 to start the new center.
