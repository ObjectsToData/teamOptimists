Kara Walker's Shock Art

By Julia Szabo
Published: March 23, 1997

In a dimly lit, subterranean gallery at Boston's Institute of Contemporary Art, a tall young woman stands near a staircase, contemplating a nine-foot-square sheet of black paper tacked to the wall. Hundreds of long, skinny braids quiver down to her waist, fraying at the tips in Botticelli-brown waves. Squinting at some ghostly faint, tentative sketches in white pencil, she breaks a blade segment off a slim X-Acto knife and lets it drop to the floor. Then with swift, sure strokes, holding the metal knife like a pencil, she begins to slice.

The woman is Kara Walker, 27, and this is her vision of the future. Her work has from the beginning featured images like a white woman in a poke bonnet and hoop skirt with the penis of a small black child in her mouth; a nude black woman vomiting human body parts, the remains of a white man lying, tellingly, nearby; and a smiling female field hand picking cotton while engaging in intercourse with a white master in hat and boots. What begins to take shape here is a tableau of subverted slavery-era icons: an African tribeswoman in a grass skirt; her prim, fully clothed white missionary counterpart; and a black woman dancing above an unfurled banner, her long skirt lifted to reveal a trumpet between her legs. Like all her work, it is simultaneously funny, grim and beautiful.

History is at the heart of Kara Walker's art -- specifically, black history and how its tellings and retellings have affected the African-American experience. It is graphic in every sense of the term -- high contrast, sexually explicit, violent, scatological. To those who are offended by what might be called, say, perpetuating negative stereotypes in the mainstream, she says, ''I can understand it, and I can't even really talk my way out of it. I can't say, 'Well, you shouldn't be offended.' Why not? It's a valid response, it's a valid way to feel.''

Walker has traveled far fast: her first New York group show came only three months after she earned her M.F.A. from the Rhode Island School of Design; her work has been acquired by the Whitney Museum of American Art and the Walker Art Center for their permanent collections, and she will participate in this year's Whitney Biennial. Large-scale cut-paper works have been purchased for $30,000 to $80,000 each by, among others, Jeffrey Deitch (Jeff Koons's art dealer), Yvonne Force (who, with backing from Laurence S. Rockefeller, is collecting work by young artists) and the Greek collector Dakis Joannou.

Some would argue that her appeal lies at least in part in her political correctness. ''In a pop culture sense, we,'' meaning blacks, ''are very hot,'' says Peggy Cooper Cafritz, founder of the Duke Ellington School of the Arts in Washington and a member of the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities. ''And that's good and bad, but a lot of it is market-driven. People like Russell Simmons have convinced white America, and certainly young white America, that we're what's happening. And that plays from the Du Bois Institute and Henry Louis Gates all the way to Suge Knight.'' Kerry James Marshall, for example, an African-American artist who was passed over for the Biennial two years ago, is included this year, and Robert Colescott, another African-American artist, will represent the United States at this year's Venice Biennale.

Still, Walker's work has its own particular merits, especially because of its form. ''We haven't seen anybody come along using the cut-paper medium since Matisse,'' notes Susanne Ghez, director and curator of the Renaissance Society in Chicago. The silhouette first became widespread in the 18th century as a style of portraiture somewhere between illustration and photography, more craft than art. (Black-shadow drawing, on which it is based, has occurred on everything from Greek amphorae to Wedgwood Queen's Ware to the ''Charlie's Angels'' logo.) Initially sitters were of high social standing and the silhouette was marked by its chaste depictions of men in tail coats and top hats, ladies in hoop skirts and poke bonnets holding flowers, sailor-capped boys riding hobbyhorses and pantalooned girls holding dolls. Many women took it up as a handicraft, like needlepoint, and the form is still used to illustrate children's books.

The silhouette art of Kara Walker, on the other hand, contains dynamic narratives centering on a young black woman called, variously, the ''Nigger Wench'' or the ''Free Negress.'' Hardly the obedient, victimized female slave, the Negress is a star player in this version of history, seducing her masters as much as being molested by them or stuffing hobbyhorses down white children's throats.

Walker's violence and scatology are tempered by a sense of humor, as in such typically over-the-top titles as ''Gone: An Historical Romance of a Civil War as It Occurred Between the Dusky Thighs of One Young Negress and Her Heart.'' On one wall of her studio in Providence, R. I., is an image in progress of a boy holding a whip and standing beside a fallen horse. What's he doing? He's . . . beating a dead horse. (Ding!) A man with a black boy clinging to his neck signifies (what else?) the white man's burden. A print of a cotton-topped black girl and a monkey reaching for the dripping do-rag she holds is cleverly titled ''I'll Be a Monkey's Uncle.'' An odd little pastel drawing Walker made, of a nappy-headed baby with large breasts straddling a watermelon, is turned to face the wall. ''That one really kind of creeps me out,'' she says. ''I can't look at it. It feels almost . . . authentic to me.''
