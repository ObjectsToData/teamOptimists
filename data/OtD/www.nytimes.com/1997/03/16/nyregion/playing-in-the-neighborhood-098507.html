PLAYING IN THE NEIGHBORHOOD


Published: March 16, 1997

SOHO

Art Is Living History By Children of Rain Forest

They number only about 150, but there is a Mayan tribe, the Lacandon people, that provides a link to an ancient culture in a remote jungle region of Mexico. But art from Lacandon children -- colorful drawings, watercolors, collages, masks and block prints -- can be seen through April 27 at the Children's Museum of the Arts.

The Lacandon, who live in the Chiapas region of Mexico, in one of the last remaining rain forests of North America, are dwindling in number. But with the help of people like Gertrude Blom, a photographer and anthropologist, and Kelynn Z. Alder, a New York artist, the oral history of the Lacandon people is being preserved through visual art. Museumgoers can learn about the rain forest and its people as well as make their own art during this exhibition.

Lacandon Children's Artist Workshop Exhibition; Children's Museum of the Arts; 72 Spring Street, between Lafayette and Crosby Streets; through April 27; Tuesday through Friday, noon to 6 P.M.; Saturday and Sunday, 11 A.M. to 5 P.M.; $4 Tuesday through Friday, $5 on Saturday and Sunday; free for those under 18 months or older than 65 years old; (212) 274-0986.

EAST VILLAGE

A 'Golem' With Puppets (Don't Look for Kermit)

In 1882, the Annex Theater of the La Mama Experimental Theater Club was a leading Yiddish playhouse, known as the Turn Theater. In keeping with this tradition the Czechoslovak American Marionette Theater, based in New York, presents ''Golem,'' a modern musical portrayal of the Jewish characters of Prague's 16th-century ghetto.

The production has no words and includes actors, dancers, musicians and four-foot puppets. (The apple-seller is shown at left.)

The Golem is an ancient Jewish mythological creature that has taken many forms. This show is based on a golem story in which a rabbi fashions a clay giant to protect the ghetto from attack. But this golem turns against its inventor.

''Golem,'' La Mama Experimental Theater Club (Annex Theater), 74A East Fourth Street, between Second and Third Avenues; Thursday through Saturday at 7:30 P.M., and Sunday at 3 P.M.; $15, children under 12 $7.50; (212) 475-7710.

FLUSHING

Invading the Orchestra For Music Lessons Up Close

Queens may be a diverse community but the Queens Symphony Orchestra ''appeals to the common denominator of family,'' said Nicole Marchand, the ensemble's associate director.

In next Sunday's Family Day Concert, the audience is invited on stage while the orchestra is performing. Each child gets a baton to take home and a chance to conduct the orchestra with it. They can also ask the musicians questions.

Queens Symphony's Family Day Concert; Colden Center for the Performing Arts, Queens College, 65-30 Kissena Boulevard, at 65th Avenue; next Sunday at 2 P.M.; $23-27, $15 for people 65 or over, $8 for students and $5 for children 12 and under; (718) 786-8880.
