Near the Giant Verrazano, a Human Scale

By JOHN RATHER
Published: March 9, 1997

PEOPLE looking for a home who are unfamiliar with neighborhoods inland from Brooklyn's great round shoreline underbelly will need to look twice to see what's special about Dyker Heights, a residential area of one- and two-family, mostly brick, homes tucked between Bay Ridge and Bensonhurst, not far from the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge.

Laid out in an unremarkable, roughly rectangular grid of six long north-to-south avenues crisscrossed by street after street of closely packed detached and semi-detached houses, the neighborhood seems at first to be unprepossessing and devoid of some defining central hub or feature.

Yet a closer look brings a dawning awareness of the neighborhood's orderly, insular essence. Mom-and-pop stores dotting the avenues emphasize the human scale of a place slightly resistant to the passage of time, where only the schools, the church spires and a few other buildings rise higher than three stories.

For people who know the terrain, there is no mystery about the charm of Dyker Heights. It is a place to aspire to, to settle into for long spans. In short, it is a good address, with home prices to match.

''Dyker is in very high demand, and if somebody doesn't know the area they are never going to understand the values,'' said Frank DiMarco, the owner and broker at ERA Real Estate Professionals in Dyker Heights.

''If I put a sign in front of a house in Bensonhurst, we get 20, 25 calls in a week,'' said Mr. DiMarco. ''In Dyker Heights we get 70 calls, and if we put it in the paper, forget about it. The phone is going to melt.''

Nowhere was demand stronger, Mr. DiMarco said, than in what he called ''prime Dyker Heights,'' a stretch from 79th to 86th Street between 10th Avenue and 13th Avenues where a house might cost $50,000 more than its mate a few blocks away.

Home ownership is the pattern, but upper-floor rentals in owner-occupied, two-family houses can be found.

Theresa Miranda, a sales agent at Dyker Real Estate, said rentals in two-family houses ranged from $800 to $1,200 for a five- to six-room apartment. She said $900 was average.

Once settled in, people tend to stay on and on. ''I'm in my house 25 years, and I'm not going anywhere,'' said Carolyn DeLuca, the assistant district director of Community Board 10, which includes Dyker Heights. ''It's stable, it's nice, and a lot of my old neighbors are still here.''

William N. Girasole, manager and broker of Aldo B. Girasole and Associates in Dyker Heights, said prices on a single block may range from ''the sublime to the ridiculous,'' or from the low $200,000's for a smaller semi-detached home without a garage to over $1 million for grander, three-story brick stand-alones, often with extensive interior renovation and plenty of garage space. Many homes were built during the 20's, but a few are more than 100 years old.

Dyker Heights, which is in the 68th Police Precinct, has the look and feel of a low-crime area, an impression local civic groups said was borne out by crime statistics.

NEVERTHELESS, local merchants take part in the Safe Haven program, posting window stickers that announce proprietors' readiness to shelter and assist anyone in distress. ''If someone needs help, if an old person needs assistance, they can get it,'' said Frances T. Vella-Marrone, the president of the Dyker Heights Civic Association.

The association was a founder of a local park alliance that looks after the 8.5-acred McKinley Park, with its playground, basketball courts and workout area. Volunteers keep the park clean and graffiti-free.

More recreation is available nearby at the 46-acre Dyker Beach Park and, next door to it, the 18-hole, city-owned Dyker Beach Golf Course. Fees are $17 for city residents and $25 for nonresidents.

By one account, Dyker Heights owes its Dutchified name to dikes built by Dutch farmers. But the name appears to be of more recent origin, and may reflect the area's location near Dyker Beach and Dyker Beach Park. A land auction flyer printed in 1922 hawked lots in the heart of what is now Dyker Heights, but referred to the area as Bay Ridge East.

The Dutch bought land including the Dyker Heights vicinity from the Canarsee Indians, and Anthony Jansen de Salee, a Dutchman, bought 200 acres in the area in 1639. In 1657, the area became part of New Utrecht, one of the six original Dutch towns that later joined as Brooklyn. Dutch farmers stayed on long after the English took control of New York in 1664.

During the Battle of Long Island in August 1776, the first great battle of the American Revolution, British and Hessian troops routed outnumbered American forces commanded by George Washington in engagements fought not far from the Dyker Heights area. New Utrecht, where about one-third of the population were slaves, remained occupied until the war's end.

In the 19th century, fishing and maritime industries attracted Scandinavians, for a time the largest immigrant group in the Bay Ridge-Dyker Heights area. By the early 20th century, when subways made the area more accessible to Manhattan, waves of Irish and, a little later, Italian immigrants arrived.
