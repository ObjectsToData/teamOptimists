Treasures Seafarers Fetched From the Americas

By RITA REIF
Published: January 26, 1997

THE ELEGANT SIMPLICITY OF the Aleut style is unmistakable in a gauzy gut parka as transparent as sheer silk, cut long and full like a Russian officer's coat with an appliqued collar of dyed sealskin. The vivid palette of reds, turquoise and black gives an expression of awe to a wide-eyed mask of the Bella Bella tribe. Just what a dancer needed at a potlatch feast. And the Apache taste for fringed deerskin is evident in a doll dressed in the sort of gown young girls dreamed of wearing to sunrise ceremonies.

These 19th-century treasures are among the exceptional pieces in the little-known and diverse collection of 28,000 Eskimo and American Indian objects here at the Peabody Essex Museum. These works, collected since the museum was founded in 1799, were left in storage -- some for more than a century -- and never exhibited.

But while the Peabody amassed sea captains' portraits, carved figureheads and engraved whales' teeth, it also acquired a wealth of other exotica from throughout the Americas, Asia, Europe and the South Seas. By the mid-19th century, the aim of the museum was to be encyclopedic in its collecting, a goal it shared with the Smithsonian and other institutions.

And Salem's merchants and missionaries were eager to fulfill this ambition. They traveled throughout the world and returned with artifacts, some of which they gave to the Peabody. Of all the museum's holdings, which include one million works on paper, two million books and manuscripts, and 400,000 other objects, only the Eskimo and American Indian art had largely remained a mystery.

In 1990, with the museum's bicentennial approaching and American Indian art collections being re-evaluated by museums throughout the country, the Peabody began to investigate its vast holdings. With the help of a $500,000 grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities, John R. Grimes, the curator of Native American art and archaeology, and his colleagues, inventoried the collection and modernized antiquated storage areas in the museum, many of which were crammed with untold relics.

''Closets were filled floor to ceiling with stacks of baskets that had been there for a century,'' he said. ''We had to tease things apart. It was like untangling ropes.''

Among the many finds in the stacks were a crushable Inuit basket, flecked with tiny threads of red wool, and a brown Puma basket, the size of a grapefruit, exquisitely woven and dotted with white glass beads. These baskets and other rarities are included in ''Gifts of the Spirit: Works by 19th-Century and Contemporary Native American Artists,'' an exhibition on through May. Along with 140 American Indian and Eskimo objects from a century ago, there are 40 contemporary works lent by artists from around the country.

The displays mix old and new objects, the works of different peoples and various crafts. ''We were intent on showing each piece as a work of art,'' said Dan L. Monroe, the executive director at the Peabody. ''Individual artists made these 19th-century objects. The names of the artists may be lost to us now, but what you see is a tremendous drive for personal expression.''

He said the arrangement of the objects was also intended to dispel the widely held notion that Eskimos and American Indians ceased making art after they came in contact with Europeans and Asians.

Among the show stoppers is a bird-shaped flute that was carved by a Dakota Indian before 1850; it resembles a simple pipe with an elongated open beak at one end. Another is a slender gray stone pipe that was produced by an artist of the Micmac people; the surface looks like an elaborately embossed book binding.

The works by coastal artists from the Pacific Northwest, both Eskimo and Indian, are among the most unusual in the show. These include ''Winking Mask,'' an Inuit carving of the face of a fox with a whimsically sly expression, and ''Hat With Walrus Whiskers,'' an Aleut hunter's hat from the 19th century, embellished with beads and carved ivory birds.

JUDGING BY MANY OF the contemporary works on view, that same creative spirit continues today, some of it laced with wit and social commentary. Teresa MacPhee Marshall, a Micmac artist, painted boldly abstract images on a work that includes a pair of boots, a helmet, a rifle and a safe. It is called ''Peace, Order and Good Government.'' Richard Glazer-Danay, a Mohawk and former construction worker, covered the surface of a hard hat with stars, hearts, a wooden nickel and a wristwatch with a beaded band and a face decorated with a bingo card, from which the piece derives its name, ''Bingo War Bonnet.''

''The museum has survived and grown by reinventing itself,'' Mr. Monroe said. ''The founders of this museum were among the first to have direct experience of the world's cultures. And now we are finding that creating links between the cultures of the Americas, Asia and the Pacific makes as much sense today as it did in 1799.''
