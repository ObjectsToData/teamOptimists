A High-Tech Library Ignites Dispute Over Computers vs. Books

By TIM GOLDEN
Published: January 26, 1997

When the City Librarian unveiled the $140 million new Main Library here last spring, his metaphor might have been the building's wide, inviting rotunda, filled with light and new patrons and a computer-age promise that the information available would grow ever more abundant -- never mind the shelves.

Less than a year later, however, the rotunda has become a symbol to the librarian's local critics of a vision skewed: of hapless mismanagement in the design of the new building, of book space sacrificed for computer terminals, of a research sanctuary where love for the humanities and social sciences has gotten lost in a rush to get on-line.

The librarian, Kenneth E. Dowlin, is still considered a leading light in his profession. He is one of two candidates for the next presidency of the American Library Association and an articulate proponent of using communication technologies to create a new kind of public space. At the triumphant opening of the new Main, he seemed a perfect fit for the San Francisco of the future, a self-styled capital of the Information Age.

But this is sometimes a narcissistic city, one that likes to think of itself as having many good sides. And most of those sides, it seems, had imagined a library in their image.

So it was that advocates for a democratic, trash-novel mainstream have bumped up against members of the literary elite, and champions of high-tech change have battled library workers in what remains a stubbornly union town. So it was that when Mr. Dowlin walked through his rotunda one afternoon last week, it was to announce his forced resignation after nearly a decade in the job.

''I've never enjoyed politics much,'' the soft-spoken librarian said afterward outside the new Main's Latino/Hispanic Community Meeting Room.

The immediate cause of Mr. Dowlin's collapse was acute financial chaos. The public library system, which had seen its budget nearly double to $35.7 million after San Franciscans voted in a 1994 initiative to set aside some of their property tax revenue for its revival, was running $2.8 million short.

To attribute his demise to the deficit would be like blaming some last, bad defenses against the barbarians for the fall of the Roman Empire.

''My real gripe about the San Francisco Public Library is that they were, without any permission from the people, transforming a great research institution into a mediocre popular library, and doing that by throwing books in a landfill,'' the novelist Nicholson Baker said.

Mr. Baker, a sometime resident of Berkeley, across the bay, was recruited to the cause last spring by a traditionalist core of San Francisco librarians. By then, the traditionalists and their union, Local 790 of the Service Employees Union, had moved from fights with Mr. Dowlin over mundane issues like staffing levels to a guerrilla campaign against what they saw as a great old library's desecration.

In an article in The New Yorker that rallied the insurgents, Mr. Baker described the new Main as a great gray dungeon where a priceless card catalogue was coldly destroyed and defenseless books were condemned to the Discard Room or the ''Deselection Chamber.'' More than 200,000 books ended up at the dump, he wrote last October, ''many of them old, hard to find, out of print and valuable.''

In interviews, seven librarians critical of Mr. Dowlin's tenure described a different process. They said that for years, the San Francisco Public Library had done very little ''weeding,'' as librarians call the standard practice of purging damaged and less-used books to make room for the new. In preparation for their move to new quarters, the old Main's librarians sometimes hurried to get rid of unused books, throwing out hundreds that they perhaps should have kept. But the librarians said the scope and the sloppiness of the weeding had been exaggerated.

As Mr. Dowlin arrived in San Francisco nine years ago, a local newspaper columnist suggested that the city would need the Clint Eastwood of librarians to deal with its lawless athenaeum.

Instead, it got what other city officials describe as a shy, somewhat nerdy bureaucrat who fought doggedly for an electronic library but spent little time retraining his staff to run it. Although he was not responsible for financing decisions that left the building without enough room for all the library's books, they said, he did grow detached from its day-to-day operations.

Some signs of a final verdict on Mr. Dowlin's library may be seen in the booming growth of its new, working-class patrons and the lines of teen-agers who wait patiently for time with its Internet terminals and CD-ROMS.

''It's so sad to be debating books versus computers, when the issues facing San Francisco are of literacy, of immigrant populations,'' said Peter Lyman, the University Librarian at the University of California at Berkeley. ''The public library needs to be a place that helps children learn the basic skills of literacy, that helps immigrants become citizens. Ken was trying to create a sense of political community through technology, and the new library is in many ways successful on those grounds.''
