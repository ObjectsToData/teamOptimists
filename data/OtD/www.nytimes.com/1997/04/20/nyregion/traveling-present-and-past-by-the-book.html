Traveling, Present And Past, by the Book

By ALBERTA EISEMAN
Published: April 20, 1997

SPRING is the season for planning trips and delving into books describing alluring destinations. Just in time to inspire the would-be traveler comes a conference entitled ''On the Road: Three Centuries of Travel Literature,'' to be held next Saturday at Water's Edge in Westbrook.

The day-long conference, open to the public, was organized by the Connecticut Humanities Council on the occasion of its 22d annual meeting.

''We believe this is the first time in the state that a conference has been devoted solely to the literature of travel,'' said Bruce Fraser, executive director of the Connecticut Humanities Council, the state arm of the National Endowment for the Humanities.

The day will start with a keynote address by Charles Kuralt, whose programs for CBS were based on extensive travel.

Nine concurrent seminars will explore topics including Europeans in search of America, Americans abroad in the 19th century and today, the rise of tourism in New England and travel in Connecticut. Participants have been assigned readings by Mark Twain, William James, John Steinbeck and other wanderers somewhat less well known.

For example, the seminar dealing with contemporary American novelists and the image of the tourist will be based on ''The Accidental Tourist'' by Anne Tyler and ''The Loss of the Creature'' by Walker Percy.

The seminar leader is Lisa Berglund, who teaches a freshman course in travel literature at Connecticut College.

''Tourism Comes to New England'' will be led Dona Brown of the University of Vermont, author of ''Inventing New England: Regional Tourism in the 19th Century.'' The book, enhanced by case studies of communities in the White Mountains, Martha's Vineyard and coastal Maine, relates how the tourist industry was created to combat economic hardship in the region.

''This new tourism,'' writes Ms. Brown, ''was driven by a profound sentimentalization of New England, a new vision of the region expressed in extensive literature . . . and in architectural and landscape reforms that began to transform the appearance of many towns and villages.''

Participants in the seminar on Connecticut have been assigned excerpts from narratives by seven travelers, starting with the diary that John Winthrop Jr. kept in 1645, detailing his arduous journey from Massachusetts to Enfield, Hartfield, Saybrook and the New London area.

Winthrop, who became governor of the colony, describes frequent encounters with Indians, who guided him across rivers and through forests, sheltered him in their homes and provided him with venison and other food for his journey.

Later voyagers offered a varied commentary on the new state. Luigi Castiglioni, an Italian author and botanist who crisscrossed the United States from 1785 to 1787, commented on the number of churches ''even in the most wretched of villages.''

Castiglioni, who writes admiringly about the state's form of government, religious practices and agriculture, devotes almost as much space to the mores of the state's young women, denouncing the custom of ''bundling'' and concluding that ''love in the United States of America and especially in the northern states is not as ardent or as refined as in most of Europe.''

''It will be interesting to explore how Connecticut presented itself,'' said a seminar leader, Herbert F. Janick, professor of history at Western Connecticut State University in Danbury. ''What did residents show the visitor? How did they define themselves, and how did visitors define the state?''

At day's end, participants will get a notebook and a disposable camera. ''We want people to go out and record their impressions of quintessential Connecticut,'' said Mr. Fraser. ''We want to know what makes them stop and say, 'That's Connecticut.' ''

Admission, which includes lunch and a reception, is $40. For advance registration call the Connecticut Humanities Council at 860-685-2260.
