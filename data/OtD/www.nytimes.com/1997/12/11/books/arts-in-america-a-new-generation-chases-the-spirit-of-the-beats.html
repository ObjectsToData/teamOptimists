Arts in America; A New Generation Chases the Spirit of the Beats

By Doreen Carvajal
Published: December 11, 1997

Whenever the sun goes down on some old broken-down river pier in America, someone is savoring Jack Kerouac's ''On the Road'' for the first time and dreaming of rucksack rebellion. And someone is studying William S. Burroughs's ''Naked Lunch,'' or perhaps just stealing it.

The Beat Generation of writers were born disillusioned, but their message of freedom and the jazzy vernacular of the street has endured and touched new generations of readers who are reviving the popularity of writers like Kerouac, Burroughs and Allen Ginsberg.

In Milan, Italy, the Benetton Foundation, the charitable arm of the Benetton chain of youthful clothing stores, is preparing to open a library in an 18th-century palazzo to house a 40,000-book collection of Beat writing and personal letters amassed by the Italian translator Fernanda Pivano.

''They had a great impact,'' said Luciano Benetton, 62, chairman of the foundation and the Benetton Group, who concedes that he personally prefers the writing of Ernest Hemingway. ''The values brought forward by the Beat Generation are still present: freedom, nonviolence, equality among races.''

Sensing a surge in popularity, publishers in the United States are rushing to prepare new biographies, repackaging out-of-print books into compilations like ''The Herbert Huncke Reader'' (William Morrow & Company) or issuing new material like Kerouac's notes and reflections on Buddhism, ''Some of the Dharma'' (Viking).

And such is the appeal of Beat classics that the titles have the rare distinction of needing special security.

At Tower Video and Books on Lafayette Street in Manhattan, a few blocks from the Chelsea row house where Kerouac wrote ''On the Road,'' copies of the book are not shelved in the general stacks because of high demand among thieves.

A small sign advises customers searching for works by Kerouac or Burroughs to seek the help of a clerk, who guards the books kept below the cash-register counter.

''On the Road,'' Kerouac's chronicle of his years roaming the North American continent with his friend Neal Cassady, seemed to take on a new shelf life in 1991 when steady annual sales of 25,000 quadrupled in one year.

Now its publisher, Penguin Putnam, is selling three editions of ''On the Road,'' including a 40-year-anniversary issue, that together have helped to push total sales of the title since 1957 to more than three million. Penguin is also planning to release a new Kerouac biography, a volume of letters, ''On the Road'' journals and early unpublished writing, ''Atop an Underwood.''

''Why the yearning for the Beats?'' said Ira Silverberg, editor-in-chief of Grove Press, an imprint of Grove/ Atlantic, which is publishing a new Burroughs reader in August and a 40-year-anniversary edition of ''Naked Lunch'' in 1999. ''It provides a certain kind of hope, a certain kind of freedom, a certain kind of fantasy of what things might have been.''

City Lights Books, the independent San Francisco publisher that is a landmark in the history of the Beats, has issued a guide to the Manhattan muses of the writers (egg creams at the Gem Spa, challah French toast at the B & H Dairy) called ''The Beat Generation in New York: A Walking Tour of Jack Kerouac's City.''

Lawrence Ferlinghetti, the Beat poet and Keroauc contemporary who founded City Lights in 1953, is also experiencing the glow of increased attention. On one brisk morning in early November, tourists from Spain paused outside his bookstore and publishing offices in North Beach neighborhood of San Francisco to gaze through its darkened windows and read passages from a guidebook.

The Columbus Avenue store was closed at that hour, but Mr. Ferlinghetti was upstairs in his office, dressed in a denim jacket, his eyes a penetrating blue, preparing to leave later that afternoon to lecture at the University of Santa Cruz on the Beat Generation.

He said he receives frequent requests from abroad to franchise the name of City Lights. Speaking invitations come from places like Argentina and Italy, Colombia and Colorado. ''I get so many that I try to go to places where I want to go,'' said Mr. Ferlinghetti, who is now considering Mexico. ''Probably I'll go to Chiapas.''

His editor-in-chief at City Lights, Nancy J. Peters, said she thinks the revived interest in Beat literature may be due to the similarity between the placid Eisenhower period of the 1950's, when Beat writing flourished, to the 1990's.

''I would go further,'' Mr. Ferlinghetti said. ''There's a great desire and need for the Beat message. It appeals to youth, the idea of freedom of the road, sexual freedom and a general unrepressed life.''

On the other side of the country, at the University of Central Florida in Orlando, two students in their 20's read the Beats through different prisms. ''The vibrancy, playfulness and honesty of Beat prose and poetry has resonated with me ever since I first discovered the style in high school,'' said Tom Amundrud, 21, who is a senior majoring in humanities and cultural studies.

His friend Juan Martinez, a junior and a creative writing student, finds the Beats a bit ''loose.'' ''With the Beats,'' he said, ''there is a deliberate severance from the world, a bleak picture of the mundane and a joy in the outlandish that resonates with Generation Xers. The beatniks and today's hepsters share a shoulder-shrug at just about everything, a fashionable ennui.''

Keroauc's former editor, Ellis Amburn, who is at work on a biography of the writer for St. Martin's Press, said Keroauc would not have been surprised that he is once again in vogue among young readers.

While researching the biography in Keroauc's hometown of Lowell, Mass., Mr. Amburn said he watched young people make pilgrimages to the author's grave, leaving notes and prayers, empty beer cans and wine bottles.

Kerouac thought it would take about 25 years to regain his popularity, said Mr. Amburn, who despaired himself when the writer's books slowed in sales during the 1960's. ''He knew he had a message of enlightenment and freedom for people. Kerouac believed that people needed to be liberated from their anxieties and worries and connect with what he saw as the universal mind.''
