A Theater Where Women Are in the Director's Chair

By ALVIN KLEIN
Published: February 2, 1997

Just as Barbara Krajkowski was talking about expanding opportunities for women's independence in the theater, a voice boomed from the wings: ''Go change the set.''

''Well, someone's got to do it,'' she responded. As the stage manager, Christa Jenkins, switched audio tapes, from the joy of klezmer to the racket of tinhorns, Ms. Krajkowski helped Sharon Morey turn a Maplewood kitchen into an Atlantic City casino. The scenario: Reset the table. Off with the tuna, the pickles and the coffee. On with the green felt, the poker chips and the cocktail glasses. Remove the shopping bags, and cover the windows with black drapes.

And back to the interview in less than 60 seconds. From assisting the assistant stage manager (Ms. Morey) to working the box office -- all those chores that make a person who's ''always running around'' inevitably call herself ''chief cook and bottle-washer'' -- Ms. Krajkowski reverted to her professional identity as co-founder and producing artistic director of New Jersey Women's Theater Cooperative. She is also the director of its current play, ''Family Wills,'' the group's sixth production, which runs through next Sunday at Playwrights Theater of New Jersey, 33 Green Village Road. (Information: 201-443-9125).

''We all really help each other,'' Ms. Krajkowski said, knowing that the word cooperative has lost its meaning through overuse. Knowing, too, that questions asked by men about women's groups carry the weight of the unasked ''What about us?'' she added: ''Men are wonderful. We love men. But we run the show. Well, some nights men do, too. They just don't design it, or write it, or tech it. Let's just say the men agree to help. And the gentlemen offer.''

Ms. Krajkowski, Keaton Douglas and Margie Schiller founded the cooperative in 1993. (Ms. Schiller has since moved to Pennsylvania.) They met as members of the short-lived Blooming Grove Theater Ensemble in Montclair. ''The plays we were cast in never happened,'' Ms. Douglas recalled. ''We were just standing by.''

Ms. Krajkowski added: ''Opportunities for women to design, to build, to have a say are scarce, and we wanted to open those up in the community. That's our only agenda.''

A sometime actress, she observed: ''As we got older, parts get fewer. That might change if there are more women playwrights.''

For two years, the group held staged readings at Tierney's, a bar in Montclair. (''Family Wills,'' by Irene Tiersten of Maplewood, was the first before its present full production.) ''But we needed a space where they didn't tell you, 'Clear the stage; the band's coming,' '' Ms. Krajkowski said.

At a meeting of the New Jersey Theater Group, the consortium of the state's professional theaters, Ms. Krajkowski said ''We need a space.'' John Pietrowski, the artistic director of Playwrights Theater, replied, ''Let's talk.''

''And we don't have to get out by 9:30,'' said Ms. Krajkowski, who lives in Parsippany with her husband, Edward. Their son, John is a corporate package designer; their daughter, Jane, is a theater and television actress, currently playing one of the leads in the Broadway revival of ''Once Upon a Mattress.'' ''Ed is a U.S. Government engineer, and the rest of us are artsy,'' Ms. Krajkowski said. ''But he still applauds for us.''

The company is an affiliate member of the New Jersey Theater Group. ''That means we're working our way up to full professional status,'' Ms. Krajkowski said.

Ms. Douglas, the company's vice president and executive director, said: ''The co-op is an arts and humanities organization that gives voice to pertinent issues in the community. We use theater as a base to open up to those issues.

''When they first heard of us, people narrowly thought that lesbianism and abortion were all that pertained to us,'' added Ms. Douglas, who lives in Montclair with her 2 1/2-year-old son. In October she played an Army nurse who enlisted five months before Pearl Harbor, in ''Waiting for MacArthur,'' by Phyllis Paullette MacDougal of Summit.

That play gave way to a post-performance symposium titled ''Wacs, WASP's and War Stories.'' Today, after the 3 P.M. matinee of ''Family Wills,'' the topic is ''The Aftermath of Alzheimer's: Readjusting to Every Day Life.''

''One of our plays, 'Letting Go,' dealt with a young mother's death, written from the perspective of the husband's second wife,'' Ms. Douglas said. ''It became an educational tool about dealing with loss, and people from hospices were contacting us because the play reached them. We're talking about more than women's roles here. This is life.''
