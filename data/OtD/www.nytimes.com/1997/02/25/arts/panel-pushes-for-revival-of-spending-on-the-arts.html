Panel Pushes For Revival Of Spending On the Arts

By JUDITH MILLER
Published: February 25, 1997

A nonpartisan Presidential committee has warned that cuts in public, private and corporate spending on the arts and humanities are undermining cultural and educational institutions in the United States. In a report to be released today, the panel recommends wide-ranging measures to confront what it calls growing threats to the nation's cultural heritage.

The 35-page report of the President's Committee on the Arts and Humanities, headed by John Brademas, a former Democratic Congressman from Indiana and president emeritus of New York University, recommends that President Clinton lead a ''millennium initiative'' to revitalize public and private support for culture.

Goals of the initiative, which President Clinton is said to support, would be restoring funds for the arts in schools and requiring arts instruction for all high school students; forming private-public partnerships to make American works accessible through new technologies; fostering philanthropy through tax reform, and expanding international cultural contacts by, among other things, requiring competency in a foreign language for graduation from high school or admission to college.

The report urged Congress to enact or to preserve several tax measures that encourage individual and corporate donations to nonprofit groups. Such donations now provide 90 percent of the nation's cultural financing, the report says, and given pressures to balance the Federal budget, educational and cultural institutions will have to tap the private sector further to survive.

In implicit criticism of Congressional conservatives who have cut public subsidies for the arts and humanities and who favor abolishing those national endowments, the committee, which includes Republicans and Democrats, strongly endorses a Federal role through direct public subsidies, tax breaks and other incentives, particularly for art that highlights ''our ethnic and cultural differences.'' It also criticizes what it calls ''a climate of intolerance for challenging works and ideas.''

Mr. Brademas said he had presented a copy of the report on Friday at a meeting with President Clinton and Hilary Rodham Clinton, the committee's honorary chairwoman.

Both the Clintons, he said, were enthusiastic about the committee's recommendations, particularly the proposal that the President lead a national initiative to galvanize private support for the arts. Mrs. Clinton, a supporter of increased public funding for culture, has scheduled a news conference to discuss the report's conclusions at the Library of Congress today.

The report surveys the strengths and failings of the complex support system that sustains the nation's 8,000 museums, 30,000 libraries, 3,665 institutions of higher learning, 2,000 local preservation commissions, 351 public television stations, 548 public radio stations, 7,000 community theaters and 1,800 symphony orchestras.

Among the steps the committee recommends to strengthen support for culture are the following:

*Congress should restore Federal funds for the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the Institute of Museum and Library Services and insure that they receive appropriations equal to $2 for each person in the country by the year 2000, compared with the 88.5 cents a person committed in fiscal 1997.

*Congress should retain the charitable deduction in the Federal tax code for gifts to all nonprofit organizations and should make permanent the deductibility of gifts of appreciated publicly traded stocks to private foundations, a provision set to expire in June.

*Fourteen states and the District of Columbia should abandon efforts to bolster revenues by challenging the tax-exempt status of non-profit institutions or by imposing payments on them in lieu of taxes.

The committee expressed particular concern about the ''disturbing signs of a weakening philanthropic spirit,'' including a decline in the average size of gifts from the wealthiest Americans. Citing other studies, the report noted that even as all charitable giving increased by 11 percent in 1995 over the previous year, private contributions to the arts and humanities did not increase, when adjusted for inflation.

This trend is particularly worrisome for cultural institutions, since the arts and humanities are more dependent on private giving than many other segments of the nonprofit sector, the committee said. Performing arts groups, for instance, receive as much as 40 percent of their total income from donations, compared with 3 percent to 5 percent for hospitals and an average of less than 20 percent for many other nonprofit groups.

The report also concludes that increases in corporate donations to the arts and humanities over the past two years, which account for about 7 percent of all philanthropic donations, ''mask a long-term decline in corporate giving'' when measured as a percentage of pre-tax income. In addition, the report criticizes the trend among businesses of tying contributions to specific marketing objectives.

It cites as a positive development the 40 percent rise in foundation support for the arts in the 1980's, ''the only source of sustained increased giving to cultural organizations'' in the past decade. Particularly striking is the explosion of community foundations, whose assets jumped from $1 billion to more than $13 billion in just over 20 years.

But at the same time, the reports adds, foundation giving, which tripled from $3.4 billion to $11.3 billion from 1980 to 1994, is both highly concentrated and unevenly distributed. Citing a study by the Foundation Center in New York City, 505 foundations with assets of more than $50 million were responsible for half the grants to charitable causes.

The committee has 13 members who hold key Administration cultural posts, as well as has 31 members from outside the Government, among them the performers Isaac Stern and Rita Moreno and business executives including Terry Semel, the chairman of Warner Brothers; Harvey Golub, chairman and chief executive officer of American Express, and Robert Menschel, a limited partner of Goldman Sachs and Company.
