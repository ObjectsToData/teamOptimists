Library's Storied Sanctuary Will Be Shut For Next 2 Years For High-Tech Renovations

By BRUCE WEBER
Published: July 4, 1997

It is a great room, a grandiose room, the largest uncolumned space in New York City, not to mention the gateway to a vast underground store of knowledge, and since 1911 it has been open to anyone -- anyone with a yen to read, to carry out a secret (silent) liaison, to pursue an intellectual obsession, to come in out of the cold (or the heat), to grab a furtive nap.

As one New Yorker who was there yesterday said: ''It's extraordinary. Anyone can walk in here, crazy or not.''

It is, of course, the Main Reading Room of the New York Public Library, and for generations of New Yorkers, it has been the embodiment of the great library itself, an oasis of welcome within the intimidating structure at 42d Street and Fifth Avenue, where you went to locate a book in the cavernous reaches of the library's collection, to have it placed in your hands and -- wonder of wonders -- to read it.

The bad news is that for the next two years, renovations will force the city to do without it.

On Monday, July 14, Room 315 will be shut for the first time in an 86-year history that has spawned inventions, both scientific and artistic, and has become familiar enough through films and photographs that one library official referred to it as ''the icon of reading rooms.'' On that day only, the entire central library building -- now called the Center for the Humanities -- is to be closed to complete the move of staff and materials to temporary quarters.

After that, until February 1999, habitues of Room 315 will have to be content in the Gottesman Exhibition Hall, an elegant but much smaller room on the library's ground floor. There will be 208 seats in the temporary space -- less than half the 499 now available. And though the exhibition hall also has access to the library's 120 miles of stacks shelves, and the library is putting on extra staff, retrieving requested books is bound to be a bit slower.

The good news is the purpose -- a two-year, $15 million project that will modernize the room for the age of technology, and restore the physical grandeur that has been diminished over the years by wear and tear and the encroachments of cumbersome equipment like microfilm viewers and makeshift services like a copy center. Once again it is to be a football-field sized expanse of long study tables, uninterrupted but for a central enclosure where book requests are filled, the massively dignified room that the great architects Carrere and Hastings envisioned. The room measures 297 feet by 78 feet, and it is 52 feet high.

There will be seats for 600 readers (up from 499 now), 80 of them with computer workstations and most others wired for Internet access for laptop users. The current book retrieval system -- a combination of dumbwaiters, pneumatic tubes and an elevator -- will be revamped, the dumbwaiters replaced by an enormous modern contraption that amounts to a fancy conveyor belt.

The project is forward looking, of course, but perhaps more important, it will look back. ''It represents a renewed commitment to the book,'' said William D. Walker, director of the research libraries. ''We'll be restoring space with proper lighting and air-conditioning and heat, so you can just go up there anonymously and work with these brilliant collections. People need to sit at big tables with lots of volumes around them, feeling the original material.''

Nathan C. Hoyt, one of the architects of the project concurred. ''The use of the room should be a great experience,'' he said. ''That's what we're working toward.''

One reason there is a sense of moment as the reading room prepares to close is that it is a place rich in cultural history. Chester Carlson, inventor of the Xerox copier, did his research there; so did Edward Land, the inventor of instant photography (remember the Polaroid Land camera?) And Norbert Pearlroth, who worked there almost daily for five decades, compiled the newspaper columns that became the volumes of Ripley's Believe It Or Not.

In 1933, Lillian Gish was the first user of the brand-new theater collection, then housed in the reading room. (It is now in the Performing Arts Library at Lincoln Center.) In the film, ''Breakfast at Tiffany's,'' a lovestruck George Peppard proved to Audrey Hepburn that he was a writer by showing her the card for his book in the card catalogue. And in ''Ghostbusters,'' the first evidence of ghostly slime was uncovered by an unsuspecting reading room page.

The list of writers who have have worked there includes Norman Mailer, Isaac Bashevis Singer, Elizabeth Bishop, E. L. Doctorow and Alfred Kazin, whose paean to the reading room -- where he researched his first book of literary criticism, ''On Native Ground'' -- appears at the start of his memoir ''New York Jew.''

''Year after year I seemed to have nothing more delightful to do than to sit much of the day and many an evening at one of those great golden tables acquainting myself with every side of my subject,'' he wrote. ''Whenever I was free to read, the great Library seemed free to receive me. Anything I had heard of and wanted to see, the blessed place owned.''

But the reading room is less known -- and less important -- for its famous users than its anonymous ones.
