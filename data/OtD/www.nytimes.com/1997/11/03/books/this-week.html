This Week


Published: November 3, 1997

EVENTS

Master Touch

LONDON -- One of the world's most extraordinary pianos is to be sold at auction on Friday morning at Christie's on King Street, where it is expected to fetch from $900,000 to $1.2 million at a sale of Victorian pictures, drawings and other works of art. The ornate Alma-Tadema pianoforte, a heavily adorned Steinway, was the centerpiece of a most generous commission. In 1884, Henry G. Marquand, a founder of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in Manhattan, granted the artist Sir Lawrence Alma-Tadema (1836-1912) a limitless budget to decorate the music salon of his mansion on Madison Avenue and 68th Street. Alma-Tadema recruited the best painters, furniture makers and decorative artists of his day. The sellers requested anonymity. (Information: 44-171-389-2468.)

Running Times

LONDON -- It's 1997 and not 1984, but the spirit of George Orwell will be very much present when the London Film Festival opens on Thursday with a screening of ''Keep the Aspidistra Flying.'' Starring Richard E. Grant and Helena Bonham Carter, this new screen version of the 1936 Orwell novel is among 180 feature films and 100 shorts to be shown at six sites around the West End before the festival ends on Nov. 23. Other attractions of the festival include a rediscovered ''lost'' Michael Powell film, ''His Lordship,'' made in 1932, and a restored print of David Lean's ''Oliver Twist'' (1948). (Information: 44-171-420-1122.)

Czar Turn

It's back to the days of Imperial Russia beginning tomorrow in the Edna Barnes Salomon Room at the The New York Public Library's Center for the Humanities at Fifth Avenue and 42d Street. There, 124 of the 3,000 Romanov volumes acquired by the library from the Soviet Union in the 1920's and 30's will be displayed for the first time. The exhibition, titled ''The Romanovs: Their Empire, Their Books'' includes lavishly illustrated volumes, photographs, watercolors and religious objects. The exhibition remains on view through Feb. 28, 1998. (Information: 212-869-8089.)

Branching Out

BERLIN -- The city gets a new museum on Friday when Deutsche Guggenheim Berlin, at Unter den Linden 13-15, opens to the public with the premiere of an exhibition of paintings by Robert Delaunay (1885-1941). Titled ''Visions of Paris: Robert Delaunay's Series,'' the exhibition runs through Jan. 4 and then will travel to the Guggenheim Museum in on Fifth Avenue in Manhattan, where it will be on view from Jan. 16 to April 20. (Information: 49-30-202-093-13.)

Mouth and Hoof

Song, dance and a touch of France come to the 92d Street Y on Saturday, when the Gallic chanteuse Liliane Montevecchi and the screen and stage star Jean-Pierre Cassel team up in the Y's ''Cabaret in Concert'' series. In the three-part, intermissionless shows, at 7 and 9:30 P.M., they will perform individually and collectively. In Part I, ''La Lecon Francais,'' Ms. Montevecchi will perform popular songs that originated in France and crossed the Atlantic. In Part II, ''Fred by Myself,'' Mr. Cassel, making his American stage debut, will offer a song-and dance tribute to Fred Astaire. The joint finale, ''Paris-New York,'' will feature classics like ''C'Est Si Bon,'' ''I Love Paris'' and ''Un Homme et Une Femme.'' (Information: 212-996-1100.)

The Handel Watch

The New York City Opera's new production of Handel's ''Xerxes'' reflects the first real empowerment over the company's repertory by its new director, Paul Kellogg. Review, page 5.
