Supreme Court Roundup; Justices to Rule On Values Test For Arts Grants

By LINDA GREENHOUSE
Published: November 27, 1997

The long-running debate over Federal spending on the arts moved to the Supreme Court today as the Justices agreed to rule on whether the Constitution permits Congress to make ''decency'' a test for the awarding of grants by the National Endowment for the Arts.

The Court agreed to hear the Clinton Administration's defense of a 1990 law requiring the endowment to take into consideration ''general standards of decency and respect for the diverse beliefs and values of the American public'' when making grants to artists and arts organizations.

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, in San Francisco, declared the provision unconstitutional last year in a lawsuit brought by Karen Finley and three other performance artists whose grant proposals had been rejected. The National Association of Artists' Organizations, representing several hundred individuals and groups, was also a plaintiff in the lawsuit.

On a busy pre-Thanksgiving day at the Court, the Justices also agreed to hear an important case on the protections available under the Americans With Disabilities Act for people who have the virus that causes AIDS but who have no symptoms of disease. The case is an appeal by a dentist whose refusal to treat any H.I.V.-positive patient in his office, without making an individualized assessment of each patient's condition, was found by two lower Federal courts to violate the 1990 anti-discrimination law.

In the National Endowment for the Arts case, N.E.A. v. Finley, No. 97-371, the Administration is arguing that Congress made ''a legitimate legislative choice respecting the expenditure of public funds'' when it added the decency proviso to the National Foundation on the Arts and the Humanities Act.

Congress acted in response to the controversy that was then raging over the work of two artists who had received grants from the endowment: the homoerotic photographs of Robert Mapplethorpe, and a photograph by Andres Serrano of a crucifix immersed in the artist's urine.

In 1989, Congress had voted to bar the endowment from spending appropriated funds to ''promote, disseminate or produce'' depictions of ''sadomasochism, homoeroticism and sexual exploitation of children or individuals engaged in sex acts.'' A Federal district court in Los Angeles declared that law unconstitutional, and the Government did not appeal.

But in 1990, Congress adopted the decency provision now at issue. In striking it down on a 2-to-1 vote, a panel of the Ninth Circuit cited the constitutional guarantees of both free speech and due process. Because the language is so vague, Judge James R. Browning said in the majority opinion, the decency standard ''gives rise to the danger of arbitrary and discriminatory application,'' handing Government officials the power to refuse to make grants to artists with controversial political or social messages.

The dissenting opinion, by Judge Andrew J. Kleinfeld, said that ''when a democratic government pays artists to stick their thumbs in the public's eye, the public naturally becomes annoyed, and attempts to exercise its ordinary authority in a democracy to control through Congress how tax monies are spent.''

''That offensive or indecent expression cannot be censored,'' Judge Kleinfeld added, ''does not mean that the Government has to pay for it.''

In its Supreme Court appeal, the Administration maintains that the decency provision has a ''much more limited reach'' than the appeals court assumed. The provision sets only a general standard of ''aesthetic concepts or guideposts'' without suppressing particular viewpoints or prescribing the endowment's ultimate choices in awarding grants, the brief asserts.

Debates in recent years over the Government's selective financing of speech or expression have produced a complex and contradictory body of First Amendment law, which the Court will presumably now review and try to reconcile.

In a 1991 decision, Rust v. Sullivan, for example, the Court upheld a regulation that withheld Federal family planning money from otherwise qualified clinics and doctors who provided patients information about abortion. That decision shows, the Government maintains in the new case, that the First Amendment permits selective financing of those activities the Government ''believes should be encouraged in the broader public interest.''

But in a 1995 case, Rosenberger v. Rector, the Court held that the University of Virginia could not disqualify an overtly Christian student newspaper from receiving a share of money from the student activity fund. In striking down the decency provision, the Ninth Circuit majority relied on that decision for the proposition that the Government cannot selectively withhold money on the basis of a ''prohibited perspective.''

H.I.V. Discrimination

The Court agreed to resolve a disagreement among the lower Federal courts over whether an H.I.V.-positive condition, without actual symptoms of AIDS, is a disability for purposes of the Americans With Disabilities Act.
