Declarations of Independence

By By Jonathan Rosen
Published: September 14, 1997

Required Reading

Why Our American Classics

Matter Now.

By Andrew Delbanco.

226 pp. New York:

Farrar, Straus & Giroux. $24.

When I was a graduate student in English literature 10 years ago, my biggest problem was that the poststructuralist hotshots who brought luster to the department didn't seem to like literature very much, while the defenders of traditional values, in whose classrooms I sought refuge, engendered a vague feeling of irrelevance. It's a lot more fun watching the Grinch steal Christmas than it is watching the inhabitants of Whoville celebrate it. Unsuited for either enterprise, I packed my books and left.

All the more reason I admire Andrew Delbanco for staying in the academy and seeking a middle ground. In ''Required Reading: Why Our American Classics Matter Now,'' he wants to put the pleasure back into reading but he wants, as well, to give great books a new urgency. Writing with energy, insight and affection, Delbanco -- a professor of the humanities at Columbia University -- examines 11 American writers, beginning with Melville, ending with Zora Neale Hurston and including Harriet Beecher Stowe, Theodore Dreiser and Henry Adams. Though his primary concerns are literary, Delbanco would like his essays to have a political overtone somewhat in the manner of Lionel Trilling's ''Liberal Imagination.''

Trilling, writing 50 years ago, asserted the importance of individual experience in the face of Stalinism. To hear Delbanco characterize academia -- he uses words like ''purge'' and ''loyalty oath'' -- Trilling should be living at this hour. Contemporary literary theory tends to pit politics against literature -- seeing ''Heart of Darkness,'' say, as an unwitting instrument of the corrupt colonial world that produced it.

Delbanco, by declaring in his preface that the ''politics implicit in classic American writing are those of liberal democracy,'' suggests that great American writers, if only through the use of an original prose style, asserted the power of the individual that gives life to our political system. In other words, he hopes to raise the stakes of old-fashioned close reading by implying, in some sense, that American classics are all declarations of independence, if only we know how to read them right. If we lose our ability to appreciate them, we lose nothing less than our freedom.

Delbanco's essays are effortlessly erudite and smoothly readable. He has a good ear for the telltale quotation and he pans the familiar streams of American literature for forgotten gold. He writes with insight not only about ''Walden'' but about the stark, mysterious passages of ''Cape Cod'' in which Thoreau examines the beached bodies of drowned Irish immigrants whose ship has broken up. He evokes the ''almost brutal immediacy'' of Richard Wright's ''Native Son,'' writing with a sensual appreciation for a novel he aptly sees as a ''book about the imperatives of the body.'' He is equally at home with the worldly, ironic, oddly wistful character of Henry Adams in ''The Education.''

But this is a book that promises a great deal -- it is, after all, called ''Required Reading'' -- and judged against its own large claims, it engenders a certain feeling of disappointment. Part of the problem may be the occasional nature of the individual pieces, most of which have been published previously. As reviews of, say, a new biography or critical edition, they are very fine examples of literary journalism. As essays bolstering grand claims about American literature and the role of the critic in society they are less effective.

I can't help thinking, however, that something else is undermining these essays, something that may have to do with Delbanco's aversion to certain soulless forms of literary criticism, particularly the sort that ask only ''What's wrong with this book?'' Eager to avoid all reductive methods, Delbanco has become a little overprotective of his writers, as if he fears that universities, which are only the foster parents of literature, will lose custody if they continue to abuse their charges.

In his conclusion, aptly called ''Reading for Pleasure,'' Delbanco writes about the need for teachers of literature to be ''professors in the old religious sense of that word: believers, testifiers, witnesses.'' Delbanco has that zeal, but there are moments when he seems determined to save his authors not merely from the depredations of contemporary criticism but from themselves. His essay on Melville concludes that the author of ''Moby-Dick,'' despite his belief that ''the only sounds audible in the cosmos are echoes of the human voice,'' has ''let us hear God.'' Delbanco ends a discussion of Dreiser, an avowed atheist, by noting that his prose is ''so compassionate that it reaches the intensity of religious devotion.''

But must Dreiser be religious, or Melville -- who himself called ''Moby-Dick'' a ''wicked book'' -- a channel for the voice of God, in order for them to be great writers? At one point, Delbanco shares the convincing insight that Henry Adams may have excluded all reference to his wife's suicide in ''The Education'' because ''he could not bear to write about this event in the ironic voice of the rest of the 'autobiography.' ''

The fact that Delbanco is probably right only makes it all the more frustrating that in a book profoundly concerned with the stylistic details of writing, he fails to wonder what it might mean that a great American writer employed a prose style unable to accommodate the single most important thing that ever happened to him. Although my sympathies are all with Delbanco, I missed some of the questions his colleagues might press -- about the failure of writing to sustain certain truths, about the multiplicity of meaning that can at times overwhelm understanding, about the nihilistic impulse that runs deep in a number of these writers. About the prejudices these writers possessed -- like the anti-Semitism of Henry Adams -- as well as the ones they wrote against.

Delbanco touches on all these things but too often hurries past them on his way to the redemptive ending he seeks in nearly every essay. At his best, however, as in the beautifully modulated essay on Zora Neale Hurston, Delbanco (perhaps because Hurston is a less conventionally great writer) shows an admirable ability to examine the valuations of recent criticism alongside his own.

His conclusion, that ''Hurston belongs among the American classics not because of her politics but because of her language,'' is correct, but the essay is stronger for the contrary impulses -- in both Hurston and the critical culture -- that Delbanco gives voice to.

It does not take many references to his colleagues' concern with ''privileged'' readings and ''subaltern'' writers to make me realize how lucky we are to have Delbanco in the academy at all. But it is precisely because he writes well and loves literature so much that I would like to see him follow the example of Abraham Lincoln, one of the more surprising inclusions in this volume. Lincoln, clearly a hero of the author, managed, despite deep distaste, to give his enemies a home in his thinking. To be sure, many critics have seceded intellectually from the sustaining truths of literary study that Delbanco understands well. But it may be an ironic inevitability that only by incorporating more of what they have to say into his own lofty understanding can Delbanco truly give the study of literature the new birth of freedom it so desperately needs.
