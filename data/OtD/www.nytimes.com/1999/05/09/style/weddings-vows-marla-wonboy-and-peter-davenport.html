WEDDINGS: VOWS; Marla Wonboy and Peter Davenport

By LOIS SMITH BRADY
Published: May 9, 1999

AT the Cranbrook Kingswood Upper School, a prep school in Bloomfield Hills, Mich., Marla Wonboy and Peter Davenport were both in the class of '85, but that was about all they had in common.

An unassuming, insecure daughter of a steel broker, Marla spent many Saturday nights at home, Cinderella-like, sewing her own clothes. Peter was flirtatious, dashing, dramatic and a great-grandson of Charles Stewart Mott, the industrialist and philanthropist.

''He had this dark brown wavy hair like someone on the cover of a romance novel,'' Ms. Wonboy said. ''I thought: 'He's so popular and cute. He'll hate me.' ''

Occasionally, she did try to communicate nonverbally. ''We had a humanities class together, and he used to sit beside this Barbie doll,'' she said. ''She had a tan and the flippy hair and frosty lipstick and a fuzzy sweater and a peach miniskirt. And she would lean on his desk and chew the end of her pencil. I used to send subliminal messages to Peter: 'You hate her -- she's a Barbie doll. You like me, someone who has sense.' ''

Her messages never appeared to reach him, and after high school, they went their separate ways. Years later, both ended up in Manhattan, she to study fashion design at the Fashion Institute of Technology and he to pursue a career in acting. In 1991, on a break from a drawing class, she ran into him in a coffee shop. She knew who he was instantly. ''I didn't recognize her at all,'' said Mr. Davenport, now an actor working in regional theater and a drama student at Sarah Lawrence College.

They talked, and soon began getting together for suppers, afternoons on in-line skates and long chats. ''We would go out and he was kind of flirty, but he was dating other people,'' said Ms. Wonboy, who worked as a shoe designer for Isaac Mizrahi from 1992 to 1997. ''I would think: 'O.K., I'm going to get hurt. I just have to remember this is a friendship.' ''

Over the next six years, their friendship grew closer and closer. Then, as he tells it, ''we looked at each other one day and said: 'Gosh, everything else has been unfulfilling. What about what we have going on here?' ''

Last July, she moved into his apartment, but soon panicked because he didn't seem serious enough about their relationship. So one day she called him on his cell phone -- he was trying on shoes at Saks Fifth Avenue -- to say she was moving out. ''Two minutes later, someone called me at my desk and said, 'Peter is here for you,' '' she said. ''He must have left the shoes and ran for my office. He said, 'I just need a hug.' ''

After that, she returned to work; he went directly to Tiffany's. ''That afternoon, I got the ring,'' he said. ''I was like: 'This will get her. This always works on women, so I've been told.' ''

Meanwhile, she continued preparing to move. That evening, she put on a moody Lisa Loeb album and began packing.

''He comes home in this great mood,'' said Ms. Wonboy, who is now developing her own line of shoes and party clothes. ''He's acting all goofy and throwing me around, and as I'm flying through the air, I picked up that Tiffany blue. I thought: 'Well, Peter probably keeps his bills in a Tiffany bag. What kind of a fool would I be to think we're going to get married?' ''

He proposed that night, and on May 1 they were married at St. James' Episcopal Church on the Upper East Side. A long silence preceded the formal 10:45 A.M. ceremony. Then the organ started and the bridegroom and ushers walked swiftly out in their morning suits, like a flock of birds flushed out of a bush.

The bride wore an off-white satin wedding dress that belonged to Mr. Davenport's mother, Joan Mitchell MacGillivray, and shimmered like the polished inside of a conch shell. During the reception at the New York Botanical Garden in the Bronx, many people spoke of Ms. Wonboy as a contemporary Cinderella -- another talented seamstress and underdog who finally got the guy.
