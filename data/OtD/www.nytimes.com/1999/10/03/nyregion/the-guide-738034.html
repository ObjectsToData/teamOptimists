THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: October 3, 1999

Wildlife Photographs

Some exceptional photographs of wildlife on the verge of extinction make up an exhibition at the Tremaine Gallery of the Hotchkiss School in Lakeville through Oct. 22.

One section contains Russ Hansen's photographs of birds in flight. He has been snapping birds for almost two decades, luring them within three feet of his camera. A former electrical engineer and physics teacher, he has recorded more than 120 species on film in North and Central America, Canada and the West Indies. He has designed and built his own equipment and received two grants from the National Geographic Society to help him do so. His photographs have appeared in numerous publications, museums and books.

The collaborative photographs of Susan Middleton and David Liittschwager make up the companion half of the exhibition. They are authors and photographers of ''Here Today: Portraits of Our Vanishing Species'' (1991) and ''Witness: Endangered Species of North America'' (1994), published by Chronicle Books.

Their intimate portraits of mammals, plants, birds, reptiles, fish, mollusks, amphibians, arachnids, and crustaceans create a vivid and disturbing tableau of what may soon be lost.

The school is at the intersecton of Routes 41 and 112. The gallery is open Monday through Saturday from 10 to 4, Sunday from noon to 4. Call (860) 435-2591 for more information.

Celebrating Goethe

The 250th anniversary of the birth of Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749-1832) is to be celebrated at Yale next weekend. The event will focus on aspects of his life that reach beyond his credential as the author of ''Faust.'' All events are open to the public free of charge.

An opening address will be presented by Charles Rosen, pianist, author and critic, on Friday at 5:15 in Morse Recital Hall of Sprague Hall, 470 College Street. It will be followed by a reception at the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library for an exhibition titled ''Goethe the Scientist,'' on display through Dec. 22. It contains prints, charts, illustrations, treatises, letters and illustrated books by Goethe and some of his contemporaries. Dr. Manfred Osten, Secretary General of the Alexander von Humboldt Foundation, which promotes scholarly cooperation between Germany and the United States, will speak on Saturday at 9 A.M. in the Whitney Humanities Center, 53 Wall Street. His topic will be ''Accelerated Time: A Few Remarks on the Modernity of Goethe.'' The afternoon will be devoted to discussions by scholars from Yale, the University of Glasgow and Humboldt University in Berlin, on the influence of Goethe on our contemporary culture.

A reading of Goethe's play, ''Iphigenia in Tauris'' will be presented on Saturday evening and again on Sunday, when the final event will be a roundtable discussion of ''Current Issues and Further Challenges in the Study of Goethe'' from 9:30 to 1. For more information call (203) 432-0781 or E-mail to german@yale.edu.

Benefit for the River

A three-day benefit for the Connecticut River Museum in Essex next weekend includes a food festival on Thursday, a lunch and lecture by Katharine Houghton, the niece of Katherine Hepburn, on Friday, and the Bruce Yenawine Challenge Cup Road Race on Sunday.

A mammoth tent set up at riverside will contain international foods from Bee and Thistle Inn, Cafe Routier, the Griswold Inn, Crow's Nest, and Saybrook Point Inn and Spa, and other restaurants and caterers at the 6 P.M. food festival. Music and an open bar will be ongoing, and at 9 P.M. a $10,000 global travel certificate will be raffled off. Raffle tickets are $100, admission to the feast is $60.

Ms. Houghton's talk, titled ''My Grandmother's House by the River,'' will be devoted to memories and anecdotes of the celebrities, friends, and the Houghton and Hepburn family members who visited or lived in the commodious residence in the Fenwick section of Old Saybrook. Tickets for the talk and a gourmet box lunch are $30.

A four-mile road race will begin Sunday at 10 A.M. from Essex Town Hall, ending at the museum on Steamboat Dock at the foot of Main Street, where awards will be presented. Registration is $12 in advance, $15 on race day. Registration for a children's fun run at 9:30 A.M. is $8. For tickets, reservations or more information call (860) 767-8269.

Indian Trails, Village Greens

Northeastern Connecticut, known at ''the quiet corner'' invites visitors to a wealth of guided historic, cultural and nature walks and talks throughout the region's 25 towns.

For the ninth consecutive year people can hike the Indian trails, pause at the village greens, explore the cemeteries, admire the Colonial and Victorian architecture, tour homesteads and mills, commune with wild fauna and flora, and backpack around the lakes.

Almost everything is free, except where an autumn festival is in progress, or for accommodations at a campground.

Fifty events on Saturday, Sunday, and Monday, Oct. 18, all suitable for families, are listed in a ''Walking Weekend'' brochure that may be obtained by calling (860) 928-1228.

Antiques in Washington

The 13th Washington Antiques Show will bring 22 dealers from Maine to California to the Bryan Memorial Town Hall in Washington on Friday through next Sunday.

American, European and Oriental antiques -- including furniture, paintings, ceramics, silver, carpets, accessories and jewelry -- may be previewed on Friday from 6:30 to 9:30 P.M. Cocktails and supper are included in the $75 ticket price, and reservations must be made by 5 P.M. on Tuesday. Regular admission on Saturday from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. and next Sunday from 11 to 5 will be $6. All proceeds go to the Gunn Memorial Library and Museum.

There's More, Folks

How can anyone resist those eyes, those ears, those incisors? It's that lovable rascal Bugs Bunny, and his pals from the Warner Brothers cartoon factory who are on display at the Barnum Museum in Bridgeport until January 8.

Adults should not resist the urge to view this exhibition without the children, since these cartoons, with their convoluted, devilish plots, were always conceived more for adults than for children.

Roadrunner, Porky Pig, Elmer Fudd, Daffy Duck and the rest of the barnyard from Merry Melodies and Looney Tunes will be there in their original forms as drawings, paintings, and cels -- the transparent celluloid images that became animated films, exhibiting the conceptual side of cartoonmaking that the public rarely gets to see.

The films, created by the Warner Brothers stable of cartoon directors including Chuck Jones, Tex Avery, Friz Freleng, Bob Clampett and more than 100 artists, designers, writers, and technicians, are as popular today on television as they were 50 years ago.

The Barnum Museum, a red sandstone Romanesque building, is an architectural treat in itself. Situated at 820 Main Street, it is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. Visiting hours are Tuesday through Saturday from 10 A.M. to 4:30 P.M., Sunday from noon to 4:30.

Admission is $5, $4 for older people and college students, $3 for children 4 through 18. More information may be obtained by calling (203) 331-9881, or using the Web site, www.barnum-museum.org. ELEANOR CHARLES
