UP FRONT: ON THE MAP; Giving the State's Literary Lights a Place to Shine

By ERIC EPSTEIN
Published: January 10, 1999

Many New Jersey residents would be hard pressed to name a famous author from their state. Dr. Herman Estrin's mission is to change that. In 1976, Dr. Estrin founded the New Jersey Literary Hall of Fame, and since then, the hall has inducted 144 writers.

To be eligible, an author must have lived in New Jersey for at least four years. The inductees include such well-known authors as Walt Whitman, Joyce Carol Oates and Mary Higgins Clark, and others not known primarily for their writing, like Albert Einstein, Thomas Kean and Richard M. Nixon.

The Hall of Fame is housed at the New Jersey Institute of Technology, in Newark, which helps finance the project. To make an appointment to visit, call the N.J.I.T. Alumni Office at (973) 596-3447. Dr. Estrin, 83, of Scotch Plains, a professor emeritus of English at the school, recently talked about the Hall of Fame.

Q. Why did you decide to create the Hall of Fame?

A. When I was a full-time professor at N.J.I.T., I put on a number of writers' conferences. And I found that there are some outstanding New Jersey writers, both past and current. Many of the people who come to the exhibits say, ''I didn't know he was from New Jersey.'' And that's what we try to emphasize, that New Jersey is not a literary wasteland, but a sea of great literature. I taught at the University of Paris, and my host professor invited me to his house. He had a Mary Higgins Clark book on his shelf. And I said, ''I know her.''

Q. What are the criteria for the Hall of Fame?

A. We decided on three criteria. They have to sell one million or more copies of their books, win a national or international award, or be on the New York Times bestseller list for 10 or more weeks. Of course, we take other criteria into account, and we sometimes make exceptions. The New Jersey Committee on the Humanities selects authors based on literary worth, which may not be reflected in the number of copies they sell.

Q. Is New Jersey the only state with a hall of fame for authors?

A. Up until 1994, New Jersey was the only state that had a literary hall of fame. Then North Carolina started one. So only two states have it now.

Q. Who generally visits the Hall of Fame?

A. We get some people from N.J.I.T. Librarians from various parts of the state come on tours. Rutgers students come sometimes. And when we had a Stephen Crane exhibit, some 200 to 300 people came to that.

Q. Who is the most unusual member?

A. Nathan C. Heard was in Trenton State Prison. He wrote a book called ''Howard Street,'' about a street in Newark. And it was about pimps, prostitutes and perverts. This was about 20 years ago. He only went to the eighth grade. But he was one of the best consultants on fiction my writers' conferences ever produced.

Q. What regions of the state have produced the most authors?

A. Most of the authors are centered in Princeton. That's the hallmark of New Jerseyans who excelled in literature. There are some from South Jersey; Walt Whitman was from Camden. But South Jersey is sparsely populated, and North Jersey is closer to New York. I've had 15 books published, and I found being in North Jersey made it easier to gain access to publishers.

Q. Will many of the recent inductees be remembered years from now?

A. That's hard to say. I think they're enjoying their current popularity, and I don't think most of them give a rap about standing the test of time. ERIC EPSTEIN
