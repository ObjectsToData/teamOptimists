I Hear America Sighing

By Richard Rorty
Published: November 7, 1999

THE REAL

AMERICAN DREAM

A Meditation on Hope.

By Andrew Delbanco.

143 pp. Cambridge, Mass.:

Harvard University Press. $19.95.

Andrew Delbanco, who teaches humanities at Columbia University, is one of America's most acute and perceptive cultural critics. Yet this ''meditation on hope'' (his Massey Lectures in American civilization, given at Harvard last year) ends on a note of hesitation. Delbanco is not sure whether the remedy for our country's ''postmodern melancholy'' is to get religion or, instead, to resacralize the United States, replacing hope for divine redemption with the secular hope for an ideally just America. In the last pages of his beautifully written book, he hints that the latter strategy will not work. But he never quite commits himself.

The three lectures in ''The Real American Dream'' are titled ''God,'' ''Nation'' and ''Self'': three candidates for the central figure in a narrative that may, by providing hope, avert ''the lurking suspicion that all our getting and spending amounts to nothing more than fidgeting while we wait for death.'' Such a sustaining narrative, as Delbanco says, is called a culture when it ''establishes itself over time in the minds of a substantial number of people.''

Delbanco thinks that the 19th-century American attempt to substitute the nation for God was, in its day, fairly successful. Lincoln, he says, ''perhaps more through his death than through his life, contributed immeasurably to the sacralization of the state as the source of justice, mercy and hope.'' Whitman's ''O Captain! My Captain!'' and ''When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd'' helped Americans identify their country not as Zion, the place where the eternal would next intersect the temporal, but rather as the site of the great secular project that its martyred president had sketched at Gettysburg. Lincoln's nation, as Delbanco says, was ''the political incarnation of the idea of universal rights'' -- rights whose achievement can be embraced as a sacred mission, whether or not one believes that we are endowed with them by a divine being.

Delbanco doubts that many present-day Americans are buoyed up by the hopes that Lincoln and Whitman shared. ''We live,'' he says, ''in an age of unprecedented wealth, but in the realm of narrative and symbol, we are deprived.'' Neither God nor Nation works anymore. Yet the Self -- the consuming, narcissistic Self -- is a nonstarter, for the whole point of a sustaining narrative is to take one beyond self-involvement. Delbanco quotes Melville as saying, ''We become sad in the first place because we have nothing stirring to do,'' and his third lecture argues that this sort of sadness has become pervasive among Americans at the end of the century.

Delbanco echoes many other recent accounts of what has happened to America when he says that ''something died, or at least fell dormant, between the later 1960's, when the reform impulse subsided into solipsism, and the 1980's -- two phases of our history . . . that finally cooperated in installing instant gratification as the hallmark of the good life, and in repudiating the interventionist state as a source of hope.'' He admits that his ''division of American history into two phases of coherent belief followed by a third phase of incoherence and nervous waiting'' is ''too neat.'' Nevertheless, his simplified sketch of our country's attempts to find sustaining narratives serves his purposes well. It helps him dramatize the alternatives he believes we now face: to go back to the secular project of Lincoln, Whitman, the Progressive movement, the New Deal and the Great Society, or to abandon secularism and return to the faith of our great-great-grandfathers, or to dream up some third, unprecedented source of hope.

The misleading word ''real'' in Delbanco's title suggests that he might have some proposals for a third sustaining narrative. He says at the end of his prologue that ''in a brief conclusion, I shall propose a few thoughts about what new idea, if any, may be gathering form and strength . . . as the old forms of hope pass away.'' But it is hard to find these thoughts in his final pages. Instead, one gets references to Graham Greene, Walker Percy and others who believe both that only God will do and that only a consciousness of sin will get us back in touch with God. Delbanco keeps hinting that hope must be paid for with guilt; that Whitmanesque exuberance cannot last; that a merely secular project cannot, in the end, fend off melancholy.

Reading these pages, one is reminded that the first lecture, titled ''God,'' was all about the Puritans -- guilt-ridden people about whom Delbanco has written a great deal in the past, and whom he takes very seriously indeed. Maybe he should take them less seriously. Harold Bloom, in ''The American Religion,'' spoke for many when he wondered why American intellectual historians seem so obsessed with tracing our roots back to that particular little group of self-flagellating sickies: people who believed (as Delbanco says Jonathan Edwards did) that ''if God should decide to pluck you from the fire . . . it would be for no reason other than his own inscrutable whim.''
