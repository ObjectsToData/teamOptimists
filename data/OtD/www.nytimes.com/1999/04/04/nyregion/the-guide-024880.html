THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: April 4, 1999

British Printmakers

An exhibition at the Yale Center for British Art in New Haven opening on Friday celebrates a new breed of British printmakers who have been exploring diverse territory over the last five years. The exhibition, ''Graphic! British Prints Now,'' will remain on view through June 6.

Recently-acquired works by 6 artists and 12 video artists are in the show, including several portfolios commissioned by Paragon Press.

Opening day will include a symposium from 1 to 5 P.M., featuring talks by Charles Booth-Clibborn, founder of the Paragon Press, and four of the artists: Adam Lowe, Ian McKeever, Ben Langlands and Nikki Bell. They will address subjects including painting and printmaking, surface and digital printing, and architecture as metaphor.

A second event on Friday will be the unveiling of a permanent installation in the center's entrance court of Rachel Whiteread's ''Untitled (10 Tables).'' Cast in plaster from the spaces underneath 10 mass-produced tables, the resulting boxes are arranged in a rectangle with an empty center. The forbidding-looking structure is associated with difficult meetings the artist had in Vienna in connection with her projected Holocaust Memorial. The sessions took place around just such an arrangement of tables.

The center, at 1080 Chapel Street, is open Tuesday through Saturday from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. and Sunday from noon to 5. Admission to the exhibits and the symposium are free.

Modern Storytelling

The Finzi-Contini lecture at Yale will be delivered tomorrow at 4:30 in the Whitney Humanities Center, 53 Wall Street, by A. S. Byatt, novelist, teacher, critic, and winner of the Booker Prize. Her subject will be ''Old Tales and New Forms: Storytelling in Modern European Fiction.''

''The Garden of the Finzi-Continis'' was an Italian movie released in 1970 telling the story of the destruction of a wealthy Italian-Jewish family.

In 1990, Judge Guido Calabresi, of the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit and former dean of the Yale Law School, established the Finzi-Contini lectureship with Dr. Paul Calabresi of Brown University, in memory of their mother, Bianca Maria Finzi-Contini Calabresi. With her husband, Dr. Massimo Calabresi, she was able to flee Italy and settle in New Haven. For many years before her death in 1982 at the age of 80, she was a professor of French and Italian at Connecticut College and chairwoman of the Italian department at Albertus Magnus College. Admission to the lecture is free.

Connecticut Ballet

Connecticut Ballet's 18th season in Stamford will feature the debut of Zig Zag, its new company of 12 dancers dedicated to commissioning new ballets, three of which will be performed on Saturday at 8 P.M. at the Rich Forum, 307 Atlantic Street.

Brett Raphael, artistic director of the Connecticut Ballet, has choreographed two of the works: ''Upside Down, Right Side Up,'' a piece with music by Bohuslav Martinu, and ''Hear Ye! Hear Ye!'' with a little-known score by Aaron Copland.

The third work is by Ann Marie DeAngelo, former Joffrey Ballet artist and guest choreographer. Her untitled work is set to the music of the singer Yanni.

''Zig Zag,'' said Mr. Raphael, ''enables us to bring in new dancers and choreographers, spotlighting new cutting-edge works over the next five years.'' The company has an international flavor, with some dancers from Germany, Korea, Ukraine, Russia and Chile.

Connecticut Ballet, originally called Ballet Today, first performed at the Jacob's Pillow Dance Festival in Lee, Mass., in 1981. It moved to Stamford that same year and became the Connecticut Ballet Theater in 1985, subsequently dropping theater from its name.

Tickets for Saturday's performance are $20 and $40. The number for to call reservations is (203) 325-4466.

Southern Houses

Tomorrow from 9:30 A.M. to 3 P.M. the Greenwich Antiques Society will hold a seminar at the Bruce Museum titled ''Early Southern Houses and Their Furnishings.'' Morning coffee and lunch are included in the fee of $65.

Carl Lounsbury of Colonial Williamsburg's architectural research department will be the first of three speakers. His subject, ''New Perspectives in Early Southern Architecture,'' draws on his background in social, cultural and architectural history at the University of North Carolina and George Washington University, and many long-term projects at Williamsburg.

Thomas Savage, director of Sotheby's Institute and former curator and director of museums at the Historic Charleston Foundation, will discuss ''Charleston and Other Low County Furniture.'' His book, ''An Architectural History of the Charleston County Courthouse'' will be published this year by the University of South Carolina Press.

''Culture and Furniture Making in the Southern Back Country'' will be the final subject to be covered by Jonathan Prown, curator of furniture and sculpture at Colonial Williamsburg. He has published several studies of Swiss, English, Scottish, Welsh and African artisans who settled in the 18th-century South.

The numbers to call for more information are (203) 869-9478 or (203) 622-1135.

Indian Weekend

A Native American Weekend is scheduled at Yale next Saturday, from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M., and next Sunday, from 11 to 9. Admission is free on both days, but donations will be accepted.

Saturday's events, at the Peabody Museum of Natural History, 170 Whitney Avenue, include performances by the Thunderbird American Indian Dancers, a talk by Delphine Red Shirt, author of ''Bead on an Anthill: A Lakota Childhood,'' music by the flutist Mixashawn, a crafts exhibition, children's trading post, and a book signing by Dale Carson, author of ''New Native American Cooking.''

A Powwow, organized by the Association of Native Americans at Yale College, will be held on Sunday at the Payne Whitney Gymnasium, 20 Tower Parkway. Jules Strong Bow will preside over a dance contest and music and drumming will be provided by Youngblood, Mystic River and the Silver Cloud Singers. For more information: (203) 433-9855.

Land Use and Water Quality

''Open Space, Smarth Growth and Water Quality,'' a conference organized by Save the Sound is to be held next Saturday from 8:30 A.M. to 3:30 P.M. at the Stamford campus of the University of Connecticut. Experts from around the country will address topics related to land-use planning, open-space protection and the availability of state and Federal funds.

Speakers include Elizabeth Brabec, landscape architect, associate professor at the University of Michigan and land use planner through her company, Land Ethics, Inc.; Robert Yaro, executive director of the Regional Plan Association of Connecticut, New York and New Jersey; Richard Klein, president of Community and Environmental Defense Services of Maryland, and James Gibbons, coordinator of the University of Connecticut's land use and natural resources program.

Fees, which include breakfast, lunch and conference materials, are $35, or $25 for students and those over 64. For more information: (203) 327-9786 or (888) 728-3547.
