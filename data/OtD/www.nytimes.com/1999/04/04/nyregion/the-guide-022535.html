THE GUIDE

By BARBARA DELATINER
Published: April 4, 1999

Best Bet

With Peter, Paul and Mary coming to the Westbury Music Fair, it must truly be spring. For 17 years, the trio has been stopping off at the fair with a concert heralding the arrival of spring, and on Friday at 8 P.M., the three legendary folk singers, who have been together for 38 years, will make their 18th appearance on Brush Hollow Road (334-0800).

Out of This World

Billed as the ''Northeast's largest convention of science fiction, fact and fantasy,'' I-Con 18 comes to the State University at Stony Brook next weekend. For three days, fans of the genres can meet more than 50 of their favorite authors, including Lois McMaster Bujold, the guest of honor, and Harlan Ellison, the special guest; get to know stars (still unannounced) of ''Star Trek'' and ''Babylon Five''; participate in role-playing games; take in an art exhibition, amateur films, cabaret or samples of Japanese animation; watch some puzzling medieval combat by members of the Society for Creative Anachronism, or just wander the aisles of the dealers' room. The convention runs Friday from 6 P.M. to 2 A.M., Saturday from 10 A.M. to 2 P.M. and Sunday from 10 A.M. to 9 P.M. (632-6045).

From the Chambers

Saturday is a good night for chamber music. With a program featuring Schubert's ''Trout'' Quintet, the Long Island Chamber Players appear in the Music at Greenlawn series at 8 P.M. at the Presbyterian Church on Pulaski Road (262-3306). At the Sayville Middle School on Johnson Avenue, the Islip Arts Council's chamber music series presents Rieko Aizawa, pianist, and the Orion String Quartet in a program that includes a Schubert impromptu, the Mozart Piano Quartet in G minor, and string quartets by Haydn and Dvorak (224-5420).

Looking Back

Two exhibitions opening on Saturday take a new look at Long Island's past. In Cold Spring Harbor, at the Society for the Preservation of Long Island Antiquities' Gallery, ''Made on Long Island: Recent Discoveries in the Decorative Arts'' begins its run through Dec. 5. The furniture, silver, textiles and other items on display include a 1770 dated and signed desk by Thomas Cooper and a needlepoint picture made by Nancy S. Burnham, a young girl who attended Mrs. Lyman Beecher's School in East Hampton during the first decade of the 19th century. The gallery on Main Street will be open weekends through April from 11 A.M. to 4 P.M. (692-4664).

In Sea Cliff, the Village Museum has ''Open Spaces, Private Places: A Focus on the Parks and Gardens of Sea Cliff.'' A joint effort by the museum and the Sea Cliff Beautification Committee, the exhibition zeroes in on two local naturalists: Shogo Myaida, a Japanese-born landscape architect who designed the gardens at Jones Beach, the 1939 New York World's Fair and the Brooklyn Botanical Gardens, and created several village gardens and a tea house adjacent to Scudders Pond, and Jacob J. Levison, a local arborist and author who designed the line of sycamore trees along the entrance to Sea Cliff by the water. Photographs, artifacts and a historical diorama of Elm Park, known as ''Spooky Park,'' that Levison created for village children are included in the show, which begins with a reception on Friday at 8 P.M. and continues from 2 to 5 P.M. weekends at the museum on 10th Avenue (671-0090).

Shall They Dance?

Dance enthusiasts who prefer to do rather than watch have three chances next weekend.

On Friday, the Long Island Traditional Music Association gives square dancers an outlet at a New England contra dance at 8 P.M. at the Brush Barn on Route 25 in Smithtown. Before the session, called by Donna Hunt to live music by Judy's Band, there is an introductory lesson at 7:45 P.M. (369-7854).

Those who dance to the sound of a different drummer -- and a later period -- by embracing the current swing craze have two opportunities on Saturday: Swing Dance Long Island holds a dance to the music of the Crescent City Maulers at 7:30 P.M. at the Veterans of Foreign Wars Post on Long Island Avenue in Deer Park (821-9883), while Bob Rotunda's Stardusters, a 19-piece big band, plays at 8 P.M. at the Kismet Shriners' Ballroom on Lakeville Avenue in New Hyde Park (546-6513).

Brown Tide Update

What's new in research on brown tide? On Saturday, New York Sea Grant, a Federally and state financed agency that sponsors statewide coastal resources research and outreach education, holds an informational symposium for the public. Starting at 8:30 A.M. and running through 3 P.M., scientists will report on what the Brown Tide Research Initiative has accomplished. The session is at Westhampton Beach High School (632-9123).

Children's Week

Once more into the breach, local institutions step up programming to keep youngsters occupied during their spring break. For pure entertainment, there are ''Willow Girl,'' a puppet show, tomorrow at 1 P.M. at the Jacobson J.C.C. in East Hills (484-1545); a double feature, ''Sarah Finds the Matzoh,'' tomorrow through Friday at 3:30 P.M., and ''Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs Mining Company,'' tomorrow through Friday at 1 P.M. and Saturday at 1 and 3:30 P.M. at the Fantasy Playhouse in Lynbrook (599-1982), and ''The Adventures of Peter Rabbit'' Wednesday through Saturday at 11 A.M. and 2 P.M. at Theater Three in Port Jefferson (928-9100).

Various workshops include a spring recess crafts program Tuesday through Friday at 2 P.M. at Garvies Point Preserve in Glen Cove (571-8010); an ''Art Journey,'' Tuesday through Thursday at 10 A.M. or 12:30 P.M. at the Art League of Long Island in Huntington (368-0018); a spring nature camp Tuesday through Friday at 9 A.M. at Roosevelt Sanctuary in Oyster Bay (922-3200); ''Science and Technology Week,'' at various times Tuesday through Thursday at the North Shore Science and Technology Center in Shoreham (821-8155), and two ''Vacation Ventures'': ''George Washington, American Symbol,'' Wednesday at 10:30 A.M. and 1:30 P.M., and ''Gardens Galore,'' Thursday at 10:30 A.M. and 1:30 P.M. at the Museums at Stony Brook (751-0066).

Free Choices

TODAY -- Practice makes perfect, and after 43 years, participants in the Easter Vintage Car Parade and Exhibit in Garden City have it down pat. The 44th edition of the holiday favorite begins at 1 P.M. on Franklin Avenue and wends its way to Stewart Avenue across from Village Hall, where the cars, accompanied by Easter bunnies distributing lollipops and sharing the spotlight with an Easter egg tree, will remain on display

(746-7724).

THURSDAY -- Intergeneration Day at the New York Institute of Technology brings the elderly together with students. Starting at 12:30 P.M. in the gymnasium on the Old Westbury campus, the two get to dance to the big-band music of Ray Alexander, take tea and then listen to a lecture, ''The Existence of God,'' by Dr. Louis Navia, professor of philosophy at the college (686-7640).

FRIDAY -- The Long Island Sierra Club begins its bimonthly meeting with a guided interpretive walk at 6 P.M. through Connetquot River State Park in Oakdale, followed by a talk by Karen Blumer, author of ''Long Island Native Plants for Landscaping: A Source Book,'' at 7 P.M.

(841-2142).

At the Libraries

On Tuesday at 7 P.M., the Babylon Citizens Council on the Arts continues its three-part humanities lecture series, ''The Discovery and Education of Arts,'' at 7 P.M. at the North Babylon Library with a slide lecture, ''Edward Hopper: The Biographer's View,'' by Gail Levin (669-4020). Also on Tuesday, the naturalist Steve (Wildman) Brill discusses ''Wild Food and Ecology'' at 7:30 P.M. at the Glen Cove Library (676-2130). Finally, Mutual Agreement, a barbershop quartet, appears on Saturday at 8 P.M. at the Plainview-Old Bethpage Library in Plainview (938-0077). BARBARA DELATINER
