PUBLIC LIVES

By JAMES BARRON
Published: April 21, 1999

A Verse, But Not Terse

''This is expected of me, so I'm going to do it,'' CHARLES OSGOOD said, plunging into a verse in which he rhymed ''millennial'' with ''any'll,'' as in, ''We're here to explore now what meaning if any'll go with the forthcoming landmark millennial.''

Mr. Osgood, the perennial poet of CBS News, was moderating a panel discussion at the New York Public Library about a calendar-turning event 255 days away that seems to be the subject of more hype and hoopla with each passing 24-hour cycle.

The poem was five stanzas long -- so long, Mr. Osgood said, that he had to shorten his introductions of the panelists. They included FRANCINE du PLESSIX GRAY, the author of ''At Home with the Marquis de Sade,'' and PETER JOHN GOMES, the minister at the Memorial Chapel at Harvard University and the author of ''The Good Book: Reading the Bible With Mind and Heart.''

Ms. Gray did not seem enthusiastic about Jan. 1.

''I don't have any thoughts on the millennium,'' she said, ''which I tend to think of as an invention of the tourist industry.'' End-of-the-century Americans were ''so overwhelmingly obsessed with personal rights,'' she said, ''that they have ignored fundamental national needs, like guaranteed universal health care.

Mr. Gomes said that the concept of the millennium (''I might call it the conceit'') was arbitrary and that 2000 would be just another year. ''I suspect that the same old people will be about the same old things,'' he said, before quoting a maxim he attributed to WOODY ALLEN: ''I have seen the future, and it is very much like the present, only longer.''

CHRISTOPHER J. McGILLIVARY says that interest in the millennium may falter ''when you get into knick-knackery.'' But an 800-pound ball is not a knick-knack.

Mr. McGillivary is the president of Waterford Wedgwood USA, which is constructing the six-foot ball that will drop -- ''slowly, we hope'' -- in Times Square at midnight on New Year's Eve. Mr. McGillivary said he had done market research before committing the company to the project and concluded, in effect, that he could not buy better publicity. ''We think this is more super than the Super Bowl.''

''Technologically, this is quite a challenge, to put together a piece like this in crystal,'' he said, referring to the size of the ball and the fact that it needs to be symmetrical if it is to descend smoothly. ''Con Edison is doing the lighting for us,'' he said. ''We can keep it plugged in all day if we want.''

A Place for Long, A Time for Short

MARK GREEN, the Public Advocate, is an official with a reputation around City Hall for being famously accessible and endlessly quotable. He addressed this on Monday, when he was the guest speaker in Humanities 447, a course at Princeton University called ''Politics and the Press.''

''I'm always amused when print and electronic journalists push me to give them a sound bite because of their space constraints,'' he said, ''and then when I do it to their satisfaction, they later say dismissively, 'He talks in sound bites.' I've written long books and given short sound bites. I much prefer the former, though politics demands the latter.''

Heaven's Gate

''Open ye the gates,'' begins a passage that the singer JOHNNY CASH has recorded for a New Jersey company that markets electronic Bibles. Mr. Cash will narrate the desktop version of Franklin Electronics' King James Bible, which is scheduled to be released in July.

The passage from Isaiah 26:2 is one of 400 that the company says Mr. Cash chose himself. ''Johnny has been such an effective spokesperson for us because he truly believes in the power of our products,'' said ANDY CROSS, the company's president and chief executive. Mr. Cash, 67, has been sidelined in recent months with a neurological disorder that causes progressive damage to the nervous system, the company said. His voice remains strong. JAMES BARRON
