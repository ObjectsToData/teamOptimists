Graduate Students Strive for Union at Yale

By JAY AXELBANK
Published: September 19, 1999

WHEN Antony L. Dugdale, a graduate student, arrived at Yale University in 1993 to pursue a Ph.D., he was shocked. Graduate students who taught undergraduates as teaching assistants had formed a labor union and were trying to get Yale to recognize it as a collective bargaining agent.

''I thought it was inappropriate and silly,'' he recalled. ''This was a university, after all, not a factory.''

Now, six years later, Mr. Dugdale, 29, who is completing his doctorate in philosophy and religion, is one of those leading the drive this fall to compel the university to recognize the union as the representative of 2,100 Ph.D. candidates in the Graduate School of Arts and Sciences.

What changed his mind about unionization was his impression that Yale, as Mr. Dugdale described it, was ''short-changing undergraduates by using cheap graduate-teaching labor instead of hiring more professors.''

''When we teach, our salary gets capped at about $12,875 a year in humanities and social sciences, which is about $4,000 below Yale's own estimate of the cost-of-living in New Haven,'' he said.

Mr. Dugdale said that over the past year the union -- the Graduate Employees and Students Organization -- has signed up ''hundreds'' of graduate students to give it a majority of Ph.D. candidates.

The Yale administration disputes the graduate students' claim that it represents a majority. Stating that graduate students ''in reality receive generous stipends'' and that a union ''would seriously harm Yale's educational mission,'' Thomas P. Conroy, deputy director of public affairs, added: ''The graduate students are not eligible to bargain collectively. The National Labor Relations Board has consistently upheld this position over the last 25 years that they are primarily students, not employees.''

Not all Yale graduate students support the unionization drive. Katherine W. Marsland, a fifth-year graduate student in psychology, supports unions in general, but not for graduate students. ''Most of my peers at other schools are required to do far more teaching for about the same funding we get,'' she said.

Universities have a long tradition of using graduate students to help teach. The American Association of University Professors, a trade group in Washington, said that since 1975 the number of graduate students who teach has risen 35 percent and part-time faculty by 103 percent, while tenured appointments went up 25 percent. The group said tenure ''may be ebbing away through attrition and accelerating use of nontenure-track appointments.''

The organization supports the unionization of graduate students.

A study by the Yale union, titled ''Casual in Blue,'' issued earlier this year, claimed the number of graduate students who teach over the course of any given year at Yale has almost tripled in the last 30 years. It now stands at more than 1,000, Mr. Dugdale said. In the same period, the report said the number of professors in arts and sciences at Yale has declined. Since 1980 alone, the roster of tenured and tenure-track faculty has fallen almost 5 percent, from 687 to 662 in 1996, the last year for which figures are available.''

The union report claimed that graduate teaching assistants and nontenured adjunct faculty do 70 percent of the teaching of undergraduates -- with graduate students alone handling 40 percent.

Typically, Yale graduate students teach part-time, up to 20 hours a week in the third and fourth years of their Ph.D. programs, but in many cases they teach for more than two years. The average duration for a Ph.D. program at Yale is six years.

Eric S. Gregory, 28, a sixth-year doctoral candidate in religious studies, said graduate students at Yale have most of the ''face-to-face'' contact with undergraduates by leading small discussion sections, through regular office-hour consultations, other informal contacts, supplying references and by grading the ''vast majority'' of examination papers.

''What rubs salt in the wound is that the more we teach, the more Yale and other schools get by with less professors,'' he said.

In April, 27 Yale professors backed the graduate student union, telling their colleagues that if their departments relied too heavily on graduate students and part-time faculty instead of tenure-track professors, they should insist that the university ''create more faculty slots.''

Yale dismissed the study's numbers as ''disingenuous.'' Mr. Conroy said that undergraduates spend about 70 percent of their time in classrooms taught by faculty and 30 percent in classes -- mainly in the discussion sections -- presided over by graduate teaching assistants.

Mr. Conroy said the overwhelming majority of graduate students lead discussion sections only in courses headed by a professor.

''Just 7 percent of graduate students teach their own courses,'' he said. ''Besides, it is an invalid basis of comparison to group graduate students with adjunct faculty.''

According to Mr. Dugdale, the union should be in a position to demand recognition from the university this year. He said this could be accomplished in one of two ways: Yale agrees to recognize the union if presented with a sufficient number of signed union membership cards representing a majority of graduate students; or the union petitions the N.L.R.B. to hold a union-representation election and wins a majority.

The graduate students' union is affiliated with the A.F.L.-C.I.O. Hotel Employees and Restaurant Employees Union in New Haven, which provides financial help..
