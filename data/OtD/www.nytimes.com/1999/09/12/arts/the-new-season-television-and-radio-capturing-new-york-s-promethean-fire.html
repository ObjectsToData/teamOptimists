THE NEW SEASON/TELEVISION AND RADIO; Capturing New York's Promethean Fire

By CHARLES STRUM
Published: September 12, 1999

A WORD to the National Endowment for the Humanities, corporate underwriters like Chase, the Corporation for Public Broadcasting and viewers like you: Ric Burns is running out of space.

Not that he's complaining. It's just that as his production company, Steeplechase Films, grows into a purveyor of ever-larger documentary sagas -- from an hour to 3 hours to 10 hours and more -- the need for Lebensraum increases. But public television budgets and the Upper West Side of Manhattan being what they are, Steeplechase now operates out of two small suites in two separate buildings a block apart, off 72d Street and Broadway.

''There's something to be said for this cordon sanitaire,'' Mr. Burns said, referring to the production team in one building, the editing team in another and the artistic negotiations that can create occasional friction. Especially now, in the frenzied final weeks of assembling Mr. Burns's most ambitious solo work to date, ''New York,'' an admiring portrait of the city from Henry Hudson's arrival, in 1609, to the millennium.

More than five years in the making, ''New York'' is 12 hours in 6 equal parts, or as Mr. Burns puts it, the story of Prometheus retold from era to era. The first five parts will be broadcast in November (Sunday through Thursday, Nov. 14-18, 9 P.M.). A sixth episode, scheduled for the spring, will focus on the last 60 years of the century.

When Mr. Burns's co-writer, James Sanders, an architect and historian, and his co-producer, Lisa Ades, sat down at the start of the decade to map their plans, it quickly became clear that New York was indeed Promethean. It had also proved to be a city perpetually engaged in creative destruction -- the process of tearing down the old and rebuilding, in many cases, bigger and better.

It was the ultimate harbor, the ultimate trading post, the ultimate real estate deal and, most important, a crucible of democracy and capitalism like no other.

''It's the simple core of the story, stealing fire from the gods,'' Mr. Burns said. ''This city in the New World, from the very beginning, felt that there was no enterprise too lofty, too difficult for human beings not to accomplish.''

''In the 19th century, in a particularly intense way,'' he said, ''the powers that were previously thought to be -- metaphorically anyway -- divine came within the grasp of human beings, transforming the physical, political, social and cultural shape of the city.''

If this sounds like the kitchen sink approach, it is. It's also the quintessential Burns approach. Why compose a short story when you can film ''Middlemarch''?

THE son of an anthropologist, Mr. Burns, 44, is a polymath with a narrative mission, at 24 frames per second. His conversation bristles with literary references and allusions, as befits a former doctoral student at Columbia University. In his dissertation, he said, he intended to trace the nature of revolutionary ideas in the 19th century and, at the same time, ''bring all the qualities of modern literature into focus -- Carlyle, Flaubert, Dickens, Rimbaud -- in explaining the transformation of cultural, political and social authority.''

Instead, he fell in step with his older brother, Ken, and helped produce the 11-hour 1990 PBS series ''The Civil War.'' That was the end of the dissertation, but the start of even more research that led him to documentaries like ''Coney Island'' (the name Steeplechase Films is derived from Coney's famous amusement park) and ''The Donner Party.'' Even now, he has begun work on a three-hour film about Eugene O'Neill, collaborating with O'Neill's biographers Barbara Gelb and her husband, Arthur, a former managing editor of The New York Times.

On his decision to switch to film, Mr. Burns seems to harbor few doubts: ''I could have spent 50 years as an academic writing four books for an audience of 3,000 or a life spent telling stories for a larger audience.''

Stepping through the office's dormlike clutter, Ms. Ades, whose next project is the history of the Miss America Pageant, remarked, ''We're all hunter-gatherers.''

Hiding out at the New-York Historical Society, Ms. Ades (pronounced AD-us) pored over uncounted thousands of prints and photographs. ''What keeps me awake at night is thinking that I might have missed one.''

For fans of Burns brothers documentaries, the format will be familiar: passionately written but with low-key narration. Interspersed with contemporary video of New York are close-ups and pans of etchings, paintings and historical maps as well as early photographic stills; original musical themes repeated in rhythms and keys both joyful and mournful; and the voices of actors and writers breathing life into the words of those who watched or commented on history as it was being made.

There are also ''witnesses,'' contemporary experts whose comments amount to a Greek chorus on the theme. They include Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan, Martin Scorsese, E. L. Doctorow and Fran Lebowitz.

''Bob Stern compared the city to the greatest fireworks you ever saw,'' Mr. Burns said, referring to the architect Robert A. M. Stern. ''Out of each cascade comes yet another, higher, higher and louder still. Narratively, this aspect of the city's history is a gift. At each stage along the way it seems to be saying, 'You ain't seen nothin' yet.' And you haven't.''
