Living In | Laurelton, Queens

Prosperous Area Seeks Shops to Match

Kate Glicksberg for The New York Times

SYMMETRY A stretch of Francis Lewis Boulevard that shows off the unity of design in Laurelton, which is known for its Tudors, both attached and free-standing. More Photos >

By GREGORY BEYER
Published: April 24, 2009

IN parts of Laurelton, Queens, you can look down seemingly endless streets lined with Tudor homes, each a collection of squat rectangles with roofs so pointed as to have come from a child’s drawing. Whether separated by sideyards or joined as row houses spanning the length of entire blocks, they create a repetitive, geometrical effect.

Living in Laurelton, N.Y.

Elsewhere in the city, such oneness of design can suggest impersonality. Here it is stately and pleasant, the pride of a community that has devoted much time and effort to protecting the homes that are its foundation.

“Mine is the eighth-wealthiest district in the city, and a large part of that is Laurelton,” said Councilman James Sanders Jr., of the 31st Council District. “One of the things that make Laurelton unique is that it sits in the only census tract where blacks make more money than whites. So you’re talking about a very unique community.” (The median household income is $59,007, versus $43,020 in Queens over all and $38,518 citywide, according to census data.)

Laurelton also turns out to be the neighborhood where Bernard L. Madoff, the convicted Ponzi mastermind, grew up. Mr. Sanders said he had been unaware of this — though he did know Mr. Madoff was a fellow graduate of his alma mater, Far Rockaway High School.

Fifteen to 20 years ago, during the city’s crack epidemic, Merrick Boulevard, the main commercial strip, was “a hot spot for all sorts of illegal activity,” said Jacques Leandre, a longtime resident who is running for City Council next year. And while the boulevard has cleaned up significantly, there is a sense among residents that it has never reached its potential. Roughly bisecting Laurelton east to west, Merrick is a corridor of takeout joints, many advertising Caribbean specialties; there are also 99 cent stores, nail and beauty salons, barbershops and the like.

To Mr. Leandre, that mix does not accurately reflect the community’s prosperity. He said he would like to see a strip more like Austin Street in Forest Hills, with restaurants, a gym and a greater variety of services. “I think if we diversify the strip a bit,” he said, “it will help to retain some of the dollars that are within the community.”

Bess DeBetham, a local activist, also thinks “quality businesses are few.” She and her husband, Leroy, paid $34,500 for their three-bedroom two-bath colonial on 230th Street near Merrick Boulevard in 1971. Richard Johnson, an agent with Century 21 Milestone Realty, estimated that the house would sell today for $350,000 to $380,000.

Ms. DeBetham, a retired accounts manager, has long been involved in the neighborhood practice of planting flowers in the esplanades that separate lanes of traffic on many streets. She acknowledged recently that support for this beautification effort and other civic projects isn’t what it used to be, but she also pointed out that indicators of civic pride abound in Laurelton. Civic and block associations are numerous, and in front of the stately Tudors, the small yards are well tended.

Yet there remain challenges to the neighborhood’s family-friendly image. Most recently, residents have rallied against the development of an hourly rate motel a block away from a high school.

“It was a big fight,” said Richard Hellenbrecht, the chairman of Community Board 13. “There were demonstrations. Busloads of people went down to the Board of Standards and Appeals,” the city agency that would decide whether construction could proceed. This month, despite the protests, the project was granted city approval.

WHAT YOU’LL FIND

The area occupies a little more than a square mile in southeastern Queens near the Nassau County line; it is bounded on the north by 121st Avenue, the east by Laurelton Parkway, the west by Springfield Boulevard, and the south by North Conduit Avenue.

Census figures put the population at 25,000 to 26,000. Ninety percent are black, 5 percent Hispanic and 2 percent white; 3 percent define themselves as “mixed race.”

The city rezoned Laurelton in September to protect one- and two-family homes against conversion and redevelopment. The catalyst for the move was community concerns about out-of-context construction.

On another front, Laurelton activists have for years urged the city to create a new police precinct in the area, since the 105th, which includes Laurelton, is so large that it may slow police response time, some say. While crime has decreased significantly, Ms. DeBetham said she and her neighbors hoped to make the issue a priority for elected officials. “The mantra is, ‘No precinct, no vote,’ ” she said.
