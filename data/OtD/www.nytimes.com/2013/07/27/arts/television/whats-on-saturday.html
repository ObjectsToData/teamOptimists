What’s on Saturday

By KATHRYN SHATTUCK
Published: July 27, 2013

10 P.M. (Sundance) THE VIRGIN SUICIDES (2000) In this first movie from Sofia Coppola, based on Jeffrey Eugenides’s first novel, four sisters in affluent 1970s Michigan dance toward their deaths after being sequestered in their home by their authoritarian parents (James Woods and Kathleen Turner) following the suicide of a fifth sister, Cecilia (Hanna Hall), and a broken curfew on prom night by the Lolita-esque Lux (Kirsten Dunst, right). Giovanni Ribisi is the narrator, a grown version of one of the adoring boys with whom the sisters communicate in their isolation by means of light signals and songs over the phone. “Ms. Coppola has carefully preserved the spirit of her source and, for the most part, succeeded in her efforts to find a visual idiom appropriate to the lush melancholy of the novel’s language,” A. O. Scott wrote in The New York Times. The book, “a hazy linguistic daydream,” he added, required, “Ms. Coppola to create a feature film essentially without characters or a story, and to hold the viewer’s interest through moods, associations and resonant images. That she has done so is impressive.”

10:30 A.M. (13) RELIGION & ETHICS NEWSWEEKLY Kim Lawton interviews Sikh leaders a year after the shooting of worshipers in Oak Creek, Wis. Bob Faw visits the artist Tobi Kahn, whose works have spiritual overtones, at his studio in Long Island City, Queens. And Bob Abernethy speaks with the Rev. Thomas J. Reese, senior analyst for the National Catholic Reporter, about Pope Francis’s trip to Brazil for World Youth Day.

NOON (13) RICHARD HEFFNER’S OPEN MIND Susan Dunn, a professor of arts and humanities at Williams College, discusses her new book, “1940: FDR, Willkie, Lindbergh, Hitler — The Election Amid the Storm.”

8 P.M. (Hallmark) CEDAR COVE A developer plans to demolish the town’s lighthouse, forcing Judge Olivia Lockhart (Andie MacDowell) to find a way to save the landmark while keeping the peace.

8 P.M. (HBO) SAVAGES (2012) Two Southern California marijuana dealers — Chon (Taylor Kitsch, left, with Aaron Johnson), a cynical combat veteran; and Ben (Mr. Johnson), a soulful Berkeley graduate — and their gorgeous significant other, O (Blake Lively), take on Mexican traffickers in what A. O. Scott, writing in The Times, called “Oliver Stone’s feverish, fully baked, half-great adaptation of Don Winslow’s ferocious and funny drug-war novel of the same name.” The film, he added, “is a daylight noir, a western, a stoner buddy movie and a love story, which is to say that it is a bit of a mess. But also a lot of fun, especially as its pulp elements rub up against some gritty geopolitical and economic themes.”

8 P.M. (TCM) THE ESSENTIALS: 1982 COMEDIES Robert Osborne and Drew Barrymore tell why Sydney Pollack’s “Tootsie,” in which Dustin Hoffman transforms himself from a struggling male actor into a female soap opera star through the use of heels, high-neck blouses and heavy makeup — the better to cover his five o’clock shadow — is required viewing. The role earned Mr. Hoffman the fifth of his seven Academy Award nominations. In Richard Benjamin’s “My Favorite Year,” at 10, Peter O’Toole portrays a swashbuckling 1950s movie star, and unpredictable alcoholic, set to make his first television appearance. Mark Linn-Baker is the new writer who is assigned to get him to the show on time and sober. And in Barry Levinson’s “Diner,” at midnight, a group of Baltimore friends (played by Steve Guttenberg, Mickey Rourke, Daniel Stern and Kevin Bacon, among others) struggle with growing up.

9 P.M. (ABC) NORBIT (2007) Eddie Murphy, below, with the help of the makeup artist Rick Baker, summons some alternate personalities — Norbit, a henpecked husband; Rasputia, his monstrous wife; Mr. Wong, a Chinese man whose restaurant doubles as an orphanage — in this comedy directed by Brian Robbins. “To play Rasputia, Mr. Murphy appears to have been encased in foam rubber and dressed in Dolce & Gabbana for Elephants,” A. O. Scott wrote in The Times. “It is when Rasputia goes out in a bikini (and, later, for a bikini wax) that the full extent of Mr. Baker’s virtuosity becomes evident.”

9 P.M. (13) THE THOMAS CROWN AFFAIR (1968) Steve McQueen plays a cunning Boston millionaire who pulls off the perfect heist in this crime caper from Norman Jewison. Faye Dunaway is the insurance investigator who applies the heat. In “The Accidental Death of Joey by Sue” (2010), at 10:50, a couple (Steve Talley and Mercedes LeAnza) ponder their future on the Day of the Dead. KATHRYN SHATTUCK
