Special Report: The Art of Collecting

One Family, 50 Years of Service to Indian Art

Anil Rane

Jitish Kallat's "Circadian Rhyme - 1," part of the "Citizen Artist: Forms of Address" exhibition at Chemould Prescott Road.

By GAYATRI RANGACHARI SHAH
Published: October 23, 2013

MUMBAI — On a balmy September evening, luminaries from the Indian art world gathered in Mumbai to mark 50 years of Chemould Prescott Road, an art gallery indelibly linked to the development of modern and contemporary Indian art. But the event was as much a celebration of the gallery’s director, Shireen Gandhy, as it was of Chemould’s illustrious past.

At 49, Ms. Gandhy is just one year younger then Chemould, the family enterprise she joined in 1988. For 25 years, she has kept her finger on India’s artistic pulse, nurturing and coaxing the arts while also seeking to probe and provoke.

“Shireen wants to show edgy art, she doesn’t want to show just art that sells,” said Saryu Doshi, an art historian and the former head of the National Gallery of Modern Art in Mumbai. “If I were a collector, I would go to Chemould to build a solid collection. I would consider it a must stop for anyone interested in contemporary Indian art”

Ms. Gandhy, with her signature mass of tight black curls, is indeed a powerful artistic force in Mumbai and beyond, putting on seven to eight shows a year at Chemould and bringing the gallery to art fairs around the world. Hers will be one of only two Indian galleries at Art Basel Miami Beach in December.

In assuming her position at Chemould, Ms. Gandhy had large shoes to fill. Her parents, Kekoo and Khorshed Gandhy, played a leading role in India’s artistic community in the second half of the 20th century, Susan S. Bean, a former curator of South Asian and Korean Art at the Peabody Essex Museum in Massachusetts, said by e-mail. “When there were hardly any art galleries in the country, they provided a venue for artists to show their work, cultivated collectors and created salon-like venues for growing and enriching India’s art world,” she said.

The gallery played a particularly central part in introducing experimentation in the arts to Bombay, as Mumbai was then called, according to Homi K. Bhabha, director of the Mahindra Humanities Center at Harvard University. It provided “an indigenous cosmopolitanism, a place for the development of a new urban movement,” he said by telephone.

To commemorate the gallery’s 50th anniversary, Ms. Gandhy has organized a five-exhibition series from September to April 2014, under the rubric “Aesthetic Bind.” The first, “Subject of Death,” which closed on Oct. 3, paid homage to Bhupen Kakkar, the first high-profile Indian artist to overtly explore homosexuality. The timing of the themed exhibition was particularly poignant for Ms. Gandhy, as her mother, who contributed to the gallery’s success for decades, died three days after its opening.

The second show in the series, “Citizen Artist: Forms of Address,” is on show through Nov. 15 and highlights political voices in art. It will be followed by “Phantomata” (Nov. 29 to Jan. 3), “Cabinet Closet Wunderkammer,” (Jan. 17 to Feb. 15) and “Floating World,” (March 15 to April 12). The series feature new and old works by a stable of well-known Indian artists, including Anju Dodiya, Shilpa Gupta, Jitish Kallat, Pushpamala N., Gieve Patel, Sudhir Patwardhan, Srinivasa Prasad and Nilima Sheikh.

The space began as the Gallery Chemould in 1963 and was founded by Ms. Gandhy’s parents, although their patronage of the arts can be traced back to the 1940s. In 1941, Mr. Gandhy helped to establish a picture framing business, and he naturally came to display paintings at his frame shop. His wife, Khorshed, handled the running of the gallery.

Mr. Gandhy “had an empathic understanding of what Indian art could become, and of the international art world,” said Ms. Sheikh, an artist in Vadodara, north of Mumbai, “and this distinguished Chemould.” The Chemould founder had close associations with Rudolph von Leyden, Walter and Kathe Langhammer and Emmanuel Schlesinger, all of whom were central figures in the arts scene of 1940s Bombay.

Although she says her parents never pushed her toward the arts, and that her interest developed organically, Ms. Gandhy acknowledges that growing up in this milieu influenced her. Armed with a degree in arts administration from City University in London, she set about renovating the original Gallery Chemould, which she described as a “tiny and awkward space of a 1,000 square feet, of which only about 600 was gallery space, including a round wall.” Rent for the space, equivalent to a total of about 93 square meters, was 500 rupees a month, or about $8.10 at current exchange rates. (In 2007 Chemould moved to a 5,000-square-foot space owned by the Gandhys, which is when it took its new name. The gallery now has nine employees.)
