AROUND TOWN

Museums and Sites

HOLIDAY FESTIVITIES Ah, the holidays: the perfect time to relax with friends and family and watch nearly naked dancers twirl their pasties, hear a lipsticked man with the voice of a teenage girl croon about death, and wear your favorite cowboy hat. Well, why not? As an antidote to the saccharine tang of the season, a slew of bitter (and bitterly funny) original holiday shows are filling clubs and theaters.

This weekend the drag king comedian Murray Hill — the self-proclaimed “hardest-working middle-aged man in show biz” — brings “A Murray Little Christmas,” his annual spectacle of burlesque, cabaret, off-color humor and too-tight clothing, to the Zipper Theater. At St. Ann’s Warehouse in Brooklyn, the Tiger Lillies, an accordion, stand-up bass/ musical saw and drum trio, present “Suicide for Christmas.” Led by Martyn Jacques, a self-trained opera singer with a castrato-type voice, this London group performed “Shockheaded Peter,” a sort of demented children’s play, here to rave reviews over the last several years; the new show brings its Gypsy-punk-Weimar style to an adult audience.

And at the Box, the normally impossible-to-get-into Lower East Side club, is the sixth installment of “Chaos & Candy,” a variety show led by the downtown musician Adam Dugas, above. The production was inspired by the pagan origins of the holiday, he said. “There’s a lot of supernatural lore about Christmas, that the 12 days are when the spirits of the dead come back,” he said. “Some countries think Christmas Eve is when animals can talk, which is where the Yule log comes in. It helps ward off the evil spirits. These are the darkest days of the year, and the message of the show is that the true meaning of the holiday is to bring the lights back.” He aims to do that not with tinsel and blinking bulbs but with a glittery, “haunted Grand Ole Opry-style honky-tonk.” There’s no saddle-riding Santa, but there is a cowboy ballad about the Maccabees.

(“Suicide for Christmas,” Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m., St. Ann’s Warehouse, 38 Water Street, at Dock Street, Brooklyn, 718-254-8779, stannswarehouse.org; $32.50. “A Murray Little Christmas,” Saturday at 7:30 and 10 p.m., Zipper Theater, 336 West 37th Street, Manhattan, 212-352-3101, zippertheater.com; $25 and $50. “Chaos & Candy 6: Chicken in the Snow,” Sunday and Dec. 23 at 9 p.m., the Box, 189 Chrystie Street, Lower East Side, 866-468-7619, ticketweb.com; $30.) AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Through Jan. 1, the annual Origami Holiday Tree, decorated in the theme of “Fantastic Creatures: Mythic and Real,” and two outdoor dinosaurs in holiday decorations. Also on display: “H2O = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter,” both through May 26; “Mythic Creatures: Dragons, Unicorns and Mermaids,” through Jan. 6; “Beyond,” large- format photographs of Earth’s planetary neighbors, through April 6; and “The Unknown Audubon: Mammals of North America,” artworks depicting animals, through Aug. 31. In the Imax theater, “Dinosaurs Alive!,” a film about museum paleontologists in search of dinosaur remains. Timed tickets to “H20 = Life,” “Mythic Creatures” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax movies, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200. Tickets: $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. Open daily, 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. amnh.org.
