AROUND TOWN

Museums and Sites

‘BEATIFIC SOUL: JACK KEROUAC ON THE ROAD’ The celebration of the 50th anniversary of the publication of Jack Kerouac’s “On the Road” continues this week, as the New York Public Library opens this new exhibition of Kerouac’s diaries, manuscripts, photographs and other personal items, including his collections of fantasy baseball and horse racing materials, his harmonicas and his Buddhist bells.

At the center of the exhibition, opening on Friday, is the original 120-foot scroll on which Kerouac wrote “On the Road.” Viking, the original publisher of the novel in its edited form, released a book version of the scroll in September.

The actual scroll has been lent to the library by Jim Irsay, the owner of the N.F.L.’s Indianapolis Colts, who bought the scroll in 2001 for $2.43 million. Its first 60 feet will be unfurled in a custom-designed set of interlocking display cases. The scroll will be on display only through Feb. 24. (Then the show will closed for a week while a facsimile of the scroll is installed.)

Aside from the scroll the exhibition is composed of materials from the library’s Jack Kerouac Archive. It includes early drafts or rare editions of his other well-known novels, including “The Town and the City” and “Big Sur.” Manuscripts, drawings and photographs from other Beat writers, including William S. Burroughs, Allen Ginsberg and Lawrence Ferlinghetti, will also be on display.

(Through March 16, Humanities and Social Sciences Research Library, Fifth Avenue at 42nd Street, (212) 592-7730, nypl.org; free.) MOTOKO RICH

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Through Sunday, the Margaret Mead Film & Video Festival will feature screenings of 29 films and videos; tickets, $10 per program ($9 for members, students and 60+), which does not include museum admission. Through May 26, “H2O= Life,” an interactive display. Through May 26, “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter,” a display with hundreds of butterflies in a re-creation of their natural habitat. Through Jan. 6, “Mythic Creatures: Dragons, Unicorns and Mermaids.” Through April 6, “Beyond,” an exhibition of more than 30 large-format photographs of Earth’s planetary neighbors, in the museum’s Imax Gallery. Through Aug. 31, “The Unknown Audubons: Mammals of North America,” a display of more than 40 artworks depicting animals. In the Anne and Bernard Spitzer Hall of Human Origins there are more than 200 casts of prehuman and human fossils and artifacts that illustrate stages in physical and behavioral evolution. Also on view: “Vital Variety: A Visual Celebration of Invertebrate Biodiversity,” an exhibition of 24 large-format color photographs by the entomologist Piotr Naskrecki in the gallery next to the Imax theater. The Arthur Ross Hall of Meteorites displays the 34-ton Ahnighito meteorite, part of the Cape York meteorite. In the Imax theater, “Dinosaurs Alive!,” a film about museum paleontologists in search of dinosaur remains. Timed tickets to “H20=Life,” “Mythic Creatures” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax movies, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200; both, $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. The Rose Center is open until 8:45 p.m. for “Starry Nights: Fridays Under the Sphere,” with jazz, a cash bar and tapas, on the first Friday of every month. amnh.org.

MERCHANT’S HOUSE MUSEUM, 29 East Fourth Street, East Village. Saturday and Sunday, noon to 5 p.m., “Called by the Bell: View the Servants’ Quarters,” a tour of the rarely seen fourth-floor servants’ quarters; included in museum admission. (212) 777-1089; merchantshouse.com. Hours: Thursdays through Mondays, noon to 5 p.m. $8, $5 for students and 65+, free for members and under 12.

MUSEUM OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, Fifth Avenue at 103rd Street. Saturday at 11 a.m. and Tuesday at 2 p.m., a walking tour from the Manhattan Bridge to the Williamsburg Bridge; reservations required: (212) 534-1672, Ext. 3395. Saturday at 2 p.m., “Latino Players and Major League Baseball,” a discussion with Jorge Arangure Jr., a baseball writer for ESPN the Magazine; Roberto González Eschevarría, author of “The Pride of Havana: A History of Cuban Baseball”; Bruce Markusen, author of “Roberto Clemente: The Great One”; and Rusty Torres, the former baseball player. Tuesday night at 6:30, “The Bungalows of Rockaway,” with the screening of a documentary in progress, slide presentation and a panel discussion; taking part will be the architectural writer Eve Kahn; the author Jill Eisenstadt; the architectural historian and author Andrew S. Dolkart; and the parks commissioner, Adrian Benepe. Wednesday at 6:30 p.m., “How the Jews Changed Broadway and Invented Hollywood,” a symposium with the actress Tovah Feldshuh; Stewart F. Lane, a Broadway producer; the film critic David Denby; and Nahma Sandrow, a historian and playwright. All events are $9 ($5 for members) and include museum admission for events before 5 p.m. Through Dec. 31, “The Glory Days: New York Baseball 1947-1957,” a display of balls, bats, uniforms and other memorabilia, with video presentations and special events. Through Nov. 25, “The Jewish Daily Forward: Embracing an Immigrant Community,” an exhibition of photographs and artifacts. Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. (212) 534-1672; mcny.org. Suggested admission: $9; $5 for students and 62+; free for children under 12 and for everyone on Sundays, 10 a.m. to noon; $20 for families.

MUSEUM OF THE MOVING IMAGE, 35th Avenue at 36th Street, Astoria, Queens. Continuing through Sunday, “Warhol’s World,” featuring screenings of films by Andy Warhol and others. Unless noted, films are directed by Warhol. Saturday: at 2 p.m., “Since” (1966) and “Salvador Dalí” (1966); at 4 p.m., “Bike Boy” (1967); at 6:30 p.m., “A Walk Into the Sea: The Danny Williams Story” (2007), directed by Esther Robinson, who will appear; at 8:30 p.m., “Beautiful Darling,” a documentary in progress by James Rasin, who will be present. Sunday: at 2 p.m., “Lonesome Cowboys” (1968); at 5 p.m., “Mrs. Warhol” (1966) and “Sunset” (1967). Wednesdays and Thursdays, 11 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Fridays, noon to 8 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays, 11 a.m. to 6:30 p.m.; and by appointment. (718) 784-0077. Films and videos, unless noted, are free with admission: $10; $7.50 for 65+ and students; $5 for 5 to 18, $5; free for members and children 5 and younger; free for everyone on Fridays from 4 to 8 p.m. (but with admission required for Friday-night screenings); movingimage.us.

OTHER ISRAEL FILM FESTIVAL, continues through Thursday, with films, discussions and exhibits that depict Arab life in Israel, at several Manhattan locations, including the JCC in Manhattan, 334 Amsterdam Avenue, at 76th Street; Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street; and Cinema Village, 22 East 12th Street, Greenwich Village. POETS HOUSE PREHOLIDAY BOOK SALE, Friday and Saturday, 11 a.m. to 7 p.m., at Poets House, 72 Spring Street, second floor, SoHo; poetshouse.org.

TIBETAN MUSIC BENEFIT, Friday at 7, at the Cathedral Church of St. John the Divine, Synod Hall, Amsterdam Avenue and 112th Street, Morningside Heights. Featuring traditional music by the Namgyal monks and the Tibetan musician Tenzin Choegyal. $20 in advance (available until noon Friday); $25 at the door; $50 for a sponsor’s tickets; proceeds benefit the Tibetan Children’s Village in Dharamsala, India. compassionatemandala.org.

VETERAN’S DAY PARADE AND BLOCK PARTY, Sunday, with a block party from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m. on 56th Street, between Avenue of the Americas and Madison Avenue; and a parade on Fifth Avenue, beginning at 11 a.m. at 26th Street and continuing north to 56th Street. Sponsored by the United War Veterans Council of New York County.

WEST SIDE Y.M.C.A. ARTWORKS OPEN HOUSE, Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m., at 5 West 63rd Street, Manhattan.

WOMEN’S DEMOCRATIC CLUB STREET FAIR, Saturday at 11 a.m. to 6 p.m., on Astor Place, between Broadway and Lafayette Street, Greenwich Village.
