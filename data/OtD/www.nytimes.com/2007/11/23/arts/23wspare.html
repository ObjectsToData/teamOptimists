AROUND TOWN

Museums and Sites

HOLIDAY TRAIN SHOW They look like scale models, those miniature city landmarks in bark, seed and other natural materials that make this annual event at the New York Botanical Garden much more than a trainspotter’s affair. But the eye is easily fooled. No, these re-creations are “replicas of how the building feels,” said the show’s designer, Paul Busse.

The Brooklyn Bridge model, for instance, is much taller in proportion to its length than in reality, while Yankee Stadium is much smaller or it would take up too much space. The George Washington Bridge, on the other hand, could only be 25 feet long because the biggest truck Mr. Busse and his staff at Applied Imagination in Alexandria, Ky., could find was 26 feet.

The bridge and the main building at Ellis Island are the latest additions to a display — now topping 140 undersize landmarks — that Mr. Busse has been creating since toy trains first whistled at the garden in 1992. He’s already thinking about next year’s show and the possibility of a mini Penn Station. “I’ll never live long enough to run out of buildings in New York,” he said.

Also new this year is Holiday Nights at the Garden, a chance to let the public in on a secret the site’s employees have long known: that the grounds are “more magical in the evening,” as Todd Forrest, vice president for horticulture, put it. The series — 12 nights between now and Dec. 31 featuring carols, cider, hot chocolate and the train show — will require extra lights, even though the plants need darkness. But don’t fear for the greenery, Mr. Forrest said; on New Year’s Eve “we’re not open till the ball drops.” (Friday through Jan. 13, New York Botanical Garden, Bronx River Parkway, Exit 7W, and Fordham Road, Bedford Park, the Bronx, 718-817-8700, nybg.org; $20; $18 for students and 65+; $7 for children 2 to 12; free for children under 2. From Tuesday through next Friday, ticket buyers will receive a $4 discount on an adult ticket (or a free child’s ticket with a paying adult) when they mention the garden as NYC & Company’s “Cultural Organization of the Month.” Holiday Nights at the Garden, Friday and Sunday night, 6 to 9.) STEPHANIE GOODMAN

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Wednesday at 6:30 p.m., a lecture on the future of the Earth by Michael J. Novacek, a curator and author of “Terra: Our 100-Million-Year-Old Ecosystem and the Threats That Now Put It at Risk”; $15; $13.50 for members, students and 60+. Through Jan. 1, the annual Origami Holiday Tree, decorated in the theme of “Fantastic Creatures: Mythic and Real,” and two outdoor dinosaurs in holiday decorations. Also on display: “H2O = Life” and “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter,” both through May 26; “Mythic Creatures: Dragons, Unicorns and Mermaids,” through Jan. 6. In the Imax theater, “Dinosaurs Alive!,” a film about museum paleontologists seeking dinosaur remains. Timed tickets to “H20 = Life,” “Mythic Creatures” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax movies, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200. Tickets: $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. Open daily, 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m. The Rose Center is open until 8:45 p.m. for “Starry Nights: Fridays Under the Sphere,” with jazz, a cash bar and tapas, on the first Friday of every month. amnh.org.

Through Sunday, “The Jewish Daily Forward: Embracing an Immigrant Community,” an exhibition of photographs and artifacts. Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. (212) 534-1672; mcny.org. Suggested admission: $9; $5 for students and 62+; free for children under 12 and for everyone on Sundays, 10 a.m. to noon; $20 for families.

Mondays and Thursdays through Saturdays, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Tuesdays, 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m.; Wednesdays, 1 to 7:30 p.m. (212) 592-7730, nypl.org; free.
