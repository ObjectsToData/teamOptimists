AROUND TOWN

Museums and Sites

CULTUREFEST NYC 2007 CultureFest, a free two-day event featuring New York City arts groups, serves as a showcase of the diverse cultural offerings around the city. The festival, held tomorrow and Sunday in Battery Park, “is the kickoff of New York’s City’s cultural season for the coming year,” said George A. Fertitta, chief executive of NYC & Company, the city’s tourism bureau and the organizer of the lineup of art, dance and interactive activities for children and adults (like balloon sculpture, above).

Some 125 arts and cultural organizations will offer previews of their programs. The live performances kick off tomorrow at noon, when a Japanese percussion group performs taiko drumming and acrobatics, and Frank London’s Klezmer Brass All-Stars will close the events on Sunday.

In between, tomorrow’s headliners include the hometown band Citigrass playing its own style of big-city bluegrass; four singers from the Metropolitan Opera’s young artists program; tango music and dancing by Avantango; and the Afro-Latin Jazz Orchestra with Arturo O’Farrill.

On Sunday at noon the Nashville Attitude, a Staten Island band, plays its versions of cosmopolitan country. Then there is Mexican folk music, some Vivaldi and a little P. D. Q. Bach. After the Kids Dash, a race at 3 p.m. (for children, there are also face painting, puppet shows, arts and crafts and historic re-enactments throughout the weekend), the music resumes with the singer Imani Uzuri and the jazz trumpeter Eddie Allen.

Also, as part of CultureFeast, various local restaurants will offer an array of cuisines, including French, Indian and Asian fusion. (Tomorrow and Sunday from 11 a.m. to 5:30 p.m., rain or shine, State Street and Battery Place, Lower Manhattan, nycvisit.com/culturefest; free.) COREY KILGANNON

AMERICAN MUSEUM OF NATURAL HISTORY, Central Park West and 79th Street. Tuesday, 5 to 7:30 p.m., the 14th annual Family Party, with workshops, scavenger hunts, behind-the-scene tours and other activities; $150, $85 for children; reservations, (212) 769-5166. Through May 26, “The Butterfly Conservatory: Tropical Butterflies Alive in Winter,” a display with hundreds of butterflies in a re-creation of their natural habitat. Through Jan. 6, “Mythic Creatures: Dragons, Unicorns and Mermaids.” Through April 6, “Beyond,” an exhibition of more than 30 large-format photographs of Earth’s planetary neighbors, in the museum’s Imax Gallery. Through Jan. 6, “The Unknown Audubons: Mammals of North America,” a display of more than 40 artworks depicting animals. In the Anne and Bernard Spitzer Hall of Human Origins, there are more than 200 casts of prehuman and human fossils and artifacts that illustrate stages in physical and behavioral evolution. Timed tickets to “Mythic Creatures” and “The Butterfly Conservatory,” as well as tickets to Imax movies, include museum admission. (212) 769-5100 or (212) 769-5200; both, $22; $16.50 for students and 60+; $13 for 12 and younger. Suggested general admission: $15; $11 for students and 60+; $8.50 for children. Supersaver tickets, including museum and Rose Center admission, the Hayden Planetarium space show (“Cosmic Collisions”) and all special exhibitions: $30; $23 for students and 60+; $19 for 12 and younger. amnh.org.

MUSEUM OF THE CITY OF NEW YORK, Fifth Avenue at 103rd Street. Discussions: Sunday at 3 p.m., “Dudes! Get Stoked! New York City and Action Sports,” a look at extreme sports with writers, athletes and business people. Tuesday at 6:30 p.m., “Yiddish Is Alive and Well and Living in New York,” the influence of Yiddish on the arts in New York. Wednesday at 6:30 p.m., “Hidden in Plain Sight: Unknown Photography and the City’s Past,” with curators and photography scholars. Thursday at 6:30 p.m. “Dorothy Schiff and The New York Post,” with Marilyn Nissenson, author of “The Lady Upstairs: Dorothy Schiff and The New York Post,” and former New York Post writers Warren Hoge, now at The New York Times, and Roberta Brandes Gratz, a journalist and author. The Sunday event is free with museum admission; all others are $9 each, $5 for members. Through Dec. 31, “The Glory Days: New York Baseball 1947-1957,” a display of balls, bats, uniforms and other memorabilia, with video presentations and special events. Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. (212) 534-1672; mcny.org. Suggested admission: $9; $5 for students and 62+; free for children under 12 and for everyone on Sundays, 10 a.m. to noon; $20 for families. The museum has been designated the cultural organization of the month for October by NYC & Company. Visitors will receive a two-for-one admission when they display vouchers obtained from the NYC & Company information booth at 810 Seventh Avenue, at 52nd Street; at Culturefest this weekend in Battery Park; or online at nycvisit.com/culturalorganization.

NEW-YORK HISTORICAL SOCIETY, 2 West 77th Street, Manhattan. Monday at 6:30 p.m., a discussion on the life and work of Albert Shanker, the former president of the American Federation of Teachers, with Joel I. Klein, New York City schools chancellor; Richard D. Kahlenberg, author of “Tough Liberal: Albert Shanker and the Battles Over Schools, Unions, Race and Democracy”; and John Merrow, who reports on education for the “The NewsHour With Jim Lehrer” on public television. Daily Thursday through Oct. 24, 6:30 to 10 p.m., “Darfur/Darfur,” an exhibition of 150 photographs documenting the humanitarian crisis in the Sudan that will be projected onto the facade of the building. Through Jan. 1, “Here Is New York: Remembering 9/11/01,” an exhibition of photographs, artifacts and oral histories documenting the terrorist attack. .Tuesdays through Sundays, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; to 8 p.m. on Fridays. (212) 873-3400; nyhistory.org. $10; $7 for educators and 65+; $6 for students; free from 6 to 8 p.m. on Fridays, free at all times for children under 12.

MY DOG LOVES CENTRAL PARK COUNTRY FAIR, Saturday, 11 a.m. to 4 p.m. in Central Park, mid-park at 72nd Street, between the band shell and sheep meadow. With contests, games and animals for adoption. Sponsored by the Central Park Conservancy and Central Park PAWS; centralparkpaws.net.

NEW YORK FILM SOCIETY INDEPENDENTS NIGHT, Thursday at 6:30 p.m., at the Walter Reade Theater, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center. Featuring the New York premiere of “Greensboro: Closer to the Truth,” a documentary by Adam Zucker about the roles of the Ku Klux Klan and the American Nazi Party in the killing in 1979 of five people at a Communist Workers Party rally in Greensboro, N.C.; Mr. Zucker and Lisa Magarrell, from the International Center for Transitional Justice, will answer questions after the screening. (212) 875-5600. $11; $7 for Film Society members.

ST. JOHN THE BAPTIST CHURCH STREET FAIR, Saturday, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m., on 17th Street, from Third Avenue to Irving Place, Gramercy Park.

THE MILLAY SISTERS — A CABARET, Friday and Sunday at 6 p.m., at Don’t Tell Mama, 343 West 46th Street. A one-hour musical about the three sisters — Edna, Norma and Kathleen — featuring Margi Sharp and Rachel Murdy, who collaborated on the production. Reservations: (917) 744-7272; millaysisters.com. $5, plus a two-drink minimum.

THIRD ANNUAL BED-STUY ALIVE!, Saturday through Oct. 20, with events that celebrate the food and cultural life of this Brooklyn neighborhood. Schedule: (718) 622.6601 or besaa.org.

WHITE ELEPHANT SALE, at the New York Theosophical Society, 242 East 53rd Street, Manhattan. Saturday, 10 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Sunday, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.

WEST FOURTH STREET FESTIVAL, Saturday, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m., from Avenue of the Americas to University Place, Greenwich Village.

NEW YORK SPLASH TOURS, one-hour tours of Midtown Manhattan on land, combined with a water tour on the Hudson River, daily through Dec. 31, beginning at 10 a.m. Reservations necessary: (877) 527-4691; newyorksplashtours.com. $29; $20 for ages 3 to 11 (online tickets, $27 and $18).
