Dispatches From the Sex War

By Mark Hussey;
Published: November 6, 1994

NO MAN'S LAND The Place of the Woman Writer in the Twentieth Century. Volume 3: Letters From the Front. By Sandra M. Gilbert and Susan Gubar. Illustrated. 476 pp. New Haven: Yale University Press. $35.

AS we near the end of this century of wars, the metaphor of the "front" has become pervasive in cultural analyses. In the final volume of "No Man's Land," a three-volume mapping of the social, literary and linguistic interactions between men and women, Sandra M. Gilbert and Susan Gubar read women's 20th-century literary productions as letters from the shifting fronts of the "sex war." Within this framework, they undertake a broad chronological survey of English and American poets and novelists that includes Virginia Woolf; Marianne Moore and Edna St. Vincent Millay; three prominent novelists of the Harlem Renaissance, Jessie Redmon Fauset, Nella Larsen and Zora Neale Hurston; H. D., Sylvia Plath and Anne Sexton.

Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar argue that since World War I, literary women have adopted a masquerade of femininity to counter the antagonism of male writers and to resolve their own anxieties about being female artists. Indeed, in the late 20th century, the authors point out, the woman poet was fetishized; this "feminizing publicity" led writers like H. D., Millay, Moore, Plath and Sexton to play a public role that wasn't available to their 19th-century precursors.

The fetishization of female writers in the mid-20th century was, according to Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar, accompanied by male insecurity. In a chapter exploring "male male impersonators," the authors examine comic writings of the 1950's and 60's by men like Kurt Vonnegut and Roald Dahl. What they find are apparently "misogynist scripts" that in fact conceal the anxiety about masculinity that they believe was provoked by World War II. The "total war" of 1939-45 closed the gap between the home and the war fronts, the authors say, and as women of letters lamented the shattering of their utopian "Herland," men of letters suffered a "masculinity complex"; they were profoundly disturbed by the metaphorical equation of the gun and the penis. In the writings of John Barth, Stanley Elkin, Thomas Pynchon, Vladimir Nabokov, Ishmael Reed and Norman Mailer, Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar sense what Woody Allen and Joseph Heller referred to as male penis envy, men's anxiety over their own lost masculinity.

Ms. Gilbert, a professor of English at the University of California, Davis, and Ms. Gubar, a professor of English at Indiana University, have amassed, with the publication of the third volume of "No Man's Land," an exhausting (but not exhaustive) account of 19th- and 20th-century women's writing.

Their reading through the lens of Harold Bloom's theories of literary production -- in which poets anxiously "swerve" from the "influence" of their "precursors" to create "strong revisions" -- admirably serves their guiding metaphor of the "sex war," but their tendency to see everything in terms of war and conflict often makes for simplistic analysis, particularly when it comes to the experimental works of modernist writers like Virginia Woolf. In the essay "Modern Fiction," Woolf described plot as an "unscrupulous tyrant" from which she and writers of her generation struggled to free themselves, yet Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar insist on discerning plots in the works of modernist novelists and even poets. This is probably necessary to their argument but it frequently obscures the radically innovative character of the works themselves and has a curiously leveling effect.

This concluding volume of "No Man's Land" is not only a work of literary criticism but also a dispatch from the front of an internecine war that's now going on in the humanities departments of American universities. The authors' brief jab at Allan Bloom and Camille Paglia in their preface and their frontal assault on "so-called poststructuralists" define their political allegiances.

Their most daring -- and some would say risky -- move comes in their final chapter, where they argue that the figure of the "mother-poet," initiated by Plath and hinted at in the "womb vision" of H. D., has come to be most significant for literary women of the late 20th century.

At the end of the book Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar come full circle, by returning to where "The Madwoman in the Attic," their first book, began -- with the story of Snow White as a paradigm for women's anxiety over the conflict between biological creativity (maternity) and artistic creativity. They interpreted Snow White as the angelic Victorian woman who had to be killed for the stepmother, the "monstrous" female artist, to thrive.

Although this is a sufficient paradigm for the conflicts of the 19th century, Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar recognize its inadequacies for 20th-century women artists. In a sequence of bravura retellings of "Snow White" they set out to illustrate the ways in which the "family plot" has been manipulated by the sex wars and sex changes of the 20th century. Their myriad versions of "Snow White" show "how the story of the relationship between the sexes can be narrated."

NOTING that many post-World War II poets were mothers who wrote about maternity, Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar suggest that maternity is "the repressed term in the family plot" not only of "Snow White" but of much of traditional literature. This observation is the common ground on which warring women might meet, for it is an undeniable material fact that exists in opposition to the idea of paternity. If female artists in the 19th century had to kill the "Great Mother" (represented by Mrs. Ramsay in Woolf's novel "To the Lighthouse") in order to create art, then the time has come for the Great Mother's resurrection.

In their final version of the plot, Ms. Gilbert and Ms. Gubar imagine that the good Queen of "Snow White" not only survives her daughter's birth, but walks with her in "a forest of stories so new that only she and her daughter could tell them."
