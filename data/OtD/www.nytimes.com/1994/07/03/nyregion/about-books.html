ABOUT BOOKS

By Shirley Horner
Published: July 3, 1994

Sights on the Road THERE is an image of New Jersey that perhaps should be lost, but not if Peter Genovese, the author of "Roadside New Jersey," can help it. The hordes of writers waxing poetic over the gently rolling hills and wide sandy beaches miss the mark, Mr. Genovese says; what is great about the state is what he found and photographed while driving over hundreds of thousands of miles of roads.

"There was another New Jersey out there," writes Mr. Genovese, a Manasquan resident who is a columnist for The Home News, based in East Brunswick.

So get ready for an entertaining, whirlwind tour of the offbeat, beginning with unlikely though not necessarily enviable residences like a windmill in Barnegat, a log in Beach Haven and a castle on Route 57 in Mansfield fronted by this ominous sign: "Harley Parking Only. All Others Will Be Crushed." Published by the Rutgers Univerity Press, "Roadside" sells for $18.95.

The ubiquitous diner is immortalized here -- there are 31 pictures of diners -- as well as motels like the "queer little place, the green-and-white motel on Route 40 in Upper Pittsgrove" that resembles "a 1950's version of a spaceship" and whose owner "can't figure out" why people stop there.

The book's more than 400 photos, in color and black and white, include the street signs of Wit's End, Mount Misery and Sodom Lane along with other reasons to pause while driving: the Dog & Cat Deli for a pet-food store in Ocean Township, the Recessions Family Restaurant in Butler and Hairdresser on Fire in Roxbury.

Call this ideal travel literature, especially appealing for the character sketches of people Mr. Genovese met during his New Jersey travels: like Hubcap Jack, the octogenarian proprietor of Hubcap Pyramid, a 25-foot pile of hubcaps in Weymouth, or Helen Ertl of Vienna, who by tying the message "Help" around 11 maple trees fought the plan of the Department of Transportation to cut down them down -- and managed to save five.

And when the time comes for a thoughtful new state slogan for New Jersey, perhaps Mr. Genovese will submit one from a sign he discovered with the following advice: "Eat peanuts while you have your teeth." Award for Art Essays

The New Jersey Council for the Humanities recently gave its book award to Dr. Carol Duncan, the author of "The Aesthetics of Power," a somewhat benign title for a book that holds many a fiery message. Dr. Duncan, a professor of art history at Ramapo College in Mahwah, did not, according to the preface, intend that critical art history be perceived any other way.

In her world, as reflected in this collection of her essays, published by the Cambridge University Press ($16.95 paper; $49.95 hard-cover), art is neither neutral nor placid, and so she does battle with "the moral and political meanings that works of art have held in the past."

"The subjugation of the female" -- that is, equating the female sexual experience "with surrender and victimization" -- motivates modern erotic art, the author says. Further, she warns that although the significance of art is believed to be purely esthetic and "never bad for anyone," many so-called masterpieces with an air of authority "affirm the ideals of male domination."

Just who is Cheryl Bernstein, the author of the final essays of this book? In an interview, Dr. Duncan said that in 1970, "wanting to puncture the self-inflated and overly verbose world of art criticism, I invented a parody and called her Cheryl Bernstein."

How Bernstein was accepted in the art world, becoming known as "an astute, theoretically advanced critic" -- until the author voluntarily retired her in 1980 -- carries a message of its own about indistinguishability of parody and art criticism.

The New Jersey Humanities Book Award is presented to nonfiction works by authors linked to the state "by virtue of birth, residence or occupation" and whose work "invites critical reflection and makes scholarly knowledge available and exciting to a general audience." Winners get $1,000, and their publishers get $2,000 for marketing purposes. Electronic Medical Manual

To say that one legendary title published in New Jersey has an enviable sales record worldwide is far from a boast, because The Merck Manual, printed by Merck & Company of Rahway since 1899, sold more than one million copies of its 15th edition. Its 16th edition, issued in 1992, is expected to exceed that figure by 20 percent.

Intended as a reference source for doctors, the $26 book now appeals to the vast variety of health care professionals and that amorphous group usually referred to as the medically curious. The manual provides information about the causes, symptoms, evaluation and treatment of virtually every disorder known to medicine.

What made news at the American Booksellers Convention, held in Los Angeles in May, was what Dr. Robert Berkow, the manual's editor, called abreakthrough: all 2,800 pages were turned -- perhaps "shrunk" describes it better -- into a half-ounce Read-Only Memory (ROM) card, two and one-fourth inches high.

The card fits into a Digital Book System, a hand-held electronic book, 5 inches by 3 inches by 1/2 inch, made by Franklin Electronic Publishers ofMount Holly. A combination of the electronic version of the manual and the digital book reader is being offered for $199.95 by Franklin (800-762-5382). Otherwise, the two items, if purchased separately, would total about $300.

Whether you enter cholesterolosis of the gallbladder or snoring, the speed with which the electronic book finds each reference is fraught with possibilities for even the mildest hypochondriac.
