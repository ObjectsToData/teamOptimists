AT LUNCH WITH: Jeff Berg; In Hollywood, Artist of Deals

By BERNARD WEINRAUB,
Published: March 2, 1994

Jeff Berg walks into Drai's, a trendy, of-the-moment restaurant where the position of the table, the quality of the service and maybe even the quality of the food are inextricably linked to a customer's celebrity. Mr. Berg is escorted to one of the best tables.

Outside Hollywood, Mr. Berg, the chairman of International Creative Management, is relatively unknown. Here, though, Mr. Berg is viewed as Very Important, a bit mysterious and the second most powerful agent, after his rival, Michael Ovitz, the chairman of Creative Artists Agency. In a place where hierarchy and status seem almost tribal, Mr. Berg's position seems less than enviable.

Not so, he says. "First of all, I define myself by my own attitudes, my own self-perception," he says, ordering grapefruit juice. "It doesn't bother me. I don't suffer from jealousy or what the Spanish call invidia," meaning envy. Then he says, in a deliberately offhand way: "I haven't pursued the same kind of public visibility as some of my colleagues. We've really made our business more about the celebration of our clients than the celebration of ourselves." He smiles. Touche.

Such comments are bound to raise temperatures at the sleek headquarters of the Creative Artists Agency off Wilshire Boulevard, a couple of miles from International Creative Management's new headquarters, also on Wilshire. But Mr. Berg, whose agency represents 2,500 writers, directors and actors, professes to be unconcerned about saying what's on his mind. Or about the power and temper of Mr. Ovitz or his partner, Ron Meyer.

As I.C.M's chairman, the 46-year-old Mr. Berg is, at this point, quite delighted with himself and his agency. After all, with the Academy Award ceremonies looming on March 21, some of International Creative Management's most prestigious clients are in the running. There's Anthony Hopkins for "Remains of the Day," Tommy Lee Jones for "The Fugitive," Holly Hunter for "The Piano" and "The Firm," Stockard Channing for "Six Degrees of Separation" and John Malkovich, a new client, for "In the Line of Fire."

And there are the superstars, like Mel Gibson, Julia Roberts and Arnold Schwarzenegger, and such formidable players as Michelle Pfeiffer, Denzel Washington, Meg Ryan, Jodie Foster, Richard Gere, Eddie Murphy, Macaulay Culkin and Billy Crystal. And directors like Adrian Lyne, James L. Brooks, Nora Ephron, Spike Lee, John Milius and Woody Allen.

Yet, even with all those stars and film makers, I.C.M. is unmistakably No. 2 (in this Oscar race, for example, Creative Artists represents the biggest names: Steven Spielberg, the most successful director in history, for "Schindler's List," as well as its two stars, Liam Neeson and Ralph Fiennes; James Ivory for "Remains of the Day," and Jane Campion for "The Piano"). If it distresses Mr. Berg -- and he never admits to it -- he handles it with cool, if not chilly, indifference.

"I'll tell you what I like about agents," he says. "I like the fact that agents look to make it happen. And a good agent can't take 'no' and expect to support himself. Agents have to develop a kind of resistance to rejection, and I think it makes you stronger and I think it makes you better." He adds, almost as an afterthought, "It's an odd community of people."

Years ago, Hollywood agents were viewed as loud, abrasive and vulgar. Today, agents talk about their art collections, wear $2,000 suits, sit on college and hospital boards and pride themselves on their financial and cultural acumen.

"The stereotype no longer fits the market," Mr. Berg says. "You go through our mailroom and you see graduates from Stanford or N.Y.U. or Yale, moving their way up, people who have made a conscious decision to go in the agency business."

Even his rivals admit that Mr. Berg is very smart. But they also say that he knows it. A conversation with Mr. Berg is not littered with Hollywood name-dropping or gossip. A mention of limousine liberals is likely to prod him into a discussion of the roots of liberalism.

Tall and thin, buttoned up like a Wall Street banker, his eyes narrowing on a visitor, his body language tense and restless, Mr. Berg laces his conversation with intellectual and academic references not often heard in the square mile or so of restaurants crammed with agents, restaurants like Drai's, Cicada, Locanda Veneta, the Grill, the Ivy.

Asked about the disparity between working as an agent and viewing himself as an intellectual, Mr. Berg replies, "Well, I guess the quickest way to respond to that is, William James felt that pragmatism itself was philosophically valid and you can engage in the exploration of ideas and also be involved in the marketplace if it serves some social good."

"I don't deceive myself," he adds quickly. "I don't think we're practicing advanced existential ideas here. Although on occasions I feel like I'm in the middle of an absurdist play."

He offsets the more absurdist elements through a restrained home life. His wife, Denise Luria, is a clinical psychologist with a thriving practice. They have two young daughters, Kate and Lily, and live in the Pacific Palisades area. "It's all relative," says Mr. Berg, who runs the agency with Jim Wiatt, a longtime colleague. "When I go home and discuss deals and business with my wife, it's in such another stratosphere that it's not even comprehensible."
