U.S. Sets Voluntary Standard in Teaching the Arts

By CATHERINE S. MANEGOLD,
Published: March 14, 1994

Education Secretary Richard W. Riley today announced a program of voluntary national standards for art education intended to reverse a steady decline in the teaching of the arts.

Voluntary national standards for math teachers were established by the National Council of Teachers of Mathematics in 1989 and are being used in 40 percent of the nation's schools, Secretary Riley said. But the new goals for dance, music, drama and the visual arts mark the Federal Government's first foray into the difficult arena of prescribing learning in the arts.

Dismissed as a luxury by school systems fighting even to keep enough textbooks in stock, arts studies have steadily dwindled in recent years. The National Center for Education Statistics estimates that almost half of all American schools have no full-time arts staff members. The new standards will provide no immediate relief. 'The Right Direction'

Instead, Mr. Riley said, they will signal the Federal Government's support of such programs. Noting that about 93 percent of all education dollars come from the state and local level, Mr. Riley said he wanted to "show the American people that we are moving in the right direction."

The goals include things like insuring that grade school students understand how to use symbols and ideas in visual art, that middle school students master improvisation and harmony, and that high school students become proficient in identifying and analyzing cultural influences in dramatic works.

Over the next year, six other standards will follow in the core subjects of English, geography, history, science, foreign languages and government.

In each case, the standards are voluntary. States or school districts that choose to adopt them will be eligible for some of the $700 million earmarked in the Clinton budget for its Goals 2000 legislation, a broad education bill stressing basic educational goals and including the adoption of voluntary national standards as a central feature. Called an Important Tool

Slightly different versions of the Goals 2000 legislation have been passed by both the House and Senate. Final wording of the bill is scheduled to be worked out by Congress next week before it is sent to the White House for the President's signature. The House bill is considered much more prescriptive than the Senate bill regarding how the standards should be applied.

Unveiling the new arts standards at a news conference today, Mr. Riley described the standards as an important tool to keep America both creative and competitive.

"We seem to be, to the rest of the world, a nation somewhat consumed by a passion for expression," he said. "Arts in education elevates and gives structure to that passion."

But arts education in American public schools has suffered drastic cuts in recent years. In New York City, one of the world's greatest centers for the creative arts, two-thirds of the public schools offer no art instruction, according to a study prepared for the former chancellor.

Elsewhere, arts instruction ranges from sophisticated programs in states like Minnesota and Mr. Riley's home state of South Carolina to virtually nothing in areas of Los Angeles. Guidelines for Schools

States hoping to attract new investment, new industry and job growth, Mr. Riley said, will be able to use the standards to show that their educational systems are competitive. The standards are guidelines only and will be left to local school districts to implement. In time, Education Department officials hope the standards will be used to compare educational systems across the country and to compare American schools with those in countries like Japan, Germany and France.

The new standards are laid out in a 142-page book that contains the specific goals established -- and then unanimously approved -- by representatives from the Department of Education, the National Endowment for the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities and the Music Educators National Conference. Artists, musicians, dancers, actors, teachers, parents and business leaders were also consulted.

Each discipline has achievement levels tied to the various grade levels. In music, for example, a grade school student will be expected to sing on pitch and in rhythm. By the eighth grade he should know breath control and how to sing in small and large ensembles. By high school graduation, students should be able to sing music written in more than four parts and be able to perform a varied repertory.

There is no system by which schools will be measured at the Federal level. Thus, states and school systems that adopt the standards will also have to measure their own ability to meet them and may have very different rates of success.

But a program is being developed to provide a broader measurer. The National Assessment Governing Board, an independent board composed of state, local and Federal officials and educators, has approved a sample test to measure student achievement in each field, said the board's spokesman, Lawrence Feinberg.

The tests will be directed in a $1.2 million program under the National Assessment of Educational Progress, a program mandated by Congress, Mr. Feinberg said, and will be administered to students in the 4th, 8th and 12th grades.

The move toward standardization grew, in part, out of alarms raised by the release of "A Nation at Risk," a 1983 report detailing widespread problems in American schools. President George Bush, who styled himself "the education President," proposed six simple goals to push American students to match the achievements of students in other countries. The Clinton Administration adopted those goals, now known as Goals 2000. President Clinton has often said he backs the notion of national standards.
