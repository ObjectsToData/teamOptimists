Books of The Times; Not-So-Timeless Musings On Topics Once Timely

By MARGO JEFFERSON
Published: March 9, 1994

The Man in the Water By Roger Rosenblatt 424 pages. Random House. $25.

Roger Rosenblatt writes or has written for Time, Vanity Fair, The New Republic, Men's Journal and Family Cirle; on PBS, he also provides the MacNeil/Lehrer Newshour with essays and commentary. His job is to produce light or serious but always civilized musings on many subjects: the gulf war, one man's heroism after an airplane crash; the meaning of Christmas, New Year's, Mother's or Father's Day; the charms and follies of mass culture; the pleasure of good talk and good literature.

When he wants to treat a serious subject at greater length, Mr. Rosenblatt writes books like "Children of War" and "Life Itself: Abortion in the American Mind." But it is his occasional pieces -- profiles of Richard Nixon and Candice Bergen, rueful meditations on going bald or taking his children to the intransigently folksy Roy Rogers Family Restaurant -- that take up most of his new book, "The Man in the Water."

Such pieces are always timely and just about never timeless. They were meant to be savored or disputed when they first appeared, not to be collected in the pages of a hard-cover volume and reread in tranquillity.

After all, it is now 1994: how enlightening can it be to read a 1981 essay on former Mayor Edward I. Koch of New York in which we are told that "he remains remarkably mysterious for an apparently open man," and that "no matter how well or poorly the city knows him, he knows the city like the back of his hand"? And while humor can thrive on the small and silly, it can and often should die there, too. Here is the opening of a little riff called "Dogstoyevksy":

"The Dog. By Roger Rosenblatt. The dog barks. By Roger Rosenblatt. The dog barks by Roger Rosenblatt who is trying to read 'Crime and Punishment' by Fyodor Dostoyevsky. He is trying to read 'Crime and Punishment' by Fyodor Dostoyevsky, but the dog barks. As Raskolnikov dodges his landlady, the dog barks. As Raskolnikov curses his sister's fate, the dog barks, too. The dog always barks. By Rodya Raskolnikov. 'Dogs and Punishment' by Rodya Rosenblatt, by Roger Raskolnikov, by Fyodog Dogstoyevsky. Barkbarkbarkbarkbark."

Mr. Rosenblatt says the journalism he most admires "does not turn its head toward the noise of a moment but instead focuses on a steady condition or continuing process," and when he focuses on the citizens of a Brooklyn neighborhood that is struggling to stay above the poverty line, or on the brutal and overlooked civil war in Sudan, the result is clean, forthright prose. And something similar happens when he is writing about books he truly loves: how nice to note, while discussing Mark Twain and Langston Hughes, that the secret of writing good dialect lies in making it part of the hero's intelligence, not a sign of his bumptious naivete.

But when he puts himself center stage he becomes suspiciously winsome. His introduction explains: "In the winter of 1975 my father died, and in the spring of that year I became a journalist . . . At the time of my father's death I was working as a director of the National Endowment for the Humanities in Washington. My 'work' was to give away approximately $31 million a year to colleges and universities. As a result, no idea I had was deemed anything but brilliant, no joke I cracked anything but hilarious. These gifts suddenly deserted me when I left the endowment."

The wryness of that final sentence is a decoy: Mr. Rosenblatt still writes as though every idea of his were brilliant and every joke hilarious. This tendency reveals itself in several ways: flurries of similes and adjectives; wisecracking asides that seem breezy on a stage or television screen but glib on a page. And he is forever quoting great or good writers, whether or not the quote advances the point he is making.

In an essay called "The Quality of Mercy Killing," he ponders the case of Roswell Gilbert, a man accused of killing his wife because she was suffering from Alzheimer's disease and osteoporosis. Mr. Rosenblatt muses: "Imagine the precedent set by freeing a killer simply because he killed for love. Othello killed for love, though his passion was loaded with a different motive." So it was, and in any case, unlike Roswell Gilbert, Othello was not sentenced to 25 years in prison without parole: Othello stabbed himself to death. If it was a reference to Shakespeare Mr. Rosenblatt wanted, he had already alluded to "The Merchant of Venice" in his essay title. Othello is not needed here.

Mr. Rosenblatt taught literature for 10 years, and in an essay called "The Odd Pursuit of Teaching Books" he observes that the relationship of a teacher to a work of art is that of a middleman. In the best of circumstances, he adds, "middleman becomes a magnifying glass ('Do you see this?')."

If only the magnifying glass he shows his readers weren't continually reflecting his own image.
