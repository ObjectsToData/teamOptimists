In Court, College Defends Separation of Sexes


Published: February 13, 1994

Virginia Military Institute, a state-financed college battling in court to stay all-male, won an endorsement this week for its plan to create a separate, but not quite equal, program for women at a nearby college. A feminist scholar told a Federal district judge here that the idea was not only constitutional but would also actually benefit women.

The endorsement by Dr. Elizabeth Fox-Genovese, an author who was the founding director of the women's studies program at Emory University in Atlanta, came in a trial that has featured hours of testimony about "the psychology of gender" and differences in the ways women and men develop in early life.

"Young women, by the time they reach college, for whatever reason, have less confidence in themselves," said Dr. Fox-Genovese, who teaches history and humanities at Emory. "We really don't need to beat uppityness and aggression and all that out of young women."

Under the V.M.I. proposal, a women's program at Mary Baldwin College would emphasize "cooperative confidence building" and sports like golf and tennis, while the male freshmen on the V.M.I. campus would continue to endure the "rat line," the traditional seven months of intensive, boot-camp-style indoctrination.

The Justice Department opposes the proposal, saying the women's program is "based on gender stereotypes," is inferior to the men's program and is thus discriminatory and illegal.

A trial to determine the constitutionality of the proposal began on Wednesday in the court of Judge Jackson L. Kiser in this western Virginia city 42 miles south of the V.M.I. campus at Lexington.

The trial is an outgrowth of a 1992 ruling by the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, in Richmond, that said V.M.I.'s admission policy was unconstitutional. The court gave the college the option of finding a compromise, and V.M.I. alumni responded by offering $6.9 million to finance the Virginia Women's Institute for Leadership as part of Mary Baldwin, a liberal arts college for women in Staunton, 30 miles north of the V.M.I. campus.

V.M.I. says the two programs would simply use different means to attain the same end: physically fit leaders of high character.

Cadets at V.M.I. live an austere life, assembling in uniform for reveille at 6:55 A.M., marching to meals and receiving demerits for things like "improper desk arrangement." Mary Baldwin's campus traditions include events like Freshmen Follies, Apple Day and Peanut Day.

The women's program would try to bridge the two cultures by requiring its students to participate in the Reserve Officer Training Corps, take calculus and laboratory science and participate in a wilderness camp-out and obstacle courses. They would gain access to the old-boy network, backers say, because the V.M.I. Alumni Association would list graduates in a monthly job-search newsletter. Not as Rigorous

But lawyers for the Justice Department say the women's program will not be as rigorous because students will not have to wear uniforms to class and will not benefit from V.M.I.'s barracks life, honor code and 155-year military tradition.

Nathaniel Douglas, chief of the department's education section, said: "Public funds are being used for V.M.I., and a woman is told, 'No, you cannot come here.' We cannot allow cadets at V.M.I., or faculty or staff, to say what women can or can't do."

V.M.I. also sought to buttress its case this week with testimony from Dr. David Riesman, a sociologist who is a professor emeritus at Harvard University. He said different programs for young men and women were justified because men are often more undisciplined and lackadaisical, while women are more often over-disciplined and subordinate.

And the dean of students at Mary Baldwin, Dr. Heather Wilson, noted that in fraternities bonding rituals include things like paddling, while in sororities, members are more apt to give flowers or write poems to each other.
