MUSIC; In Danbury, Musicians at Work

By ROBERT SHERMAN
Published: October 16, 1994

AS a result of Gov. Lowell P. Weicker Jr.'s designation of October as Arts and Humanities Month in Connecticut, the Danbury Music Center has decided to hold open rehearsals of its principal performing groups.

One of the center's directors, Dale Miller, said that it could be fascinating to what goes into producing a public concert, and invited the public to "listen as the musicians work to perfect the music they will be performing in November and December."

Tomorrow evening at 7:30, one can watch and listen to James Humphreville working with Danbury Symphony in music of Ravel, Elgar and Shostakovich. Tuesday, at the same hour, the Concert Chorus rehearses Mendelssohn's "Elijah" under the group's music director Charles Matz.

Wednesday, at 7, Richard Brooks takes the Danbury Community Orchestra through a set of Movie Themes. There is no admission charge for any of these sessions in the Marian Anderson Recital Hall; for more details or other information about the center, call 748-1716.

Several other orchestras and choirs, having completed their rounds of rehearsal, are ready for their first public performances. This evening at 7:30, Richard Coffey leads Connecticut Choral Artists in "A Very Special Concert" at the Church of St. Thomas the Apostle in West Hartford. What makes the event special is not so much the program -- Schubert's Mass in G and other shorter pieces -- but the participation of the high school choirs of Canton, Farmington, Manchester and Simsbury (224-7500).

Mozart and Mahler are the composers of choice as the New Haven Symphony ushers in its second century, the 8 P.M. program at Woolsey Hall bracketing the D Minor Piano Concerto with Mahler's First Symphony. The group's music director, Michael Palmer, will conduct; the soloist is Alicia de Larrocha. The first of the orchestra's Young People's Concerts at Woolsey arrives Thursday morning at 9:30 and 11:30, as Mr. Palmer explores the pre-Halloween world of "Witches' Waltzes and Phantoms Fantastique" (776-1444).

When the Hartford Symphony presents its first subscription concerts at the Bushnell on Tuesday and Wednesday evening at 8, the group's music director, Michael Lankester, conducts Beethoven's Seventh, Bruch's "Scottish Fantasy" with the guest violinist (and Hartford native) Kurt Nikkanen, and Peter Maxwell Davies' "Orkney Wedding with Sunrise" (246-6807).

At the opening of the Ridgefield Symphony season, 8 P.M. Saturday at the high school, the group's music director, Beatrice Brown, conducts the overture to Verdi's "Nabucco," Brahms's Second Symphony and, with the guest soloist David Allen Wehr, Rachmaninoff's Piano Concerto No. 2 in C Minor (438-3889).

A good way to generate chills and nail-biting suspense is to see the climactic chase amongst the mummies of the British Museum in "Blackmail," one of Alfred Hitchcock's earliest thrillers. Using a restored print of the 1929 silent film, and a new score by the British composer Jonathan Lloyd, the Matrix Ensemble under Robert Ziegler provides live accompaniment to 8 P.M. screenings on Tuesday at the Stamford Palace (325-4466) and on Thursday at the Garde Arts Center in New London (444-7373).

Several campus concerts provide offbeat listening this week. Today at 2 the Savin-Iadone Consort presents medieval and Renaissance music for voice and lute, opening Quinnipiac College's guest artists series in Hamden. Also scheduled in Alumni Hall is an 8 P.M. Wednesday visit from the jazz master Lee Konitz (281-8937).

Wesleyan University is honoring its longtime faculty composer and modern music pioneer Alvin Lucier with a six-day celebration.

Subtitled "Collaborations" at Mr. Lucier's own request, the proceedings involve seven concerts, four exhibition installations, a film and assorted other activities.

"The man has persisted uncompromisingly with his music and his vision," said Anthony Braxton, chairman of the music department and organizer of the tribute; "that is genius, and the only kind of talent I bow down to."

Two afternoon lectures and a book-signing anticipate Tuesday's 8 P.M. opening concert of new works for percussion and gamelan ensembles; a multimedia piece called "Skin, Meat, Bone" concludes the tribute week Saturday evening at 8, although some of Mr. Lucier's sound works will continue to be part of the Gallery exhibitions through Dec. 9. For a complete listing of events and programs, call 685-3355.

New Music New Haven's 8 P.M. Thursday concert in Sprague Memorial Hall at Yale presents recent works by Betsy Jolas and four younger composers. On Friday and Saturday evenings, same time same place, student artists of Yale Opera are featured in staged scenes from works of Mozart, Massenet, Verdi, Strauss, Rossini, Berlioz and Vaughan Williams. No tickets are required for these free events (432-4158).

Also without admission cost in New Haven is a 3 P.M. recital next Sunday at the Yale Center for British Art, the cello-piano duo of Ling Yan and Russell Hirshfield displaying the art of Benjamin Britten, but otherwise drawing on composers from other countries, namely Respighi, Faure and Arvo Part (432-2850).

It's a busy week at the Hartt School too. At 8 o'clock tonight the mezzo Siggal Veira appears as the "Joy of Singing" competition winner, with the pianist Gila Goldstein. Neely Bruce conducts his American Voices Group at 8 P.M. Thursday. The Hartt Symphony plays Berlioz, Messiaen and Stravinsky at that hour on Friday, and "A Little Night Music" is on the boards Saturday evening at 8 and next Sunday afternoon at 3 (768-5566).

Fairfield University's Evenings of Music Series is off to a start at 8 P.M. on Saturday, with a recital at the Quick Center by the pianist Peter Serkin. His program lists two staples of the classical-romantic repertory, Brahms's "Variations on a Theme by Handel" and Beethoven's "Appassionata" Sonata, but true to Mr. Serkin's belief that it is "degenerate and decadent" to ignore music of the 20th century, he'll begin with the 1941 Toccata of Stefan Wolpe (254-4010). Mr. Serkin will repeat the program on Oct. 27 at Connecticut College in New London (439-2787).
