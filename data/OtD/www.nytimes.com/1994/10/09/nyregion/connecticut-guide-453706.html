CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: October 9, 1994

STEVENSON CENTENARY

Robert Louis Stevenson died 100 years ago at the age of 44, leaving behind a legacy of novels and other writings that are beloved around the world.

In celebration of the centenary, the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library at Yale, which contains a prodigious collection of the Scottish author's manuscripts, letters, first editions and books, will put them on display from Friday through Dec. 22. In addition, a symposium will be held at Yale's Whitney Humanities Center, 53 Wall Street, on Friday from 2 to 4:30 and Saturday from 10 to 12:30 and 2:30 to 5, open to the public free of charge.

Speakers during the two-day program include two members of Yale's English Department, Ian Duncan, a specialist in Scottish literature, and the poet John Hollander; Catherine Kerrigan, editor of the Edinburgh edition of Stevenson's works will give a talk as will other scholars from Scotland, Ireland, England, Canada and the United States.

A lecture about Fanny Stevenson, the writer's American wife and literary collaborator, will be given by Alexandra Lapierre on Friday at 5:15 in the Lecture Hall of the Yale Art Gallery. Ms. Lapierre's biography of Fanny Stevenson is to be published early next year by Carroll & Graf in New York.

Nearly 3,000 of Stevenson's letters have been included in an eight-volume collection issued by the Beinecke Library. Other items to be seen are a 24-page draft of "The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde," manuscripts of "Inland Voyage," "St. Ives" and "Catronia," letters from Stevenson to his parents and friends, and photographs taken at Vailima, the Samoan estate where he spent the last five years of his life.

The library, at 121 Wall Street, is open Monday through Friday from 8:30 to 5, Saturday from 10 to 5. The number for more information is 432-2977. LOVERS AND ELDERS

Moliere's "Les Fourberies de Scapin," his last play, will be performed at Miss Porter's School, 60 Main Street in Farmington on Wednesday at 7 P.M. The company presenting the show is called Theatre Rural D'Animation Culturelle from Avignon, France. The troupe performs all over Eastern and Western Europe, presenting classic French theater in areas where it not usually accessible.

This particular play is set in Naples and concerns the comic machinations of two young lovers and their disapproving elders. For more information or free reservations, the number is 693-8549. COUNTING BIRDS

Now that so many people are working at home, watching movies at home and shopping at home, the New Haven Bird Club is joining the parade and having an at-home bird count. Instead of trudging through some damp, isolated wilderness, you can participate in The Big Sit next Sunday in your own back yard.

Just draw an imaginary 17-foot circle, plunk down a lawn chair or blanket at the center, fortify yourself with hot cocoa, cold beer, or whatever, lift those binoculars skyward and start counting. After 24 hours turn in your tabulation in writing. If you are prone to loneliness, call another birdwatcher or two and form a team. Last year the winner sighted 65 species. For more information, the number is 663-3225. CELEBRATING SUBWAYS

The world's only private subway car, built by August Belmont and named Mineola, is the centerpiece of a Shoreline Trolley Museum celebration of 90 years of subway service in New York City.

From 11 to 5 today, the East Haven museum will have IRT cars from 1904 to 1994 on display, some restored, some not -- including the Mineola, some under restoration, some in operation, and some that visitors may operate. Altogether there are more than 100 public transit vehicles to see, including horse-drawn streetcars.

The museum is off Exit 51 of Interstate-95, at 17 River Street. Admission is $5 for adults, $4 for people 62 and older, $2 for children from 2 to 11. Call 467-6927 for more information. SPIRIT OF WOMEN

Ann Chernow's gutsy paintings of women may be seen in many museums, public buildings and galleries in the Northeast. Their bold composition and color, and the artist's insightful affection for her subjects make for a compelling viewing experience.

Currently a professor of art at Norwalk Community-Technical College, she has a one-woman show of new paintings and etchings on view in the college gallery through Oct. 28.

Chorus girls and glamour queens, shopclerks and secretaries of the 1930's and 40's are the source from which Ms. Chernow's imagination take nourishment. Enlarging a narrow path opened by Edward Hopper and Isabel Bishop she captures the robust spirit of women who worked at the limited kinds of jobs available to them in those days.

The gallery, at 188 Richards Avenue, is open Monday through Thursday from 9 to 9, Friday from 9 to 4, and Saturday from 9 to noon. Call 857-7199 for more information. WESTWARD HO!

The Hartford Ballet opens its season with three ballets set on the far side of the Pecos in a program titled "Western Legends." Performances are scheduled at The Bushnell on Friday and Saturday at 8, and next Sunday at 2, with pre-performance talks for ticket holders one hour before curtain time.
