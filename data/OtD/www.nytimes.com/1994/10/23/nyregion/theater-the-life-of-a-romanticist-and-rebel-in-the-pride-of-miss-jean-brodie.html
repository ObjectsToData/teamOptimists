THEATER; The Life of a Romanticist and Rebel in "The Pride of Miss Jean Brodie"

By ALVIN KLEIN
Published: October 23, 1994

IT'S been more than 25 years since theatergoers were introduced to the eccentricities of "Miss Jean Brodie." Thoroughly deserving of top billing in the gallery of unforgettable stage characters, the iconoclastic schoolteacher has been claimed by theatrical legend in the performances of Vanessa Redgrave (London) and Zoe Caldwell (Broadway). Then came Maggie Smith bearing for Jean Brodie the assurance of cinematic immortality -- and an Oscar.

Given antecedents of such intimidating luster, one wondered how a small theater like Centenary Stage Company could render an outsized star turn. For "The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie," adapted by Jay Presson Allen from the novel by Muriel Spark, is less a play than a display piece, and if it has anything to say, it's that the magnificently odd can be done in by the hopelessly mediocre and the haplessly vindictive.

In the glaring star part, Ruth Reid attempts the sounds of a Scottish dialect as the schoolmistress of a girls' -- that is, gels' -- school in Edinburgh. Wafting through the classroom and through the campus, Ms. Reid appears to have alighted from the streetcar that stopped at the plantation. With her airs and gentility intact, this lady wears the armor of confidence that contains the flutter and grounds the flirtatiousness of Tennessee Williams's neurasthenic belles.

Graceful and eloquent in gesture, Ms. Reid's Jean Brodie rises above the pettiness of her surroundings, trilling her r's with authority. She dispatches her very own curriculum of beauty, truth, gallantry, honor, courage and art; the names of the galleries come with the anecdotes (lectures, never).

And don't expect historical dates or factual backup. The names Miss Brodie drops -- Sybil Thorndike, Anna Pavlova, Joan of Arc, Florence Nightingale -- are strictly in the interests of inspiration, or in illustration of a star. Miss Brodie has no regard for a supporting cast.

Not a fabricator, though clearly a precursor of Lettice Douffet, the fantastical tour guide of the play "Lettice and Lovage," Jean Brodie is more a romanticist and a rebel whose course content does not exclude cleansing creams, Charlotte Bronte's love life and recurring references to the tale (and the music of) "La Traviata." So what if her gels have to count on their fingers?

It is, therefore, not unreasonable for the otherwise completely unreasonable old snoop, the headmistress, Miss MacKay (a most appropriately haughty performance by Rebecca Darke) to ask: "Just what is your subject, Miss Brodie?" Humanities and heroism might just be Jean Brodie's answer, certainly not "the mysteries of multiplication," which is more to the point of Miss MacKay's agenda.

That Miss Brodie regards birth control as "a political issue for the working class" proves she is not frivolous. That she espouses Mussolini ("a man of action") -- the time is 1933 -- and somehow inspires one of her gels, Mary MacGregor of the martyr complex, to go off and fight for Franco indicates Miss Brodie's confusion between adventurous fun and facism..

The play is, in the end, about how Jean Brodie's prime was cut short. She is betrayed, she is not a winner.

In glamor, shee embodies the teacher some lads, too, wish they had, especially as personified by Ms. Reid, the most sensual and svelte Jean Brodie I've yet seen. But the teacher is also dreadfully insensitive and capricious. She is adored and she is resented. She oversteps. She plays favorites. She is, by now, altogether quaint. "Try to do as I say, not as I do," is one of her less original credos. "I do not believe in talking down to children" is by far the most useful one.

As director, Carl Wallnau's attempts to balance an imbalanced play are defeated by the playwright's abrupt way with character and resolution. As actor, Mr. Wallnau's performance as Teddy Lloyd, the philandering, supremely egotistical and quite mean art teacher, who has a wife and six children and takes up with Miss Brodie and then, with one of her gels, is particularly good when he is given the piddling self-revelation the playwright has doled out to the character.

Lauren Sweeney is Monica of the spilling tears and Robin Goepfert is Mary MacGregor, who stutters. That explains why Miss Brodie's hopes are not pinned on those two non-stars But then there's Jenny (Jennifer Morales Avila), the pretty one ("Venus incarnate") for whom Miss Brodie predicts an illustrious sex life. And Sandy, who is dependable, especially for her vowel sounds. Watch out for Sandy, a dependable nemesis to boot.

In a modest inexact production, the efficient, compact set by Bruce Goodrich enhances the fluidity of an otherwise episodic three-act play and as costume designer, Mr. Goodrich has provided Ms. Reid with a smashing wardrobe. But where was the director when the music went on? Given Miss Brodie's ravings about "Traviata," how come its "Trovatore" we hear on the gramophone in a recording that post-dates the 30's by more than 20 years?

"The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie" produced by Centenary Stage Company on the campus of Centenary College in Hackettstown. Remaining performances: today at 1:30 P.M., Thursday at 7:30 P.M., Friday and Saturday at 8 P.M. Box office: (908) 852-1400, extension 348.
