UNITY TO BE THEME OF TOWN MEETINGS

By WILLIAM H. HONAN
Published: January 16, 1994

The Clinton Administration is planning to finance several nationally televised New England-style town meetings to explore the bonds that hold American society together.

Public forums using a variety of formats, topics and participants will be tested without television cameras in four or five cities sometime in the next several weeks, said Sheldon Hackney, the chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities, which is directing the project. Televised meetings would take place next winter.

"We have been fraying at the edges for 30 years by focusing on our differences," Dr. Hackney said in a recent interview. "We should have a fairly clear idea of what our nation is as we go into the next century."

The humanities endowment, which distributes about $150 million a year to support research in history, philosophy, literature and related fields, has budgeted $1 million for this project; state humanities councils and foundations will also be asked to contribute.

The project quickly attracted the attention of critics, who contended that the differences in American society were too profound to be affected by discussion. Still others fear that the Clinton Administration may use the programs for political profit. Atmosphere of Respect

Dr. Hackney first proposed the idea of "national conversations" to counter the ethnic rivalries he called "the new tribalism" in a speech in November. Dr. Hackney, a historian of the American South and former president of the University of Pennsylvania, said one purpose was to learn "how to establish an atmosphere that is civil and where people respect each other."

He said the phrasing of each topic could be crucial. "For example, immigration policy is likely to be a very divisive issue until we have a shared sense of how immigrant groups fit into the American dream," he said. "Consequently, it's much better to start talking about American identity before getting into immigration issues."

Supporters say the discussions will focus attention on one of the chief problems in American society and may reduce tensions between groups. 'Concept of Community'

Administration officials clearly support the project, which William Galston, deputy assistant to the President for domestic policy, said was close to Mr. Clinton's heart.

Donna E. Shalala, Secretary of Health and Human Services, expressed her support, saying, "He is trying to re-create the concept of community."

Dr. Hackney said the plan would unfold in four stages.

In a few weeks, pilot programs will be conducted in Boston, Detroit, Houston and perhaps one or two other cities. Libraries, museums, foundations and state humanities councils are expected to underwrite the cost of these events.

From March 4 to 6, about 20 academics who study social diversity will meet in Chicago to develop guidelines for future conversations. The conference will be financed by the John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation.

Beginning on April 1, the humanities endowment will accept applications from communities seeking financial assistance to hold conversations that follow the guidelines, with moderators chosen by the applicants.

Next winter several of the meetings will be televised. The endowment will also produce a one-hour television documentary based on the previous year's conversations. The object, Dr. Hackney said, would be "to give people a sense that they have been heard" and to stimulate "the next generation of national conversations." Dialogue Called Overrated

Critics are skeptical. "Fragmentation is a very real problem, but this solution is that of a fool," said Martin Anderson, a senior fellow at Stanford University and a former adviser to former Presidents Richard M. Nixon and Ronald Reagan. "National conversation is a contradiction in terms."

Joseph Epstein, a professor of English at Northwestern University who is a member of the National Council on the Arts, said, "So-called dialogue is vastly overrated."

Roger Kimball, managing editor of the conservative journal The New Criterion, said the project was politically motivated and "sounds like window dressing at the taxpayer's expense."

But many scholars and public officials thought there was little risk and definitely some gain to be made. "It may persuade us that our differences are less serious than we imagine," said John Kenneth Galbraith, the economist, author and professor at Harvard. "People who participate in it may even find it amusing and come to realize that now that the cold war is over we must have other sources of conflict."

Representative Sidney R. Yates, the Chicago Democrat who heads the House subcommittee that oversees the budget for the humanities endowment, said, "Anything that gets people thinking together about how to solve their problems is useful."

Stanley Katz, president of the American Council of Learned Societies, said he admired Dr. Hackney for seeking to be "a convener rather than an ayatollah," but he worried that "not all participants will be of good will."

Gertrude Himmelfarb, an author and professor emeritus of the Graduate Center Program in History at the City University of New York, said: "You get people in these meetings and they repeat what they've said before. I'm not sure that's the way to combat fragmentation and divisiveness."
