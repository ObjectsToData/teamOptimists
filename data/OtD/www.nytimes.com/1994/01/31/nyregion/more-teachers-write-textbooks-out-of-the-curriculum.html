More Teachers Write Textbooks Out of the Curriculum

By LYNDA RICHARDSON
Published: January 31, 1994

In a library at Public School 6 in Manhattan, teachers went on an unusual shopping trip the other day. As part of their first "book fair," they were looking for children's literature and historical novels that their classes will use instead of textbooks.

Teachers at the elementary school, on the Upper East Side, do not use textbooks as the framework for their classes in the way most Americans remember. The curriculum is based on themes, so there is no mandated textbook for every subject, every class and every pupil.

Increasingly, reform-minded educators across the country are questioning the value of the standard textbook. Some have chosen to abandon textbooks altogether, while others have grown ever more critical of their content.

Educators say the movement, which cuts across all grade levels, stems in part from changing instructional styles, a growing emphasis on a diversity of opinions and a disdain for today's textbooks, which they complain are oversimplified, poorly written and often outdated. A recent report by the United States Department of Education says that many standard textbooks have decreased in difficulty by two grade levels in the last 20 years and that few if any publishers produce textbooks aimed at above-average students.

"It's trying to get away from mundane textbooks and make learning more interesting and relevant to children's lives," said June Million, director of the National Association of Elementary School Principals in Alexandria, Va., referring to the new attitude.

No one knows how many teachers are moving away from the conventional use of textbooks. But educators say elementary reading instructors are farthest ahead in using nontraditional methods. Many teachers are experimenting with the "whole language" approach, which says that reading, writing and vocabulary are best taught together and that reading skills are more quickly picked up by seeing words in context, not simply spelling them on a chalkboard. In that approach, which usually involves children's literature, daily writing projects and language activities, what words mean is more useful initially than how they are spelled.

"It used to be assumed that people were programmed and learned in bits and pieces," said David K. Cohen, a University of Michigan professor who studies instructional styles and how textbooks are used. "Now researchers have decided that learning to read is learning to think and make judgments." 'It Has More Meaning'

For the P.S. 6 book fair, the principal invited a representative for several publishing houses to display children's books, and the teachers used the $200 allotments they had each received from the school's book budget to snap up reference books on the Middle Ages, Japan, colonialism and even teddy bears to guide children through reading, math, science and history.

"I feel like it has more meaning than a Dick and Jane book," said Randee Sacher, a 28-year-old kindergarten teacher who placed an order for a dozen different books on bears. During graduate school, she said, "I never even learned the textbook way."

And so in her class in Room 102, teddy bears, not textbooks, are helping children learn to read and write and count. Little teddy bears, made by the children from construction paper, dangle on clothes lines in the sunny room.

"Hoy my bars name is tadey Hes fun to play withe," one girl scribbled across her bear in a writing lesson that is clearly more intended to teach the role of words in expressing thought than precise spelling. The children had just finished "Hairy Bear," a storybook that they read together. The teacher, Ms. Sacher, then asked them which character they liked best.

On a big, blue chart, the pupils wrote their names beneath their favorite characters, then tallied up their math results to see which bear got the top vote.

" . . . 17, 18, 19, 20," chanted a cluster of children as they huddled by the chart. "Excellent," one boy yelled out.

At P.S. 6, a consistently top-ranked school with reading scores that recently rose to seventh in the city, Ms. Sacher said the teddy bear theme captured her class's imagination. While pupils still learn their A B C's, she said, the point is to expose children to real books early on.

New York teachers have more freedom than those in other states to choose whether to use textbooks. In 22 states, textbooks are ordered in bulk through a formal adoption process, giving local school officials little control over what is taught and what textbooks are used. But even in some of those states, educators in recent years have altered their philosophy of what makes a good textbook.

From Michigan to South Carolina, state policy makers have revised their curriculum guidelines to encourage the use of real literature. Many textbook publishers, in turn, have scrambled to respond by including literature in their offerings. They are trying to protect their stake in a $2.2 billion-a-year market.

California, which is a top target of textbook producers, significantly overhauled its curriculum in the late 1980's and adopted untraditional textbooks with a stronger emphasis on literature in every subject and grade. The state adopted language arts books, for example, that contained long passages of authentic literature. Different Schools, Different Emphasis
