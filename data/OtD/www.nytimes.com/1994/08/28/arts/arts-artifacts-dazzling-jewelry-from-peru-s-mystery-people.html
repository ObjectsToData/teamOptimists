ARTS/ARTIFACTS; Dazzling Jewelry From Peru's Mystery People

By RITA REIF
Published: August 28, 1994

THE LORD OF SIPAN, A FIERCE warrior in third-century Peru, wore gilded armor, feathered headdresses and dazzling jewelry. As part of his glittering garb he had back flaps, metal decorations shaped like ax heads, hanging from his belt to impress observers and protect his rear.

"When he walked, they resounded like rattles," says Craig Morris, curator for South American archeology at the American Museum of Natural History. "And all those round things he wore on his chest and his head were fitted with bells."

While the ringing and clapping noises are left to a viewer's imagination, the warrior-priest's possessions are richly documented in "The Royal Tombs of Sipan," an exhibition of 200 objects at the museum through Jan. 1. The Lord of Sipan, who was about 40 at his death, just before the year 300, was discovered in a 12-acre pyramid near the Peruvian village of Sipan in 1987. He was a member of the Moche people, who flourished in northern Peru from the first to the eighth centuries.

Looters were the first to uncover his ceremonial jewelry and pottery, objects depicting spiders, moon monsters, scorpions and warriors with their victims. When these artifacts appeared on the market, archeologists were alerted and over the next two years excavated three tombs at Sipan. The site is now described as the richest ever found in the Americas and one of the most significant of the century.

"Our knowledge of the Moche people doubled or tripled with these discoveries," says Mr. Morris.

The Moche, little known until recently, left no written record of their existence or the reason for their decline. Their pottery and metalwork, however, exceeded in sophistication the works by the more familiar Mayans, who lived 800 years later. The first items of the earlier pre-Columbians were uncovered in the 1890's at Moche, a village in northern Peru. But the Moche name was not applied to these people until the 1960's, after their artifacts were extensively studied.

Mainly farmers, the Moche irrigated desert valleys between the Pacific and the Andes, which explains why themes of fertility -- plant forms and the earliest known images of the peanut in art -- are so common. A necklace of peanut-shaped beads (10 in gold on one side perhaps representing the sun and 10 in silver on the other for the moon) also reflects the duality in nature.

Duality is again expressed in a necklace of beads the size of hockey pucks, each topped by the gold body of a spider in the form of a human head sitting on an exquisitely crafted web. "The spider is a metaphor for the warrior who traps the victim in his web," Mr. Morris says.

In their glorious metalwork, the Moche hammered paper-thin sheets of gold, silver and copper, which they further enhanced by plating the surfaces with other metals and adding inlays of turquoise or shell. While the technology is always impressive, the glowing images are what prove memorable. A haunting plaque from a parade banner depicts a headless creature, arms raised skyward, feet solidly planted on earth, with a small man at the base in the same pose.

"No one knows if the larger image is the small man's shadow," Mr. Morris says.

Curiosity and wonder, the usual responses to Moche jewelry, are tinged with horror for some people encountering images of the decapitator -- a ferocious deity with a human head in one hand, a knife in the other. "The Moche practiced human sacrifice but only after one-to-one combat," Mr. Morris says. "There was never the sort of mass militarism we know from Western history."

The mysteriousness of the Moche is what gets to people, he adds. "They're surprised by an art depicting spiders, decapitators, peanuts and things. And the scale of these pieces is so much larger than the jewelry in other cultures." For example, the back flaps (the first ever to be unearthed) hang down 18 inches and weigh up to two pounds each. Moche pottery, on the other hand, is literal and the most informative of all Peruvian ceramics. Vessels depict people realistically; indeed, they were probably portraits. And many paintings on pots are so detailed they illustrate with comic-strip intensity how the Moche hunted, fought and paraded in rituals.

The objects in the exhibition were selected by the co-curators: Sipan's chief archeologist, Walter Alva, who is the director of the Bruning Archeological Museum in Lambayeque, Peru, and Christopher Donnan, the director of the Fowler Museum of Cultural History at the University of California, Los Angeles. Financed primarily by the National Endowment for the Humanities, the show opened at the Fowler last September; it moves next year to Detroit and Washington. The Fowler also produced the catalogue ($40 in cloth, $25 in paper).

"Viewers appear so overwhelmed by the glitter and scale of this stuff that the intellectual content seems only secondary," Mr. Morris says. "We hope the deeper anthropological questions will surface later."
