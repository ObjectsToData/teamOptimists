NEIGHBORHOOD REPORT: MIDWOOD; School Aide's Death Grows Into Case of Multiple Mysteries

By AMY WALDMAN
Published: May 17, 1998

Midwood High School prides itself on a culture of achievement. Students come from outside the district for its special humanities and premed programs. This year, the school had more semifinalists in the Westinghouse Science Talent Search than any school in the country. It is not a place where misbehavior is common, and violent incidents have been unheard of there, many say.

As a struggling special-education student who had already been held back a year, Jose Hiraldo, 16, did not fit well into Midwood's milieu. And, after an incident last fall, this student who did not fit became implicated in violence Midwood rarely sees.

That event has now become a case of multiple mysteries. Why Jose argued with and punched a 66-year-old teacher's aide is something perhaps only he knows. Whether the argument and assault caused the death of the aide, John Morale, who suffered a heart attack minutes later and died the next morning, is something a jury may eventually determine. And whether the aberrant and, for Midwood, possibly unprecedented nature of the incident affected its officials' reaction is something only they know. They aren't commenting.

Jose has been charged by the Brooklyn District Attorney with second-degree manslaughter. He will be tried as an adult, and faces 5 to 15 years in prison if convicted. Among other arguments, his Legal Aid lawyers claim that he did not know his punch, or punches, could cause the death of Mr. Morale, who was taking heart medicine. So they have asked a judge to dismiss the charge or reduce it to misdemeanor assault, and expect a decision on Thursday.

The school, meanwhile, is undergoing a trial of its own. In an April report, Edward F. Stancik, the Special Commissioner of Investigation for the city's schools, criticized Midwood for not promptly reporting the assault to police, attempting to conduct its own investigation and withholding information from the police.

''The criminal investigation had been adversely affected by the school's decision not to call the police immediately,'' Mr. Stancik wrote of the more than 24 hours that passed between the assault and the notification. By then Mr. Morale's body had been embalmed and physical evidence had been handled by numerous members of the school staff.

J. D. LaRock, a spokesman for the Board of Education, said the school's delay may be understandable given the trauma of the situation.

But Mr. Morale's daughter, Josephine, also criticized the school's conduct, and praised the Brooklyn District Attorney's office for charging Jose with manslaughter. ''He's walking around, out on bail,'' she said. ''That's the only thing we're angry about.''

As the Morale family copes with grief, Jose's family grapples with fear. ''It's like a monkey on my back,'' said Jose's mother, Olga Estrella. Jose said the event and its aftermath -- he spent a week at Rikers Island before his mother could raise $15,000 in bail -- haunts him. ''Every time I go to sleep, I get a lot of flashbacks,'' he said.

Jose is now at a new school that he said is better for him. He has what he called a ''guardian angel'' there -- a teacher who is determined that he pass. AMY WALDMAN
