The Parrish Marks a Century

By PHYLLIS BRAFF
Published: May 17, 1998

AS the art museum founded in Southampton by Samuel Parrish in 1898 celebrates its centennial -- making it the oldest on Long Island -- it is pushing for recognition as one of the country's leading small museums.

''It's time to think about what a regional museum can be,'' explains Trudy Kramer, the Parrish's director since 1981. Ms. Kramer, who previously served as director of the Gallery Association of New York State, also speaks of ''a long interest in how small entities can have meaning and be as resourceful as possible.''

When Samuel Longstreth Parrish (1849-1932) set about establishing the museum, he raised none of the questions that Ms. Kramer has explored through planning documents, audience evaluations and community surveys. Like others in his wealthy, well-traveled social group, he was confident in the inspirational value of paintings, plaster casts and copies representing Renaissance Italy, ancient Greece and England.

Parrish cared deeply for Southampton, where his parents had a summer home and where he eventually retired after a career in corporate law.

In the mid-1890's, while traveling in Italy, Parrish decided it would be appropriate to build a museum in Southampton for his growing art collection. Within a year he bought land next to the library and commissioned a fellow summer resident, Grosvenor Atterbury (1869-1956), to design a building. The Italianate structure was dedicated in August 1898. Major expansions were ordered in 1902, when a large exhibition hall (now the museum's concert hall) was added, and again in 1913, when three more galleries and a traffic pattern reorientation changed the entrance from a Main Street walkway to a portico facing Job's Lane.

Today, Parrish would find the museum's functioning quite different and the building very familiar. Reproductions of the Parthenon frieze (taken from the Elgin Marbles in the British Museum) still grace the upper sections of the Transept Gallery; a della Robbia Madonna is on the West Garden wall, and various copies of antique carvings are in their original positions around the exterior walls. The arboretum, laid out in 1897, still offers a number of unusual tree specimens.

The greatest changes involve lighting, ultraviolet filters, a climate-controlled storage vault and a heating system that permits year-round use of the museum. In Parrish's era, it was closed half the year.

The big plans now, sparked by the coming relocation of the adjacent Rogers Memorial Library, are modernization and expansion. Mildred C. Brinn, chairman of the museum's board, is directing the first-stage committee efforts, although she is careful to describe the plan as ''involving all of the community and all of the village officials.''

''We look forward to their plans,'' said Southampton's Mayor, Douglas Murtha. ''Expansion is an exciting prospect.''

One great impetus behind the upgrade is the need to meet the environmental standards required to borrow art from other institutions. When the Parrish mounted a Mary Cassatt show more than three decades ago, it borrowed selected paintings from the National Gallery and the Metropolitan. Today, that would be impossible without a major investment in climate-control equipment.

For its centennial year, the museum has also devised a sequence of exhibitions. Last month, the galleries featured contemporary Suffolk County artists. Currently, the community is targeted in an ambitious show that documents sites in Southampton Township and their changes over time. During the summer, the museum plans an exhibition of prominent artists associated with eastern Long Island during the past 50 years.

In the fall, ''Sea Change,'' treating the ocean as a subject in art, will highlight the way a museum can expand upon an idea, as a marine biologist shares catalogue space with the guest curator, Klaus Kertess. Finally, acquiring and preserving evidence of a cultural heritage will be the focus of a late 1998 presentation, ''Gifts for a New Century.''

With the exception of the museum's educational commitment, all of this is far removed philosophically from the quiet contemplation of the European past that was central to Parrish's vision. After his death in 1932, the effects of the Depression and changing tastes in art resulted in fewer people visiting the museum.

In 1941, trustees of the museum corporation, set up in 1897, gave the building and grounds to the Village of Southampton. The first village board of trustees established annual events showcasing local talent: Jackson Pollock and Lee Krasner were included in a 1949 group exhibition.

Early in the 1950's, the museum turned toward its present character when Rebecca Bolling Littlejohn was named president of the board of trustees. She secured a New York State charter, set up membership categories, introduced year-round operations and forged a close relationship with local schools.

Since Mrs. Littlejohn was a knowledgeable collector of American painting herself, it is not surprising that her ambitious programming for the museum explored the history of American art. In the decade of her stewardship, there were shows on the Hudson River School, American Impressionism, American Primitives from the Smithsonian and 19th-century painting from the Karolik Collection. For other projects she borrowed from New York galleries and from the Metropolitan and the Museum of Modern Art.
