Improving on Nature

By M. R. Montgomery
Published: May 31, 1998

CATS' PAWS AND CATAPULTS

Mechanical Worlds of Nature and People.

By Steven Vogel.

Illustrated. 382 pp. New York:

W. W. Norton & Company. $27.50.

One gets over envying youth in general, but one cannot help hoping that students at Duke University appreciate the fact that their faculty includes two of the finest explainers working in the United States: Steven Vogel, whose books previous to ''Cats' Paws and Catapults'' include ''Life's Devices,'' and Henry Petroski, best known for ''Pencil'' and ''Engineers of Dreams.''

''Life's Devices'' popularized Vogel's academic specialty, biomechanics, and a reviewer in the journal Nature deemed it ''brilliant and eccentric.'' That last word is not a compliment in the scientific world, and Vogel has a long history of not much caring about playing the game of purely scientific (meaning emotionless and valueless) writing. As a young instructor, he assumed the task of writing a series of laboratory manuals for the Duke biology department. Cost considerations demanded that they not be illustrated. Thus began a career of explaining things with words to the uninitiated. But even then, Vogel could not resist an idiosyncratic, if hardly eccentric, prose. He wrote, in a manual on dissection, that ''two nostrils grace the front of a frog,'' and some undergraduates snickered at the normative verb ''grace.'' One hopes they did not become plastic surgeons.

Vogel's new book truly treads on dangerous academic ground. This makes it perfect for the lay reader, the undergraduate and any scientist with a sense of humor. This work is a comparison of natural mechanical devices, the cats' paws of the title, and human inventions, the catapults and dozens more of the components of modern life. Biologists like Vogel are not supposed to trespass over into the history and practice of engineering, but he is not an intruder. He is a trained observer with the rare ability to bring both professional knowledge and innocent-eyed enthusiasm to the task. That is all to the good; otherwise we would be looking at book called ''Feline Pedal Extremities and Tension Operated Armaments: a Case Study in Convergent Evolution.''

In ''Cats' Paws'' Vogel sets out to puncture several inflated views of nature, most especially that nature is a greater designer than human imagination, a tradition that goes back to Aristotle's aphorism: ''If one way be better than another, that you may be sure is Nature's way.'' But as an investigator of biomechanical systems, Vogel knows better: ''The biomechanic usually recognizes nature's use of some neat device only when the engineer has already provided us with a model. Put another way, biomechanics mainly still studies how, where and why nature does what engineers do.''

But surely we all know that many of our human inventions are modeled on nature. Did not the wings of the Wright brothers' first airplane emulate the cross-section of a curve of a sea-gull's wing? Did they not steer it by bending the wingtips, as birds do? No and yes. The Wrights built a wind tunnel and tested various wing designs. Many contemporaries of theirs had surpassed slavish imitation of the bird wing by 1885, decades before the Wrights achieved powered flight. Their ingenious, imitative, bending wing-tips were immediately replaced by much more efficient ailerons, the moving flaps we all peer at through the windows of jetliners. There are a few useful inventions that were inspired by nature. Barbed wire was meant to substitute for the prickly limbs of the slow-growing Osage orange hedgerows once planted in Midwestern states. The chunky teeth of a chainsaw are copies of the efficient cutting ''teeth'' (lips would be more like it) of a common wood-boring beetle.

Why it is so difficult to imitate nature, and why it so often would be foolish, requires an understanding of mechanics -- the rules of physics and the applications of devices like springs, bearings and engines. That understanding is the greatest contribution of this book. Slowly, carefully, with simple formulas and understandable analogies, Vogel explains the basic concepts of structures and mechanisms: work, energy, momentum, shear, load, tension, compression. Have you ever wondered why airplanes are round in cross section, and why airplane windows and ship portholes are also roughly circular? In both cases, the purpose is to distribute two forces -- air pressure and shear stress -- evenly.

Except for airplanes and boats, we create a habitat of flat surfaces and right angles, while the natural world is all curves. That fundamental difference has many causes, not the least of which is that we bipeds are very uncomfortable walking on anything but level, stiff, surfaces. Another reason that our buildings are so resolutely right-angled (except for pitched roofs in snowy climates) is that the easiest way to build a safe structure is to stack flat pieces in exact verticals and let gravity help hold it up by maximizing compression and minimizing tension and shear. This rule applies to modern wood-framed houses (the studs in the wall are just very skinny flat pieces) as well as to masonry structures. Even in the most flower-powered Southwestern communities, rectangular adobe houses will always outnumber geodesic domes.

It is Vogel's particular genius to illustrate the principles of structural forces with the most commonplace examples. The complex interaction of tension, compression and shear in a cylinder is what makes water come out of a wrung dishrag. Or take an example where human ingenuity far surpasses nature's. Spiders do not ''spin'' a web. They extrude single continuous fibers, monofilaments. Humans spin, and manage to turn short, weak fibers (a bit of cotton or wool or hemp) into long, strong yarns and ropes by simply twisting them together: ''Even though their fibers aren't joined end to end, these ropes are remarkably strong. The fibers are kept from pulling apart by their reistance to shearing (sliding) across one another when they're compressed. Pulling on a spun thread or a twisted rope has the same effect as shearing a structure; it presses together the fibers in the middle. . . . This tension-induced compression is especially obvious with fluffy yarn.''

''Cats' Paws and Catapults'' belongs in all libraries; bright high school students will profit from it, as will college students attempting to cross over from the humanities into the sciences. And noneccentric science wonks would gain a great deal by getting Vogel's message: Knowledge couched in professional jargon is elitism disguised as learning.
