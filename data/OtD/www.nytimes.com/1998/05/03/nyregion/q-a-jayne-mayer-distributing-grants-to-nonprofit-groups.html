Q&A: Jayne Mayer; Distributing Grants to Nonprofit Groups

By SUSAN KONIG
Published: May 3, 1998

EVERY day, advances in communications technology are dramatically changing our lives. To deal with some of these changes, Bell Atlantic, the telecommunications giant, has created a foundation to finance nonprofit organizations so they can apply this technology to their programs and services, further their missions and improve the quality of life in their communities.

The Bell Atlantic Foundation focuses on the cities and towns that are served by Bell Atlantic, from Maine to West Virginia. Its mission is to make technology more available to students and teachers, and service organizations and cultural institutions with youth programs. Special priority is given to projects that focus on education, health and human services, and the arts and humanities.

Jayne Mayer is Bell Atlantic's director of community affairs for Long Island. She reviews applications and helps determine where the foundation's funds might best be used.

The Bell Atlantic Foundation is a combination of two separate efforts to ''give back to the community,'' initiated by NYNEX and Bell Atlantic, before their merger last year.

Ms. Mayer has worked for Bell Atlantic since 1990 and has been involved with the foundation since last May. Before joining the company, she worked for the Town of Oyster Bay's public information office for seven years.

Ms. Mayer grew up in Hicksville. She earned a communication arts degree from the New York Institute of Technology in Old Westbury. She lives in Woodbury.

Q. How does the foundation work?

A. People like me, throughout the Bell Atlantic region, get a predetermined amount of money to put toward philanthropic efforts in our communities. I review grant proposals all year long. For 1998, I was allotted $250,000 and am currently working with about 25 organizations.

Q. What are some projects you're currently involved in?

A. One project we feel is really great for Long Island is something we're working on with the Caroline Baldwin Breast Cancer Research Fund and SUNY Stony Brook. It's a breast-cancer education program for Long Island. We're developing a series of seminars right now for several different audiences. One will be a support group for women who've already been diagnosed with breast cancer and their families. We're planning a Bell Atlantic seminar for our own employees. And then, we're doing something for the general public, so that everyone can get more information about this disease. Most of these seminars will be held at SUNY Stony Brook, which can accommodate a lot of people, so we're expecting pretty large turnouts.

Q. You say the foundation's overall focus is to make information technology more available to students, teachers, service and cultural organizations?

A. To all aspects of the community, really. We try to enhance the quality of life through education. We want to get our students ready for the work force. We want to break the barrier between the ''haves'' and the ''have nots'' with technology. We try and take school districts with lower budgets and bring them on par with other districts, so that all of these students will have the same job opportunities. We teach kids the importance of staying in school.

We're also involved in ''inter-generational'' learning. We sponsor a senior computer literacy program. This gives the elderly, many of whom have disabilities or are shut-ins, another way to communicate, and we think that's critically important.

Q. As a telecommunications company, do you provide telecommunications networks?

A. No. The money cannot come back to us. What we try and do is foster the use of technology. For example, we're funding a program this year at Bay Shore High School which will hopefully improve the academic performance and work force readiness of their students. This grant is going toward a robotics club. The school is purchasing supplies, computer hardware and software so their students can learn to make robots. This will give them another skill set. They'll be able to compete with other schools, and, later, in the work force.

We're also sending volunteers from our engineering group to share their expertise, talk with students about careers in engineering and discuss how what they learn through this project can be applied in the work force.

Q. Do you help the groups come up with ideas?

A. Not really. When someone applies for a grant, they have to supply me with a budget indicating what they plan to use the funding for. We try and make sure they have the instructional portion already covered -- they must either have someone on staff, or be able to bring someone in, who knows the technology they want to use and can train them.

Q. What percentage are accepted?

A. We're now able to help, in some capacity, 50 to 75 percent of applicants. The number of applications I get is growing all the time because of increased publicity. I can't always fund groups in the dollar amount they request, but we always try and help in some capacity -- if the project fits within our program. If we can't fund them with dollars, I'll look for other ways to help, such as sending in speakers and volunteers.
