Few Teacher Vacancies as New York City Recruits 5,000 to Schools

By RANDAL C. ARCHIBOLD
Published: September 8, 1998

After years of chronic difficulty attracting qualified teachers to the nation's largest school system, New York City education officials say they have hired 5,000 teachers in one of the most competitive hiring seasons in recent memory -- enough to fill virtually every classroom when the city's 1,100 public schools open tomorrow.

Beyond its widely publicized hiring of two dozen math and science teachers from Austria, the Board of Education started recruiting earlier and more aggressively, drawing not only from teachers' colleges in the city but also from State University campuses throughout New York, as well as from Massachusetts, Spain and Puerto Rico.

Board of Education recruiters said that as of Friday, they had hired 5,249 teachers, leaving only about 200 vacancies in a work force of 72,000 -- a marked contrast to last year, when schools had nearly 3,000 vacancies the week before they opened.

''We expect to open fully staffed for only the second time this decade,'' said Askia Davis, superintendent of District 5 in Harlem, which hired 70 teachers this year.

Moreover, officials say, the early recruiting paid off in better credentials among those hired: Nearly three-quarters of the new teachers hold state and city certification, reflecting steady improvement from 10 years ago, when only a little over half were fully certified. Then as now, new teachers have to pass three state-administered written exams and a far simpler half-hour, oral city exam.

But as it fills the final vacancies, the school system is struggling to address stubborn teacher shortages in math, science and bilingual education. Late last week, the board's hiring center at 65 Court Street, Brooklyn, buzzed with a flurry of last-minute hiring in those areas, and officials scrambled to fill surprise openings created by city teachers who bolted for more lucrative suburban jobs.

Officials estimate that half of the new teachers are white in a school system that is 83 percent black, Hispanic and Asian. Most have no classroom experience, yet they are older than previous groups of recruits, with the average age now 28, up from 24 several years ago.

The higher average age is due mainly to the greater numbers of people like Mary Jean Walsh, who opted for teaching as a second career.

Ms. Walsh, 46, who rose from bank teller to a vice presidency during 26 years at Chase Manhattan, will leave the cubicle-lined offices of the bank's Metrotech offices in Brooklyn for the classrooms of Thurgood Marshall Academy for Learning and Social Change in Harlem. There she will teach social studies to seventh and eighth graders.

''I have heard lots of stories about teaching middle-school children, from, 'They are very difficult' to, 'You love them,' '' said Ms. Walsh, a certified teacher who received a master's degree in education from the New School for Social Research in the spring. ''But I have a lot of energy and I am certainly willing to try it.''

Like her, most of the new teachers have never held teaching jobs beyond their year of student teaching required for certification. That means the system continues its struggle for a more seasoned staff; the median number of years of experience is 13 in the city but 16 statewide, according to the State Education Department.

Another neophyte is Bayana Charles, a 22-year-old graduate of Brooklyn College who has never taught a class but, after a five-day seminar put on by her school, is preparing for her first day as a biology teacher at George W. Wingate High School in Brooklyn.

''I think I'm going to stare at 30 pairs of eyes and I'm going to be really nervous,'' said Ms. Charles, a biology major still seeking certification. She decided to go into teaching late in her studies at Brooklyn College and so missed her student teaching stint.

By and large, say people inside and outside the system, the recruiting process worked better this year. Instead of waiting until August, when most qualified prospects already had jobs in other districts, the board began searching in the spring, holding job fairs and sending recruiters to campuses. The board's human resources division also sought to streamline an infamously confused and frustrating hiring process that one recruit, according to a 1995 report by a school watchdog group, likened to ''the Motor Vehicles Department times 10.''

In 11 districts the board identifies as ''hard to staff'' -- generally those in low-income areas with low-performing schools -- satellite offices were set up to handle hiring. That and the early recruiting eliminated the late August cattle call of past years, in which overflow crowds filled the downtown Brooklyn building as the board sought someone, anyone, for thousands of positions, said Gary Barton, who oversees hiring for the board. The scene scared hundreds of recruits away, said the 1995 report by the Education Priorities Panel, an independent monitoring group.

Aside from filling vacancies, the recruiters are seeking to remake the teaching force. About two-thirds of all New York City teachers hold their certification, compared with 76 percent statewide, according to the Education Department.
