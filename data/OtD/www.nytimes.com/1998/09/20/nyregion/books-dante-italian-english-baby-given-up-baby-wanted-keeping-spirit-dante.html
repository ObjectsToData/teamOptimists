BOOKS: Dante From Italian to English, Baby Given Up and Baby Wanted; Keeping the Spirit Of Dante Alighieri

By MARJORIE KAUFMAN
Published: September 20, 1998

A professor of English and humanities at Dowling College has produced a book from hell. ''Inferno'' (Pantheon) is Elio Zappulla's English translation of the first of three canticles of Dante Alighieri's 14th-century epic poem, ''The Divine Comedy.''

Why another translation when more than 100 exist?

''I did this translation not only because of an inner compulsion, but also because most modern English translations available didn't quite hit the mark,'' said Mr. Zappulla, 65, during an interview at his home in Stony Brook. ''They are accurate, but a boring read. Many are lacking fire. They are not a joy to read. With the exception of Robert Pinsky, who did an excellent job poetically, many of the others, although brilliant scholars, have not turned out fine poetry.''

The 1994 translation by Mr. Pinsky, the poet laureate of the United States, is in terza rima, the rhyme pattern Dante invented. But like many others, Mr. Zappulla chose blank verse, unrhymed iambic pentameter, for his translation.

''The smoothness of the read and some kind of fidelity to the original would be destroyed if I tried to rhyme,'' he said. ''But I was conscious of each line, and used alliteration and internal rhyme and other poetic devices whenever possible.''

''No one who attempts to render the complex simplicity of Dante's Italian into English will succeed in capturing much more than the atmosphere of the poem,'' he said. ''The challenge to create a clear, readable English version that retained some of the original's spirit was a motivating force.''

From the final verse of ''Inferno,'' Mr. Zappulla's translation reads:

Here, my guide

Ahead, we walked along a secret road

And thus could find our way back to the world

Illuminated by the sun. We did not care

To rest, but climbed and climbed until we saw

An opening, and through it were laid bare

Eternal beauties that the Heavens hold.

And walking out,

We saw once more the stars.

Mr. Zappulla said he was introduced to the poetry of Dante as a child when his father, Giuseppe, read to him in Italian.

''There was something quite Dantean about my father,'' the younger Mr. Zappulla wrote in the acknowledgement to his book. ''His fierce pride; his intolerance of mediocrity (especially in literature); his tremendous love for both his native Italy and his adopted country, America. It was his abiding interest in Dante, in literature, history and the study of languages that greatly influenced my own choice of career.''

Mr. Zappulla, born in Brooklyn, has lived and taught on Long Island for more than 30 years. Although the translation was conceived in the 1960's, it didn't materialize until the 1990's, when over a four-year period Mr. Zappulla divided his time between the project and a busy teaching schedule. It is illustrated with black-and-white reproductions of paintings by the contemporary artist Gregory Gillespie.

Mr. Zappulla is at work on the translation of ''Purgatorio,'' the second book of ''The Divine Comedy,'' and has plans to complete the trilogy with a translation of ''Paradiso.''

Dante's purpose in writing the poem was to examine the nature of sin for the rest of us, Mr. Zappulla said.

''I think of Dante as one of the Hebrew prophets, writing to the rest of us, pointing the right way,'' the translator said. ''I think he'd be flattered by the comparison. He had great plans for humanity. We could use him around today.''
