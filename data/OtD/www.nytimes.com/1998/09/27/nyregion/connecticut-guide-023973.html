CONNECTICUT GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: September 27, 1998

Reading Banned Books

In observance of Banned Book Week, which began yesterday and continues through next Saturday, Thomas Rylander, the First Selectman of Madison, will read from a selection of banned and challenged books tomorrow at noon at R. J. Julia Booksellers, 768 Post Road. Visitors may bring bag lunches and join the discussions that will follow this and additional noon readings scheduled through Thursday.

On Tuesday, Stephen Dannenberg, headmaster of the Country School, will read from ''The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn,'' banned and challenged across the country since 1885. Kay Griffin, superintendent of Madison's public schools, will read from her choice of books on Wednesday, followed on Thursday by Paul Collard, executive director of the International Festival of Arts and Ideas, reading from Roald Dahl's ''James and he Giant Peach.''

At 5 P.M. on Thursday the bookstore will sponsor a discussion of censorship for teen-agers, their parents and teachers. The number for more information is (203) 245-3959.

Collecting Art

Olivier Bernier, the historian, author and Metropolitan Museum of Art lecturer, will address the Greenwich Antiques Society's first luncheon of the season tomorrow at the Greenwich Country Club on Doubling Road.

His subject will be Madame de Pompadour, and the exceptional collections of paintings, furniture, silver, porcelain and books that she acquired, courtesy of Louis XV. As his mistress, she also commissioned art works, and she was responsible for the founding of the Sevres porcelain factory. Her impeccable taste and hands-on interest in these ventures produced a remarkable body of fine and decorative art.

Mr. Bernier is a familiar figure to audiences of ''Biography'' on the Arts and Entertainment network for his program on Marie Antoinette.

Tickets to the lunch are $50. The number is (203) 637-9598 for more information or reservations.

Horse Show in Bethel

The Nutmeg Benefit Horse Show will be in progress today from 8:30 in the morning until mid- to late afternoon at the UConn Cooperative Extension grounds in Bethel. Spectators are welcome free of charge.

Some 125 riders will put their mounts through a variety of trials in the ring and on the out-of-the-ring hunt course. English-style jumping events and Western trail trials are scheduled, along with a Gambler's Choice jumping competition. Riders range in age from small children on ponies to adults on hunters. Awards of ribbons and trophies will be distributed at the close of each segment.

To reach the site take Exit 8 eastbound or Exit 9 westbound off Interstate 84 and follow the signs to Route 6, then proceed on Route 6 east from Exit 8 or west from Exit 9 to the entrance, near the Stony Hill firehouse.

Westport Arts Festival

The first Arts Festival to be given by the Westport Arts Center will run from 11 A.M. to 5 P.M. next Saturday at the Westport Town Hall, 110 Myrtle Avenue. More than 35 events will be available at no cost, including craft activities for children, art exhibits, jazz and chamber ensembles, the jug band Washboard Slim and the Bluelights, a petting zoo, Amazing Andy the magician and juggler, and the life-sized puppets of Arm-of-the-Sea Theater.

Ever since it lost its headquarters at the Greens Farms Elementary School, which was reclaimed by the town to meet increased enrollments, the arts center has sought a new location in vain. It maintains a small administrative office at 431 Post Road East, but given its limited budget and the high cost of Westport real estate, it has not turned up a suitable space.

Meanwhile it carries on a minimal number of programs at the ''Y'' and the Nature Center, and in Southport's Pequot Library.

Celebrating the Potato

The Third Annual Potato Fest will be celebrated at the St. James School in Stratford on Friday from 5 to 10 P.M., Saturday from noon to 10, and next Sunday from noon to 5. Visitors can take part in potato games for children, musical entertainment and caricatures drawn while you wait.

For serious potato-eaters, vendors will offer for purchase an array of dishes, including cheddar potato soup, seafood potato chowder, sweet potato pie, garlic-mashed potatoes, stuffed potatoes and French fries.

The school is at 2110 Main Street, a block away from 1145 West Broad Street, where a painted brick and brownstone building stands on the site of Benjamin's Tavern. Legend has it that on Sept. 19, 1780, the Marquis de Lafayette met with General George Washington at the tavern. The innkeeper proudly served them what was then a delicacy discovered in South America by the Spanish conquistadors -- potatoes.

Admission will be $3. The number for more information is (203) 375-5994.

Strolling Concert

Laurel Masse is an a cappella soloist who will give a walking concert tour of the Aldrich Museum of Contemporary Art in Ridgefield on Friday from 7 to 9 P.M.

Her program, sung without accompaniment, includes ancient Greek songs, Scottish Highland laments, excerpts from J. S. Bach's suites for the cello, and other equally disparate selections.

A founding member of The Manhattan Transfer, she left the popular singing group after seven years and began pursuing a solo career, weaving together a variety of legends, songs, poetry, chant and improvisation, all without instrumental or vocal backup. She is sometimes compared to the bards of old, who strolled through towns and entertained at court with similar a cappella repertories of song and story. Admission to the program is $15 and includes wine and snacks. The number is (203) 438-4529 for reservations or more information.

The museum is at 258 Main Street, and on Saturday it will waive admission fees to its new Roy Lichtenstein and Artists' Interventions exhibitions in celebration of National Arts and Humanities Month. A free gallery talk will be conducted at 2 P.M., and cider and doughnuts will be served in the sculpture garden. During the month of October discounted admission vouchers will be distributed for lectures and workshops. Call the same number for more information.

Vows of a Hermit

The life consecration of a hermit, a ceremony that is customarily held in private, will be open to the public next Saturday at 6 P.M. in Trinity Church, 484 Lime Rock Road, Lakeville. The Rev. Randall Horton, who has spent his 13-year novitiate with the Order of the Holy Cross hermitage in West Park, N.Y., will speak his vows to the Rev. Richard Grein, Episcopal Bishop of New York from The Cathedral of St. John the Divine in Manhattan.

''I wanted to do this as a teaching experience for the public,'' said Brother Randall, who has translated the rite from a 14th century middle-English ceremony and composed a musical mass setting for the occasion.

His life as a Benedictine monk will continue at his solitary hermitage in Lakeville, but, he said, ''I spend 20 percent of my time as a staff member of Trinity Church, and as Chaplain of Mountainside, an alcohol and drug abuse center in Falls Village.

''Few people are called to this vocation,'' he said, noting that there are only a couple of dozen hermits in the United States, the most famous being the trappist monk, Thomas Merton. Medievalists and people of no particular religious faith, he believes, will find the ceremony of unusual interest.

The church phone number for directions is (860) 435-2627. ELEANOR CHARLES
