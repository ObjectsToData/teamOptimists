THE GUIDE

By ELEANOR CHARLES
Published: January 18, 1998

The Harlem Renaissance

Black History Month is to be observed at the Hudson River Museum in Yonkers from Friday through March 8 with an exhibition of 35 black and white photographs of Harlem between the two World Wars -- the period known as the Harlem Renaissance.

The pictures were taken by James Van Der Zee, a self-taught photographer who established a studio in Harlem in 1916 and proceeded to capture on film the cultural, street and family life of the inhabitants. In addition to his portraits and scenes, he provided documentation of the Back-to-Africa movement as the official photographer for Marcus Garvey, leader of the movement.

Dr. Regenia A. Perry, owner of the Van Der Zee collection, will present a slide lecture on highlights of the exhibition next Sunday at 3 P.M., followed by an opening reception at 5 P.M. Dr. Perry is an art historian and was a friend of the photographer for several years before his death in 1983 at the age of 97.

Future programs on various aspects of the exhibition include a talk on Feb. 8 by Michael Henry Adams, an architectural historian whose book ''Harlem Lost and Found'' will be published this year by Monacelli Press. Two documentary films, one about the Harlem Renaissance and the other of Van Der Zee himself talking about his work, will be shown on Feb. 15.

The museum, at 511 Warburton Avenue, is open Wednesdays through Saturdays from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M., Sundays from noon to 5 P.M. and Fridays until 9 P.M. Admission is $3, half-price for children younger than 13 and adults older than 64. Call 963-4550 for more information.

Schools and Religion

How the public schools should or should not involve themselves in religious teaching or activity, drug and alcohol abuse and prevention and other issues will be the subject of a forum on Thursday at 8 P.M. at the Jewish Community Center, 371 South Broadway in Tarrytown.

The program will be moderated by Avi Azrieli, an Israeli-born lawyer who will compare the handling of such controversies in Israel and in Westchester, where he now lives.

Some of the discussion will focus on a lawsuit filed last March by three families against the Bedford School District, charging violation of the 1st and 14th amendments to the Constitution regarding separation of church and state and students' privacy, respectively. Admission is free. For information, call 366-7898.

Dates for Eagle Watching

A series of free eagle watches at Georges Island Park in Montrose has been scheduled by the Westchester County Department of Parks, Recreation and Conservation beginning today and continuing on Jan. 31, Feb. 21 and 28 and March 1, all at 4 P.M.

The park and its fish-rich waters have been attracting bald eagles for the last few winters, said Christopher Letts, the Hudson River educator who will lead the watches. The birds have been sighted cruising the river aboard ice floes and people-watching from their roosts in trees.

Bring binoculars if possible, or use one of the telescopes at the site. To get there, take the Montrose-Buchanan Exit off Route 9, go north on the Albany Post Road past the Veterans' Affairs Hospital to the Dutch Street entrance into the park. Call 242-6300 for more information.

Hirschfeld Exhibition

Seven decades of Al Hirschfeld's line drawings, paintings and graphics go on display today through April 5 at the Katonah Museum of Art. Museum officials say this is the first comprehensive exhibition of Mr. Hirschfeld's work.

Later this year the 95-year-old artist will be represented in a major exhibition on caricature at the National Portrait Gallery in Washington, and his drawings will be featured in a show at Harvard University. Best known for his incisive portrayals of the famous, fledgling and upwardly mobile people of show business, he has rooted his own celebrity in stinginess: he never wastes a line.

Many special programs have been organized by the museum in connection with the show. Among them are a children's drawing workshop on Feb. 8 from 1:30 to 3:30 P.M. for a charge of $10, and drawing workshops from a model, scheduled for adults on Feb. 23 and March 2 and 9 for $20 each or $50 for three.

The museum, on Route 22 at Jay Street, is open Sundays and Tuesdays through Fridays from 1 to 5 P.M., Saturdays from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. Guided tours are conducted Tuesdays through Sundays at 2:30 P.M. Admission is $2, free to children younger than 12. Call 232-9555 for more information.

Focusing on Leisure Time

How people spend their leisure time is to be the focus of the Westchester Great Books Council's fifth annual event next Saturday from 9:30 A.M. to 3:30 P.M. at Briarcliff Manor High School.

Susan Orlean, a writer for The New Yorker Magazine since 1987 and author of many magazine articles, will be on hand to talk about her book, ''Saturday Night.'' Published in 1990, it examines what various people from around the United States do on that night of the week.

A second work to be discussed is an essay by Mortimer Adler, a co-founder of the Great Books movement, titled ''Work and Leisure.''

The Westchester Great Books Council was formed five years ago as an affiliate of the Great Books Foundation in Chicago. It holds regularly scheduled discussions of classic literature, philosophy and modern fiction.

Registration for Saturday's program costs $45 and includes a copy of ''Saturday Night,'' morning coffee, lunch and snacks. Call 762-8691 to register or obtain more information.

Society of Comedic Kids

Children 7 through 14 who have more than a passing interest in stand-up comedy, either as performers or members of an audience, may want to look into a new organization in New Rochelle called S.O.C.K., the Society of Comedic Kids.

Founded by Harris Karlin, a fifth- grade student at the Webster Magnet School for the Arts and Humanities, with a little help from his parents, the group held its first meeting last November at the Boys and Girls' Club, 79 Seventh Street.

A second session is scheduled today, and two more will be held on March 8 and May 17, all from 1 to 3 P.M. Included in the programming are performances by professional comedians and magicians, screening of classic comedy movie and television tapes and visits by comedy celebrities.

A membership fee of $10 will be waived for children who cannot afford it. Members receive a membership card and other benefits. For more information, call 636-3784.

Trout and Fly Fishing

''Fishing the Frying Pan River'' in Basalt, Colo., will be the subject of a slide presentation by Dr. Richard Jogodnik at the Tuesday meeting of the Croton Watershed Chapter of Trout Unlimited.

Dr. Jogodnik, a longtime member of the organization, is a well-known fly tier and speaker in Canada, Europe and the United States. He teaches Trout Unlimited's School of Fly Fishing each spring as part of the Katonah Adult Education series, and his gift for photography will be evident in the pictures accompanying his talk. The program will begin at 7:30 P.M. in the Fox Lane Middle School on Route 172 in Mount Kisco. Refreshments will be served, and guests are welcome without charge. For more information about the meeting or Trout Unlimited, a nonprofit ecological organization, call 273-5630 ELEANOR CHARLES
