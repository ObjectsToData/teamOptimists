Operators Are Standing By

By Martha Bayles
Published: January 18, 1998

MADE POSSIBLE BY. . .

The Death of Public Broadcasting

in the United States.

By James Ledbetter.

280 pp. New York:

Verso. $25.

IT is not an exaggeration to say that James Ledbetter would destroy public broadcasting in order to save it. While making a forceful case for public broadcasting as a national forum accessible to all, protected against direct political interference and free of commercial imperatives, he also delivers a blistering critique of the existing system's many failures. His model for public television seems to be public radio, which he admires for its scrappy eagerness to deal with unpopular topics and its responsiveness to local constituencies. His policy recommendations include a proposal that the Corporation for Public Broadcasting ''be liberated from direct Presidential control'' and a proposal to radically restructure the ''preposterously inefficient'' way money flows through the system -- an arrangement that would make ''Rube Goldberg blush.''

Wisely, Ledbetter, a staff writer at The Village Voice, acknowledges that it is not possible to depoliticize public broadcasting. He objects not because the board is susceptible to political pressure (that is inevitable) but because, as directly appointed by the President, it is too susceptible. He offers several examples of blatantly political appointments, from the Republican Party campaigner Thomas Moore (under Richard Nixon) to the Mississippi state legislator Igby Turner (under Jimmy Carter) and, of course, Reagan appointees like Sonia Landau.

Some of the targets in ''Made Possible By. . .'' may be familiar, but to his credit Ledbetter does not mount some familiar hobbyhorses. Perhaps because (by his own admission) he grew up with Sesame Street, he chooses to put away childish things and defend public broadcasting in terms of its importance to the grown-up life of the nation. So much of the culture war is a children's crusade, with every argument framed in terms of what is good for children, that Ledbetter's refusal to hide behind Big Bird is refreshing.

Ledbetter's weakness is a clear ideological bias that leads him to see a golden age in the 1950's and 60's, when National Educational Television (the Ford Foundation-sponsored precursor of PBS) and the Public Broadcasting Laboratory (the Federally underwritten pilot program) broadcast several left-wing public affairs shows. For example, he relishes the impact of programs like ''Inside North Vietnam,'' a British documentary that was shown on NET after being rejected by CBS and denounced in Congress.

Ledbetter is entitled to his bias, of course. But it distorts his argument in two ways. First, it injects a note of paranoia into his otherwise reasonable critique of corporate underwriting. There are good reasons to be concerned about the growing power of corporate money in public broadcasting. But Ledbetter neglects the many cases of support of fair-minded public affairs programming. For example, he offers a detailed account of how, when the Archer Daniels Midland Corporation was accused of price fixing, the story was played down on ''The MacNeil/Lehrer Newshour,'' sponsored by the company. If true, this is a fair criticism. But does it justify his refusal to acknowledge the program's consistent record of presenting serious policy debate?

Second, Ledbetter's anticorporate bias cripples his discussion of cultural programming. He admires public radio for its emphasis on public affairs; as he points out, there has long been a tug-of-war between public affairs and cultural programming on National Public Radio. It's clear what side he is on: in this book, he regards cultural programming as a distraction at best.

Consider his treatment of the most prominent example of corporate underwriting of cultural programming: Mobil Oil's longstanding support of British imports like ''Masterpiece Theater'' and ''Mystery!'' The former he defines as little more than a weapon in the class struggle: costume dramas about haut-bourgeois life that deliberately marginalize the workers. Pieces that do not fit this description are simply not mentioned. As for ''Mystery!,'' based on undeniably popular fare like Sherlock Holmes and Hercule Poirot, it is likewise ignored.

Along with class bias, he accuses Mobil-supported fare of politically useful Anglophilia, charging at one point that the only workers seen on PBS are the servants in ''Upstairs, Downstairs.'' Yet this overlooks some of the best programming to have appeared on PBS in recent years, like ''American Playhouse,'' a drama series that can hardly be accused of sticking to upper-class themes. Even more glaring is Ledbetter's refusal to acknowledge ''The American Experience,'' a series that has presented superb film biographies of Presidents from Theodore Roosevelt to Harry Truman to Richard Nixon, as well as documentaries about American lives (including those of working people and the poor). From this cornucopia he plucks ''The Liberators,'' a film about black American troops opening Nazi camps that was shown to be inaccurate.

In his crusade against commercial television, Ledbetter points out that the History Channel is compromised by its corporate self-interest and says that history is too important to leave to market forces. Yet he expresses great contempt for ''The Civil War,'' by Ken Burns, which he faults both for its popularity and for treating a subject safely in the past. The historian S. Frederick Starr recently quipped that American intellectual life would be enriched by the presence of ''real Marxists,'' who would instantly grasp the importance of historical understanding to the maintenance of any political regime. Ledbetter, who has cut his teeth on the mongrelized Marxism of The Village Voice, lacks this edge.

This book is valuable for its many illustrations of how both political and corporate meddling have damaged public broadcasting. Both reveal the stupidity of the censor, all the more insidious when the censor is also the patron. On ways to guard against political meddling, Ledbetter's recommendations are cogent. It's too bad he doesn't devote the same attention to finding ways to guard against corporate meddling. If the stakes for would-be underwriters are so high, then surely there is room for negotiation between them and the good folk who create sterling programs like ''The American Experience.'' It's too bad Ledbetter allows his biases to keep him from tackling this vital question.
