Streetscapes / 19-25 St. Marks Place; The Eclectic Life of a Row of East Village Houses

By CHRISTOPHER GRAY
Published: November 8, 1998

IN the cavernous former Electric Circus discotheque on St. Marks Place, the Rev. Joyce Hartwell is locked in an unusual battle against social problems as she seeks a developer to build a hotel tower.

Ms. Hartwell, a neighborhood activist, is juggling a 10-year-old plan for a 176-room tower rising from the rear of her property with a service-industry training program for its staff. She says she is actively looking for a socially responsible developer.

St. Mark's Place, an extension of Eighth Street east of Third Avenue, once had the cachet of Sutton Place -- a separately numbered, ritzy enclave set apart from the city. In 1833 the developer Thomas E. Davis built three neo-Federal houses at 19, 21 and 23 St. Marks Place.

By the 1850's, at least three of the private homes on the block became commercial boarding houses. At the same time, Cooper Union was completed across Third Avenue from St. Mark's Place and the neighborhood developed an institutional character.

Gradually, the area emerged as a German quarter, and in 1870 the Arion Society, a German singing and musical club, bought 19 and 21 St. Marks Place, and their architects, Schulze & Schoen, added a mansard roof and additional door trim. In 1874, The New York Times reported that at the society's annual carnival, which included a musical skit lampooning William Marcy (Boss) Tweed, members were required to wear hats in the shape of dolphins.

By the 1880 census the house next door, 23 St. Marks Place, had been turned into a multiple dwelling. Its occupants included the family of 54-year-old William Schwind, a costumer born in Westphalia, and a 32-year-old Herman Zillon, a musician born in Pomerania.

In 1887 the Arion Society moved up to a new clubhouse at 59th Street and Park Avenue and George Ehret, a brewer and real estate investor, bought 19 and 21 and, next year, 23 St. Marks Place. He joined the buildings and leased them out as a ballroom and community hall, which became known as Arlington Hall.

In the 1920's the Polish National Home bought 19, 21, 23 and 25 St. Marks Place, combining the four buildings to house Polish organizations and a Polish restaurant open to the public. By the 1950's this section of Greenwich Village was attracting the Beat Generation.

Stanley Tolkin, a bar owner, took over part of the complex -- apparently the basement area of 19 and 21 St. Marks Place -- as a performance space. The Fugs -- famous for songs like ''Group Grope,'' and ''Slum Goddess of the Lower East Side'' -- often performed there.

The lower level, the Dom, developed into a nightclub, and in 1967 the dance club Electric Circus opened upstairs. Its pulsating lights, rock music and white wool tent-ceilings attracted everyone from hippies to Tom Wolfe and George Plimpton. In The Village Voice, Jack Newfield described it as the place where ''the New Frontier met the Underground.''

The Electric Circus mixed light shows, music, circus performers and experimental theater; one of its press releases invited people to ''play games, dress as they like, dance, sit, think, tune in and turn on.''

IN 1969 Gwathmey & Henderson, architects, renovated the space with undulating white walls, curving windows and light shows. But the Electric Circus closed in 1971, and over several years Ms. Hartwell acquired the buildings as head of the All-Craft Foundation. She says the purchase price was gained through fund raising and donations.

In the early 60's, when she was in her 20's, Ms. Hartwell, who says she was affected by the hands-on training from her first grade at the progressive Little Red School House on Bleecker Street, founded the Lady Carpenter Institute to teach women carpentry skills. This evolved into job training, self-help and theater programs, and she has since bitten off a good-sized chunk of the world to save in the decrepit, empty-looking halls.

On the street-level floor of 25 St. Marks Place she has opened Imami Cyber Cafe, an alcohol- and drug-free meeting place with Internet hookups. On the street level of 19 and 21 she has set up selling/working booths for Native American and local craftspeople.

Upstairs, in the old Electric Circus space, she has plans for a New Age cabaret, also alcohol- and drug-free, and elsewhere in the building she runs a short-term shelter for addicts waiting for admission to treatment programs.

Ms. Hartwell envisions her 19-to-25 St. Marks Place as a point of departure for the street, and she is distressed by the drug dealing and cheap trinket shops that people associate with the street.

''When I started out with Lady Carpenter,'' says Ms. Hartwell, who came from a labor background, ''I wanted to make millions, like Ben & Jerry's, but in a socially responsible way. We want to make money, but also to get people to contribute to their own recovery.''
