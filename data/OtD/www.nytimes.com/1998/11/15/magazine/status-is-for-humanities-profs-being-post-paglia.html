Status is ... for Humanities Profs; Being Post-Paglia

By Daniel Mendelsohn
Published: November 15, 1998

When asked what he planned to do with himself after leaving Oxford, where he'd distinguished himself as a brilliant classics student, the young Oscar Wilde replied, ''I don't know, but whatever it is, I won't be some dried-up old don.''

But since the late 1970's, when the deconstruction guru Jacques Derrida started becoming a household name, professional intellectuals have been able to achieve the kind of A-list status that had once enticed the limelight-loving Wilde away from academe. In the 1980's, Allan Bloom hit the best-seller lists by decrying the closing of the American mind. In the early 1990's, Camille Paglia weighed in on everything from Madonna to Princess Di on the nation's op-ed pages, Henry Louis Gates Jr. and Simon Schama were snapped up by Tina Brown's newly ''hot'' New Yorker and, more recently, the Princeton feminism diva Elaine Showalter was churning out TV reviews for People and musing on Courreges boots in the pages of Vogue.

But the latest crop of humanities Ph.D.'s -- people in their late 20's and early 30's -- seem to be returning to a much older paradigm of intellectual status: the purer, glitz-free life of the mind. ''We all thought those celebrity academics were kind of fascinating at the time,'' observes Nancy Worman, an assistant professor of classics at Barnard who completed her Ph.D. in 1994. But, she adds, ''they turned out to be pretty silly.''

Indeed, for these younger members of the academy, popularization is, if anything, accompanied by a loss of intellectual status. ''Camille Paglia,'' says Iakovos Vasiliou, an assistant professor of philosophy at Georgia State University, ''is the equivalent of those late-night infomercials. They keep telling you how simple everything is and how you can get rich quick. But you're not going to find serious thinkers on these shows.''

In this respect, the humanities are once again resembling the sciences, where acclaim is earned through results, usually in the form of hard data, and status is conferred not by television appearances but by one's peers. Edward Beltrami, professor of applied mathematics at Stony Brook University, is blunt: ''You don't see mathematicians on 'Charlie Rose.'''

In hindsight, it may be that the rise of ''academic superstar'' status in the late 80's -- the decade, after all, that gave us other bizarre hybrids, like writer-models -- was the superficial byproduct of the loss of status that academic and intellectual life suffered during the upheavals of the 60's and 70's. The result, according to D. Graham Burnett, a 28-year-old historian at Columbia's Society of Fellows, is that ''we invented a kind of public intellectual that fit our commercial culture -- a public intellectual you could imagine seeing on MTV. But it certainly doesn't describe the people I know who are graduate students and post-docs.''

It certainly doesn't describe Peter Nickowitz, a seventh-year Ph.D. in English at N.Y.U. who is studying under Harold Bloom and who, despite model-high cheekbones and a wardrobe that would get past anyone's velvet ropes, seems to have both Kenneth Cole-shod feet firmly on the ground. ''To me, the real achievement would be to make a lasting intellectual mark,'' he says, ''to become an adjective'' -- Bloomian, say, or Foucauldian. After all, Nickowitz says, even celebrity is fleeting. ''There are certain people who are trendy and starlike for a while, but let's face it, do you think Elaine Showalter gets a front table at the Mercer hotel bar? I doubt it.'' Daniel Mendelsohn
