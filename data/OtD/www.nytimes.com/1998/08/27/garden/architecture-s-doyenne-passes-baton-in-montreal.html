Architecture's Doyenne Passes Baton In Montreal

By JULIE V. IOVINE
Published: August 27, 1998

PHYLLIS LAMBERT, the formidable doyenne of architecture, whose youthful rebellion against her father, the liquor tycoon Samuel Bronfman, resulted in Mies van der Rohe designing the Seagram Building, announced last week that she had chosen her successor as director of the Canadian Center for Architecture in Montreal. Kurt W. Forster, founding director of the Getty Center for the History of Art and the Humanities in Los Angeles and, currently, chairman of the art and architecture department at the Federal Institute of Technology in Zurich, will assume the directorship on March 1, 1999.

The Swiss-born scholar, 63, is expected to bring a considerable measure of international cachet and academic integrity to the center that Ms. Lambert, 71, founded, using her own inheritance money, in 1979.

''I found myself worrying about what would happen if I were knocked down by a bus,'' Ms. Lambert said last week, ''and I wanted to be sure that the center was in the most imaginative and competent hands.''

Over the last two decades, the Canadian Center for Architecture -- a museum, library and study center wrapped in a $40 million edifice designed by Peter Rose -- has amassed a collection strong on contemporary architecture and sponsored timely and intellectually challenging exhibitions. ''The C.C.A. is one of the few institutions that regularly has exhibitions of international importance of the sort that MOMA once did but hasn't done in a long time,'' the architect Richard Meier said. The exhibition now on display, ''The American Lawn: Surface of Everyday Life'' (through Nov. 8), has attracted significant attention for its provocative scrutiny of the seemingly mundane suburban lawn.

Ms. Lambert will continue as chairwoman of the board of trustees and remain an active member of the acquisitions committee. She is presently developing an exhibition dedicated to Mies van der Rohe's years in America, which will open at the Whitney Museum in 2000 and travel to the center in the summer of 2001.

Mr. Forster, who has been living in Zurich since 1992, will be moving to Montreal next February. He anticipates a smooth transition from academia to the museum world. ''It's not like the Getty, where there were 35,000 books and a lot of confused ideas about what might be possible,'' Mr. Forster said. ''My first obligation is to follow exactly the course Phyllis has set, and then broaden and sharpen it.'' He added that he was contemplating a series of exhibitions exploring defining moments in architecture, like the year 1910, when Le Corbusier decided to leave Berlin for France.

Mr. Forster's appointment, in fact, may be a kind of defining moment of its own. ''This is terrific news,'' said Terence Riley, chief curator of the department of architecture and design at the Museum of Modern Art. ''He's a natural to follow Phyllis. And to her credit, she has selected a personality as strong as her own. This will heat up the scene, and that's good for everyone.''
