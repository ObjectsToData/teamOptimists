Surf Cities

By Alexander Frater
Published: August 2, 1998

THE BEACH

The History of Paradise on Earth.

By Lena Lencek and Gideon Bosker.

Illustrated. 310 pp. New York:

Viking. $25.95.

YOU can sometimes tell a book by its bibliography, and the one in ''The Beach,'' an engagingly eccentric ''history of paradise on earth,'' has 335 entries, ranging from Dante, Shakespeare, Sophocles, Herodotus and the Holy Bible to Laurel Blossom, editor of ''Splash! Great Writing About Swimming.'' Lena Lencek and Gideon Bosker are chroniclers of popular culture who have written three previous books together. They start this one with a line from T. S. Eliot's ''Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock'' -- ''Do I dare to eat a peach? / I shall wear white flannel trousers, and walk upon the beach'' -- and conclude with an appendix, likely to cause controversy, listing the world's 80 best resorts, with asterisks marking their personal favorites; the authors have apparently left footprints in the sands of some 20 countries. They have opted for an approach that is impersonal, scholarly and briskly good-humored.

Each chapter carries a dateline giving it a historical perspective, often imagined. Thus in ''Dorset, 1826,'' we encounter a nameless ''young, thick-haired British aristocrat'' arriving at a lonely beach, where he will demonstrate the authors' occasional willingness to shift their prose into Roget-powered overdrive: ''Suddenly, as if compelled by an untamable passion emanating from the root of his being, he throws himself into the turbulence, and feels a glorious, epiphanic wave crash against the very interstices of his soul.'' Bosker is an assistant clinical professor of medicine at Yale, so he presumably knows exactly where one's soular interstices are located. Yet, despite these lapses, he and Lencek, a professor of Russian and the humanities at Reed College, have an unexpectedly interesting tale to tell.

Popular beach culture began with the Romans. In season the Via Appia became clogged with traffic bound for resorts growing so fast that to protect sea views Justinian issued an imperial edict banning construction within 100 feet of the shore. Today anyone bathing off Antium, the Palm Beach of classical times -- Caligula and Nero had holiday palaces there -- may look down ''and see scattered remnants of terraces, galleries and precious marbles.'' During the Dark Ages the ocean became the ''great abyss'' of Genesis, and the Garden of Eden remained landlocked while apocalyptic notions of the Great Flood created fear and dread well into the 18th century. Beaches demarcated the known world. Beyond lay chaos.

It was the English who challenged this notion. In 1736 a bathing establishment opened in the northeastern coastal town of Scarborough. Other seaside communities followed suit, most offering horse-drawn ''machines'' that took customers out to a decent depth. (There women scrambled through a tunnel-like ''modesty hood'' into the arms of the ''dipper,'' a brawny local woman who taught the revolutionary concept of dog-paddling and, by all accounts, only released them when they were half-drowned.) British doctors prescribed cold plunges for everything from leprosy to the king's evil, and seawater became a popular bottled beverage sold at inns. Britain's first passenger trains were operational by 1830, and as beaches grew more crowded, social rules had to be devised -- ladies at Blackpool, for example, being summoned to the water by a bell rung at flood tide (any man caught loitering had to forfeit a bottle of wine, which, presumably, later helped stabilize the ladies' equilibrium and body temperature).

Sea bathing, or Anglomania, as the Continentals knew it, proved particularly popular among aristocrats and intellectuals. Goethe, who attributed the dash and beauty of Englishwomen to the fact that they could swim, visited Sir William Hamilton's villa on the Bay of Naples, where, at Hamilton's request, a dozen local boys gamboled daily in the sea, ''grouped themselves and posed as they played.'' Byron, ''the English Fish,'' swam the Hellespont; Shelley suffered such misery away from the ocean ''that only by plunging his head into a washbasin could he revive his spirits.'' Swinburne spent hours floating on his back off the Isle of Wight, and Matthew Arnold, who since childhood had nurtured the fantasy of being a corpse at the bottom of Lake Windermere, found solace lying on the seabed. Drowning was a rapturous part of the Romantic vision, and it was Shelley, off Viareggio with a volume of Sophocles in hand, who actually pulled it off.

In the United States by the turn of the century, resorts on the Jersey Shore could boast a seasonal influx of eight million day-trippers. In Atlantic City surf dancing, or aquatic quadrilles, became so popular that sections of the beach were set aside for the activity. The Methodists who ''discovered'' Martha's Vineyard in 1835 attracted 12,000 worshipers to their Sunday services -- until a craze for cricket raged through the island, with a devastating effect on attendance. (They moved on to Ocean Grove, N.J., where 150,000, many in dripping bathing costumes, routinely turned up to revival meetings.)
