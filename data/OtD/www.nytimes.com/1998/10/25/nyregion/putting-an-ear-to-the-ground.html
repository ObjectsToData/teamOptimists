Putting an Ear to the Ground

By GEORGE JAMES
Published: October 25, 1998

AS darkness enveloped Chestnut Hill Cemetery, a Union soldier leaned against a tall granite tombstone -- his own

Seeing strangers approach, he straightened. ''How are you this fine, lovely evening?'' he asked. ''I just have to say that I enjoy coming out and seeing the greenery, breathing in the fresh air -- a nice mixture of Burger King and The Kumquat Restaurant.'' He motioned with his rifle toward the lights of strip malls in the distance.

He said his name was Jacob Smock, born 1827, and that after the Confederates fired on Fort Sumter, he had joined the 14th New Jersey Regiment that defended Washington against a rebel incursion, saw combat until the end of the war and returned to his family in Old Bridge Village, where he died in 1909 at the age of 82.

Throughout the cemetery you could see men and women in the apparel of their respective eras standing next to their gravestones, lantern-lit spectres in the deepening twilight, telling the stories of their lives and deaths to visitors as the last of the crickets complained about the cool autumn night.

It was a cemetery tour, one of more than a dozen under the auspices of the New Jersey Cultural Alliance that are being conducted in October as part of Arts and Humanities Month, and conveniently in time for Halloween.

There are about 400 cemeteries regulated by the New Jersey Cemetery Board, and many others affiliated with churches, synagogues or the military. And there are hundreds of abandoned cemeteries hidden within the woodlands or surrounded by housing developments.

New Jersey has its share of famous people buried here, from signers of the Declaration of Independence to modern writers, and little-known people like Jacob Smock who make graveyards come alive to history buffs, art lovers, students of architecture and landscape design, geneaologists and preservationists. And to increasing numbers of the baby boomers who are beginning to face their own mortality and want to check out the neighborhoods.

''As offbeat as it may sound, just because someone has stopped living doesn't mean that they have to stop teaching,'' said Dominick DeMarco of the New Jersey Council for the Humanities, one of the groups that comprises the New Jersey Cultural Alliance.

Last Sunday evening at Chestnut Hill Cemetery, as visitors followed a path illuminated by candles in paper bags anchored with sand, Mark Nonestied, the tour organizer, stood in early 19th-century garb -- a straw hat, cravat, vest and puffy cotton shirt -- holding a 20th-century walkie-talkie to keep in touch with the re-enactors and workers directing traffic.

''This tour is done at a time when people are bombarded with wrong impressions of ghouls and graveyards as haunted, scary places,'' said Mr. Nonestied, president of the New Jersey Graveyard Preservation Society, which was sponsoring the Chestnut Hill Cemetery Tour. ''We bring people in to show they are educational and historical in nature and there's a lot to learn.''

''There's nobody jumping out from tombstones trying to scare somebody,'' he added.

Mr. Nonestied, 27, museum outreach coordinator for the Middlesex County Cultural and Heritage Commission, helped to found the Graveyard Preservation Society about four years ago with Richard Veit, a history professor at Monmouth University and Janice Kohl Sarapin, an educational consultant, after they met while working on Ms. Sarapin's book, ''Old Burial Grounds of New Jersey: A Guide,'' (Rutgers University Press, 1994).

The society's purpose is to protect old, abandoned burial grounds that preservationists feel are being endangered and wiped out by a building boom in the state.

A LTHOUGH Mr. Nonestied has organized tours of Chestnut Hill Cemetery for the last five years, this is the first year that the New Jersey Cultural Alliance has included cemetery tours as part of Arts and Humanities Month.

Mr. DeMarco of the New Jersey Council for the Humanities said the idea for the program, titled ''Buried in Jersey,'' came together during an ''informal talk session'' in the Alliance on how to get more people involved in the arts and humanities.

Riverview Cemetery in Trenton has sponsored the most events. A tour earlier this month dealt with Civil War veterans buried there, including the Union commander, Gen. George B. McClellan.

Another Riverview tour concentrated on stonework, how markers are cut and the meaning of symbols on them. Still a third, scheduled for Halloween weekend, will deal with ''strange and fascinating stories'' of some people buried there, including those lost on the Titanic and Lusitania.

Evergreen Cemetery, a 115-acre graveyard that straddles Hillside, Newark and Elizabeth, will also be the scene of a tour. The author Stephen Crane is buried in Evergreen, one of the few cemeteries in New Jersey designated as a national historic site on its own and not as part of a church or historical district.

Other tours include St. Mary's Episcopal Church Cemetery in Burlington, one of the oldest in South Jersey dating to the late 1690's, and a number of graveyards in Cumberland County that will be visited by bus.
