Another Eli as the Hero Of the Industrial Revolution

By ALBERTA EISEMAN
Published: October 18, 1998

CLOCKS are taken for granted nowadays. People wake up to the summons of an alarm, keep an eye on the kitchen clock while making breakfast, check the time at work and in the car on the way home.

But a visitor to the American Clock and Watch Museum in Bristol is reminded of how rare clocks were until the middle of the 19th century, how they changed lives and how the evolution of clock manufacturing influenced the start the Industrial Revolution in the United States.

The Bristol area was central to the development of clocks and in 1952 a group of businesspeople decided to form a museum that would preserve the history, artifacts and memorabilia of the industry.

Over the years, the collection grew to more than 1,700 clocks, 1,600 watches and a sizable library of books, catalogues, photographs and documents. Recently, the museum opened a permanent exhibition entitled ''The Origins of the American Industrial Revolution in Connecticut Clockmaking.''

If the title sounds somewhat academic, the show itself seeks to please, with audio presentations, mural-size photographs, attractive artifacts and lively graphics. Supported by grants including one from the Connecticut Humanities Council, the show explores Connecticut clockmaking through the story of Eli Terry, who transformed timekeeping from a craft into an industry.

The first presentation evokes a shop circa 1790, and introduces Terry as a young apprentice, describing his work and wondering how clocks could be made more efficiently, so prices could be brought within the reach of average people.

Until that time, it took a craftsman working alone about a month to produce a single clock movement, the mechanical part of the clock. Then a carpenter would make a tall case to fit the movement.

In 1806, two merchants, Levi and Edward Porter, offered Terry a contract to buy 4,000 clocks if he could deliver them in three years.

''It was the key to Eli Terry's dream,'' said Nancy Connelly, the museum's director, who calls the enterprising Terry ''Connecticut's most important clockmaker'' and ''the hero of our story.''

With the help of Seth Thomas and Chauncey Jerome -- who later established factories of their own -- he developed the machines, patterns, gauges and techniques that enabled him to mass produce clocks.

''It was the first complicated mechanical product made in America with truly interchangeable components,'' said Ms. Connelly.

''Despite the credit given to Eli Whitney as father of the American Industrial Revolution,'' she continued, ''scholars have now determined that Whitney was never able to produce guns with truly interchangeable parts.''

Be that as it may, by the year 1810 Terry had developed a new, shorter clock that could stand on a shelf -- easier to crate and sell around the country -- and established a riverfront factory to manufacture both movement and case -- the most affordable mechanical timekeeper of its time.

One display presents silhouettes of a peddler and his customer standing before a cart bearing several of Terry's handsome pillar and scroll shelf clocks.

''As to the Yankee clock peddler in Kentucky, Indiana, Illinois, Missouri and in every dell of Arkansas and in every cabin where there was not a chair to sit on there was sure to be a Connecticut clock,'' reads a quotation from the memoirs of an Englishman who traveled to America in 1840, G.W. Featherstonebaugh.

The second half of the 19th century saw the growth of extensive clock factories in Bristol and in nearby Terryville, Plymouth, Forestville, Thomaston and Waterbury.

Photographic murals depict men and women at their workstations, while text panels speak of the social and financial chasm that grew between owners and workers. In 1880, workers, many newly arrives from Europe, labored 10 hours a day, 6 days a week and took home between $1.25 and $1.50 a day.

The low wages, long hours and dangerous working conditions led to Bristol's first strike in 1889, and eventually to the organization of labor unions.

Groupings of clocks, springs and other artifacts highlight intriguing aspects of the story -- the achievements of Chauncey Jerome, for example, who, as early as 1834, developed a way to make inexpensive clocks with movements made of brass rather than wood. ''He is the second hero of our story,'' said Ms. Donnelly of the man who was once Eli Terry's assistant.

The decorated panel on the clock -- known as the tablet -- was painted locally, with images of famous men, landscapes, ships, trees or other subjects. It was a skill learned while painting tinware, an industry that had taken hold in Bristol even before clockmaking and it was done exclusively by young, single women. ''They were expected to stop when they got married,'' said Ms. Connelly.

The exhibition concludes with a display from the golden age of clockmaking in Bristol, 1870 to 1920. Timepieces made to complement every decor are on view, from an austere but handsome octagonal clock, used mainly in schools, to a gaudy porcelain number in shades of pink, designed, no doubt, for a Victorian parlor.

True clock fanciers will not want to miss the Barnes wing -- a majestic gallery paneled with woodwork from Bristol's and the ticking, striking and chiming of many clocks.

The American Clock and Watch Museum, 100 Maple Street in Bristol, is open seven days a week from 10 A.M. to 5 P.M. until Dec. 6; it reopens in April. Admission is $3.50, $3 for elderly people and $1.50 for children.
