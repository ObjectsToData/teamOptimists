SUNY Board Approves Core Courses

By KAREN W. ARENSON
Published: December 16, 1998

Over complaints that they were ramming through a policy that had never been publicly discussed, the trustees of the State University of New York approved a new core curriculum for about 150,000 students yesterday.

While many other universities allow their students wide latitude in choosing what classes they take, SUNY's board saw a firmly defined core curriculum as a cornerstone to its efforts to raise standards and enhance SUNY's prestige.

''This will elevate the value of the State University degree, and that our graduates will possess the skills and know-how they need,'' said SUNY's chairman, Thomas F. Egan.

The trustees, who met at the State University Health Science Center in Brooklyn, also adopted a budget asking for a 3 percent increase in state financing, to $1.63 billion -- less than the 8 percent increase they had been considering in recent weeks.

Although the trustees had different views on the budget, the more contentious issue was the curriculum, which is to be required of freshmen starting in the fall of 2000.

Under the policy adopted yesterday, by a 10-to-3 vote, all students at the 29 undergraduate campuses that SUNY operates directly will have to take 10 individual courses, including mathematics, natural science, social sciences, American history, Western civilization and other world civilizations. Students will also have to take arts and humanities, basic communications and reasoning, information management and foreign languages.

Supporters of the core curriculum called it a measure that would give students the broad and rigorous education they need to be educated citizens and wage earners, and to be ''fulfilled personally.''

''We want to make sure that the value of the degree we award to students is meaningful'' and that it justifies the student's and the state's investment, said Randy A. Daniels, one of the trustees who was co-chairman of the board's subcommittee that shaped the policy.

Some trustees had reservations about specific requirements, like foreign languages. Others asked why the curriculum included some broad fields, like the sciences, but was very specific in calling for American history and Western civilization.

''I would have concerns for academic freedom,'' said Louis T. Howard, a trustee. ''Why in these two areas are we so specific? Why not Franco-American history?''

But Mr. Daniels argued that American history was a must. ''We are all Americans and we should all be knowledgeable about our own history,'' he said. He said people could not participate in ''the dialogue of this country,'' including the discussion of impeachment, without it.

But the biggest complaint was the board's hurry to pass the proposal without campus discussion, which some critics viewed as an effort to put the policy in place before people could attack it.

''Everything seems to be very well rushed,'' said Celine Traylor, the sole student trustee, who called for the vote to be delayed and was one of the three trustees who voted no.

''I'm paying $12,000 a year,'' she said. ''When you are dealing with something that is going to affect everyone in the university community, there should be some time spent discussing it.''

Paul R. Perez, a trustee and co-chairman of the curriculum subcommittee, said that the topic had been publicly discussed since 1995. But presidents of several SUNY colleges said that they had not seen the specific proposal until yesterday, when they arrived at the board meeting. And some trustees said they had not seen copies until late last week.

''I only got mine faxed Friday afternoon at 2:20,'' said Edward S. Nelson, another trustee who voted against the proposal. ''It looks to me like this is a rush to judgment.''

Mr. Egan, the board chairman, tried to reassure Mr. Nelson and others, saying that there would be plenty of time for discussion and changes before the policy takes effect in 2000. He said that the policy would be discussed with the college presidents in January, and that amendments were possible.

SUNY's provost, Peter D. Salins, also tried to reassure critics, saying there was room for faculties and presidents to set their own requirements within the framework. ''I don't see it as a straitjacket,'' he said. ''I see it as having a great deal of flexibility.''

But Arnold B. Gardner, the third trustee to oppose the measure, called it ''a milestone in increasing the intrusion by the central office'' into campus affairs. ''There is no reason to act so quickly except to claim the ground and then act to defend it,'' he added.
