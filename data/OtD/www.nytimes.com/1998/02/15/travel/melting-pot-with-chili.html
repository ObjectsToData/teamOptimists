Melting Pot, With Chili

By SHERMAKAYE BASS
Published: February 15, 1998

ONE way to sum up Austin is to paraphrase an old Texas saying: Defining the town is like herding cats. As the capital of Texas and home to one of the largest universities in the country, Austin is an ideological melting pot: a literary haven and a slackers' den, a music mecca and a film-industry outpost. Dreadlocks and multicolored coifs are as common as pompadours, shags, Hillary bobs, flattops and, in the folksy diners and suburban malls, the Big Blond Do.

Austin has claims to Lyndon B. Johnson and Janis Joplin, Gov. George W. Bush and the columnist Molly Ivins. It nurtures musical generations ranging from Shawn Colvin and Abra Moore to Willie Nelson and Jimmie Dale Gilmore. The Butthole Surfers reside here; so do the world's first permanent photograph, a copy of the Gutenberg Bible and a colony of Mexican bats.

A person is as likely to pass Jimmie Vaughan walking down South Congress or Richard Linklater in the grocery store as to see Jim Wright on a sidewalk near the Legislature or Ethan Hawke and Uma Thurman on break from a film shoot, checking out Beckett at the bookstore. Like Portland, Ore., and San Francisco, Austin is a city that feels like a town.

Topographically, Austin presents a similar paradox. I learned this recently from the caretaker of the O. Henry Museum on Fifth Street. Referring to a map made in 1887, the docent outlined the geographic traits that make this the city where the South ends and the West begins. On the west side of Austin, the rugged Balcones Escarpment rises up to mark the beginnings of ranch country, where scrub cedar, cattle and limestone crags freckle the landscape. Just east of town, the Blackland Prairie rolls to a lazy halt, and with it the cotton and soybean fields of the Deep South.

The old map gave me a sense of history and place. But as a relative newcomer to Austin, I had yet to find a cultural blueprint that encompassed the Sixth Street club scene, the grass-roots east side and the annual influx of tourists in March for the South by Southwest music and film festival. I decided that the best solution was to do what Texans do: get in a car and drive.

First, I parked myself in the perennially popular Austin Java Company, off Lamar Boulevard at the western edge of downtown, and over the French Roast-Sumatra blend, planned my attack. I would start with lunch at Cisco's, a patriarch of Tex-Mex dining on the east side, where autographed photos of Kevin Costner and other celebrities dress the dark-paneled walls. Sated with an enchilada plate, I would set out with my friend Joyce in her big-finned 1960 Plymouth Fury. The music would come later.

With highs in the 70's -- just as typical of Austin in midwinter as is an ice storm -- the day called us outdoors. We wandered to Red Bud Isle, a tiny oasis in the middle of Town Lake (created by dams in the Colorado River), where live oaks and cedar and pecan trees shade the sandy, rutted drive. We watched a man fish idly, and gazed at the green outcroppings on the bluff.

From there, we steered north on Exposition Boulevard toward the lush grounds of the Austin Museum of Art at Laguna Gloria on West 35th Street, a hacienda-style space that featured a William Wegman exhibit. We grabbed a spot behind the museum's art school and dangled our feet from a limestone ledge into Lake Austin. Mallards paddled past, a crane glided overhead, a woman paddled her kayak soundlessly.

Austin is physically defined by water. Fed by the Colorado, a strand of lakes and dams nestles against the western edge of the city, flowing down the escarpment from Lake Travis (about 25 minutes west of Austin and favored by scuba-divers), past Mount Bonnell, through Lake Austin and into Town Lake, which bisects the city. Sprinkled throughout town are Big Stacy Pool, a hot-springs-fed swimming pool; the trout-cold depths of Barton Springs and Deep Eddy pool, fronted by Deep Eddy Cabaret, which serves some of the coldest, cheapest beer in town. By boat, visitors could almost see a fourth of the city.

But my friend and I were navigating the greenways in the big white Plymouth, and we headed northwest from the museum to Ski Shores, a 43-year-old hole-in-the-wall a few miles farther up on the lake. Sitting on white plastic lawn chairs in front of the dock, we sipped beer and chatted with the proprietor, George Levy, who said that like many gems of Austin, his establishment battles dueling desires to draw new customers and maintain a laid-back atmosphere.

As the sun began to set, we headed back toward town. Driving an old car is the perfect way to negotiate Austin, not only because Texans love big automobiles, but also because the city nurses a longstanding love affair with vintage cars. You see them wedged between Mercedes Benzes, Land Cruisers and Buicks with ''Save Our Springs'' and ''Bicycles Not Bombs'' bumper stickers.

Likewise, Austinites cherish vintage clothing, furniture, Western wear, eyeglasses, you name it -- evidenced by the bustling funk of South Congress, where a strip of contra-chic second-hand stores and antiques shops represents Austin cool: places like New Bohemia, Electric Ladyland, Terra Toys, Yard Dog (a folk art gallery) are interspersed with restaurants that double as music venues, like El Sol y La Luna, Guero's and Shaggy's. Anchoring the night scene is the ever-retro Continental Club, a spacious hangout where the fashion is slick hair and Lucky Strikes.
