PHOTOGRAPHY VIEW; The Hill Where Elitists and Populists Meet

By Sarah Boxer
Published: March 15, 1998

IT takes too long to get up the hill. There isn't enough parking for everyone. People are confused about the layout. There is no contemporary art. The white is too white. The complaints you hear around the J. Paul Getty Center are complaints about elitism, the inherent snobbishness of a big white compound high above the grit and smog of Los Angeles.

Inside the Getty too, there are anti-elitist stirrings. And I'm not referring to the Getty Trust's well-known policy of reaching out to the community, teaching poor children about art and helping the world conserve its treasures, from Prague's facades to Mali's walls. All this can be explained perfectly well as noblesse oblige. I'm talking about photography.

Fifteen years ago, the Getty Museum created a monumental photography collection out of thin air by buying up some of the world's finest reserves: Samuel Wagstaff's collection in New York (strong on Nadar, Julia Margaret Cameron and Carleton Watkins); Bruno Bischofberger's in Switzerland (early photographers like William Henry Fox Talbot and Sir John Herschel); Arnold Crane's in Chicago (daguerreotypes, more than 1,000 works of Walker Evans and modernist photographs by Man Ray and Laszlo Moholy-Nagy), and Andre and Marie-Therese Jammes's in France (some of Emperor Napoleon III's photographs, including rare works by Gustave Le Gray).

For good measure, the museum also swallowed up a number of more focused collections, including Seymour Adelman's holdings of Thomas Eakins photographs, Krystyna Gmurzynska's cache of Alexander Rodchenko photographs, William Innes Homer's collection of Alfred Stieglitz's circle, Gerd Sander's collection of August Sander and Lisette Model, and Jurgen and Ann Wilde's works by Albert Renger-Patzsch. When the buying was over, the Getty had over 25,000 photographs, hundreds of daguerreotypes and a lot of albums and books full of mounted prints.

The Getty's collecting strategy could look like pure gluttony, a take-all policy. But in fact it's pure elitism, with no excuses. Weston Naef, the photography curator of the Getty Museum, said: ''I think there is such a thing as genius in photography. And I don't want just one of each photographer. This is a serial art. I don't want to choose one big work. I like to see masterpieces in their context, the works leading up to the masterpiece.''

This logic taken to its extreme explains why the Getty Museum, despite its rarefied taste, has so many photographs. The museum's study room has albums and portfolios on many photographers in its collection. Paul Strand's, for example, holds not only classic works like ''Portrait, Washington Square Park,'' a picture of a woman with a wrinkled face, and his abstract still lifes of bowls and pears but also shots that never made it into the canon, like a portrait of his friend Kurt Baasch, some little-known pictures of his wife, Rebecca, and ordinary travel shots. All are there to support the idea of genius.

Since the Getty Museum's first photography shows, most of the exhibitions have been devoted to acknowledged masters: Julia Margaret Cameron, Edward Weston, Alexander Rodchenko, Gustave Le Gray, Gertrude Kasebier, Imogen Cunningham and Doris Ulmann. And the current exhibition, ''Capturing Time,'' is a parade of masters -- from William Henry Fox Talbot's picture of a deserted street in Paris to one of Stieglitz's early portraits of Georgia O'Keeffe to Frederick Sommer's ''Arizona Landscape'' to David Hockney's knockout color collage ''Pearblossom Highway.'' Even the theme of the show, time, is based on what is now viewed as an elitist idea: that photography has a particular essence.

''I wanted to choose an aspect of photography that is fundamental to its character,'' Mr. Naef said. And that aspect is time. ''I am interested in how the photographer used time as a malleable element, not just a historical moment but as a plastic, palpable element. In painting it is painterliness, taking a liquid and putting it on with brush marks, and in sculpture it is the hardness of the stone. This is what time is to photography. If you don't like the medium of time, you don't appreciate photography. I have tried to propose that the element of time is the most fundamental thing about photography. It is the paint in the tube.''

THIS kind of high modernist talk about essentialism, formalism and genius hasn't been in vogue since the heyday of the art critic Clement Greenberg in the 1950's and 60's. Indeed, by the time the Getty Museum was putting together its photography collection, a number of critics were floating the non-elitist, post-modern idea, now wildly popular in universities, that photographs are not primarily esthetic objects but social documents, that their meaning has little to do with their formal qualities and everything to do with the circumstances under which they were taken, how they are used and who is looking at them.
