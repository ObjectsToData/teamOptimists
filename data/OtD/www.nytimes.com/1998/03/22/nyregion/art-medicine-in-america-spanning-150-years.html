ART; Medicine in America, Spanning 150 Years

By VIVIEN RAYNOR
Published: March 22, 1998

AS a concept, art for art's sake seems to have been superseded by art that illustrates or preaches. Whether this is the will of artists or art educators is a moot point, but there is no doubt that, like the squeaky wheel, the show promoting a blameless cause is the one most likely to get the Federal, state or corporate grease. A current example is ''Say Ahh! Examining America's Health,'' which has nothing to do with art but is nonetheless on view at the Hudson River Museum here.

A combined effort by the Strong Museum of Rochester, the Health Museum of Cleveland and the College of Physicians of Philadelphia, this World's Fair-type spectacle has many sponsors, including Johnson & Johnson Clinical Diagnostics, the New York Council for the Humanities, Blue Cross and Blue Shield in the Rochester area and, during its Westchester stint, the Bayer Corporation of Tarrytown.

From ''Dr. Kildare'' to ''E.R.,'' American medicine has never wanted for favorable publicity, and ''Say Ahh!'' appears to be more of the same except that it spans 150 years or so and is bombastic and tentative by turns.

Implying that Americans are somehow unusual in their concern over health hazards, a wall label explains that the show is about their efforts to understand the risk of disease, the steps they took to prevent it and the measures they believed would cure them.

When it comes to the cost of what is now known as health care, Americans are again accorded a share of the responsibility because, as stated in another caption, they ''believe in progress and feel entitled to it'' and, of course, ''medical technology represents progress.''

To judge by the quoted price of $230,000 for a liver transplant, it also represents astonishing wealth. But the same Americans resist government aid because they believe that ''health problems are largely individual and private.''

On the other hand, Europeans see them as ''social and public'' and, regardless of their political convictions, are content to take advantage of government assistance. Whether this covers transplants, the going rates for hospitalization in Germany and Canada suggest that livers come cheaper in countries where the government picks up the tab.

Of course, ''Say Ahh!'' comes out against drinking and smoking -- with statistics and, in the case of the smoking, an anti-cancer poster portraying a woman whose face has been, as it were, doctored to resemble a lung coated with tar.

Still, an exhibition that defines the Hippocratic vow against doing harm to a patient as a maxim, consists largely of stands painted in pastel colors and delivers much of its information by way of pseudo board games and wheels of fortune (''interactives'' in museum parlance) seems like a crude attempt to tranquilize a public 15 percent of which must do without health insurance.

To make matters worse, the show asks questions like ''Has modern medicine taken the caring out of health care?'' but leaves its observers to supply the answers.

To be fair, this is not the jingoistic production it might have been, say, 10 years ago, and at times it comes close to being a serious investigation of cultural attitudes.

Though it covers the early epidemics of cholera and tuberculosis, together with the present one of AIDS, ''Say Ahh!'' is less a documentary than an anxious collage of diseases and the methods and tools used to treat them.

As such, it leaves viewers uncertain as to the future of American health care but grateful for the non-American invention of antibiotics.

Meanwhile, those who fear that the country will run out of transplants may take comfort in knowing that, because of its system of capital punishment, China has an apparently limitless supply of spare parts.

The closing date of ''Say Ahh!'' is May 24, and the number to call for more information is 963-4550.
