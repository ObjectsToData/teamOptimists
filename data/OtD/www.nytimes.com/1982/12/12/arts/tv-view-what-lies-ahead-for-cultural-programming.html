TV VIEW; WHAT LIES AHEAD FOR CULTURAL PROGRAMMING

By John J. O'Connor
Published: December 12, 1982

his week, sometime around 4 in the morning on Friday, CBS Cable will go out of business. This season, public television's ''Great Performances'' series is celebrating its 10th anniversary. And thereby hangs a tale of television programming.

Since its earliest days, way back with radio in the 1920's, broadcasting has held out to the public the promise of cultural riches. The first programs featured a great deal of chamber music and delicate sopranos. Then somebody discovered that the machine could be used to sell products. Commercials were born. Sopranos were out, and popular entertainment was in, with a vengeance.

The inevitable drift toward ''lowest common denominator'' programming was so pronounced that the creation of public television became something of a necessity, if only to take the heat off commercial broadcasters. So, about 15 years ago, the current public system was established by Congress with a clear mandate to bring the best of the world's arts and artists into the nation's living rooms. And, indeed, with each passing season, the ratings have substantiated the fact that there is a receptive audience for quality programming. There was, however, only one problem: methods for financing that system have always been completely inadequate, a failing that has triggered continuing money crises over the years.

In the meantime, two of the commercial networks, anxious to get into the new and burgeoning business of cable, took note of publictelevision's sizable audiences and came up with their own classy ''cultural'' channels. CBS Cable, launched in October of last year, was the most ambitious of these ventures. About 60 percent of its programming would be original, created specifically for the new service. The rest would be acquired from outside sources, in this country and abroad. The three-hour programming segments, presented four times daily, would encompass classical music, drama, films, jazz and interviews. The interview half-hour entitled ''Signature'' was an immediate success as it relegated the interviewer, Gregory Jackson, to the off-camera shadows and kept a close-up focus on the show's guests. ''Songs of Innocence and Experience,'' an original musical by Elizabeth Swados, imaginatively explored the poetry of William Blake. ''The Gift of Friendship,'' written expressly for television by John Osborne, featured a splendid performance by Alec Guinness. CBS Cable was off and running with encouraging gusto.

And its record has remained impressive to the end. Recent highlights have included an Irish Television biography of James Joyce, keyed to the centennial of his birth this year, and a documentary, ''Piano Players Rarely Ever Play Together,'' about three generations of New Orleans musicians. Thursday's swan song will include a new production of Gilbert and Sullivan's ''The Mikado,'' starring William Conrad.

But CBS Cable was soon plagued by rumors of troubles. Quality programming is expensive, a fact of production life that has long hounded public-television producers working on chronically slender budgets. Worse, as far as a commercial enterprise is concerned, advertisers were not rushing into the cable scene. Dick Cox, president of CBS Cable, assumed he had a longterm commitment from the parent company, CBS Inc. He still feels that two or three years down the line, the national penetration of cable will be widespread enough to attract sufficient advertising. But so-called cultural programming is never likely to have the kind of large audiences needed to ''maximize'' profits. In any event, by last June, William S. Paley, who would soon retire as chairman of CBS, was convinced by his accountants that CBS Cable would be too much of a drain on the company's finances for too long a time. The decision to cease operations sent shock waves throughout the cable industry.

All the while, cable's culture channels - including ABC/Hearst ARTS and Bravo - have been in fierce competition with public television. They were all going to the acquisitions marketplace for basically the same product, in the process driving up prices. How could the always financially strapped public-television system do battle with the enormous money resources of the commercial networks? Even this year's public-television schedule became distressingly crammed with old movies, many of them far removed from ever being considered classics. The future of the non-profit system looked dim indeed, as long-range funding for major new projects was non-existent. An ''Adams Chronicles'' would be unthinkable for public television at this stage. And now the collapse of CBS Cable has revived questions about the extent of any profit-seeking venture's commitment to cultural programming, which is notably less rewarding financically than such popular fare as ''Laverne and Shirley.''

Given the overall record of broadcasting in this country, it is not difficult to predict that there will always be a need for some kind of public television. Whatever its past failings, and current serious troubles, the system has compiled some remarkably splendid credits.

Consider the record alone of ''Great Performances,'' put together with financing from, among others, the Exxon Corporation, the national endowments for the arts and humanities, the Corporation for Public Broadcasting and public-television stations. Ten years ago, beginning as primarily a showcase for serious music, the series offered the Bach Mass in B Minor and discovered that more than a halfmillion people in the New York metropolitan area were willing to watch and listen. Over the years, it has taken a number of other series under its umbrella: ''Theater in America,'' ''Dance in America,'' ''The Shakespeare Plays,'' ''Live From Lincoln Center.'' The fare has included everything from Luciano Pavarotti in concert to a cycle of three Monteverdi operas, from Blythe Danner and Frank Langella in Tennessee Williams's ''Eccentricities of a Nightingale'' to Joan Hackett and Roberta Maxwell in Eugene O'Neill's ''Mourning Becomes Electra,'' from George Balanchine to Martha Graham.

This year's highpoint was the superb ''Brideshead Revisited.'' An early entry next year will be the complete cycle of Wagner's Ring. In brief, the sheer scope is extraordinary. The general quality is stunning. And one thing is quite clear: Very little of it would have been broadcast without public television being on the scene. As CBS hastily retreats from the cultural programming arena, the need for devising effective means to fund the public system is more crucial than ever.
