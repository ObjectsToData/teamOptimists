ANTIQUES VIEW

ANTIQUES VIEW; STARTING WITH THE VIKING REVIVAL

By Rita Reif
Published: September 19, 1982

Scandinavian design, which revolutionized interiors internationally in the 1950's, had its roots in much earlier, highly innovative 19th-and early 20th-century works. This is one of the many messages delivered in the multifaceted ''Scandinavian Modern: 1880-1980,'' on view at the Cooper-Hewitt Museum, the Smithsonian Institution's Museum of Design, 2 East 91st Street. This stunning and comprehensive review documents for the first time in this country the strong links mid-20th century Scandinavian designers had with their past.

What is that past? David Revere McFadden, curator of decorative arts who organized the show, has focused on the beginning of, and what followed, the 1880's Viking revival period. Mr. McFadden, who is also responsible for the superb catalogue published by Abrams ($45) that documents the 330 selections in the show and gives biographical sketches of over 250 artists and artisans, has unearthed many surprises from the late 19th and early 20th centuries in works by Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, Finnish and Icelandic designers.

The Viking revival style represents an undiluted expression of nationalism in the decorative arts, a movement that made Scandinavian designers, after centuries of looking to the continent of Europe for design inspiration, investigate their own national heritages. And what dynamic heritages they proved to be - in weaving, metalwork, cabinetmaking, ceramics and glass - with each country differing from the other.

The designs that emerged relied, at first, on the obvious - dragons and Viking ships abound in revival period works that are, however, distinguished by technical dexterity and a sensuous appreciation of materials. By 1900 the design vocabulary of these Nordic nations was dramatically enriched, incorporating medieval, archeological and ethnic motifs in surface embellishments or in the shaping of objects. This added up to a new and rich, distinctly Scandinavian expression. One of the most memorable designs in the show is Gerhard Munthe's outrageous turn-of-the-century armchair for an Oslo hotel - its back carved in the shape of a dragon, its front legs joined by a sunburst. The fanciful statement painted in green, black, white and yellow anticipates with enviable wit the post-modern furniture of today.

The Art Nouveau style was adopted in Scandinavia by individuals and factories at the turn of the century and modified to suit their own purposes. The Rorstrand factory in Sweden produced some of the most exquisite porcelain tablewares, as can be seen in Alf Wallander's coffee service, the handles and knob-topped lids shaped like dragonflies, and in Algot Eriksson's white porcelain vase, its mouth crafted to resemble a blossoming flower, its body framed in large leaves. Neither is as sinuous - nor as sinister - as are the works of the French and Belgian masters of the Art Nouveau style.

Naturalism brought out the best in other artists and architects, including Akseli Gallen-Kallela, the designer of a 1913 woven-wool bolster cover which is dramatized by leaping flames; Eliel Saarinen, whose 1918 oak and leather dining chair is framed in three scallopped vertical slats, and Carl Bergsten, who was responsible for the bent birch chair painted black that is a tour de force of ovals and circles.

The organic shaping of these and other such turn-of-the-century designs as Andreas Ollestad's earthenware fish-encrusted vase and Georg Jensen's dragonfly brooches, are precursors of the curvilinear Scandinavian modern style of the 1950's, something not understood 30 years ago when this style became the pervasive look in modern in the West. By the 1920's, in fact, virtually all of the Scandinavians - among them, Alvar Aalto in Finland, Bruno Mathsson in Sweden and Johan Rohde in Denmark - had mastered the undulating line in their work. Rohde's 1925 silver pitcher, Aalto's curvaceous 1929 bentwood armchair and Mathsson's curvilinear bent beech 1934 ''Eva'' chair, its seat and back of interlaced fabric strips designed to firmly cradle the body, are classics of the modern style.

In sharp contrast, some turn-of-the-century Finnish designers opted for another tradition, choosing geometric motifs in the design of textiles and earthenware, patterns that are rooted in the folk art of the Finnish people. A case in point is a 1902 bulbous-based white vase splashed with green and yellow diamond and pinwheel motifs. Another is Louis Sparre's squarish chair from around 1906, a design as geometric as those of Gustav Stickley in the United States, Charles Rennie Mackintosh in Scotland and Josef Hoffmann in Vienna.

Some designers were equally adept in executing furniture based on curves and angles. Kaare Klint of Denmark moved with ease in both directions, relying on traditional furniture for his ideas. The 1933 Safari chair, echoing the campaign furniture of early 19th-century England, combines both angles in its seat and back and curves in its legs and leather-strap arms. The chair made history for another reason - it was the first design produced in Europe to be sold ''knockdown'' to facilitate shipping.

Sponsored and administered by the American Scandinavian Foundation, with support from the National Endowment for the Humanities and the National Endowment for the Arts, Volvo and Atlantic Richfield, the show, after closing here Jan. 2, will travel to the Minnesota Museum of Art in St. Paul (Feb. 27-April 24) and to the Renwick Gallery in Washington (July 8-Oct. 10).

In the revival of early 20th-century decorative arts over the last 20 years, most of the Scandinavian designs have been overlooked or underplayed. A major exception is Georg Jensen silver, which has been available in shops (notably Lillian Nassau, 220 East 57th Street) and is regularly gaveled in the major auction houses. Mrs. Nassau also stocks Swedish glass from the turn of the century through the 1930's, as does Leo Kaplan, 910 Madison Avenue. New to the New York scene is Scanform, a SoHo gallery at 100 Thompson Street, where period Scandinavian ceramics, glass and silver are stocked. Eduardo Garrido, the proprietor, has offerings ranging from $75 for a Swedish ceramics vase to $4,000 for a Georg Jensen silver pitcher designed in 1928 by Johan Rohde and executed in the 1950's.
