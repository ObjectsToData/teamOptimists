NEW YORK TO CELEBRATE SCANDINAVIA

Published: September 6, 1982

''Scandinavia Today,'' a 15-month celebration honoring the cultural and intellectual life of the five modern Nordic nations of Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway and Sweden opens next Monday in New York City. Works of art from Scandinavian countries will be shown at major museums and institutions in the city, and dozens of programs of music, theater, dance, film and lectures are scheduled over the next few months.

Royalty, including Prince Bertil and Princess Lilian of Sweden, heads of state and noted personalities from all five nations will be in New York to open a number of functions. And elsewhere in the United States - Washington, Minneapolis, St. Paul, Seattle, Chicago, Los Angeles and Philadelphia - Nordic culture will be celebrated with simultaneous events until next year.

Scandinavia Week in City

Mayor Koch has proclaimed Sept. 13-20 as ''Scandinavia Today Week.'' A keynote address by President Vigdis Finnbogadottir of Iceland at 4 P.M. next Monday at Alice Tully Hall will begin this month's New York events, which will include:

- Scandinavian Modern: 1880-1980,'' a design exhibition from the five Nordic countries of more than 330 exhibits of furniture, glass, ceramics, metalwork and textiles, will be shown at the Cooper-Hewitt Museum, 2 East 91st Street; Nordic printmaking will be seen at the National Academy of Design, 1083 Fifth Avenue, and three exhibitions of 20th-century art will be given at the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum - all opening Sept. 14.

- Lars Roos, Swedish pianist, will present a concert at Alice Tully Hall on Sept. 14; the Tapiola Choir of youths 9 to 20 years old from Finland will appear at Tully Hall at 8 P.M. on Sept. 16, and dramatic readings of Scandinavian plays will be given at the Bruno Walter Auditorium at Lincoln Center on Sept. 17 and 24 and Oct. 1, 8 and 15.

- The Pierpont Morgan Library will be the host of ''Icelandic Sagas, Eddas and Art,'' an exhibition opening Sept. 16 of medieval manuscripts that have never before left Iceland, and at the same time, the Fashion Institute of Technology, 227 West 27th Street, will be showing ''The Scandinavian Touch,'' a show of 80 textile works by 20 artists.

- The Guggenheim Museum will be the site of a lecture Sept. 28 at 8 P.M. on ''Scandinavian Poets Today,'' including Henrik Nordbrandt of Denmark, Goran Sonnevi of Sweden, Pentii Saaritsa of Finland and Paal Helge Haugen of Norway. Danish Royalty to Attend

Other leaders attending the Nordic events include Queen Margarethe II and Prince Henrik of Denmark; Par Stenback, Finnish Minister of Foreign Affairs, and Crown Prince Harald and Crown Princess Sonja of Norway. And the exhibitions will also include works by Evard Munch; photography ranging from contemporary portraits to images of Arctic terrain encountered by 19th-century explorers; rya rugs and hanging sculpture, wall tapestries and even woven hair. There will be Ibsen and Strindberg theatrical works; concerts of Grieg and Sibelius; jazz and chamber works, and performers ranging from Birgit Nilsson to the Icelandic Male Chorus.

Ten films will be shown, including ''Broken Sky'' (1982), directed by the actress Ingrid Thulin. And there will be lectures and panel discussions on such topics as state support of the arts, Swedish women writers, prison reform and the social welfare state.

Among the future events will be a major exhibition of Scandinavian painting from 1880-1910, ''Northern Light: Realism and Symbolism,'' opening Nov. 10 at the Brooklyn Museum; Hakan Hagegard, Swedish baritone at the 92d Street Y on Oct. 5 and again at the Metropolitan Opera in ''The Barber of Seville'' from Oct. 18 to Dec. 2; the Norwegian National Ballet in ''The Tempest'' at the Brooklyn Academy of Music on Oct. 12-17, and ''A Strindberg-O'Neill Celebration'' with artists from the Royal Dramatic Theater of Sweden at the Circle in the Square, 159 Bleecker Street at 8 P.M. on Nov. 11. In National Endowment Series

The Scandinavian festival is the largest and last of a six-part series of ''Today'' programs begun by the National Endowment for the Humanities in 1976. According to one organizer, the belief was that much information about other countries had become so stereotyped that it was time for the United States to look at what was going on today in the cultures of foreign lands.

The series began with Mexico, followed by Canada, Japan, Belgium and Egypt, and the Nordic celebration was cooridnated through the Secretariat for Nordic Cultural Cooperation, a branch of the Nordic Council of Ministers.

The council provided $1.2 million of the $4.5 million budget of ''Scandinavia Today.'' Other contributors were the National Endowments for the Arts and Humanities, and corporations including Volvo and the Atlantic Richfield Company.

The program will open last in Chicago and Los Angeles, and then move to smaller communities throughout the United States. Although activities will vary from city to city, the core will consist of six exhibitions with representative works from each of the five countries. It is expected that a total of 62 cities in 32 states will have at least some events from ''Scandinavia Today.''
