HIS OBSESSION IS A CULTURE IN DECLINE

By LESLIE BENNETTS
Published: May 30, 1982

For more than 20 years A.R. Gurney has written a play nearly every year and seen them attain modest appreciation at best, with regional theater productions or the occasional run in London. At worst, however, his plays met with devastating reviews and a swift demise. The playwright figured that his habitual lack of success had something to do with his habitual choice of topic, which is upper middle-class WASPs. Despite the occasional comparison of Mr. Gurney with John Cheever or Philip Barry, reviewers often seemed to regard his preoccupation more as a mildly amusing anachronism than as a compelling subject for serious drama.

These days, however, Mr. Gurney's obsession with what he sees as the decline of the WASP culture may finally have come into its own. ''The Dining Room,'' a humorously poignant series of vignettes on the changing role of the classic formal dining room over the course of three generations of WASPs, has already enjoyed an extended run at Playwrights Horizons and has been moved to the larger Astor Place Theater for an open-ended engagement.

A second company of ''The Dining Room'' is expected to open at the Kennedy Center on Saturday. Another Gurney play, ''The Middle Ages,'' had its New York premiere in March at the Ark Theater Company in SoHo and will open Off Broadway in the fall. A third, ''What I Did Last Summer,'' will be produced at Westport in August; and in between shepherding all those on their way, Mr. Gurney has begun writing a new play.

Some dismay notwithstanding, he has never found it particularly surprising that the commercial theater greeted most of his work with something less than wild enthusiasm. Mr. Gurney's themes have characteristically explored the reverse of the classic American success story beloved by Broadway: ''I tend to write about people who are looking over their shoulder, who are not so much on the way up as on the way down,'' the playwright observes.

Indeed, in ''The Dining Room,'' a young man goes to see his greataunt to interrogate her about her Waterford crystal, and is particularly fascinated by her fingerbowls. She is delighted - until she discovers that the young man's interest is due to an anthropology class project on ''the eating habits of vanishing cultures'' for which he has chosen as his subject ''the WASPs of the Northeastern United States.'' To her horror, he goes on to explain that the fingerbowls demonstrate ''a neurotic obsession with cleanliness associated with the guilt of the last stages of capitalism.''

Mr. Gurney is pleasantly surprised, if somewhat bemused, by the unaccustomed favor his work is now finding. ''I feel as though all the labors of the years have paid off,'' says the playwright, who is 51. ''This year, with 'The Dining Room,' has been the first time in my life that I've had a play that's run for any length of time and has received such general critical enthusiasm.''

He is also pleased by his current productivity, which is partly a function of a temporary release from full-time labors of the wageearning sort. Mr. Gurney is now on a one-year sabbatical from M.I.T., where he has taught American literature and the humanities for the last 20 years, and he is reveling in being able to write all day, instead of only during summer vacation. He and his wife have rented their house in Newton, Mass., and Mr. Gurney looks positively joyous as he talks about becoming part of the theater community in New York, of going to plays any night of the week instead of on the occasional weekend, and of the creative stimulation provided by daily contact with his colleagues in the theatre.

In his own mind, however, he has also reached some kind of turning point in his work. To date Mr. Gurney's formidable output stands as a kind of long-running exorcism of the demons - well-bred demons though they may be - that have dogged him ever since his own upbringing sent him onto a direct collision course with a society in the throes of radical change. In some ways Mr. Gurney has spent the last 20 years reeling from the impact of that crash.

Born Albert Ramsdell Gurney Jr. -thereafter known exclusively as Pete - the playwright grew up in privileged affluence in Buffalo, the son of a father who was ''in real estate and insurance,'' he says. ''I knew at a very early age that I didn't want to go into my father's business, and I announced it - at the dining room table - when I was eight.''

He wrote his first play while in kindergarten, that opus featuring a raccoon as its protagonist. Mr. Gurney's choice of form was not mysterious; his grandmother was ''in love with the theater, and nobody else seemed to want to go to matinees with her, so I did and I just loved them,'' he explains. Although he has written two novels, he adds, even as a child he preferred writing the spoken word to description, ''and when I read I always used to skip the descriptions to get to the dialogue.''

After putting in the requisite well-bred WASP stint at St. Paul's, Mr. Gurney went on to Williams College, where he started off as a pre-med student and finished as an English major. He also had the good fortune to arrive two years behind Stephen Sondheim, who had written the first script for what then became an annual student musical. Following in his hero's footsteps, Mr. Gurney wrote the book for the musical two years in a row. After college came three and a half years in the Navy, where Mr. Gurney's job as a special services officer entailed writing shows for the entertainment of the troops. He then earned his graduate degree at the Yale School of Drama and, he says, began there to take himself seriously as a writer.
