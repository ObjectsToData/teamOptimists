    A College Degree, and Still No Clear Path

    By TRYMAINE LEE
    August 19, 2009 1:15 pmAugust 20, 2009 1:33 am

    During these rough economic times, nearly everyone has had to sacrifice. Everyday decisions like buying or packing lunch or indulging in the smallest luxuries — say, a newspaper or a bagel — have joined the list of hard choices.

    College students and recent graduates are no exception. While they are partly shielded by the protective cloak of academia, with support from their families and institutions of higher learning, the decisions they now make can have a profound impact on their futures.

    Go to graduate school or test the job market? Big-name school or CUNY? Alter career plans or pray to the job gods for guidance and a cushy entry-level position?

    On a hot and sticky day in Washington Square Park, just off the campus of New York University, a handful of students and recent graduates talked recently about altering their plans to survive in this down economy.

    Many said they are choosing the terra firma of graduate school over the less-than-stable job market. They are taking unpaid internships over the prospect of no internship at all. And still others, with opportunities in the their chosen fields dwindling, have picked up a class or two while looking for a job.

    “At this point it almost makes more sense to go back to school rather than try to find a job in this economy,” said Parisa Vaziri, 23, a recent graduate of New York University who plans to apply to graduate school to study in the humanities field.

    Another recent graduate, Shana Griffith, 22, of Trenton, said there are stories of both struggle and stability from college students and other graduates she knows.

    “Everybody’s story is a little bit different,” said Ms. Griffith, a Howard University graduate with a communications degree, who is taking classes at New York University while applying for jobs. Ms. Griffith travels from Trenton to New York each day for class.

    “I have friends that are kind of secure, they’re going to law school and medical school. I have friends who have jobs, and I have friends that don’t know what they’re doing.”

    S.C. August 19, 2009 · 2:06 pm

    I am a recent graduate, in Oregon. Knowing how the job market was going (nowhere), I started applying to positions before I left school. No luck. I gave myself 2 months, then started applying to the ‘back up’ position: teaching English in South Korea, which pays decently and allows for savings. 6 weeks after I applied to a recruiting agency, I’m packing to leave for my new job.
    It’s not my top choice, and has almost nothing to do with my eventual career goals (foreign policy and development work). However, I’ll be gaining valuable experience and ‘waiting out the market’ while saving money, not spending it. I have to admit that a graduate program was tempting, but I’d prefer not to go another $40,000 in debt.
    Recent college grads have to be more flexible in what they consider for a job, and more focused. I chose to only apply for jobs that were relevant to my career field or major in some way, and required a bachelor’s degree so that I wasn’t downgrading my hireability. That meant considering options like EFL teaching, which I am now going to do. Setting those criteria for myself helped, since I was applying for jobs that would interest me or help me, and that I was more qualified for than the random resume send-outs that other recent grads are resorting to.

    Parisa Vaziri August 19, 2009 · 2:52 pm

    I am disappointed in the way that my words were applied in this article, and the way I was represented in the video. I was unfortunately caught on my morning off in Wash Sq Park, having just woken up and been accosted by a camera as I was reading by the fountain.

    Give me a break. Yes, I know that the “it almost makes more sense to go back to school” is an attitude cultivated from a rather entitled viewpoint, and no, I don’t deny that I come from a background where education has always been pushed as a priority–the most important purchase an individual can make. This is something I have had to deal with in my own right.

    I still owe over ten thousand dollars to NYU, and make no mistake: I plan only to pursue a graduate degree at a university prepared to provide me full funding, and I would never advise anyone to do otherwise. Graduate school is NOT something you pay for, unless you’re working toward a law degree, in which case you should be able to make your money back within a reasonable period.

    Re: Joe Smith’s comment, no, I absolutely do not agree that a BA in the humanities is worthless. I graduated from NYU with a degree in comparative literature in three years, was immediately hired at a law firm as a researcher/translator, making over $65,000 a year (overtime included, of course) in 2007. I left my job right before the economic crisis because I had had plans to further my education long before I finished undergrad, and was not satisfied with the work ethic the law firm was inculcating into its employees.

    My father is a professor, and it has always made sense to me that someone who enjoys academia, someone who is “good” at academia, should remain in school as long as possible, especially if it can lead to a professorship where one is free to pursue one’s own research, and is allowed the time to do that.

    Parisa

    Jeremy August 19, 2009 · 2:56 pm

    I would agree with Michelle, here… the notion that the only degrees “worth” something are in math or engineering is very narrow. The humanities won’t give you a strong math or technical background, to be sure, but they do expose you to a wider range of knowledge that is ultimately more useful. I don’t think it matters much what the particular roster of courses is. Exposure to different ways of thinking and new perspectives that challenge old ones DOES teach you to think more critically, not just about an academic subject, but one’s own ideas and conceptions of the world around oneself. Ideally, this is what a traditional liberal arts degree will do – force one to re-examine the “way things work.”

    Like Michelle, I also feel that the ability to write and write well is higher on an employer’s wish list. I agree with Peter about the general “dumbing down” of academia. I continue to be shocked at how poorly my peers at my university write. Being able to clearly, succinctly express one’s thoughts and arguments seems much more valuable than a degree in math or engineering. It’s not enough to know how to do something; you have to be able to communicate, gather often disparate pieces of information together, make an argument or claim, and spit out something that makes sense. There’s a certain kind of thought process that entails, one that isn’t as explicitly present in a more technical field of study.

    But as previous comments have pointed out, part-time employment seems to be the key to getting hired and developing oneself professionally. I have been working part-time, between 15-30 hours a week in addition to being a full-time student, since I was 16. It has helped me develop interview and professional skills, take on more responsibility and manage my time well. More importantly, it’s shown me the value of work – not only in the financial sense, but also in terms of a good work ethic, which (if done right) will take you very far. If an employer knows they can count on you to get things done, 99% of the time, it’s amazing how much you can advance.

    As for me, well… I debate the value of an NYU degree versus its cost (which is being borne by my name in loans, federal and private, as well as my meager income) but hope that it will ultimately let me break even. I plan on finishing my undergraduate degree and going to grad school here for urban planning, using tuition remission from full-time employment with NYU (a perk I received by working part-time as a student here for four years).

    Tauros March 25, 2010 · 11:22 pm

    It isn’t surprising that universities in the United States have completely oversold the financial returns to post secondary education. They have every financial incentive not to be fully honest with the rest of society that they are often just diploma mills.

    The argument of why forking over $120,000+ for a bachelor’s degree always seems to fall back on the same nebulous government data comparing lifetime earnings of college graduates compared with high school graduates. It’s time to consider additional perspectives.

    The sad truth is that the supply of post secondary degree holders far exceeds the demand for them in today’s economy and the problem has just gotten worse since the Millenial Generation tidal wave entered the higher ed pipeline. My recommendation to the wise is to pursue “learning for learning sake” and don’t get caught up in credentialism and waste energy and resources on graduate study due to mistaken anticipated financial rewards.
