    The New York Public Library Goes Fluorescent

    By SEWELL CHAN
    October 3, 2007 5:50 pmOctober 4, 2007 12:10 pm

    The New York Public Library completed the replacement today of all 5,311 incandescent light bulbs at its landmark Beaux-Arts headquarters on Fifth Avenue with compact fluorescent bulbs, a shift that is intended to make one of the city’s best-known landmarks much more energy-efficient.

    The move will save both energy and money, as well as reduce carbon dioxide emissions by 1.5 million pounds and power consumption by 1.4 million kilowatt hours each year.

    For most library users, the changes will not be particularly noticeable, though the bulbs are particularly concentrated on the 18 chandeliers in the third-floor Rose Main Reading Room and the four chandeliers in the Bill Blass Public Catalog Room, which form the architectural and spiritual heart of the library. There was some worry among library officials, at first, that the switch to fluorescent lighting might adversely affect the atmosphere and readers’ experiences in the two rooms.

    “That was one of the big concerns they had, but they found that the warm feel of the room was not affected,” said Gayle Snible, a library spokeswoman.

    Christopher A. Williams, a senior property manager and the library, said the new light bulbs are virtually identical to the old ones. “They look almost exactly the same,” he said. “They’re frosted bulbs. They look a little bit brighter and whiter.”

    Paul LeClerc, the library’s president, made the final decision to approve the switch to the new lighting, after approving design plans, Mr. Williams said.

    The vast library complex is actually powered by two different underground electrical grids. The south court, an office and visitor area that occupies what was once an open-air courtyard, already had modern lighting and was not affected by the change. The switch to the new bulbs mostly affects the oldest parts of the building and parts of the book-shelving areas under Bryant Park, which run on the Times Square grid. Consolidated Edison has been urging the big buildings in the Times Square area to conserve power.

    One of the city’s most recognizable and beloved buildings, the “main branch,” as it is still commonly called, is now known as the Humanities and Social Sciences Library. All the holdings are reference materials. (The system’s largest circulating library, the Mid-Manhattan Library, is diagonally across Fifth Avenue, at 40th Street.)

    The marble masterpiece, at Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street, was begun in 1902 and dedicated in 1911. The library system, which combined the private libraries of John Jacob Astor and James Lenox with the Samuel Jones Tilden Trust, was established in 1895.

    In a few parts of the library — notably the ornamental candelabras in the ground-floor Astor Hall and the third-floor McGraw Rotunda — the the lighting was not changed for aesthetic reasons, Mr. Williams said.

    “We decided the incandescents had a sparkling quality to them,” he said.

    At a morning ceremony, officials threw a giant switch, officially marking the change to a new lighting system. Attending the ceremony were Heike Kordish, director of the Humanities and Social Sciences Library; Steve Mysholowsky, a Consolidated Edison manager; James P. Maitilasso, president of Quality Conservation Services of Palisades Park, N.J., the project manager; and Ellis Yan, chief executive of Technical Consumer Products, the lighting manufacturer.

    Mr. Yan, whose company is based in Aurora, Ohio, noted that today is “Change a Light, Change the World” Day, an event organized by the federal Energy Star program.
