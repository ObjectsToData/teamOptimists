    JPMorgan’s Chief Gets a Pay Raise

    By SYDNEY EMBER
    January 24, 2014 7:27 amJanuary 24, 2014 7:43 am

    JPMORGAN’S DIMON TO GET RAISE  |  Despite the legal troubles that JPMorgan Chases faced in 2013, which amounted to more than $20 billion in fines, Jamie Dimon, the bank’s chairman and chief executive, is getting a raise, Jessica Silver-Greenberg and Susanne Craig write in DealBook.
     The pay increase follows the board’s move last year to cut Mr. Dimon’s compensation in half, to $11.5 million as reprimand for allowing a multibillion-dollar trading mistake known as the London Whale.

    Conversations this week over Mr. Dimon’s pay were fraught. Some on JPMorgan’s board argued that Mr. Dimon’s compensation should remain flat because of the bank’s legal penalties. But others argued that he should be rewarded for his strong leadership in helping the bank move past its legal problems.

    Ms. Silver-Greenberg and Ms. Craig write: “JPMorgan’s directors may have decided that Mr. Dimon, as his peers may, should get a raise, but to ordinary Americans — and possibly to regulators — the decision to increase his compensation may seem curious given the banner penalties that federal authorities have extracted from the bank.”

    F.B.I. WAS AFTER COHEN, SAYS DOCTOR IN SAC TRIAL  |  On his second day of cross-examination, Dr. Sidney Gilman, the star witness in the government’s insider trading case against Mathew Martoma, a former portfolio manager at SAC Capital Advisors, testified that the F.B.I.’s real target was Steven A. Cohen, SAC’s founder, not him or Mr. Martoma. “I am only a grain of sand, as is Mr. Martoma,” Dr. Gilman said under questioning by Mr. Martoma’s lawyer on Thursday, Alexandra Stevenson and Matthew Goldstein write in DealBook.

    Dr. Gilman, taking the witness stand for the third day in a row, testified that when F.B.I. agents began questioning him about providing inside information to Mr. Martoma, they told him they were “really after a man named Steven A. Cohen.”

    Throughout the cross-examination, Mr. Martoma’s defense lawyers continued their barrage of questions aimed at discrediting Dr. Gilman, who is 81 years old, by casting doubt on his ability to remember key events. Though Dr. Gilman served as a consultant to analysts and traders working at many hedge funds and money managers, he was unable to recall specific details about some of his meetings.

    SAGA BETWEEN TIME WARNER AND CHARTER HEATS UP  |  “Time Warner’s response to Charter Communications’ $37.8 billion offer is all about subtlety,” Steven M. Davidoff writes in the Deal Professor column. When Charter Communications proposed a purchase price of $132.50 a share last week, Time Warner Cable criticized the offer as being “grossly inadequate,” insisting that Charter’s price was much too low.

    But Time Warner’s chairman and chief executive, Robert D. Marcus, also said in a statement that his board was “open to a transaction with Charter at a price of $160 per TWC share.” With the statement, Time Warner’s board is essentially executing an “Airgas defense,” Mr. Davidoff writes, meaning it is giving the board a way to fight a hostile bid. But Time Warner’s board may not be planning to use a true Airgas defense, instead setting a price to both convince shareholders that Charter is undervaluing the company and to pre-empt shareholders, and possibly eager hedge funds, who could try to set a lower price.

    Mr. Davidoff writes: “The Time Warner board is thus setting up a repeat situation. By doing the financial and legal work to show that Charter’s bid undervalues the company, the Time Warner board is going to make it harder for Charter-nominated directors to accept a lower price.”

    In more Time Warner news, T. Rowe Price, which is one of the biggest investors in the country and has stakes in both Time Warner and Charter, is urging the two cable companies to sit down at the bargaining table, David Gelles writes in DealBook. In a letter to Time Warner, T. Rowe did not specify a price at which it would like to see the deal done, but the company’s holdings — 5.2 percent of Charter and about 2.3 percent of Time Warner Cable — suggests that it wants to see Charter get a good deal rather than overpay.

    ON THE AGENDA  |  The World Economic Forum continues in Davos, Switzerland. The forum’s last official day is Saturday. Lloyd C. Blankfein, the chief executive of Goldman Sachs, is on CNBC at 8 a.m. and Bloomberg TV at 8:45 a.m. Eric E. Schmidt, the chief executive of Google, is on CNBC at 8:30 a.m. Howard D. Schultz, the chief executive of Starbucks, is on CNBC at 9 a.m. Chefs Daniel Boulud and Marcus Samuelsson are on CNBC at 12:30 p.m. and 2 p.m., respectively. George Soros is on Bloomberg TV at 6 a.m. Representative Eric Cantor is on Bloomberg TV at 11 a.m.

    ACKMAN’S HERBALIFE FEUD CONTINUES  |  Herbalife, a nutritional supplements company that has been under scrutiny since late 2012, has put up a good fight against William A. Ackman, who has repeatedly said he thinks the company is a pyramid scheme. But on Thursday, Herbalife faced a new foe in Senator Edward J. Markey of Massachusetts, who sent letters to federal regulators urging them to investigate the company, William Alden and Michael S. Schmidt write in DealBook.

    But here’s the twist: Mr. Ackman donated $32,400 to the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee last year, the same day Mr. Markey won the Democratic primary to fill the seat previously held by John F. Kerry. Then, a little more than a month later, the Democratic senatorial committee gave $45,400 to Mr. Markey’s campaign. While there are no direct links between Mr. Ackman and Mr. Markey’s campaign, Mr. Markey’s action on Thursday nevertheless introduced a political element into the Herbalife slug fest. Shares of Herbalife dropped more than 10 percent after Mr. Markey’s letters were disclosed.

    GOLDMAN CONSIDERS CHAT BAN  |  On the heels of a rough few months for multi-dealer chat rooms, Goldman Sachs is considering prohibiting its traders from using person-to-person instant-messaging services to communicate internally, including those created by Bloomberg, Yahoo and AOL. The move is part of an effort to prevent sensitive information from being transmitted beyond the bank, The Wall Street Journal reports.

    Last month, a number of banks, including JPMorgan Chase, Deutsche Bank, the Lloyds Banking Group and the Royal Bank of Scotland, banned traders from communicating in multi-bank chat rooms because the chat rooms were thought to play a role in currency manipulation. And a few months before that, Bloomberg News admitted to using Bloomberg terminals, which allow users to track markets and trade, as well as instant message, to spy on companies. But while banning chats could stem the flow of private information, it could also have a deleterious effect on one aspect of trader life: romance.
